Chapter 1: rapture in the dark

It begins, like most things do, as a dream.

Something real and something not.

Or maybe it begins with Death, thrust into being at the start of all things milliseconds after the first star blinks to life. For Death, there are no grey areas. Only the time before It is created and the time after. A soul that has died and a soul that is yet to die. There’s a certain loveliness to the absolutes, a sort of freedom to be found within their harsh boundaries.

Death, in all of Its divinity, casts Itself outward and fills the gaps between all things with Its steady presence. It curves along the record grooves of time with neither disappointment nor pride at what it finds, merely listening to the harpstrings of fate chime their unending melody to the tune of the mortal cycle for an audience of one. There is only this song to note each soul as it passes and Death’s swift answer to mark the hours or the eons.

That, and the dreaming.

Reaping is a mostly passive act, all told. Death expands to fill the space where the soul is and then retracts, shifting as needed to fulfill Its sacred duty. But a creature that dies in its dreaming demands an extraction of sorts– a foray into the murky realm where all dreams come to pass rather than a simple passing collection. When beings began to dream in a time now long past, Death had what It considers to have been a first realization: there are perhaps a great many things that take place in a mortal life beyond their creation and their dying. Worlds dense with emotion and thought, cut apart and pasted haphazardly back together by their subconscious minds as they sleep.

So when a creature dies in its dreams, Death grants Itself permission to linger. In time It learns to drift from one dream to another in a catalogue of minds, collecting pieces of what occupies their thoughts. An small indulgence, if It were to have the capacity for such a thing. A passing observation of the way their customs always yield to time and are replaced by some strange new set. Death pieces together mosaic tiles of aspirations and horrific plagues and the rise and fall of empires; children and lost lovers and even skeletal visions of Itself, flimsy imitations clawing half-heartedly at their lifelines.

These are the simplest to reap, Death has found. The souls that die in dreaming are compliant, convinced that death is merely another part of their subconscious narrative. This interests Death as well– there is always a narrative, even if it twists and curls around itself in the telling.

Dogs dream up birds to chase, children craft half-formed misadventures, a work horse runs free in fields it will never see. Through their eyes, Death learns of the many worlds it will never inhabit and the creatures it will never see. Souls are all the same at their ending.

Perhaps the only drawback to the dreamscape is the thinning of the veil between life and death, suspending the soul in a curious sort of limbo where the door between the two is no longer so tightly shut. It is the difference between a lone, hushed rhythm and the chatter of a crowded city street, bombarding Death’s consciousness with a wall of unadulterated sound. With time, Death has learned to drown their voices to a dull static– present, but not as oppressive as it once was.

But some still dream loudly, a persistent clawing at the back of Death’s consciousness following It through each passing dream.

None so much as the Witch Killer. 

Other killers have dreamt of conquest, of valor, of retribution. Alexander the Great dreamt of the same kill night after night, plunging the sharpened tip of his pike through the chest of a faceless enemy only to find that the man was himself. Julius Caesar dreamt of blood flowing through the Rhine, Pompey either at his side or at the end of his sword. The Witch Killer, Death finds, dreams of things that are soft; hazy visions of a gentle life somewhere far beyond her reach, a place to rest her head beyond packed earth and the straw mattresses of nameless lovers. The dawning of a new day without a thought spared to her hunt.

Death does not have to enter the Witch Killer’s dreams to know this. It can hear her mind ricocheting through the dreamscape as though a voice calling out across a vast ocean and catching on the wind. As much as Death is discontent with the volume, It cannot help the intrigue that blooms slowly beneath the barrage of her dreaming.

Witches have killed and witches have been killed, certainly, though it has never before been such a closed-group affair. Death has knowledge of civil wars, but this crusade doesn’t fit their definition. It has seen women kill, but hardly so brazenly as this one does. It has watched the souls of other killers burn bright and fast, destroyed by their peers or their own hand as punishment for their hubris. But this one stays and stays, winding up and down the Eastern Seaboard unchallenged as she amasses power.

Death decides, at what feels like long last, to dip into her dreams just once. Perhaps the booming noise might lessen after being indulged, a pressure valve being relieved. It slips from the final dream of a blue whale and finds Itself peering through a twisting landscape of overlarge tree branches that wind in and out of sight. Death looks down to where the forest floor should be and finds only an endless dark beneath the maze of limbs. Above, an impenetrable canopy leaves no glimpse of the sky.

Beyond the dark, it’s unnaturally quiet here. Not in the way of rustling leaves or a lone woodpecker rapping in the distance, but as though the shell around the Witch Killer’s mind is not the semipermeable membrane that Death is used to and instead resembles a heavy iron door. There is no human clatter seeping in at the edges. The melody of fate barely trills here– it would be unnoticeable altogether, probably, if Death were not so entirely attuned to it. The deprivation brings about an odd sensation to take the place of sound, a near-relief from duty that Death has never thought to conceptualize. A lessening that should not be possible; there either is duty or there is not. Death has a purpose or it does not exist. 

This shift should be jarring. It should be an aberration that Death takes note of and tucks into Its expansive memory before leaving this place, resolving not to revisit unless unavoidably necessary. Notably, it is neither.

A nearby branch sways and Death tracks the motion, finding a purple and brown blur leaping through the scene.

The blur is singing, maybe, or else talking to itself. Thick rays of sunlight find their way through the dense foliage overhead like rivulets of water in each place the blur steps, a spotlit path glistening in the darkness. The shafts fall so quickly that it’s hard to tell if the light is following the figure or vice versa, a synchronicity that feels almost unsettling in its precision.

The whole dream is rather empty, Death decides. No clear antagonist for the blur to be running from, no visible goal to be chasing. The blur, which Death knows to be the Witch Killer, bounds onward through the trees anyway, carrying no traceable sense of urgency. Only a floaty sort of feeling, buoying her from one branch to the next for what must be hours until at last she stops, entirely motionless at last.

Death watches the dark locks of her hair arrange themselves down her back, unruly in a way that a less attentive being would mistake for unintentional. Her purple shift dress billows down to mid-calf, the garment a cross between a night dress and a ball gown. The outline of it shifts the longer Death looks, as though the Witch Killer’s mind can’t be bothered with the details so long as the concept comes across. After a beat she looks around the branch she’s found herself on, settling down into the crook between trunk and limb of the tree that rivals a house in size. Death halts Its pursuit as well, taking in the transition from blur to woman. The dress falls to the Witch Killer’s ankles when she tugs her knees up, brushing the tops of her bare feet.

Most of all, Death notes the peace she exudes– head tilting back and eyes closing as she rests beneath the latest beam of light. It’s thicker than real sunlight, Death notes. Almost like silk where it slips through the leaves. The Witch Killer must be in a pleasant mood tonight to have such a tranquil dream (if it is night, even. Death has learned that mortals dream at all kinds of curious times and in all manner of places.) Death knows it isn’t always so– there are times when the call of her dreaming turns violent, the scratching at It’s consciousness shifting to an angry, clawing thing.

A dusting of freckles coats her otherwise unmarred face, eyelashes fluttering restlessly against high cheek bones. The killers that Death has seen before have borne scars and calluses and shattered bones that never settle right beneath their skin, imprints of hard-won battles or unexpectedly resilient victims leaving their mark. But this killer could be anyone– any woman walking through any town with no blood on her hands. It’s unusual in a way that Death can’t help but linger on, gaze tracking the arched bridge of her nose.

This must have been enough, Death thinks. The Witch Killer’s presence no longer tugs at It, quieted here at last. Now it needs only stay quiet, joining the hum of all other minds in docile compliance and freeing the space it has occupied in Death’s consciousness. Death is rounding out this thought when it feels the weight of the Witch Killer’s eyes, twin blue laser sights flitting back and forth with a calculated indifference.

The narrative of her dreaming must have changed, forming a purpose at last. Death turns Its focus around to where the Witch Killer must be staring but finds only the same branches as before. Death waits for whatever has drawn her attention to appear. Not a leaf stirs. Death settles Its attention back on the Witch Killer, who remains fixated.

It’s a curious thing. No aspect of a dream has been concealed from Death before, leaving it to wonder at a mortal’s thoughts. Though perhaps the Witch Killer is also waiting for something that has not yet come. Maybe this dream is recurring, a pacing that she has figured out by now and Death has not.

“Who might you be?” The Witch Killer asks at last. “Someone powerful, surely, to have slipped past my defenses. I have to admit, it’s impressive. No witch has managed it yet.”

Death turns again, beginning to feel a faint irritation at the still empty scene.

The frustration is mirrored in the Witch Killer’s sigh. “What are you looking at? Who else could I possibly be speaking to?”

The Witch Killer cannot see It, Death knows. There is nothing to see. Formless at Its inception, Death exists as a concept, a brief segment in the cycle of all mortal things. But she stares anyway, surveying the space where Death has taken up Its vantage point. There is an inkling before there is a thought, a shiver up the spine that serves as a lightning strike and the responding clap of thunder in one.

Death’s consciousness narrows to this moment, retracting Its reach through entire galaxies like a star fusing before a supernova. It turns away from the safety of the empty forest a final time and faces her, an entirely new sensation beginning to take root. More than anything, it is the lazy grin growing on the Witch Killer’s face that forces the feeling to bloom.

She clicks her tongue in admonishment, angling her body more fully in Death’s direction. “Came all this way in your finery with nothing to say? You must know how pathetic this is becoming.” The words are harsh but the tone is lilting, a juxtaposition that Death has yet to interpret the use for in human communication. Still the words register, each syllable processing individually before linking together to form a notion that demands to be acknowledged. Acquiescing, Death pulls itself free from the snare of the Witch Killer’s gaze and looks down.

Where there should be nothing at all stands the lower half of a human form in a gown of sorts, layers of skirts in forest green falling from an embroidered bodice. Contrary to the constantly shifting foliage, the body and its adornments are meticulously defined. Each stitch of the little yellow and pink flowers has a clear point where it enters and exits the fabric, each swirling emerald vine following a path all its own. A breeze rustles the skirts, rippling just so to press against the outline of what can only be a thigh.

Death, acting of a will that feels not entirely Its own, forms a neural pathway and lifts Its hands. Hands that It has never had, should never have had. Hands that stare back up at It with pink palms turned skyward, faint blue veins running down to forearms covered in soft downy hair before disappearing beneath the cuff of a sleeve. Light falls through the leaves as it had for the Witch Killer’s wandering, thick and honey sweet where it pools in the new-formed cradle of Death’s fingers.

It comes with a suddenness that borders on sharp, the awareness of inhabiting a body. The weight bears down on Death all at once, the pressure of bone settling beneath flesh and the fabric of a cloak draped across a throat nearly unbearable. Death’s focus– Its eyes– flicker between still-raised hands oozing light and the Witch Killer, unperturbed and awaiting a response.

For the first time, Death gives a name to the sensation that has rooted and bloomed and climbs to fill Its throat, pressing in from all sides: terror. It tries to pull Its consciousness away, to rise above the canopy of the Witch Killer’s dreaming and slip away to safer ground far beyond this plane. But the terror is occupying too much space and Its consciousness no longer seems vast enough to hold it, confined to only this tiny mortal skull. This new form– Death’s body– responds to the command with a jerky motion, reeling backwards with an unanticipated amount of force. 

One foot slips from the branch beneath it and the other follows until the world is tilting, the Witch Killer’s puzzled face slipping from view before the branches and leaves overhead are all that there is to be seen.

Death is falling.

It is as much a relief as it is another layer to the unshakable fear, hastening the unrelenting pounding that Death is coming to understand as Its heartbeat. Air whistles past Its ears, sharp and high, until at last Death falls free from the clutches of the Witch Killer’s dream entirely. The heartbeat is gone, along with the body that housed it. Released from Its cage of bone, Death’s consciousness is eager to drift out once more, shaking off the final vestiges of inhabiting a mortal shell.

The terror leaves, but its echo does not.

After the incident, Death makes a clear decision not to return to the Witch Killer’s dreams. Instead It delves in and out of the dreamscape with an intentional swiftness, pulling Its focus to far reaching galaxies before her call can register.

Death reaps, as It was made to do. Death pays close attention to the harpstrings of fate, diligently tending to fragile strands. Time passes as usual, which is to say all at once and not at all.

It returns to the practice of perusing mortal dreams only when the sharp line of her jaw blurs in memory, however slight. Important global affairs could be taking place that Death should prepare for, It reasons. It can be nice to be aware of a massacre before it comes to pass, or spot an erupting volcano before a city has turned to ash. So Death allows Itself to glide through the dreaming of a great soaring egret, passing from the daggered end of the bird’s beak and into the fish that it strikes. Death slinks with a panther as it stalks imagined prey through the night, a creature so succulent that the panther can already taste the tender flesh. The beast wakes with its canines still angled to bite.

It whispers through the fraught dreaming of a child, sleeping for the first time after the death of her mother. She perches midship on a small wooden boat, adrift on swift-moving waters. The girl cries into her hands as Death watches, raising her face only to watch helplessly as the current bears her onward. The water grows choppier, splashing at the sides of the boat, until at last a great bird soars down from the sky. Or least it begins its descent as a bird, for it turns into her grandmother by the time it lands, extending impossibly long arms outward to envelope the child. At once, the waters are tranquil. In the safe harbor of her grandmother’s arms, the child’s sleep becomes dreamless.

Death tries and tries not to think about the palms, about the feeling of blood rushing beneath tender skin and strands of hair tickling at an ear. It tries not to think of the way it felt to have a pair of eyes stare directly at It and not through, how that piercing gaze may as well have been a blade to the throat.

The Witch Killer, never one to be mistaken as merciful, does not grow any less sonorous. If anything, the call of her dreaming begins to sound like a siren song echoing in every other dream It enters. Beyond them, even. Not that Death would know of such a thing, not really, but It’s quietly startled to find that It can’t shake the curiosity of what it might be like to have a physical body for a few moments more. To have arms that reach and bones that break. To be watched by those eyes and asked questions by that mouth. No one has ever asked Death a question and been heard; souls have long been wrested from their mortal shells by the time It arrives to make Its claim. Any attempts at bargaining or final pleas have long passed, falling only on the ears of the ones who make them.

But Death thinks of the way her lips formed the words, barely parting from one another as though any further effort would have been unthinkable. For the Witch Killer to have felt anything more than a surface level curiosity at Death’s appearance in her dreams would have been unthinkable. Though it wasn’t Death that she saw, not really. Only a wayward intruder, a nameless witch come to meddle. Death considers this anomaly with only a partially falsified disinterest. It reasons that there is no harm in trying once more, just to check that it hadn’t been a fluke. Maybe the Witch Killer hadn’t actually seen It at all, or maybe she was only capable of doing so once. It’s worth finding an answer, Death decides.

When Death returns to the Witch Killer’s dreaming mind, It does not find the tranquil woods It leapt through before. Tonight it is a crowded tavern, bustling with noise and motion. Death, confined once more to the body that has plagued Its thoughts, struggles to adjust to the damp air and the tang of sweat from the swell of bodies that fill the room. Candelabras swing above them, radiating further heat that stings against Death’s already oversensitive face.

Somewhere in the room a band plays a jaunty tune, though the fiddle carries far louder than its counterparts. Each pass of the bow over the strings grates against Death’s eardrums, though It must admit that the melody is rather pleasant. A bout of nausea passes that has Death curling Its hands into tight fists– a brief but overwhelming discomfort that mimics what It knows of seasickness. Death breathes in once and out again, slowly learning to wield the machine of Its body. When finally the music is more a tune than a wailing sound and the light does not force It to squint, Death returns to taking stock.

There is no ceiling above them to support the chandeliers, only a great endless dark that the top of each wall fades into. And the walls themselves shift if one looks too close, the Witch Killer’s mind too restless to flesh them out enough to maintain full stability. Most jarring of all, though, is that everyone around It is faceless.

The dancers in swirling dresses, the woman pouring drinks behind the bar, and even the fiddle player that Death can see after moving further into the space. Everyone is indistinct save for the woman twirling in the middle of the room, rosey-cheeked and grinning.

The Witch Killer dances wild and free, her long hair swinging out in a loose braid behind her. Tonight her dress is white, with great round sleeves that cinch at her wrists and a skirt that billows out each time she twirls. Her purple throughline remains, found in this dream as a sash around her waist. Death latches onto the image, watching her move so that It can minimize the brand new and utterly unpleasant sensation of sweat rolling down Its back beneath the bodice of tonight’s dress.

The Witch Killer is not dancing with anyone, noticeably. Rather the crowd ebbs and flows around her, near enough to call her a part of it but never close enough to touch. Her movements appear to be an indistinguishable mix of proper form and improvisation, like someone who took lessons when they were very young and remembers the footwork on muscle memory alone. Or like someone who has only ever seen the dance performed from behind cracked doors or through windows, always sure to be out of sight.

Death doesn’t notice that the band’s song has ended until all that’s left to fill the space is a dense buzzing. Were it to inspect this, Death may find the sound to be a passable imitation of dozens of overlapping human voices in conversation. The harsh breaths from Its own lungs, forced out through Its nose, seem to be just as loud. The Witch Killer takes note of the music’s ending and sways towards the bar, the imagined patrons parting as she passes. Death notices that she doesn’t seem to be walking, not really– it’s more a glide, the mechanics of real life lost on the dreaming mind. The slant of her spine curves downward as she leans against the bartop and signals the shimmering form on the other side for a drink. The Witch Killer sighs, tossing her long braid back over her shoulder. Death doesn’t notice that It’s followed her across the room until it can see the errant strands that have escaped from the braid and frizz outward in the humidity of the room, clinging to the fabric at her back. When her voice comes, low and almost bored, Death flinches at the sound’s proximity.

“Back again to haunt me?” She drawls. A glass appears in one of her outstretched palms, filled to the brim and already sweating against the wood of the bar. Death watches her fingers curl around it from over her shoulder, the Witch Killer still not turning to face It. This, oddly, manifests as a sharp twinge that Death can feel at the base of Its skull. 

“If I’ve killed you, I don’t remember your face. You won’t find the retribution that you seek here, ghost.”

Until recent events, Death has felt very little by way of judgment or glee throughout the course of time. The exception to this steadfastness has come exclusively in the form of elusive souls, somehow slipping past Death like thieves in the night and refusing to settle. Instead they torment the mortal plane beyond their allotted time, creating chaos both in and outside of the dreamscape. For these, Death harbors considerable animosity.

“I am no ghost,” Death asserts.

The voice comes out light and melodic, a conjoined effort from lungs and vocal cords and a brain and a tongue that have never been there before. It is far different from the scraping transmission that Death is used to associating with Itself, carrying a weaker projection and a tone that It isn’t entirely sure how to wield.

The Witch Killer turns around at last, keeping one arm propped on the wood to hold her glass while she pins Death down with a lone raised brow.

“Would you look at that, she speaks. Do I get a name this time?”

Death opens Its mouth. Closes it again. “What is yours?”

There’s no real reason for It to ask this beyond a remembrance of human custom. It already knows the answer. Still the Witch Killer seems taken aback, darting her eyes around the room before they settle back on Death. She gives a slight nod, as though conceding something. “Sure, I’ll play. It’s Agatha. Agatha Harkness.”

Agatha.

Agatha Harkness. Death rolls the name along the back of Its tongue. Not the Witch Killer, then, however fitting it may be. Death wonders for the first time if the title is something It gave the mortal of Its own volition or if It had heard the name once, somewhere now forgotten. Death decides that it doesn’t matter anymore either way, now that she’s Agatha Harkness.

Agatha lifts her hand from her glass and brings it around, extending it out towards Death. Water droplets gather at the edge of her palm before sliding down to where the bones of her wrist swell beneath the skin, left over from the condensation on her beer. Death watches them fall, wondering what the motion is meant to convey. Maybe Agatha is going to perform a display of magic, some greeting that witches give one another.

But after a moment her hand merely falls, coming to rest limply at her sides. A muscle in her jaw flexes as she squints her eyes at Death.

“So you’re really not going to give me a name? Even after breaking into my head?”

“I have none to give you,” Death answers honestly.

“I’m going to pretend to believe you. Do I get any sort of clue, then? Any hint as to what the hell you might be up to in here?”

Death falters, the silence stretching out. “I’m not up to anything. I’m not sure this is ‘up,’ exactly. Direction can be difficult for me to intuit.”

“Great. And now when I tell you that I have killed witches for far less than this arrogant and nosy little display, are you going to pretend not to know that, either?”

Death shakes Its head. “That I do know. You’ve killed a great many witches, Agatha Harkness.”

This gives Agatha pause, her mouth closing where she had been preemptively ready with a half-formed retort. She purses her lips, cocking her head and taking her braid with it.

“And you are not afraid?”

“I am not,” Death confirms.

Agatha shakes her head and rolls her eyes– a vaulted ceiling appearing for the moment that they’re angled upwards before disappearing again. She gives Death a pointed look. “So you’re saying that the beautiful woman that may or may not have been conjured by my touch-deprived subconscious is not the least bit bothered to hear I have a penchant for murder, is that it?” She laughs, but it’s a stale sound. “How very convenient for me.”

But Death’s attention was snagged long before Agatha finished her thought, a strange belated heat from the bodies around It flashing in Its cheeks. Beauty is not a concept It has ever thought to associate with. Past Agatha’s shoulder, beyond the bar’s edge, a framed mirror hangs on the wall. Death cranes Its neck to catch Its reflection.

For all the blurriness of Agatha’s dreaming, the body that she has conjured for Death is startling clear. Not a detail is lost in the haze, from Its wide brown eyes to the curved tip of Its nose. Dark lashes bat against Death’s ruddy cheeks when it blinks. It raises Its hands and splays them wide, running Its touch from jaw to brow. The feeling of skin on skin is jolting, nearly electric in the places that catch against the grooves of Its fingertips.

Death decides that there must be something mischievous about Agatha’s spirit, some sort of peculiarity that It cannot yet see. Whatever strangeness it is has allowed her enough awareness to spot Death in her dreams, but not enough to recognize It. The limitations of her mortality, perhaps, demanding that Death have a form. Something tangible for her to conceive.

Death again drinks in the dress swathed around Its body, the forest green hood laid delicately over it. Glinting at Its hip is the hilt of a knife, onyx and menacing when the shifting of Its cloak allows the weapon to be seen. A small touch, but nonetheless a show that Agatha understands Its capacity for danger even if she cannot yet place it. To have been assigned this body– or gifted it, rather– fascinates Death to no end. It lifts Its hand from Its face, watching through the mirror as the fingers flex and relax on command, tendons straining beneath the skin. How interesting it is to wield such an instrument in the undefined space between true human function and the malleability of dreaming, bound to an incomprehensible mix of the two dictated entirely by the mind of Agatha Harkness.

Death supposes It could alter this form if It so chose. Could take the build of some other creature, so long as It was visible to Agatha. Death thinks again of the soaring egret and the great blue whale, sinking endlessly to the ocean floor. But Death finds the black pits of Its own irises through the glass and unabashedly revels in the sensation of being something that no one has ever been before or ever will be again. There is a thrill to being singular.

Agatha clears her throat. “If you’re not a ghost and you’re not going to make an attempt to kill me at the moment, do you know how to dance?”

Death angles away from the mirror at the words, sucked in Agatha’s orbit once more. She still leans against the bar, though the glass in her hand is empty now. Death would have liked to see her drink it.

“What?”

Agatha shrugs. “Sooner or later I’ll figure out what you’re doing in my dreams, and I’ll kill you and be done with it. But right now I’m sleeping and quite honestly I would like to shut my brain off. Do you know how to dance?” She spaces out each word of the question, saying them slow and loud as if they’re rocks she can pelt Death with. Death spares a glance to the room full of fake patrons. Some are clustered around brown blobs serving as tables surrounded by too many chairs, others are already dancing as the music starts up again. She– It– looks back at Agatha’s waiting face.

“No. I’ve never tried.”

Agatha’s nostrils flare before her expression settles back to neutrality. The drink in her hand disappears before Death’s eyes, leaving only a ring stained into the wood.

“Suit yourself. You can either figure it out or have a seat, but I’m done talking.”

Death watches her go, winding back to the center of the room where she starts to dance again with her eyes closed and her arms flung into the air. It stays as the band plays song after song, until Agatha turns into a twirling, candlelit blur and the already blurry patrons fade into nothing more than the concept of a crowd. Death, who has never been good with the passing of time, does not know if it has been minutes or hours by the time Agatha finally stops, rounding out a final spin just as the song’s last note plays. She opens her eyes just as the dream begins to shimmer and dissolve, the world around them turning to dust, and fixes her gaze on Death. It catches the briefest wink, mischief alight in her shining eyes, before Agatha returns to her waking life once more.

This time, when the floors disappear and Death begins to fall, the terror does not come.

After an existence comprised entirely of drifting by and passing through, Death finds an unexpected delight in tarrying in Agatha Harkness’ dreams. It’s not quite settling in, but something close to it. The nearest to stillness that It can get. 

For her part, Agatha brings to each of their meetings an air of exasperation that seems semi-permanent, at least when Death comes to call. At any moment she swings from annoyed to amused, one or the other invariably winning out by the night’s end. Death enjoys the grey area between them though, when Agatha’s carefully chosen words land like sugar coated barbs and laughter bubbles out of her seemingly by accident. The sound begins to occupy a secondary space to the song of fate in Its mind, recognizable when it chimes across the great dark of space and time, familiar in its refrain.

Death learns quite quickly that Agatha’s mind is both sharp and deeply imaginative, sending her on dreams unlike anything It has seen before. It finds her walking overtop the glassy surface of a vast grass ocean surrounded on all sides by a boundless blue sky. The landscape gives the illusion of being two dimensional save for Agatha gliding easily across the water, peering down at the unnatural emerald green of the kelp forest waving below. Death would almost assume the sea is frozen over if it weren’t for the periodic glint of fish scales catching the light, swimming merrily through the crystalline water and rustling the kelp stalks.

The brightness of the sky is startling. Death lifts a hand to shield Its eyes, though there’s no sun in sight. Death refrains from saying that she wasn’t entirely opposed to the situation, per se. Surely it was frightening, but is that not part of the thrill?

What is fear to one that can never truly die? What is pain to a body that can be abandoned at a moment’s notice, if Death so chooses?

For all of the unpleasantness she has already felt in this body, It finds It has a vested interest in learning how it works, limitations and all. No harsh boundaries or sacred orders to uphold. There is only the loose guidance of Agatha’s mind and the unwieldy mechanics of this body– the rest is wonderfully unknown.

And then there is this: a thickly shrouded allusion to a future being offered by Agatha’s hands. If Death will only answer her questions, Agatha will continue to look Its way. To share her thoughts with It. Perhaps even to teach It about this physical form of magic, waiting just beneath the skin to be called forth again.

Death has never lied before. Had never spoken in any true sense until recently, as it is. But in the spirit of trying new things she sighs, straightening her shoulders. “What would you like to know?”

Agatha’s smirk deepens– someone used to being declared victorious on every battlefield she’s ever crossed. She flips her hand in the air impatiently. “Good girl. I’ll take your name, for starters.”

Death thinks back to the grieving child’s dreaming, nestling into the soft downy feathers of her great bird-grandmother’s chest. ‘Tú mamá está mejor ahora, Benita. Es simplemente parte del río de la vida,’ her grandmother had cooed, calming the waves with the trill of her voice. The girl peered over a wing to find that the river had never been water at all, but was instead made from the glittering souls of all who had been lost. The girl took in their tranquility, and her grandmother’s conviction, and let her fears turn to doves.

“Rio,” Death says, wondering if it always feels this good to tell a lie. “My name is Rio Vidal.”

Death discovers a veritable flood of new and unpleasant smells when Agatha dreams of a village that is more a cluster of structures around a wide bend in the road than it is an intentional settlement. A horseshoe-shaped assembly of outcasts that gave up before making it to the real towns that lie in either direction.

All of their houses and shops are the same greyish brown, old wood slatted together and covered by thatched roofs leaking grey smoke into a grey sky. Death has to admit that the whole scene feels rather bleak. Rudimentary, she decides, when she thinks of the worlds she has seen in dreams from far flung galaxies– whole cities made of glass, cathedrals built from beams of light. Death wrinkles her nose.

It appears to be a market day here, with stalls built from overturned crates and tables dragged out of their kitchens lining the road. The doors of businesses sit propped open to entice potential customers, some calling out their prices to the passing crowd. Women fist the skirts of their rough-hewn work dresses as they bustle back and forth, some boasting baskets full of vegetables and fabric scraps and other items that Death can’t keep in her sight long enough to make out. The few men she sees seem less harried in their dealings, haggling lazily with one another or else discussing town news that doesn’t seem particularly surprising to any of them. It reminds her of the tavern with all of the bodies and the noise, but here Agatha is not carefree and dancing, and there is no fiddle player to liven up the scene. In fact, Death doesn’t spot Agatha until she physically runs into her, slamming her shoulder into Death’s back and sending her stumbling forward.

Death turns to look at Agatha and finds herself smiling in spite of the pain still settling between her shoulder blades. Agatha has a work dress on to match the uniform of every other woman in the town, but hers is, of course, a deep plum. Perhaps Death is not the only one who revels in singularity. She looks down at her arms to find that she, too, is wearing this new garb in place of her usual designated gown. Notably, it’s still green. The fact leaves a feeling that Death can’t quite place in her chest.

“Oh Rio, how nice of you to show up and stand directly in my fucking way,” Agatha spits, an octave above her usual tone to denote her displeasure. Dusting off her dress as if touching Rio had stained her, she pulls the wicker basket on her arm higher up into the crook of her elbow from where it had slipped, tucking the limb against her body.

Death has thought of the name that she gave Agatha since it first left her mouth. Reaping souls and wondering what could have driven her to say it, or if Agatha really believed it to be the truth, or if she should try again to pick something new. But hearing Agatha call her by her name, even in anger, settles something that Rio didn’t know had grown restless in her chest, picking it up and smoothing it back out again.

“You seem stressed,” Rio observes. Agatha shoots her a withering glare, raising her head from where she’d been reading from a list in her hand. She doesn’t dignify the comment with a response before stomping off into the crowd.

Rio trails behind as Agatha shimmies her way up to the front of each stall she passes, exchanging a handful of murmured words with the vendors before storming back off again. The street seems to grow unnaturally broader with each stall they stop at, a new crush of bodies pouring in to fill what little available space there is until Rio has to slow down to weave through them, shoulder checking the townsfolk as Agatha had done to her just to keep her in sight.

“What are you looking for?” She calls to Agatha’s back, mildly surprised when she actually turns.

The dream flickers, momentarily turning into a ghost town when Agatha’s eyes find Rio. It’s as if her mind only has enough energy to focus on one or the other and decides to tunnel in, but if Agatha herself notices, it doesn’t show. No longer boxed in, Rio takes a breath and enjoys the split second of silence that her ears are afforded.

Agatha brandishes the paper in her hand, mostly crumpled in her fist. “I just–” she huffs, pushing her hair back out of her face with the back of her free hand. “I know one of these stalls has got to have it. The potion won’t work without it.” She rolls her eyes like the answer should have been obvious. Like Rio’s childish questioning has cost her vital time. Agatha looks down at the list again before raising her head to scan the crowd, once again surging to fill the space at her mind’s silent command. She zeroes in on a stall further up the road that Rio can’t quite see through the swarm of people already trying to get to it, squaring her shoulders and preparing to take off.

Rio’s brow furrows. Agatha’s answer didn’t really clear things up one way or another despite her adamance, but she knows that to ask her the same question twice would only add to her frustration. Rio quite vividly remembers what happened the last time she pushed Agatha too far. In hopes of gaining some semblance of understanding, Rio considers what she knows of mortal dreaming. Perhaps, if she guesses correctly, she can avoid a night spent trudging through this grimy village and encourage Agatha to conjure something more exciting.

She takes in the tension laced through Agatha’s frame and pieces together tattered fragments of thousands of dreams, smudging the memories together until they make themselves into something useful. Bits of not-quite nightmares centered around running out of time or getting the words wrong no matter how many ways they try to say them. The frantic beating of a heart pulsing at the dream’s edges, no matter how low the stakes appeared from the outside. 

Rio tilts her head. Agatha’s dream is not quite so forthcoming with its narrative direction, but Rio knows enough to sense the mounting anxiety that she exudes as she works her way towards yet another vendor, ceaselessly thwarted in her search. It’s a quieter sort of stress, but it could be guiding Agatha’s dream nonetheless. And there may be nothing on her list after all, just a meaningless urgency driving her from stall to stall, dirtying the hem of her dress and winding the coil of frustration in her throat tighter and tighter.

Rio wonders what it is in Agatha’s waking life that has her so on edge, following her here and causing her subconscious to torment her so. If that’s something she’s allowed to ask.

A goat being led on a rope by a bored looking child bleats near Rio’s knee, the burst of sound jolting her from her thoughts. She looks down at it curiously, studying its slightly blurry shape and glassy eyes that point in two distinctly different directions. It stamps the dirt impatiently, as if it, too, is unnerved by the busy street and Agatha’s restless mind. Rio bends slightly at the waist to scratch at its head, studiously ignoring the barnyard stench radiating off of it. As soon as her fingers make contact, the goat’s fur turns to dust, leaving thin pink skin exposed to the air. The creature bleats again nervously, tugging at the rope slung around its throat to get further away from Rio. Her eyes shift slowly between her hand and the goat, synapses firing as one thought connects to another and forms an idea.

Death has witnessed all other dreams as a passing visitor, no more involved in the story than a reader is in a novel. Rio, however, is a part of this scene. She takes up just as much space as each of the stalls that Agatha stops at, just as much as the paper in her hand. A prickling sensation takes up residence behind the skin of Rio’s palms and has her spine straightening. Would there be any harm in testing how much control she has here in Agatha’s dream? In seeing if she can drag her off of this predetermined course and assuage whatever ails her, even if only slightly?

“Agatha,” she calls again. But Agatha doesn’t stop, squirming to fit between two broad-shouldered men blocking her path and slipping from view. Rio starts after her, wrestling her own way through. “Agatha!” She tries, louder now. “Can I help you find it?”

Agatha becomes visible again when she turns, the crowd stuttering out of sight once more. Her brows draw together and her eyes dart across Rio’s face. “Why?”

Rio shrugs one shoulder, darting the tip of her tongue out to wet her lips. “You said your potion won’t work without it. Would it not be easier to find if we were both searching?” She appeals. And then, almost as an afterthought, “let me help you.”

Agatha parts her lips before something in her face shifts, her nose scrunching when she shakes her head. “No. What good could your help do me? You’ll just get in my way again.”

Rio grinds her teeth. She is a god, by all definitions. A being of unimaginable power. Surely there is a way she can change the course of this dream. Surely Agatha’s stubbornness doesn’t outrank her on this. She tries a different approach, lowering her head just enough to look up at Agatha through her lashes like a lesser mammal showing deference.

“Could you perhaps remind me what it looks like, if nothing else? I’ll be sure not to impede your pursuit, I’m just having a difficult time picturing it at the moment,” Rio bashfully confesses.

Agatha’s hesitation is brief. Rio takes it as a win. “Notoriously, it’s lavender. You’ll know it when you see it.” Agatha gives Rio a final once over, adding “or perhaps you won’t” at a volume that doesn’t quite count as under her breath.

She scours the scene for signs of anything at all that could fit the vague description, misguidedly believing it’ll be a simple task given that all of her surroundings are a dirt and ash-stained monochrome. It doesn’t take long though to give up the conviction. The only glimpse of purple to be found in the marketplace is the occasional flash of Agatha’s dress as she darts past, back to ignoring Rio’s presence entirely.

Rio reasons that Agatha’s mind was never going to lead her in here, but still she scans the watercolor room and hopes to spot her target. Unsuccessful once more, she turns away.

The fences bear no gates, built from uneven wooden stakes crowded together in a row to keep out intruders. Some nestle beneath the small awning serving as their coop, while others poke their beaks into the yard’s soft soil exploratorily. So enthralled by this smaller, secondary society, Rio almost misses the lavender bush growing near the base of a large tree in the corner of the yard.

Relief washes down her spine. She goes to brace her hands on the fence and leap over it only to rip them away when the sharp points of the stakes dig into her palms. Rio looks again at the lavender bush, then at the stakes, then at the street still visible behind her. She shuffles slightly further from view before extending an arm, locking her elbow and willing magic into her veins. Her blood pulses green once, twice, before the magic bursts from her fingertips and obliterates the fence in front of her.

The chickens startle away from the blast site, tittering as they take up residence closer to the house. Rio shoots them an apologetic pout as she slips through the newly formed gap and crosses the yard, bending beside the bush to carefully pluck a handful of flowers. It’s not clear how much Agatha needs, but she can always come back if this isn’t enough.

Rio goes to leave the yard and find Agatha only to find herself face to face with two men. She knows there were no approaching footsteps to have warned her, only the split-second change of Agatha’s dream providing a roadblock. They say nothing, but their posture emanates an angry distrust, clenched fists and feet firmly planted.

Death feels alive inside the animal of her body, remembering how it felt for Agatha’s mind to try and drag her to a watery grave. Rio wonders exactly how murderous her subconscious might be, and how much of that is actually targeted at herself versus being Agatha’s general state of existence. She is a killer after all.

It’s a line of thinking worth following, but the soft hairs on the back of Rio’s neck and arms stand at attention to warn her of an impending attack. On an instinct that doesn’t feel entirely aligned with this body, she surges forward from the waist up and hisses at them. Though the change is fleeting, Rio feels her glottis become mobile like a snake’s, punching the air from her lungs to produce the sound. The men rear back in bewilderment and Rio takes the opportunity to dash back through the gap in the fence and insert herself into the horde once more, tucking the handful of flowers tightly to her chest.

When at last she finds Agatha, it’s not by the blur of her hair beelining through the crowd. It’s standing stock still with her list crumpled entirely to a pulp in her fist, staring ahead at what Rio assumes is their town square, or at least what passes for it. The space is less an intentional gathering place than a broad gap between two structures, blocked off at the far end by the only building in the village built from a white-grey stone instead of wooden boards. A tall, thin spire on the center of the roof  rises into the sky, capped with a sharp, rusted-looking cross.

Rio notes that Agatha doesn’t look afraid as some around them do, but is instead almost contemplative. A wistfulness that doesn’t entirely match the way she holds her muscles taut, poised to strike or flee at a moment’s notice.

Rio tracks where her eyes are focused– where everyone’s eyes are focused– and is surprised to find a witch tied to a thick wooden stake. The noise of her senseless wailing follows the sight, as though Rio had only needed to give the woman her full attention for the show to begin.

The witch’s limbs wriggle in a way that’s closer to vibrating, her shoulders tugging forward and out in an attempt to free her bound arms. The motion is so non-human that it draws Death’s eye, and she wonders if it strikes Agatha as odd too. She almost turns to ask but is cut short by a cheer going up from the mob, sounding off in response to heat and light springing up from the base of the stake and licking at the witch’s ankles.

They’re surely meant to be flames, but they don’t look quite right– a little too red and a little too bright, searing their outline into Rio’s retinas. As the flames grow higher, the witch begins to melt like candle wax, skin sloughing off of bone in wet waves. Rio blinks, shifting her gaze to Agatha’s profile.

“Is this something that happens? Outside of your dreams.”

Agatha huffs what she means to be a laugh, but it comes off too dry to be the sound Rio has come to know. “Of course it is–” she inclines her head, recalibrating, “or it was. Now they just hang us from the trees in the night. It’s quieter that way.” Agatha’s eyelashes flutter, dispelling the thought. “But everyone knows that.” Her lips pull down into a frown as she turns to Rio with a quizzical expression. “Why don’t you know that?”

Rio blinks at her, parsing through her memories. She supposes that maybe she does know that, if she pieces together a great many other things. But it’s not a phenomenon on the scale of the Black Plague or the Trojan War, so it doesn’t register as something that would have qualified for Death’s attention. Rio, however, sees the sweat beading at Agatha’s temples and the way her fist tightens impossibly around the paper and understands that it is certainly something that has qualified for hers. She deflects.

“Is this how you kill them?”

Agatha isn’t quick enough to hide the shock on her face before Rio sees it– brows lifting high and lips parting reflexively. There is something skeptical too, a sort of disbelief that she clings to. She drops her face back to neutrality, the witch’s cries growing noticeably quieter when Agatha’s focus gathers.

“No, not at all.”

“How do you do it then? Does it feel like this? So loud?”

Agatha’s nostrils flare, her eyes scanning Rio’s earnest face. Neither of them notice at first that the townsfolk are gone again, the village around them falling utterly silent. They’re standing much closer than necessary without the other bodies crowding them in, and Rio can see the smattering of freckles across the bridge of Agatha’s nose.

As if returning to herself, Agatha takes a step back, putting space between them. The motion draws her gaze down, snagging on the lavender bouquet still tucked tightly against Rio’s ribs. Something in the stormy blue of her eyes clears briefly before it’s hidden again, shifting back to their steadfast calculation.

“If I show you, what will you give me in return?”

“Anything you want.”

Lying, Rio finds, does not always feel so nice. Sometimes feels like something slimy settling in the pit of her gut and burning her throat on the way down, even in the face of Agatha’s responding devilish grin.

“Then you have yourself a deal, Rio Vidal.”

Unlike in Agatha’s other dreams, Rio’s surprised to note that each woman she sees has a face. Some are more obscured than others, but all have distinct features; green eyes here, a curved nose there, a head of coiled hair disappearing as she dips into a tent. Rio wonders if they have names, too. If Agatha would even know them.

Close by to the bonfire, another witch looks to be training three young girls in the craft, flexing her own fingers to form a ball of orange magic that sparks and then condenses into a smooth sphere between her hands. She lets the ball dissipate and steps out of their way, inclining her head towards a nearby tree. Rio watches the girls cup their palms and furrow their brows, imitating the witch’s earlier motions. Magic sparks in their hands, forming fuzzy orbs that spark and quiver before the girls fling their arms outward and release them.

They’re weak little things, hitting the tree as nothing more than a gust of wind. Still the older witch praises them, cooing as she ruffles their hair and offers an encouraging smile. Their responding excitement is palpable, a lightness in the air that seems to buoy the whole camp.

A pang of something Rio doesn’t recognize strikes behind her sternum, a tenderness with edges so sharp that it pains her to hold. She breaks her own rule in a futile bid to dispel it.

“I thought you were going to show me how you kill them.”

“I am.”

“From all the way up here?”

“No. We’re waiting, Rio. You’ll see.”

Rio drums her fingers on the side of her knee, studying Agatha’s profile. “How do you know you’ll be able to do it tonight? I thought you couldn’t choose the subject of your dreams.”

Agatha hums noncommittally, still not pulling her eyes from the coven below. “I can’t, but I guess this sort of thing stays top of mind.” The corners of her lips twitch up in the ghost of their usual snark, something still far away in her eyes when at last she turns to Rio. “Maybe it’s just a lucky break that you’re here tonight. Did you ever consider that?”

Rio, not entirely sure what to make of the ambiguous disclosure, nods her head once in acknowledgement before returning to her observation of the coven on the forest floor. She tracks the witches winding their way through the camp just as intently as she does the stationary ones, noting their collective likeness to ants running along the tunnels inside of their hill. They all work in unspoken tandem. Rio watches with rapt attention as they brew potions and use their powers to cast further wards around the site, chanting incantations in one collective voice. The essence of magic shimmers in the air around them, glittering where it brushes against the trees, and Rio can almost feel the way the magic beneath her own skin longs to respond to it. She clenches and unclenches her fist, looking down to the blue veins running parallel at her wrist and wondering if the green lies in wait just beneath it.

“Can you feel it when you choose to use your magic? Or is it more like the beating of your heart in your chest?”

Agatha doesn’t answer at first, and when she shifts her gaze from the camp to the orange sky beyond the oaks, Rio thinks that maybe she won’t answer at all. This, too, is one of Agatha’s traits that she’s picked up on– sometimes her questions earn a deflection, others outright silence. But every now and again, like in this moment, Agatha will surprise her. She breathes in until the air fully inflates her lungs, pushing it back out while picking at the bark beneath her fingers.

“The beating of my heart started in the moment that I was born. I couldn’t stop it if I tried. But my magic had to be worked at, fought for. It can be a wild and unpredictable beast, but it also lives within me. I work in tandem with it.” She pauses, her focus still somewhere far beyond Rio and this moment. The air grows still around her, drawn closer by the unexpected sincerity in her voice. Rio finds herself leaning in too.

“Calling upon my magic feels like being human but also something so much more. Something better. It’s the only thing that’s ever been just mine.” She shifts her eyes but not her head in Rio’s direction, like this performed distance keeps her from having to confess to any degree of vulnerability. “What’s it like for you?”

The question catches her off guard. Rio blinks, flicking her eyes from Agatha to the coven to the moon and back. She runs her tongue over her teeth, the muscle snagging slightly on the gap between the front two, and presents the question again to the vastness of her consciousness. In response, she is supplied with a careful arrangement of sights and sensations from her limited bodily experiences: breaking free from the kelp, sucking water into her lungs, gathering lavender with her hands. Agatha twirling with abandon on a candlelit dance floor and tossing a wink in her direction with the precision of a heat seeking missile.

Rio doesn’t feel the responding tide of magic building in her bloodstream until it breaks. A daffodil blooms in one of the palms resting in her lap, tinged a glittering green around the edges that fades in the time it takes to blink. She finds herself stunned, staring down at the delicate petals. Something grown by her own hand, something lovely at her fingertips.

Despite the reputation crafted on Its behalf, Death has never been an inherently destructive force, instead functioning more as a recycler of cosmic matter. The particles of a soul disbanding and dispersing beneath the coaxing of Death’s careful guidance before becoming something else, something new. But this blossom in her hand isn’t recycling, it’s outright creation– the revelation that she can make something without having to first tear something else apart.

In the space between breaths she has become Death and Life in one, no longer merely taking part in the cycle but embodying it entirely. Rio runs her thumb over the flower and watches the petals bend to accommodate the touch, their buttery yellow hue almost garish in this pseudo-evening light. It’s the only thing that’s ever been just mine.

“How did you do that?”

Agatha’s voice is a low hum in her ears, a hornet assessing the best place to embed its stinger and make the kill. Rio wets her lips with her tongue, eyes darting between Agatha’s drawn face and the flower in her hand as though either will help her form a tidy explanation of how, possibly, there has just been a great and terrible shift on an interdimensional scale. How she hadn’t known that her hands could do anything so wonderful, and how there may yet be consequences for this folly that even she can’t imagine.

“Magic is a manipulation of the natural world, Rio. Using what already exists to our advantage,” Agatha explains when Rio’s too slow on the uptake, tracing her eyes across Rio’s face as though any hint of deception will reveal itself at any moment now. “So, how did you do that?”

Rio closes her fingers around the daffodil, shielding it from view. “I don’t know.”

The muscle in Agatha’s jaw feathers in the way that Rio is learning means she’s frustrated. Rio hopes that she might be benevolent enough to let her keep her teeth this time.

But Agatha’s ire never comes, halted in its tracks by the snapping of a twig far below that has her head snapping back to the campsite. Rio cranes her neck until she finds what Agatha has surely already seen– a lone witch calling to the others that she’s going down to the river to fetch more water, bucket swinging in one hand with the other still raised in a farewell wave.

Agatha turns back, eyes unnaturally sharp and bright. “We’re tabling this conversation. Right now, it’s show time. Follow my lead.”

Rio isn’t entirely sure what ‘follow my lead’ is meant to mean exactly, or for how long it’s applicable, but when Agatha pushes off with her hands and leaps down from the tree she repeats the motion. There’s a brief feeling of falling from a great height, her stomach flipping out of her body and hovering in the air above her head, before her feet land on the forest floor with as much force as hopping in place would have produced.

There isn’t long to marvel at the mix of sensations before Agatha is on the move, prowling quietly between trees and stepping gingerly over roots and stones. Her single-minded focus as she tails the witch reminds Rio of a predator’s indomitable prey drive. Rio floats far enough behind Agatha to not impede her hunt, but close enough that she can still hear each of her panted breaths.

The trek to the river isn’t far. A lone current has carved its way through the trees here, splitting apart the earth to send its waters downhill between craggy banks. When they’re close, Agatha takes Rio by the arm and pulls them both behind a tree that surely isn’t wide enough to conceal them. Agatha turns back for a split second to hold Rio’s eyes while lifting a finger to her lips, instructing her to remain silent before returning her focus to the riverbank.

Rio watches the young witch reach its edge, tucking long blonde hair behind her ear before crouching and gripping her bucket between both hands as she holds it in the stream. The witch is careful to navigate around the fallen leaves and twigs that the current pulls into the bucket’s path, swaying it back and forth until it’s full at last. Rio wonders if they’re always this unaware– the witches Agatha claims. Or if this is the idealized version of her hunt, where something innocent and fragile backs itself into a corner and has no choice but to turn itself over to her wanting maw. 

The witch struggles for a brief moment when she moves to lift the bucket from the river, water sloshing against its sides and wetting her sleeves. At her misstep, Agatha squares her shoulders like this is the exact moment she’s been waiting for. She holds up her hand, pressing the backs of her knuckles blindly to Rio’s sternum before she slips out from behind the tree with alarming fluidity. Rio wonders if she’s seen this sign for do not move before or if part of being in Agatha’s dreaming mind is hearing her even when she doesn’t speak. An inescapable knowing of sorts. Either way, her skin feels fragile after the touch, as though a gust of wind could tear it from the bone. Rio lifts a hand to rub at the spot but otherwise keeps herself exactly as Agatha has left her, watching the carefully rehearsed performance unfold. 

Agatha displays a terrible feline grace, sweeping across the rocky terrain until she’s looming between the witch and the path they’d both taken. Close enough to touch.

“Need some help with that, sister?”

The witch jumps, spilling water down the front of her dress. Rio watches her forearms strain at the unexpected motion, her stomach tighten against the water’s cold onslaught, her eyes widen at the glint of Agatha’s toothy smile.

“I– who are you?” She squeaks, half curious and half afraid.

“It’s a lovely coven you have up there. Do you want me to carry your bucket back to them? Those poor arms are already shaking.”

The witch’s cheeks bloom a blotchy red, her brow furrowing at the accusation. “I’m no weakling. I can carry it back on my own.” She sniffs haughtily, tilting her nose up in the air. “My coven isn’t looking to take in stragglers anyhow. Leave this place, stranger. You’re not wanted.”

Agatha’s lips curl into a lazy half smile at this, disregarding the warning entirely. “Is that why they sent you to fetch water, then? So big and strong that you can’t teach the children, or set up the wards, or even keep the fire burning?” She throws in a laugh– a pointed jab timed down to the millisecond. “You’re useless, darling. That’s alright. Every coven has a runt.”

The witch drops the bucket, not looking down to see the water spill back out and saturate the stones, running down to join the river. She’s too focused on Agatha’s smug face and the clench and release of her own fists, alight with crackling yellow magic.

Rio catches the sound of her own rushed breathing in her ears and thinks to cover her nose with a hand, but neither witch so much as twitches in her direction. She is merely a spectator, surprised to find herself thankful for the return of the anonymity she had come here to shirk.

“You’d better do as I said and take your leave,” the witch grinds out through her teeth, taking a step forward with the clear intention of intimidation. Agatha takes three backwards, though Rio feels it looks less like a retreat and more like she’s taking her place for the start of a dance. The witch seems to make no such observation, grinning victoriously and raising her hands into the air in emphasis. “Or you’ll find out exactly what this runt can do.”

When she speaks, Agatha’s voice takes on a sharp, high tone. An imitation of cowardice. “Oh my! I sure would hate to see you toss a pebble at me! Or zap my little toe! What a fright!”

Agatha barely finishes her taunt before the witch releases a frustrated yowl, thrusting her hands forward. Power races forward, striking the ground where Agatha was seconds before. Agatha cackles into the air at the miss, smiling at the witch as though the two of them are merely playing a game. Agatha hops around, dodging a second blast and then a third until at last the witch winds back, screaming her fury and lurching forward to deliver a final blast.

The stream of power is twice as dense as those before it. It strikes clean through Agatha’s chest.

Belatedly, panic finds Rio as if she’s the one that’s been struck. Her chest tightens directly beneath where Agatha’s knuckles had touched only minutes before, sweat beading at her brow in response to the small sound that the blast punches out of Agatha’s lungs.

The witch’s power seems endless as it courses through Agatha, yellow electricity thrumming where the blue of her veins should be. But the witch seems stuck in some internal battle of her own, feet digging further into the mud beneath her. Agatha’s face pinches inward and the tightness in Rio’s chest twists into an angry throb.

She almost reaches out, almost disobeys the command to stay and inserts herself into the demonstration. Surely, Agatha can’t die at the hands of something she herself has made up. It feels a silly thing for Death to consider whether or not something qualifies as a manner of dying, but Rio finds herself racking her brain anyhow. Maybe there is merit to stepping in before she receives an answer. Maybe she doesn’t want to know how Agatha does it anymore.

But then Agatha releases a sigh that sounds oddly like relief, and Rio unfurls her tight fists, even if only slightly.

The witch’s yellow blast blurs to a gradually muddy brown, spreading outward from where it reaches into Agatha’s chest, before melting into a vibrant purple. The flow of power reverses direction and takes hold of the witch’s wrists, drawing endlessly outwards. The witch finds no words but releases a garbled whine as Agatha mercilessly hollows her out, leaving nothing more than a withered grey husk in her wake.

Agatha, Rio realizes, is a siphon. Death-touched long before Rio got here, a spirit with one mischievous finger stroking the seam between worlds. The discovery is staggering- a release on the pressure valve of her curiosity.

The tether of power snaps as the body falls, ricocheting back into Agatha. She hunches over and takes heaving breaths, the rough sound finding its way to Rio’s ears. Rio, presuming herself freed from Agatha’s silent command, shuffles out from the trees and glides closer, taking up residence just shy of Agatha and her fresh kill. Rio wonders if Agatha’s been grievously injured, if she’s waiting for Rio to give some sort of commentary on what she’s just seen. With her hair cascading down over her face, Rio can’t look to see what her expression might be.

“Is it just flowers?” Her voice comes, cutting off Rio’s thoughts. Though they catch on a wheeze, her words are clear.

“What?” Rio asks, entirely lost.

“What about a gun? Can your magic summon a gun?” Agatha straightens at last, using one hand to pull the hair from her face and setting the other on her hip. Her expression is untroubled and slightly quizzical, as though they’d never left the tree branch. 

The whiplash of every moment since she’s entered this dream coils in Rio’s muscles and breaks of its own accord. She throws an arm out as green power erupts from her fingertips. A dense cluster of hellebores blooms to life where her magic strikes the earth. She stares at them not in shock, but with the confusion she’s coming to associate with most aspects of being alive. Or as close to it as she’ll ever come.

Agatha’s assessment is brief. “Hm, guess that’s a no.” She shrugs, turning back to Rio. “Anyway, that’s how I do it. More or less.” Her smile is small but proud, almost like a secret she’s only used to sharing with herself.

Rio takes this as permission to finally round on the body, rotating her face ninety degrees exactly and clasping her hands behind her back to study it.

It’s intriguing. Agatha’s prey isn’t real, but Death still finds something akin to catharsis in seeing the events that lead up to when she– It– comes into play. Her hands unclasp so that one can shift idly to rest on the hilt of her knife, grounding in a way she wasn’t expecting.

The longer Rio stares the more she feels a tingling up her spine to announce an unexpected adrenaline spike, overwhelming her already frayed nerves. There is no soul that leaves this body. She can tell by the way the death has been ringing hollow in her gut, but she imagines what it might be like if there was. Something for her to take, a way to carve out a space for herself in this ritual that isn’t hiding behind a tree.

“Aren’t you going to ask me why I do it?”

Rio pulls back, looking over to take Agatha in with the same intensity she’d afforded the husk. A purple ring glints around the blue of her eyes, and again Rio is reminded of a predator’s hunt. An electric undercurrent thrums through not only Agatha but the entirety of the dream, as if her mind can’t think of the act without conjuring the full body delight that it draws out. Rio can’t help but feel a matching thrill.

“Is that what you want me to do?”

For a beat, Agatha’s only response is silence. They seem to be studying one another, though Rio can’t tell what it is Agatha’s hoping to find. The buzz that surrounds them picks up, if anything, like an earthquake leaving the parting kiss of an aftershock.

Agatha’s lips twitch up at the corners before smoothing back into practiced indifference.

“No, I guess not.”

Rio nods, like the matter has been settled.

“What do you want to know?”

Rio takes her turn to pause, looking down at the witch’s discarded bucket and, with little ceremony, kicks it down into the river. She tracks as the current catch it and ferry it downstream. She takes the oversized question and whittles it down into something that can fit in her palm. “Is it night where you are?”

The purple of Agatha’s eyes recedes slightly to be replaced by a tinge of pink at her cheekbones, the buzz in the air settling to a gentle hum. “It was when I went to sleep, yes. Is it night where you are?”

If Rio catches the subtle shift between answer and question, she doesn’t say. She scrunches her nose, contemplating how to explain that there is no day or night in the vast vacuum of space. Nor is there time, really. But she finds that, regardless, she would like to sate Agatha’s desire for knowledge.

“It’s difficult to say,” and then, as if making up for her inadequate answer, she adds “I’ve never truly seen the dawn.”

Agatha’s brow furrows. Rio is coming to find that she likes it this way, when Agatha seems just as inquisitive about their coexistence as she is. When it’s a volley and not just a one-sided torrent from Rio herself.

“Never in your life?”

“Never.”

Agatha opens her mouth to continue her inquisition, but the dream wipes away all at once as though Agatha awakes abruptly, leaving Rio suspended once more in the great void that envelops all things. It’s a strange thing to go from breathing to having no lungs at all. She finds fate's harp an unsatisfactory filler for the space that Agatha's next question would have occupied.

With Agatha’s end of their bargain fulfilled and the ease with which she orchestrated a kill fresh on her mind, Death begins to wonder what she’s gotten herself into.

Death– Rio– calls upon the endless expanse of her consciousness once returned to her true form, gingerly unfolding and presenting Agatha’s question to herself.

What do you want to know?

Which is, in itself, many questions. You– is Rio a personification of Death? Is Death allowed to make a claim on personhood, even if her feet never touch the earth’s soil? Even if she only exists to one mortal in one way, never to understand what it means to suffer or love? For this there seems to be no one answer to be pieced neatly together, only a handful of ill fitting parts that have yet to reveal their whole. It seems to depend, in a sense, on what she wants the answer to be.

And then there is this: can Death want? Are there things It is allowed to covet, desires within her to be made plain? It feels like the breaking of a rule, though she can think of nothing that outright prohibits her from this selfishness. Death is meant to collect, and It does. Death is meant to stand as the warden between planes, and It does not abandon this post when It takes the form of Rio Vidal.

She– It– had, after all, enjoyed the world of dreams long before It encountered Agatha Harkness. Had wanted to linger, to see the curious planets It envelops through the eyes of the creatures that inhabit them. It wanted to hear fate’s steadfast tune, even though It had never been deprived of the sound before.

There is space, then, for things like want. And if there is space to want, then perhaps there is space to be.

Knowledge, of course, is the hardened kernel waiting at the center of all things. Truth at the heart of the matter; meted out by gods and mortals alike, waiting to be found in the makeup of each blade of grass and each burning star and all of the endless space cradled between them. Death maintains a trove of knowledge on what is both seen and unseen, remembered and forgotten. What is Death if not an owner of the past and reminder of all things yet to come?

It was, after all, a quest for knowledge that drove her into Agatha’s dreaming to begin with, first in hopes of silence and then in pursuit of her voice.

If pressed, Rio could not assign a number to the questions she longs to uncover answers for. She wants to know why her magic manifests as it does in her strange new body, and where her own face came from, and what it feels like to be rendered both lifeless and powerless by Agatha’s cruel hands. At this the desire comes to flex her fingers and feel the tendons pull, but Rio remembers she bears no hands to perform the motion. This truth, simplest of them all, takes root most firmly.

Beyond Agatha’s dreaming, she is Death– It is Death– a concept, a duty, a central gear within a machine that has never known stillness. A blanket thrown across all worlds, an accompanying force winding endlessly around the corkscrew of time. And yet now she is becoming something else.

Or learning to, at least.

She is learning to own a name, to choose for herself, to bear witness through a lens that has never before been available to her. Learning not only that wanting is an option, but so is action.

She is learning to be Rio Vidal.

When the call of Agatha’s dreaming next twines with fate’s serenade, Death finds herself awash in an odd sense of clarity. There is no moment’s hesitation on behalf of duty, no resistance at parting with her godly nature to inhabit a limited human form. No half-truth about being spurred on by purely intellectual curiosity. Instead, a simple chain reaction: Agatha’s dreaming mind ignites like a lighthouse in a barren harbor and Death takes note of her desire to heed its call. The simple relief of doing what she wants is a reward in itself.

So involved in this first true bout of introspection in her existence, Death misses the tone of Agatha’s dreaming, the frantic transmission emanating from her mind and trembling across the dreamscape.

The hairs at her nape stand at attention, the air growing thin before it passes through her lips. The linen pools on the floor around her bare toes, the garment clearly meant for someone twice her size.

Just as before, Agatha doesn’t seem surprised by the shadow in her door or the performance it puts on, only acquiescing to the tide of a long-suffering terror. This, above all else, is most unfamiliar to Rio.

Her tone is scalding, the shrapnel of her assault embedding itself in Rio’s own chest and leaving behind a feeling that she has done something irreparably wrong, a sense of guilt that Death has never known. It is an unfathomable sight even for Rio, who has seen each of the great heroes of humanity succumb in turn to their mortal fragility. The likes of Achilles and Gilgamesh were praised for their innate gifts, lauded as minor gods only to fall prey to their own hubris. Agatha, likely shunned and reviled for hers, has bested her foes nonetheless.

The figure flickers. A woman’s gaunt face appears, a sickening smile exposing rotted teeth and pulling at the shriveled skin of her cheeks before she is only a shadow once more. Unused to seeing one of the demented creatures so close, Rio blinks, instinctively moving a hand to grip at the hilt of her knife. This is no mortal night terror, but the monstrous efforts of a ghost infiltrating Agatha’s dream.

It doesn’t matter to Rio who the ghost is or was, if she knew Agatha or if she’s simply here to sow chaos out of blind envy for the living. All that Rio knows is that this ghost is here now, exerting its will when it should have no will, no say at all in Agatha’s life or any other. Death, the keeper of all worldly boundaries, will not let this stand. There is a sacred balance to be upheld, a debt to be paid.

There is an unexpected lurch as Rio seems to leave her own body, replaced by Death’s full consciousness. Her vision stutters before settling to encompass more than her mortal eyes can see, a pressure pounding in her temples as her divinity surges forth at the call of injustice and shows little regard for the limitations of this fragile body. She can only distantly feel as blood drips from her nose and curls around the swell of her lip. Rio is shoved aside in favor of almighty Death.

Fate’s harpsong swells in Death’s ears, drowning out the ghost’s horrific voice as It draws Its knife from Its sheath and lunges forward, slashing decisively at the shadow. The weapon feels like a natural extension of the arm It has never had, a necessary tool of righteous doom outfitted specifically for Death’s capable hands.

Only the blade’s pointed tip connects with the shadow, but a piercing wail rings clear through the room as though the ghost has been cleaved in two. Her hold on Agatha recedes like a whip coiling inward and the girl crumples to the floor in a heap, unmoving. The ghost shatters from a solid form and down to its atoms, the particles quivering like static in the room. A sickly green glow emanates like a silhouette around the cloud of matter, pulsing as the atoms scramble to fit themselves back together. Death moves to strike again, but the cloud dissipates as the ghost flees Agatha’s dream. The door slams in her wake though no hand has pulled it, plunging the room into near darkness once more and depriving Death of Its rightful claim.

Rio squeezes her eyes shut against the foggy feeling behind her forehead, adjusting a sweaty palm around her knife as the pain at her temples fades and she is returned to herself once more. Her body handed back over by Death, she swallows and reopens her eyes, squinting into the dark. This sensation gets added to her ever growing list of questions–

When did Death stop being her identity and instead become this power– this duty– separate from herself? When had she lost control? Will she ever truly feel like one or the other again?

But nothing is so pressing as the girl still crushed into the corner of the room, this small version of Agatha who has only just endured unknowable torment. Her nightgown is stained black where the ghost had taken hold, a stark juxtaposition to its original state. Though Rio can no longer see her eyes, she thinks of their unmoving hold on the slant of light mere minutes before, the preemptive tension rippling through her. How long this ghost has tormented Agatha, Rio cannot guess. She decides not to wonder at all of the unknowns, expecting that there may never come a time that she will understand Agatha in her entirety. 

Rio slides her knife carefully back into the holster at her hip without looking away, taking one tentative step around the foot of the bed but still maintaining her distance.

“Agatha?” Rio calls, her voice barely above a whisper. She’s not sure what instinct within herself it is that calls for quiet in this moment, but she complies nonetheless.

No response comes, but the harsh press of Agatha’s shallow breathing seems to swell to fill the space as though activated by Rio’s voice. Her whole body shakes with the failed effort to get oxygen to starving lungs, each inhale an audible gasp. Rio’s brow furrows. She reaches a hand towards Agatha slowly as though approaching a skittish doe, feeling leagues beyond her depth in the realm of human interaction– of interaction with Agatha– yet incapable of leaving her be.

“Agatha,” she tries again, voice only just louder and more assured, “are you–”

“Get out!” Agatha bellows, the sound ripping out of her and ricocheting around the room. Despite her girlish form, Agatha’s voice is as deep and snarling as it ever was. The strained quiet around them shatters, replaced by the boiling intensity radiating off of Agatha and burning into Rio’s skin. Rio lifts the heel of a palm to cover one ear, blinking in confusion at the rapid change and parting her lips to try once more for clarity. But the girl lifts her face in the same moment, blue irises framed by the stark red of dilated blood vessels finding Rio with a fury and forcing her into silence.

“I said get out! You’re not supposed to be here!” she cries, the words beginning to overlap into a blast of shrieking sound as they hit the walls and find their way back to Rio’s eardrums. “Leave me alone, Rio! I don’t want you here, I’ve never wanted you here!”

The girl pounds her fist against the ground where she’s still yet to rise from her knees, her nightgown darkening at the collar where a stream of blood flows freely from a cut across her cheek. The floor begins to tear itself apart, radiating outward from the point where her hand connects with the wood. The beams splinter and form fractures that spread their long fingers quickly, carving around the ground beneath Rio’s feet until she is disconnected from the room entirely.

Agatha spares her a final dark glance before the slab tilts at such a sharp angle that it sends Rio careening backwards. A gasp escapes her at the hollow thump of her back hitting the space where the ground had been only seconds before, but no feeling connects to the sound– the once-solid surface now only a thin membrane. Rio’s body tears through it with ease, falling from the dark room and into a blinding, endless white.

She tries to blink but the light burns furiously through the thin skin of her eyelids, the sting of it drawing tears to the corners of her eyes. The sensation is so overwhelming that she doesn’t catch the wounded sound ringing somewhere above her, muffled as it refuses to fully leave the fortress of Agatha’s inner mind. Rio wonders if this unbearable emptiness is what witches usually find in place of Agatha’s true thoughts. If this is where all those who are not welcome must dwell, preserved in breathless nothingness until Agatha deigns to release them or they manage to escape.

Rio falls through the empty air until, she presumes, Agatha wakes again, spitting her back into the waiting plane of the dreamscape. As is becoming ritual, Rio snags briefly on the transition from witch to god, the violent white still burnt into the retinas that she no longer has.

Though Death cannot know true pain, the harsh sting of rejection follows her into formlessness. The feeling of wrongdoing instilled by the ghost’s antagonizing voice seems to take root and flourish instead of disappearing with the rest of Rio Vidal, forcing Death to endure a weight heretofore unknown to any god. Her divinity does not protect her from this discomfort.

Rio– Death– had only done what she was meant to do. Had only performed in the exact way that she was formed to. Ghosts do not belong anywhere within the mortal realms, let alone tormenting Agatha in her dreams. It was Death’s duty to remedy the situation.

But had Agatha wanted the ghost to stay? Some inconceivable human ritual of facing something feared for the sake of claiming triumph in enduring?

Had Rio said something wrong? Though she spoke very little, it was her voice that seemed to spark Agatha’s agitation.

The guilt lingers even when there is no body to hold it, scratching against the fabric of Death’s consciousness. For all that she has learned from her foray into human dreaming and their interpersonal relationships with one another, there seems to be no blueprint to follow as a means of remedying this situation. Truly the more Rio considers it, there seems to be no remedy at all. There is only the fact of the matter: Agatha told her to leave. Agatha removed her from a dream, which it now seems she could have done at any time.

Agatha does not wish to see her again.

Death, who has held the beginnings and endings of all things within Itself, understands what it is to be unwanted. It knows how to receive a directive and obey. Though there is still an inescapable longing to know if Agatha is in an improved state, Death understands that the desire is a selfish one. If Agatha were dead, Death would know. This– that she yet lives– will have to be answer enough.

Death, harbinger of all transitions, must move on.

It continues onward through what was once the only existence It had ever known, steadfastly ignoring the way that Agatha has stopped dreaming altogether. Death knows it is so because the tug of her dreaming does not ripple at the fabric of Its consciousness. Her voice does not grow talons and claw for attention. Fate’s resounding song is the lone tune to accompany Its existence, as it has ever been. As it ever will be.

Death tries, fleetingly, to visit the dreamscape as it once did– entering when a soul is in need of extraction and lingering sparingly as a means of ascertaining mortal current affairs. In the dreams of humans It finds the distinct lack of a now-expected thrill, a sourness carried in by Its disappointment that permeates the experience entirely. Nobody sees It– her. Nobody calls her name. She goes to ask a question, anything at all that comes to mind,  but of course there is no sound that leaves her. There is no mouth to permit it. Death wretches herself– Itself– back out with a finality and decides that the dreamscape is best left alone beyond the fulfillment of Its duty. All things, surely, are best left alone beyond what duty necessitates.

A mass outbreak of an illness sweeps across a planet far from Earth, and Death finds reprieve in the handling of a global catastrophe to keep Its attention hundreds of solar systems away from where it has continued to wander. It focuses on the influx of souls and does Its best not to notice that Agatha must be using some spell or draught or outright forcing herself to remain constantly awake, never letting her mind fall from the living world and into the cradle of the dreamscape. And it’s all probably for the best, Death reasons. It does not like the way It has become fixated on this mortal, she– It– finds it wholly unsettling to be unable to detach from the situation as It once believed It could.

It was never productive to Death’s work to try and imitate the living anyhow. Death should be an observer and nothing more. Death was never meant to be, and should never have allowed herself– Itself– to play at such a foolish notion. Death thinks of the daffodil blooming in her palm, the hellebores bursting to life on the forest floor. The urge to grind Its teeth. Death thinks again of duty.

It would be futile to give Agatha– the Witch Killer– and her dreams any more thought. The other witches will kill her soon, surely. Humans are never long for vengeance. She will die, and Death will see her a final time, and then it will be over. Perhaps years have already passed since It last bore the name Rio Vidal. Perhaps decades have slipped by like grains of sand.

It was all so brief an endeavor, really, that Death may forget the whole ordeal ever happened. Rio Vidal will fade into nothing more than another passing dream, filed away with all others.

Or so It tells Itself, ignoring the way the heart It doesn’t have picks up its pace when Agatha starts to dream once more.
