Summary

Jen Barkley meets April Ludgate: a series of what ifs.

Spring 2012

Tuesday morning, twelve days and roughly twelve hours to go, seven if she was lucky – not that she was counting – found Jen Barkley in the corner booth of the best coffee shop Pawnee, Indiana had to offer.

 

They loved her here, thanks in large part to the fact that Jen knew how to get people to like her and wasn’t shy about using that knowledge to her advantage. The Daily Grind had the best coffee in town (which wasn’t saying too much; this was, after all, Pawnee. Indiana, in case you’d also missed that part) and Ellen, the owner, happened to have been in need of a few minor repairs around the place that had been accumulating for a while. It had been easy for Jen to cover them, and almost even easier to leverage Ellen into a spot where she’d accepted the help.

 

Now Jen got decent coffee – often free, not that it really mattered – and Ellen was free to focus on hiring more people or rethinking the wallpaper or her dad’s home health service. Which was good, because Jen had been dangerously close to the point of losing her fucking mind if she’d had to go to JJ’s Diner one more time. Jesus Christ, the end of this nothingburger campaign could not come quickly enough.

 

By 9:30, Jen had finished her daily skim of seven international newspapers and had fifteen minutes to spare before her meeting with Newport Senior. And therefore also fifteen minutes before resigning her olfactory senses to the saccharine smell that always stuck around in her nose for three hours, minimum, after a visit to the Sweetums offices. Jen took a preemptive breath of freedom and coffee and stretched in her seat.

 

The morning rush had filtered out when Jen hadn’t been paying attention. Now it was down to Jen at her corner booth, a table of geriatric men who’d apparently all collectively forgotten their hearing aids, a wannabe tech bro in a zip up fleece vest and a plaid shirt waiting on somebody to show up for a meeting, a romantic reading a Jane Austen novel at a window table and waiting for someone to sweep her off her feet, and the woman whose order Ellen was taking at the counter.

 

Something about her, though, made Jen sit up straighter and take a second look. What, exactly, she wasn’t sure – but Jen hadn’t honed her intuition like a weapon to be wrong when it lit up like her old Lite Brite.

 

She was young, mid-twenties, maybe younger. Surprisingly put together in neatly fitting jeans, clean white Vans, and a dark blue hoodie. Average height. Attractive. Dark, shoulder length hair. A wedding ring on the hand tucking her card back into her wallet – God, how young did people get married and start popping out babies in this hellhole? And the thing that had probably piqued Jen’s interest in the first place – an understated charisma that Jen could just tell that this woman hadn’t bothered to fully learn her way around.

 

Mystery Woman finished paying and turned around to go wait at the end of the counter and – uh oh. Busted. Oh, well. Jen hadn’t made a practice of backing down from twenty-somethings in hoodies to this point, and she wasn’t about to start now.

 

But it seemed like Mystery Woman might like that, because she was coming over.

 

“Hi,” she said. “Why are you staring at me?”

 

To the point, no time wasted: a woman after Jen’s own heart. Jen thought about bullshitting her for the fun of almost certainly being called on it, but opted for the truth. “I don’t know,” she said. “You seemed interesting.”

 

Mystery Woman seemed skeptical about this reply. “You thought I seemed interesting… from me ordering coffee?”

 

“What can I say,” Jen said. “I have an eye for these things. And I’m usually not wrong.”

 

“Are you hitting on me?” Mystery Woman asked bluntly as she crossed her arms.

 

Oh. Interesting. “I wasn’t trying to,” Jen replied. “Do you want me to?”

 

“I’m married,” she said.

 

That wasn’t a no… per se. Jen let it go anyway, allowing herself a little preen and smile that, delightfully, made Mystery Woman scoff, but with a chuckle.

 

“So do you usually have a hard time believing people when they tell you that you seem interesting?” Jen asked instead. “Because I’m getting the vibe that mystery and intrigue might kinda be your whole schtick.”

 

“No, I just find it hard to believe coming from you.”

 

“Coming from me?” Jen said in exaggerated disbelief. “You don’t even know who I am.”

 

“Yeah, I do,” Mystery Woman said. “You’re trying to sabotage Leslie’s campaign.”

 

‘Leslie’, huh? “Last time I checked, I was succeeding in sabotaging Leslie’s campaign,” Jen corrected, because Leslie was currently eleven points down in the polls thanks to the outstandingly lackluster efforts of yours, truly. Let it never be said that Jennifer Barkley did subpar work, even at her most bored in a town that was making her more brain-dead by the second. “I guess that makes you one of her campaign staffers, huh?”

 

Mystery Woman shrugged. “I guess.”

 

Oooh, definitely part of her schtick. “Don’t take this the wrong way, but you’re pretty much exactly the opposite of somebody I’d expect to see on Leslie’s team of earnest optimists.”

 

“Yeah, well,” said Mystery Woman, “I guess all the spots for earnest optimists were already filled.”

 

“Jokes, too,” Jen observed, daring to lean her chin on her palm. “Aren’t you just the full package.”

 

“That’s me, an apathetic pessimist with jokes.”

 

The picture was starting to fill in a little. Smart, quick, loyal. Ready to fight. Apathy or pessimism aside, both of which Jen already doubted, she could see how Mystery Woman was just the kind of person Leslie Knope would want to take under her wing and foster like a baby bird.  

 

“Hmm,” Jen said, drawing it out just to see if Mystery Woman would bristle. (Verdict: yes, but while keeping a pretty good lid on it.) “So what is an apathetic pessimistic doing on a contentious, losing campaign for a city council position in Pawnee, Indiana, then?”

 

Mystery Woman only smiled like she couldn’t figure out why Jen was asking such a stupid question. “None of your business, really,” she said slowly. “Why do you care?”

 

Great question. Opposition research, probably. But it was always bad form to actually say that to the opposition’s face. “Like I said,” Jen said, “you seemed interesting. And given that the amount of interesting things in this town is close to zero – “

 

“Yeah, so, why are you here?” Mystery Woman interrupted.

 

“Oh, money,” Jen answered honestly. “Lots of money. Really, an absurd amount.”

 

Todd, the acne-challenged teenager manning the counter that morning, chose that minute to call “Satan’s Niece?”

 

Mystery Woman tilted her head. “That’s me.”

 

Of course it was.

 

“Hey, good luck with the campaign!” Jen called as Mystery Woman/Satan’s Niece deftly grabbed two trays of drinks and headed for the door.

 

“Thanks,” Mystery Woman replied as she opened the door with her back. “Good luck with the sabotaging.”

 

“Oh, thanks! I don’t need it!” Jen called cheerfully after her with a wave, and had no doubt that if both of her hands hadn’t been occupied by coffee trays, she would have received a casual middle fingered salute. Jen watched her get to her car and set down her trays on the roof.

 

“Hey, Todd,” Jen called as he passed by to take out the trash. “That woman, the one who just left. What’s her name?”

 

Inexplicably, Todd looked back at her with terror on his face. “I… can’t say,” he said.

 

How deeply uninspiring. She hadn’t even given him a reason to be terrified of her. She had, in fact, been extremely nice to him every single day she’d come in during her interminable stay in Pawnee. “Can’t say, won’t say, what?” Jen pressed. “Come on, I’ve got fifty bucks for the tip jar.” That would probably buy him a decent amount of weed in this town… though she wouldn’t speculate about the quality.

 

Todd looked tempted, but only for about half a second. “She’ll know,” he said. “I’m not taking the chance.”

 

“Who, Satan’s Niece?” Jen demanded, incredulous. “What did she do to you?” The possibilities suddenly stretched entertainingly out in front of her. Was it something freaky? God, Jen hoped it was something freaky.

 

“Don’t mind Todd. Her name’s April,” Ellen said as she passed by with armful of dishes for the dishwasher. “Nice girl. She works at City Hall, pops by a couple times a week and gets coffee for the office, usually in the afternoon.”

 

Huh. ‘Satan’s Niece’ actually did seem more apropos than April.

 

“Thanks, Ellen, you’re a gem,” Jen said and glanced out the window again. Mystery Woman – April – had long since disappeared.

 

Well. God willing, there wasn’t much longer to go in this campaign, and then Jen could go home and would never have to return to this town ever again, and Mystery Woman/Satan’s Niece/April would be just a blip in an otherwise mindnumbing six weeks of Jen’s life that would eventually grind to a merciful end.

 

For now, there was a meeting she had to take to make that happen. Jen dropped the promised fifty in the tip jar on the way out, making sure to return Ellen’s smile and wave. Two weeks. Two weeks. That was all.

Summer 2012

“April, great timing,” Ben said as she slid silently into his office about thirty seconds after Jen Barkley had swept out of it to catch a flight back to Columbus. “Look, we both know he’s a robot, but Jen wants to focus on Murray’s record in the home stretch, so we’re gonna – "

 

"Who?”

 

Ben was never one hundred percent on what was going on in April’s brain – actually, he’d been coming to terms with the possibility of any understanding of April he might ever have maxing out at about thirty, thirty-five percent – but right now he was operating at about a ten percent. He raised his eyebrows. “Jen.” Nothing. “Jen Barkley. She – she was literally just here, April. The woman who just left my office.”

 

April’s expression didn’t register a change in understanding.

 

“The person we came here to work for,” he tried again, to similar effect. “Do you at least remember her from Leslie’s campaign? She was on Perd Hapley’s show, like – a lot. Aaaaand you’re fucking with me, aren’t you?”

 

April shrugged, but there was a hint of a smirk that was starting to give her away. Ben was getting better at spotting it. He only had about a twenty percent error margin in either direction. He thought.

Ben raised his eyebrows. “But… you were fine with it?”

 

“Yeah, I’m not dating Leslie, so it’s definitely weirder for you.”

 

Ben doubted he’d ever get straight answers out of April why she’d agreed to come with him to D.C. as an intern for a congressional campaign, but the questions he had were getting more complex all the time. “I guess that’s true,” he allowed. “I’ll guess I’ll try not to let that get in my head, thanks.”

 

“You’re welcome.”

 

Oooookay. “So, now that we’re here and not in the middle of Leslie’s campaign, what do you think of Jen, anyway?” Ben asked while they were on the topic of Jen, genuinely curious. Ben himself had been a little apprehensive of taking this job and working with Jen, but like he’d been telling Leslie on their Skype calls, he was surprised by how much he was coming to actually like her. She was still a hurricane, but a hurricane Ben didn’t have to beat, and damn if it wasn't a thrill to get to work with it instead.

 

“Oh,” April said, completely deadpan, no expression at all. “I’m obsessed with her.”

 

Ben squinted at her. Was she fucking with him again? She did really, really like to fuck with him.

 

“Look, April,” he said just to cover his bases, because that look in her eyes was making him a little nervous. “I know you know this, but just in case: Jen Barkley’s a very powerful and highly connected person. She’s not playing around, especially here, on her own turf.”

 

April only rolled her eyes and turned away. “Yeah, yeah, I won’t fuck it up for you,” she said as she pushed the door open and left.

 

“Wait, April,” Ben called after her, but the door was already closing behind her. “We need to talk about – okay. Later.”

 

                                                        

So it wasn’t like April went seeking Jennifer Barkley out herself. But it also wasn’t like April could help it if she walked into D.C. campaign headquarters at 10pm on a Friday while April was the only one there.

 

Ben had gone home hours ago to have a Skype date with Leslie. April’s choices in things to do had been to either go out with the other interns to whatever dumb bar Smellis had picked and hope he didn’t follow her around with giant, terrified, turned-on puppy dog eyes, or molt in the darkness of her own bedroom and try not to listen to the boring lovesick conversation Ben and Leslie were having in the living room. Or whatever conversation they weren’t having, and nope, no, God, get her out, get her out get her out –

 

She’d had her own Skype date with Andy yesterday since Andy was supposed to be out tonight with Tom at the Snakehole. April had told him to go, no reason for them both to be miserable, even if it made her more miserable, and so April had decided to just lean all the way into the misery and stay at the office. In the dark. And the quiet. All the better to commune with the forces of darkness, so.

 

Which was how the office was when a quiet beep and click of a lock disengaging signaled that someone had used their keycard to get into the office. April’s ears pricked despite herself. Maybe things were actually about to get interesting. Political espionage? A plot to embezzle campaign funds? Stowing a hostage from the principality of Lichtenstein over the weekend where nobody would notice? Agent Burt Macklin coming to rescue Janet Snakehole from an incredibly sad and boring Friday night?

 

It was none of those things. The quick, confident sound of high heels across the floor resolved into the form of Jennifer Barkley striding directly to Ben’s dark office and poking her head inside. April’s interest shifted as she watched silently.

 

“Ugh, Ben,” Jennifer was saying to herself as she spun back around. “The one time you aren’t the workaholic I know you are.”

 

Which was about the time she caught sight of April’s form slouched at her desk ten feet away, yelled, and staggered backward.

 

“Holy shit,” Jennifer shouted, holding one hand against her chest. “Who the fuck are you and what the fuck are you doing here? Actually, before we do anything else,” she said, thinking better of it, and simply fumbled around on the wall until she found a light switch.

 

April blinked at the onslaught of light and squinted, displeased.

 

“Oh. It’s you,” Jennifer said when she recognized April. “Wait, no, my original question stands. What the fuck are you doing here?”

 

April decided to stick to the simplest answer. “Uh, I work for Ben.”

 

Jennifer’s look was skeptical, but she said, “Okay. You know, I don’t really care enough to ask follow up questions. Where is Ben, anyway? He’s not picking up his phone.”

 

April sighed heavily, suddenly intensely sure that she’d made the right call to stay out of the apartment. “Skype date with Leslie.”

 

“Oh. Ugh,” Jennifer said, wrinkling her nose. “Wait, are you two Pawneeans living together?”

 

“Yup.”

 

“Adorable,” Jennifer said flatly. “Well, whatever this is is pathetic. And I happen to be in desperate need of a drink. And seeing as how my D.C. campaign coordinator, who is also your roommate, has done the equivalent of putting a sock on his doorknob, come on. Let’s get out of here.”

 

April sat up. Was her night actually getting interesting? “Wait. Really?”

 

“Do I look like I’m joking?” Jennifer demanded with a double handed gesture up and down her body – one April’s eyes followed obediently. “I mean, you’ll have to remind me your name, because I absolutely forgot it five minutes after you told me the first time, but I do remember that you’re not boring. So are you coming or not? Don’t make ask again.”

 

April didn’t.

 

 

Because D.C. was a swamp heated by Satan and the fires of hell, and because Jennifer Barkley was apparently loaded, she called them an Uber Black to take them five blocks.

 

April wasn’t completely unused to feeling out of place next to ambitious, competent, intelligent, and professionally dressed women in politics, but Leslie had always been kind of an outlier. Leslie would probably say something that would make April want to crawl into a hole about it, like I’ve had the great privilege of watching April grow into the beautiful and competent young woman she is today, give or take a few adjectives and the degree to which April wanted the hole to close over her, but the truth was that she’d just kind of fallen into Leslie’s orbit when she’d been some dumb kid who hadn’t known or cared about anything.

 

April wasn’t that dumb kid anymore, which also meant she was extremely aware, actually, of the differences between her and Jennifer Barkley. April was still in her work clothes, sure, but Jennifer was on a whole other level with her power suit and pearls and heels and shiny perfect hair. And sure, April was a little afraid of her, but honestly she was also kind of into that. So sue her: April was married, not immune to the effects of hot, rich, powerful older women doing… whatever this was.

 

“So what’s your angle,” Jen asked around a sip of her drink where they’d managed to get two seats at the bar. “You don’t really strike me as somebody who’s looking for an in in federal politics. Or who’s besties with somebody like Ben Wyatt. But I’ve been wrong before.”

 

Gross. April hadn’t even meant to get this far in municipal politics. “No,” she deadpanned. “I actually hate everything about everyone, including Ben. And Congressman Murray.”

 

Jennifer only looked bemused. “Okay,” she said, drawing out the word. “What exactly are you doing here, then? Ben’s not incompetent. It’s why I hired him. And he’s too endearingly anal retentive to do anything but surround himself with overachievers. Which is also why I hired him. So what does that make you?”

 

“Uh, his illegitimate daughter from a summer fling with a Puerto Rican witch. So a nepotism hire, I guess.”

 

“Ben’s what, ten years older than you?” Jennifer said, unimpressed. “Max?”

 

“Okay,” April conceded. “I’m his current hot young summer fling. Shh. Don’t tell Leslie.”

 

“Nice try, if Ben weren’t grossly into Leslie. Try again.”

 

“Fine, I’m the Puerto Rican witch. I hexed him and it all went horribly, horribly wrong and now I’m here, help.”

 

Jennifer only leaned an elbow on the bar and stared at April like she was the most fascinating thing she’d ever seen. “You are so fucking weird,” she said as she leaned her head into her hand. She was definitely at least a little drunk, but April wasn’t going to call her on it.

 

“Thank you,” she said and drained the rest of her own drink.

 

“But I’m curious, though – do you ever give real answers to these kinds of questions?”

 

“No, not really,” which was mostly the truth. “You’re a campaign manager – "

 

“Political consultant,” Jennifer corrected with an unapologetic smile. “I don’t like to be boxed in.”

 

“Okay, you’re a political consultant in like, big time federal politics, and you’re out for drinks on a Friday night with me,” April said. “Puerto Rican witch-slash-lowly campaign intern. What does that make you?”

 

“Bored,” Jennifer answered and sucked up more of her drink through her straw. “It wasn’t hard to guess that you were, too.”

 

That was fair. This bar wasn’t exactly screaming “not boring”, though, but it was definitely a step up from where her night had been before Jennifer had shown up.

 

“Do you really like it in D.C.?” April asked abruptly.

 

Jennifer gave her a sidelong glance. “You don’t,” she guessed.

 

April missed Pawnee. “It’s okay, I guess.”

 

Jennifer stared at her for a minute, halfway to a smile. Then she put down her drink and sat up. “I like being a power broker,” she said. “I like being the one who moves the chess pieces on the board, especially if a ton of those pieces are stupid – and they are,” Jennifer said, rolling her eyes. “God, they are so stupid.” But just as quickly, she got serious again, and said, “I like winning. And I usually do. That’s why everybody comes to me. I’m the best, and they know it.”

 

“But what’s the point, if everybody’s stupid?”

 

“Oh, now you’re sounding like Leslie Knope,” Jennifer said with a smirk; and horrified, April realized she wasn’t wrong. Jennifer laughed. “It’s not a bad thing. I actually like Leslie’s idealism. It's cute. It’s just hard to hang on to. There have always been people moving other people behind the scenes, people tapping other people for other people’s ends. I’d just rather be one of the tappers than one of the tappees.” She trained a shrewd look on April. “You know, I've got a feeling you might, too. Generally speaking, that is.”

 

“You don’t even know me,” April said, but her thumbs smoothed compulsively through the condensation on her empty glass over and over and over.

 

If Jennifer noticed – which she probably did – she didn’t say anything about it. April wasn’t sure if that was better or worse.

 

“Okay, well, you said you were bored,” April said to change the subject. “Are you still bored?”

 

Jennifer’s eyebrow quirked and she turned to face April more fully. “You got something else in mind?”

 

April took that as a yes and pulled out her phone, flicking to the Uber app. “Yes, Jennifer.”

 

“It’s Jen,” Jen said with a lazy, growing smile.

 

“Okay, Jen. Finish your drink in…” April’s screen changed as a driver was confirmed. “Seven minutes. And get ready for gay karaoke.”

 

 

“April? April? Come on, April, wake up and let me know you’re alive.”

 

April opened one eye and groaned.

 

“Signs of life!” Ben praised her. “Good morning, sleepyhead.”

 

He was spending way too much time with Leslie. Because he wasn’t Leslie, April muttered at him to fuck off.

 

“Yeah, I’m actually about to,” he said, which meant that he was getting too used to April. “I got a message from Jen this morning that she wants to meet about something before she flies out. I’m not even gonna ask what happened to you last night, but I’ve put a large bowl next to you just in case you need it, and there’s water, Advil, and a granola bar on the table.”

 

April made flopping motions in the direction of the generic Ikea coffee table without lifting her head. Leslie would have taken pity on her. Back when he’d had been her and Andy’s roommate in Pawnee, awkward and unsure Ben almost definitely would have. Today-Ben just laughed and said, “Yeah, you’re fine. I’ll be back this afternoon. Let me know if you want to have dinner in tonight. Whenever you actually want to think about food, that is.”

 

“Wait,” April said around the fucking cotton balls in her mouth as his earlier words caught up with her. “Jen?”

 

“Yeah. Jen Barkley.”

 

“I know who Jen Barkley is,” she groaned, irritated. For – God, so many reasons. Jen had texted Ben this morning? She’d gone shot for shot with April! Who the fuck even was she?  

 

Ben laughed again, the motherfucker. “I’ll see you later. Feel better.”

 

April flipped him off, but only in her head, because anything else was too much effort. She could always count on Ben to get the idea, though, and he laughed again as he shut and locked the door behind him. Whatever. She hoped his meeting sucked. She hoped Jen’s head was pounding and she was wearing dark sunglasses and that she was kind of a bitch to Ben about it.

 

If there was a recording on her phone of Jen Barkley drunk off her ass and still managing to perform the shit out of “Since U Been Gone” to a crowd of also drunk-off-their-ass gays screaming with her, who really cared? April was going back home in less than a month, and then she’d probably never see her again. And if April pulled it up later when she finally reached for her phone (sixteen texts from Andy since three in the morning, two from Ben), still slouched down into the couch, nobody really needed to know that, either.

If Jen did get Leslie to run, and win – which she would, if Jen got her to run – it wasn’t hard to assume that she'd drag Maybe-Avril right along with her to Indianapolis, probably with a title change to chief of staff, and probably also insisting all the way that she hated it.

Some quick deductions led Jen to a conclusion she felt was probably pretty damn close to the truth.
