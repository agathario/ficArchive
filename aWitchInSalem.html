1688, Salem, MA, USA

The sun had set long ago, and the night was cold, frost growing like lichen on the wood and stone buildings of the seaside town of Salem, Massachusetts. Outside the town, up a small track that may have been left by deer or rabbits passing through, the faintest glimmer of firelight could be seen- a bonfire lit just behind the dip of a hill, not visible to the town or any travelers on the main road. 

Here, the witches of Salem were gathered.

 

Of course, they did not publicly proclaim themselves witches; nor did they claim to be Puritans; they styled themselves as another branch of Christians, calling themselves the Covenant of the Separatists. They, too, descended from forbears that had been hunted and hounded from their homes in England, and remained unwelcome in Europe. But here, they could find a home. Salem was a fortification and a rapidly growing merchant town; it offered protection and opportunity, but also demanded order, modesty, and piety.

As outsiders, the Covenant were always apart from the rest of the townspeople; they could not worship in their churches, or socialize with their womenfolk, or bury their dead in the hallowed ground of the churchyard. But they were tolerated, because they served a purpose and kept to themselves. The townspeople knew that medicines, and small charms, and useful trinkets like needles that never dulled and matches that always burned, even when wet- could be gotten from these outsiders living among them. And the witches relied upon the strong walls and fortifications of town, as well as the ability to purchase the things they could not make or grow themselves. 

Thus an accord existed between the town and the small group of families of the Coven of Salem. 

Their fates were intertwined. Both struggled under the burdens, the cost, and the labor of carving a European settlement into a space there had never been one before- they mutually benefited, and thus, tolerated each other, but only up to a point.

What was often whispered about, though, was how fragile this peaceful coexistence truly was. And so, both groups had rules- inviolable, rigid rules no one dared break. The price for doing so would be devastating for both groups.

 

The fire that night marked a very special occasion. A hooded group of women stood in a circle, faces in shadow. Closer to the fire knelt 7 young girls, none older than 12. Their eyes were all fixed upon one imposing figure. She stepped forward and lowered her hood.

Evanora Harkness’ fingers traced the locket she wore around her neck. She peered down her sharp nose at the kneeling figures silhouetted against the firelight, her eyes lingering one extra moment on the shivering form of her young daughter, Agatha.

”Speak the words,” Evanora commanded.

”On the eve of my thirteenth year,” came the quavering voices of the kneeling girls,  “I pledge my newly emerging powers to the Coven. I will serve the Coven in all things. I will obey the Coven without question. I will never stray from the path set before my feet by the will of the Coven… so mote it be.”

The fire in the stone ring flared, showering the dark night sky with sparks. Evanora nodded, once, satisfied. She replaced her hood and stepped back into the ranks of the Coven, face masked by shadows. Agatha and the rest of the girls remained kneeling beside the fire, awaiting their next instructions.

Another witch stepped forward; this was Rosemary, the second in command.

”With these words, the Coven welcomes you to the ranks of our initiates. In your new role, you will learn to serve the Coven.” 

Rosemary fixed each of the girls with a serious look, steel in her eye.

”You are an adult now, and will be given no leeway. Transgressions will be punished harshly.” The cold voice spoke decisively. “Allow me to remind you of the tenets of your time as an initiate. I will only tell you these rules once, and then it is up to you to remember.” Rosemary took another step forward. Her stiff posture reminded Agatha of witches she’d seen broken under the lash, carried away from the Gathering Place barely conscious. She did not want to be one of those witches.

“Initiates serve the coven. They have no agency, they entertain no thoughts of independence. The Coven must be protected, and in return, initiates are protected by the Coven. The work you will do will make you strong; initiates will draw water from the well, tend to the hearths, and forage for food,” recited Rosemary. “Magic is a gift and a privilege, and must not be wielded for menial tasks. Thus, you will not be taught the art of the craft until you have proven yourself to the Coven.”

Agatha risked a glance at the imposing woman standing above her, dropping her eyes immediately. Rosemary noticed the lapse and frowned.

“When we decide you are ready, you will leave the ranks of the initiates and begin to learn the craft. Now, speak your final oath.”

Agatha spoke the words that always felt like ashes in her mouth: “For the Coven, I will.”

Evanora spoke again, not moving from her place, as Rosemary reclaimed her own place and lifted her hood: “And never forget- the townspeople would not suffer a witch to live, even if she never interfered with their God or with their lives. We live in the shadows, or we die in the light. Remember this well- for if you break this rule, your life, and the lives of the entire Coven will be forfeit.” 

Finally, the initiates were allowed to rise and to begin the silent walk back to the town. They filed past the Coven members, who remained behind to attend to business.

“Daughter, do not disappoint me.”

Agatha heard the whispered words as she walked past her mother. It felt like an indictment, or a curse- as if Evanora was already disappointed, already justified in her disdain for her daughter and all Agatha’s best efforts would be in vain.

There was nothing to be said in reply. Agatha bowed her head, telegraphing contriteness and obedience, stamping down the anger at already being singled out as ‘not good enough.’ She did not speak or look up from her feet until she reached her home and closed the door firmly behind her.

Raising her head, Agatha crossed to the shelf that held her mother’s spell book, and the books of witches the Coven had defeated. She did not need a candle to see them; to her, they glowed, always, glimmering and whispering to her of the secrets within their bindings. She fisted her hands in her skirts, not reaching out to touch.

“I’m going to get to learn magic,” she whispered, promising herself it would happen. Holding herself back from using the magic twisting and pulsing within her made her skin itch and her throat feel tight. Sometimes, she didn’t succeed at keeping her magic down. Power would burst from her with unpredictable results- a contrary cow suddenly becoming docile and allowing her to milk it; a stubborn well bucket suddenly creaking to the top of its rope without her touch.

Agatha remembered one such occasion, wincing at the memory; she’d been six years old, and her mother was teaching her to lay a fire; to stack the wood, to shred the tinder, to strike the flint. But Agatha had not been able to get the flame to catch. Evanora had watched, offering no help. Growing more and more frustrated, the young girl had let out an angry huff and made a swinging backhanded motion towards the hearth. A fire had flared immediately. Agatha had been so proud, but Evanora had frowned and chastised her at her lack of control. In Evanora’s eyes, Agatha had seen something like fear. She didn’t understand why- her mother made fires like that frequently, and no one had seen them. After that, Evanora had been more careful not to perform casual magic around Agatha.

So yes; as a child, she’d been shown grace, protected from punishment. But now, as an initiate, she would not be shown the same leniency. She knew she’d need to find an outlet, to hide her burgeoning powers and her inability to keep them inside. 

For now, she knew, the only way forward was through.

—

A hive shares one mind, and one purpose: to serve the queen. And in exchange, the queen ensures the hive survives. Thus did the Salem Coven operate. There was no room for dissent, no space for any member to step out of the ranks, or to challenge their predetermined role. Maternity did not mean maternal feelings: no tenderness, no pride, no expressions of love passed between the queen and her children. 

The queen’s attendants were the most powerful witches, and they, too, produced offspring, but did not cherish or coddle them. They nurtured them to adulthood, and bound them to serve the coven with iron ties. An oath spoken on the last night before a witch’s thirteenth year tied their lives and their magic to the queen and her inner circle. 

It was into this society that Agatha Harkness was born. Her prospects as a girl in the wider society of the new world in 1675 were not better; nor would her chances of survival have been improved by being born to parents without magic. Still, perhaps she would have been better off being raised by wolves. Wolves, at least, showed mercy. Wolves were capable of tenderness and affection.

Evanora Harkness, reigning queen of the Salem Coven, was not.

—

Water duty was the most thankless of tasks: each Coven member’s cottage needed water, endless amounts, and the well never seemed to be close to the home that needed it.

The filthy, sweaty toil of carrying water through the dirt streets, frequently dusty or muddy or icy with slush, was the worst of the tasks the initiates could be assigned, and it was used by the Coven as punishment. Agatha was frequently on water duty, though she rarely knew why. Older Coven members would find fault with her at any opportunity, and assign her the punishment.

She longed for the task of foraging; her mind wandered sunlight dappled paths, and she dreamed of crisp greens snapped off the stalk, and earthy mushrooms gathered in a basket. She sighed when she thought of berries, ripe to bursting, swaddled in her handkerchief. But instead, she trudged under the yoke of the water buckets. Sore, exhausted, and unfulfilled, she fell asleep in her cold, narrow bed and dreamed of winding paths through the woods.

—

Time passed, and the Coven marked the cycle of the seasons, celebrating the birth of the new year, and the death of the old. Agatha grew tall, learned to be self contained, tried to pass by without notice. She was fourteen now, and she watched as many of those who joined the ranks of the initiates at the same time she did became fully fledged Coven members, having met some undefined requirement to move up. But it never seemed to be Agatha’s turn.

Her powers burned inside of her. She didn’t know how the others lived with this fire licking along their nerve endings. She wasn’t sure how long she could last before her desperation to know peace outweighed her fear of the Coven’s retribution.

The Coven witches were a secretive bunch, and jealous of their knowledge. The “lessons” Agatha longed for were withheld from her, and she chafed more and more with every passing day spent hauling water, rather than casting spells. The other girls worked without complaint. They found moments to chatter and dance, to skip and sing. 

But they never asked why they weren’t being given the chance to study magic. Perhaps they knew something Agatha didn’t, something that gave them hope. She tried not to believe that she was being held back, tried to understand how to be good enough for her mother. To be allowed to become a witch.

—

Agatha screamed as the lash came down again across her back. Her hands had gone numb at the place they were tied, and the sharp edges of the Meeting Place stake cut her.

Sweat stung her eyes, and her hair hung loose and tangled around her face. Even with her vision obscured, she knew her mother’s eyes were on her, dispassionate and unaffected. Maybe even pleased with Agatha’s pain.

”Mother-” she whimpered, “Please- no more.”

Her latest transgression had been trying to peek into the training hall to watch the Coven witches wielding their magic. She just wanted to learn, just wanted to know everything .

She’d managed to see sparks of color, to memorize a few of the simpler hand motions, before she’d been seen. She was dragged here for her public lashing.

Evanora did not acknowledge her daughter’s plea. She simply watched the lash fall again, and tracked the blood trickling down Agatha’s back.

Breathing through the pain, Agatha raised her head, baring her teeth at the crowd of gathered witches. They were laughing at her behind their hands, delighting in her humiliation and her pain. Agatha saw no friendly faces in the crowd and heard no calls for mercy. Something in her snapped, and she felt all her emotions freeze, shatter, sink deep within her. Her magic swarmed to the surface, and she held it in, barely, but allowed it to fizz along her fingers, longing to break her bindings.

Her mother looked at her, appraising, then nodded at the witch holding the lash. It fell again. Agatha did not scream. Evanora frowned, motioning for the whipping to continue. Agatha didn’t cry, and she didn’t scream again. With each strike upon her back, the crowd’s noise changed, becoming an anxious, unsettled buzz.

When it was over, she rose shakily, donned her discarded dress, and strode home on legs that felt like water. Blood streamed down her torn back. Everyone she passed moved aside silently. Some turned away altogether. Evanora ordered Agatha be given water hauling duty, and didn’t allow her to be healed with magic.  Each night, she gathered herbs to make her own poultice, knowing that if she caught a fever, she would likely die. For weeks after, Agatha carried heavy pails of water, blood blossoming through the fabric of her dress. Pale, teeth gritted, and powered by rage, her steps never faltered. Witches on the street would stop to watch her stride by, whispering amongst themselves.

No one was laughing at her now.

—

At 15, Agatha saw a man die.

It was an accident- she was gathering mussels from the rocks as the tide slipped back out to sea. She was a child of the ocean and knew how to walk on the seaweed slick stones and keep her feet. But she watched a man on a nearby rock spit jutting out into deeper waters misjudge his footing, and fall into the churning, tide-torn bay. He never came back up.

And Agatha had needed to run- she couldn’t be seen, lest the townspeople blamed her. 

As she ran, she glanced back, and stumbled to a stop- there was another figure on the rocks now. Cloaked in a dark covering, cowl covering their face, she couldn’t make out their features. She choked on her breath in fear- had she been seen? Would she be blamed?

But the figure looked different, somehow, from the upright, neatly dressed townsfolk. As she continued to watch, the hooded figure reached into the water and pulled the man back onto the rocks. But he wasn’t- he looked wrong. He wasn’t solid… and he’d been under the water for so long…

Agatha had seen enough- she continued her race back to safety. When news reached the Coven of a man drowning, Agatha shivered. She knew there were powers beyond her understanding in this world, and she felt sure she’d just seen one.

Apprentice, devoted to thy craft

Chapter Summary

This interpretation of Agatha's history and early life finds Agatha beginning to break away from a community that rejects her. As she is ostracized from her Coven, she embraces secrecy and learns to be self-reliant. Then, one terrible day, she casts her eyes upon Death, eyes meeting over a newly fallen corpse...

When she turned sixteen, Agatha stole a book from her mother’s shelf. She was skilled enough now to manage it without being caught immediately, and foolish enough to think, as time went on, that she would never be caught.

And for a time, she wasn’t. 

Agatha alone among her peers remained in the ranks of the initiates. Her nearest year-mate was a girl of thirteen. Everyone else had been tapped to become a full Coven member. Agatha knew Evanora watched her going about her chores, as a hawk watches a rabbit. Her mother’s disapproval and exclusion had left Agatha friendless; none dared befriend a witch marked with disapproval by the Coven’s leader. Agatha performed every task set before her silently, competently. She was beyond reproach, hoping one day to earn approval, but also knowing that she had a secret that would get her through this impossible time. She would take what she knew to be her birthright.

Agatha had consulted the chickens on her decision; “See, hens, the rules do not say I may not learn from a book- only that I will not be taught by Coven members until they say I’m ready. And, the rules only require that I do chores without magic.” She held one warm, speckled egg up to the sky before placing it in her basket. “I am not using magic now, and I don’t use it for anything menial. So that isn’t breaking any rules.”

She set her full egg basket down on the straw to nudge her hand below a broody hen’s rear. The bird, ruffled, squawked shrilly. Agatha shushed it, and went on- “And it is all in service to the Coven, after all,” Agatha insisted. “A witch must be strong to serve her Coven. I only wish to be stronger.” A hen pecked at her boot, demanding to be fed. Agatha sighed, bringing crushed cornmeal from her apron pocket and sprinkling it about. The chicken coop burst into life, each hen jostling for position. One hen, bedraggled looking and lacking feathers, seemed to be the coop’s whipping boy- no matter how she came at the gaggle, she was not let in. Agatha frowned, and placed some grains at the sickly looking hen’s feet. She ate. “Grow strong, little hen.” She muttered, stroking the fitful feathered back gently. 

Convinced of the justice of her actions, Agatha took the tome that whispered to her from the shelf the most loudly. Those whispers invaded her dreams, tickled at her waking thoughts, enticed her with its promises of power. She stopped fighting the call; when she picked it up for the first time, the whispers intensified, then died down to a peaceful shushing noise, like the sound a mother makes to calm her child… Agatha felt like she’d finally come home.

The book opened an entire world before her. She learned to summon her power from deep within, to concentrate the tingling heat along her palms, to explode the gathered light from her fingertips, bending it to her will as it went.

Thus, Agatha became a thief. She stole time outside the town, far from the Coven’s rune protected walls which would have alerted the Coven to her use of magic, hiding in a distant clearing near a field used for haying. 

One hot summer day, like the dozens of days before, Agatha snuck away with the black bound spell book stuffed in an apron pocket. 

She ran through the endless woods, bare feet nearly silent on the soft carpet of moss and leaves, not minding the sweat gathering at her hairline. She felt free, alive.

Standing in the center of the clearing, she looked at the sky, re-read the book resting in the grass in front of her, and began the incantation.

The heat of the summer, the scent of flowers and grass curing into hay, and the quiet fizz of her magic leaving her hands was her happy place.

At least, it was until she was discovered.

The witch was named Prudence, and she was one of Evanora’s spies. She was not of the inner circle, but  was a toady always grasping at opportunities to ingratiate herself.

Agatha had been totally unaware that she was being followed that day; Prudence had moved so silently, perhaps aided by a spell. 

Prudence charged into the clearing, shouting, “Stop this at once!”

Her screech of rage at the purple magic dancing along Agatha’s fingers startled the girl, and the fizzing ball dissipated, leaving her cold and defenseless. 

“How dare you,” Prudence seethed. Her chest was heaving, eyes wild.

Agatha tried to defend herself- “I was only curious- I was just borrowing the book-”

But Prudence could not be reasoned with. “Agatha Harkness,” she screeched, “I accuse you of performing magic beyond your station. I claim the right to punish you for your transgression, in the name of the Coven!”

Agatha took a step back, holding her hands up, knowing it would be futile to try to defend herself, but needing to try-

And that was when the other witch lashed out at Agatha. Overly eager, perhaps, to show Evanora that there was no situation where she would hesitate, she struck with murderous intent. Any other nascent witch would have crumbled beneath the attack.

Agatha braced for pain, for the darkness of the end of her life- but even as her eyes closed, she realized something was happening that she did not expect. The power flowed into her body, but instead of tearing at her, it curled there, settled down, and stayed, making space for more.

Instead of pain, Agatha felt a growing ecstasy. Her body drew in the magic like her lungs drew in air, naturally and comfortably, but the magic felt like pure, unadulterated joy. Heart racing, Agatha leaned into the feeling greedily.

This couldn’t be right.

Agatha opened her eyes and saw the terror, the rage, the fear on the pinched face across from her. She watched Prudence try to break away, and then panic as she realized she couldn’t. Agatha took a deep breath in, and instinctively felt along the magical line connecting her to the other witch. She could see into the well of the other witch’s power. 

Agatha felt euphoria like she had never known- every drop of power she took in brought the delirious joy up another notch. She laughed out loud, on an unbelievable high. Each moment made her feel more fulfilled, made her feel like she could take up the space she was owed. The feeling was so incredible, she never considered that it couldn’t last. Seeking more and feeling the flow slowing, Agatha gasped, devastated at the sudden loss. Without thinking, she pulled at the line she held, and suddenly, it all went wrong.

The well of power within Prudence dried, crumbled, and so too did the vessel for that power, crashing to the ground, then lying still.

Agatha felt like a puppet with its strings cut, staggering and barely keeping her feet. 

She gaped at Prudence's crumpled form. Her brain wasn’t understanding what had happened. She’d never heard of such a thing as stealing another witch’s power. She didn’t know that it could kill them.

Her body and mind felt completely split- her body thrumming from the influx of magic, the sensation of it making her feel elated, celebratory- but her mind could not stop screaming “She’s dead, you killed her, they’ll kill you, you have to run, run away, RUN!” over and over and over.

And then, a cloud passed overhead, and the day suddenly felt dark and cold. Agatha realized that the roaring in her ears was now accompanied by the absolute absence of the sound of birds, insects, or rustling leaves.

There was a sound like fabric tearing. A figure, cloaked in green, appeared on the edge of the clearing.

Agatha panicked, and turned to run, her heart in her throat.

“Wait,” commanded a voice. Agatha felt the word rather than hearing it. It echoed through her rib cage like the beat of a great drum and she froze.

”Look. See what you have done.” There was something like admiration in the voice, although Agatha could not have said how she could identify admiration in a voice she could not hear. She stared at the cloaked figure, but could not see a face, and did not recognize them, though something niggled at her subconscious… Trembling, but obedient, she turned and looked at the corpse she had made. Prudence was an unrecognizable husk, desiccated and shrunken. Her skin was gray, her eyes staring and accusatory, mouth still wide in that final scream of agony.

Agatha felt ill, and fell to her knees, slamming hard into the gravel. She tried to focus on the pain in her knees, tried to ground herself. She knew that the other witch would have killed her, but guilt and blame castigated her. She was not supposed to raise a hand to another member of the Coven, even for her own self-preservation. The Coven, the hive, demanded all of her, and would take what they could wring from her, and if she transgressed, she would die. 

But now- she had killed, rather than die for the Coven.

Her mother would be the one to kill her.

It was that final thought, and the visual image of Evanora's grim satisfaction as she cast the fatal blow, that decimated the last of Agatha's control. She turned her head and was sick, losing the sour contents of her stomach into the grass of the clearing. She felt a hand on her shoulder; it brushed her hair away from her face.

”This was not your fault,” that faceless voice said simply into her body, the words penetrating her distress. “Self-preservation, saving yourself, is natural. Death as a result of losing to a stronger foe is natural too.” The hand withdrew. “Look at me, young witch.”

Agatha looked up into the green cloak.

She saw there a terrible face, devoid of flesh, muscle or sinew. Without the coverings of life, the face was the stuff of nightmares but compelling all the same. She was fascinated by the terror it inspired in her heart, and the interest it spiked there as well. She did not flinch away- though she felt terror, she did not feel threatened. She felt something else, too- power radiated off this being. Intoxicating, seductive, otherworldly power. And she had felt strength in that hand, fleshless as it was.

Flushed though she was with the power she had taken from Prudence, Agatha wanted more. She had always coveted power and longed for strength, and now, beside this terrible and wonderful being, she wanted to be closer to all that delicious, enticing power. She could not move away, did not break eye contact. Trembling, she waited for what would come next.

The empty sockets of the grinning skull below the hood were focused upon her. The mouth did not move, but she heard the voice say, “You are not afraid, though you must know who I am.” 

“You are Death,” Agatha whispered, not bothering to temper the reverence in her voice.

An impossible chuckle, like the rolling growl of timpani, sent shivers along Agatha’s spine. She clutched at the grass, trying to calm her racing heart.

”That is one of my names,” the voice drawled, “but I have also been called Reaper.”

“Because you reap life?” Agatha asked hesitantly.

Yes, but I do not kill, I only take a soul leaving this life to the Next place. I am less an executioner than I am a gatherer.”  Death surprised Agatha by settling into the grass beside her, and pulling back their hood. In the shadows of the clearing, Agatha took in that face, familiarizing herself with its graceful lines and stark voids.  Agatha wondered if Death, too, felt this pull between them- like kindred spirits, meant to meet, even under these terrible circumstances.

Death’s mind-speak came again, thrumming behind her breastbone.

“I also participate in the creation of life, as much as I do in the ending of life. I am the guardian of the sacred balance, the one who turns the wheel of the cycle of life.”

”Creation?” Agatha asked.

Death held up one skeletal hand, holding it out for her to see a small brown grain. “Death leaves behind a seed,” Between the bleached bones, Agatha watched a tiny seed split, and a vine curl out, expand, sprout leaves, produce a bud, and then burst into riotous bloom.

“The seed becomes a plant, and blossoms.” The flower was unlike anything Agatha had ever seen before, bright and delicate- white leaves cupped golden, dewy stamens; a pink and purple speckled throat descended to a sharp closed point. The bud was standing as proudly upright as a queen. It was fragrant in the heat, and Agatha could smell its sweet scent even from a distance.

“In time, the flower dies, and leaves behind a seed.” In their hand, Agatha watched the vine and the flower whither. She immediately mourned its loss, but then, miraculously, it grew again, and blossomed, just as beautifully as before.

“This cycle is entrusted to my care.”

Agatha stared, mouth agape, eyes wide with wonder and delight. Death held the flower out to her, and she hesitated before taking it, but then reached out and did. Her fingers brushed the bone of one long, thin finger, but she did not feel revulsion or disgust. The bone was dry, somewhat rough, and warmed by the sun. She gazed at the perfect flower in her hand, completely charmed by its beauty. Holding it to her nose, she breathed it in; pollen tickled her nose, and the rich scent of the flower enveloped her, taking her away from this place and her conflicted heart for a brief moment.

Death slowly pulled their hand back, tucking it below their robe.

”I must go now, young witch.” If a skull could convey regret, Agatha imagined that would be what she saw on the permanently grinning face as skeletal hands pulled the hood back over their head.

”Oh-“ Agatha glanced at the fallen form of the witch. She was glad she was already on her knees, as she felt her legs go weak again. The weight of her actions fell on her anew. “I suppose… I must have created more work for you.” She looked down at the ground. “I’m sorry,” she whispered.

Death said nothing as they stood, but their hand came out from the folds of their robes holding a black knife. The blade was wickedly sharp, and looked as if it had been carved from the horn of a great beast. Agatha did not shrink away from the brandished blade; if it was her time, she would not fight her fate. It had not served her well to do so last time. But she had manners, at least.

“Thank you,” she murmured to the figure looming above her. “For the flower, and… for comforting me in my last moments.”

But the knife did not swing down for her; instead, it swung up, and ripped a hole through the air. A path through a dark wood, shrouded in mist, was barely visible to Agatha through the tear. She looked, leaning towards it-

“It is not your time yet, young witch,” rasped that deep voice from inside her. “And it will not be for a long, long time to come.”

Agatha blinked at this pronouncement, but held her tongue, and observed.

Death turned slowly, black knife raised again, and with a twist of their wrist, the body of the fallen witch became enveloped in a black and green mist, then melted into the earth, leaving behind a patch of grass that looked much like any other grass in the clearing, if a bit greener and better fed.

Agatha cried out, relief and fear mingling together. She felt overwhelmed, and her eyes welled with tears. Death looked at her again, reaching one white bone finger out to wipe a tear from Agatha’s cheek. She leaned into the touch, knowing instinctively comfort was being offered to her, and that it was rare enough in her day to day life. The bony hand curled into a loose fist, knuckles brushing along her cheekbone, stopping short of her lips. Agatha took in a shuddering breath, and the hand dropped away, one long black sleeve falling over the limb. 

“Death is my bread and butter,” the voice rumbled in her chest. “I love all aspects of life, and death is just one more piece of that.” 

 Death opened their robe and sheathed their knife; below the robe, Agatha saw more palely glinting bone: ribs, a pelvis. Something dark but sharp glittered in their chest, right where a heart would be- Agatha recognized a fist sized chunk of obsidian, carved into a multi-faceted round shape and bound with black leather.
