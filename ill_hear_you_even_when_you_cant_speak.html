First impressions

The bar was loud in the way that makes her teeth buzz—a steady, thrumming pulse of bass beneath overlapping conversations, glasses clinking, laughter spilling high and loud from the next booth. The air smelled like wine and someone’s spilled vodka soda. The kind of place where people either shouted to be heard or spoke too close, lips brushing ears intimately.

Rio stood just inside the entrance of The Witches Road, her eyes squinting against the purplish lighting that flickered like a headache waiting to happen. She hadn’t wanted to come. She hated the way bars overloaded her senses—sticky floors, chaotic music, faces moving too fast. But she’d said yes days ago, when it had felt like a good idea. Before she knew that there would be someone new joining them.

She scanned the room, searching out familiar shapes, her body tensing the way it always did when she knew she was about to have to mask and perform Normal.

There they were—tucked into the corner booth, squeezed tight like a pack of sardines who had also pre-gamed heavily.

Billy was already halfway through a pint, gesturing wildly like he was narrating a tale. Alice leaned into her new girlfriend Jen, eyeliner smudged into a smoky blur. Lilia, always mid-toast, glass raised and laughter spilling out in bursts.

And then her. The new person that had thrown off her usual routine.
Agatha.

Jen’s friend. The one Alice had casually mentioned would be there, she’d gone on to say something else about the woman but Rio had tuned her out. Too busy filing her away under potential threat.

She was seated at the end of the booth, a glass of something pale and fizzy cupped in one hand. Her face was composed, her features so sharp it was like someone had sculpted her. Sleek chocolate hair hung in curls down her back, a fancy jacket tailored within an inch of its life, skin lit coolly under the bar’s violet glow.

Her hands, Rio noticed, were unmoving, except for the way one thumb rubbed a slow, repetitive circle on the glass. A tic, maybe. Or a tell.

As Rio approached, Agatha’s gaze flicked toward her—icy eyes catching hers. A momentary lock. Intense. Unreadable.

Rio’s breath caught, stupidly. Her heart gave one loud, traitorous knock. No. Nope. Not that. She squared her shoulders against the reaction. That kind of beautiful had never meant anything good in her life. It was the kind that knew it, that used it. The kind that made eye contact with you just to watch you flinch.

“Rio!” Billy bellowed, nearly toppling over his chair in an effort to reach her. “Finally!”

“I was on time,” Rio muttered deadpan, squeezing into the narrow booth seat beside Agatha. The vinyl stuck unpleasantly to the backs of her thighs. “You’re just drunk.”

“Guilty,” Billy said, raising his glass like a confession. “But it’s fun!”

Alice grinned. “Rio, you’ve met Jen, right?”

“Once, yeah.”

Alice gestured toward the woman at the end of the booth. “And that’s Agatha. Jen’s friend.”

“I remember.” Rio turned toward Agatha, managing a nod. “Hi, Agatha.”

Agatha looked at her. Her mouth curled into something polite. She nodded towards her, smile warming her face as she looked at Rio.

But she didn’t speak.

Rio waited. Two seconds. Three. She tilted her head. The silence stretched, unbroken.

The others were already slipping back into their own conversations. Lilia was laughing about something. Someone had ordered fries. Jen was saying something about a horror movie premiere.
But here, on the edge of the booth, Rio sat suspended.

She cleared her throat. “So…you’re just gonna pretend you didn’t hear me say hello?”

Agatha turned slightly, enough to face her more directly. Her mouth opened. Then closed. Her eyes darted briefly to the table, then back up.
She opened her mouth again—lips shaping something, soundlessly.

Rio blinked, trying to follow. “Are you seriously… not answering me? Is this, like, a thing you do?”
There was no shift in her expression, just a flicker of something—confusion? Anger? This woman was not following the steps that politeness demanded.

Agatha raised a hand like she might gesture something—but then seemed to think better of it. She lowered it again. Her nails were short, almond shaped with a french tip. Her fingers trembled faintly. A faint pink line peeked out from beneath the sleeve of her jacket, barely noticeable. A healed burn, maybe. Or surgical scarring.

Rio felt the tight knot of irritation start to form in her chest. Her brain didn’t like ambiguity. She’d done her part—she’d greeted her, she’d been civil. There was a script for this sort of thing. When someone doesn’t follow the script, it meant something. It meant they were being rude, or dismissive, or worse—mocking you.

“You’re just sitting there,” she said, quieter now, under the buzz of the music, too low for the others to hear. “Looking at me like that’s enough. Like I’m supposed to what? Feel honoured that you glanced in my direction?”

Agatha’s face twitched with something—something Rio didn’t know how to name. She opened her mouth a third time. Her throat moved, a dry sound escaping—almost like a breath caught on a word that never formed.

Rio’s stomach tightened.

“Oh my god,” she snapped, voice still soft but sharp-edged now. “Don’t pretend now that you’re shy. That would be so—” she made a noise of frustrated disbelief, “—pathetic.”

Agatha’s jaw clenched. Her cheeks flushed, not with embarrassment, but something else. Panic, maybe. Her hand gripped the edge of her glass so hard her knuckles whitened.

Something isn’t right, a quieter part of Rio’s brain noted. But the louder part—the one that remembered high school, and college, and all the times girls like this had stared through her like static—kept talking.

“You think you can just sit there, because you’re pretty, and no one will call you out for being rude? That only works if your actually able to pull it off. Otherwise…” She shook her head. “You just look broken. Or stupid.”

The flinch that followed was immediate and sharp, a crack in the other womans marble like face. Agatha jerked back a half inch, eyes wide, like Rio had hit her without lifting a hand.

A loud crash from the other end of the booth broke the moment. Billy had dropped his glass, and suddenly everyone was moving again—voices rising, the server hurrying over with napkins, someone laughing too hard, too loud.

But in the corner, Rio sat frozen.

The heat in her chest wasn’t pride. It wasn’t victory.

It was acid.

Agatha said nothing. Didn’t try again. Just folded her hands in her lap—tightly, like they might shake apart otherwise—and stared down at the drink she hadn’t touched.

The music shifted. The lyrics blurred into the ambient noise, but the tension clung to the corners of the booth.

Rio looked away. Her jaw ached from clenching.
She didn’t know what she’d wanted from Agatha.
Maybe a witty comeback. Maybe a fight.

But whatever it was, it had curdled the moment it didn’t happen.

And now it sat between them, heavy and awful.

Rio swallowed against the burn in her throat. It had nothing to do with the whiskey she hadn’t even ordered. Gone were the days where she’d let hot women make fun of her for the way she was born.

Agatha’s hands trembled beneath the table.
She kept them there, hidden in the shadows where the others wouldn’t see the way her fingers quivered, too tight and too raw. Her glass—still mostly full—sat untouched in front of her, the fizz long gone. A ring of condensation bled steadily onto the table beneath it, its edge trembling in time with her barely controlled breathing.

She didn’t look up. Not at the others laughing, not at Jen pressed into Alice’s side, her girlfriend’s eyeliner smudged from kissing or laughing or both.

Not at Rio, whose voice still rang in Agatha’s skull like the tail end of a slap.

The words hadn't even been loud. That’s what made them worse. Cruel, but quiet. Whispered. Like Rio didn’t even need volume to cut her down.

Agatha clenched her jaw until it ached.

She kept her face composed, posture still. But inside, everything was lurching.

A curl of hair had come loose from behind her ear. She pushed it back, fingers brushing the thin, pale scar near her temple that ran along the curve of her skull—barely noticeable unless you knew what to look for, unless the light caught it wrong. One of many. The rest were hidden beneath sleeves, beneath layers. A stitched history of the night it all cracked open.

The music thumped on. Billy was halfway through an elaborate retelling of a karaoke incident involving a bottle of Fireball and a broken ankle. The others laughed. Someone snorted. Someone spilled a drink.

No one noticed the way Agatha was slowly unraveling.

She ducked her head and pulled out her phone beneath the table, screen light casting her features in a cold, artificial blue.

[Agatha → Jen]
Hey. I’m gonna head out. Not feeling great. Can you let them know? xxx

She sent it with aching fingers. Even typing felt clumsy now as her anxiety heightened.

Across the booth, she watched Jen glance at her phone. Her brow furrowed in concern. She looked up, already leaning forward to ask something—but Agatha met her gaze and shook her head subtly. She forced a smile. Small. Reassuring.

Jen didn’t push. She never did. That was why Agatha liked her. Loved her, maybe, in the quiet kind of way that didn’t need to be said.

Jen just nodded, and under the table, reached for her hand and gave it a brief, gentle squeeze.

Agatha held on to that like it was air.

Then she stood, slow and careful, testing that her legs weren’t going to wobble before fully committing to the movement. She gathered her bag without fanfare, chucking a fifty bill onto the table to cover her drink and a decent tip.

Still, no one really noticed. They were too wrapped up in each other, in the warm, sticky joy of being drunk and with their closest friends, friends who had all welcomed Agatha with open arms. It should have made her feel safe. Instead, it made her feel like a ghost.

She moved around the booth quietly, mouthing soft goodbyes. Lilia was the first to notice.

“Leaving already?” she asked, standing to hug her. “You sure? We haven’t even started the bad singing yet.”

Agatha nodded, arms folding around her gently. Lilia smelled like jasmine and beer. Her hugs were always full-bodied, warm. Agatha appreciated the softness of it, even if she couldn’t quite lean in all the way.

Then Billy stood. “Aww, c’mon, Ags! It’s just about to start getting weird!” He pulled her into a lopsided hug, laughing against her shoulder.
“You’re gonna miss me butchering Bohemian Rhapsody.”

She smiled, thin but real. Let herself be held. She didn’t have the energy to correct the nickname. Not tonight.

Alice hugged her too, murmured something sweet and slightly slurred into her cheek—something about how it was nice to see her again, and they should catch up properly soon. Agatha nodded, grateful for the affection but too tired to promise anything back.

And then she turned—and there was Rio.

Still seated. Arms crossed, shoulders squared, jaw clenched like she was bracing for a fight that hadn’t come. Her gaze was sharp, confused.
As if she didn’t understand why Agatha was leaving but found relief that she was.

As if she thought she’d won something.

Agatha didn’t look at her directly. Couldn’t. She gave a faint nod, just enough to be polite, to acknowledge the existence of a person who had made her feel like she was there again, back in that hospital room, struggling to remember anything.

And then she stepped past her.

Rio didn’t say anything. But Agatha felt her eyes on her back the entire way to the door.

Outside, the air slapped her skin cold. She didn’t bother zipping her jacket, just shoved her hands into her pockets and walked.

She didn’t cry until the next block.

Even then, it wasn’t dramatic. Just a few hot, tears leaking down her cheeks as she pressed the back of her hand to her mouth and kept going. Fast. Determined. She didn’t want anyone to see. Didn’t want anyone to know that one girl’s quiet cruelty had undone her like this.

She had come so far.

Speech therapy. Months of relearning how to even make sound, how to breathe around the structure of a word. How to feel okay stumbling over even a simple hello in front of strangers. Her accident had left her with a brain injury that snarled her language processing, tangled it like thread too fine to grasp. The damage was catastrophic. Exhausting. Most people noticed but gave her space, unless they were impatient. Or unkind.

Rio had been both.

And it stung worse because—god, she was Agatha’s type.

That sharp jaw. Those intense eyes. That messy kind of presence, like she wasn’t trying to be magnetic, she just was. Jen had said it offhandedly the week before, while scrolling through her texts.

“You’ll like Rio,” she’d said, grin lazy. “She’s kinda feral. Different. But in a hot way. Definitely your type.”

Agatha had laughed. She’d even worn her favorite jacket tonight, the one that made her feel powerful. Polished. Whole.

She should’ve known better. Jen hadn’t told her the other part, that Rio was cruel. That Rio would take one look at her struggling to commute her brain to her mouth and think it was weakness.

That thought burned. Shame and fury mixing hot in her chest.

Agatha wiped her cheeks with the sleeve of her jacket and forced herself to keep walking.

She’d worked too hard to be treated like a broken thing.

Too hard to be mocked.

She didn’t text Jen. And she wouldn’t. Jen had looked so happy—Alice’s arm around her, that tipsy grin pulling at her mouth. Agatha didn’t want to crack that. Didn’t want to be the reason the night soured.

She would go home, she told herself. She would cry in the safety of her own home where no one was around to judge her. She would take off the jacket and wash her face and maybe, eventually, she would sleep.

But Rio’s words, quiet, precise, surgical, would haunt her sleep.

Back inside the bar, Rio sat stiffly, arms crossed too tight across her chest, her spine straight and pressed to the back of the booth. She stared at the door, unblinking. Watched it swing shut behind Agatha with a soft hiss. Watched like maybe Agatha would come back. But the door stayed closed.

“She just left,” Rio muttered, the words almost to herself.

Alice looked up from her drink. “Who?”

“Agatha,” Rio said. Her tone was clipped, sharp without meaning to be.

Jen, across from her, stopped tracing the rim of her glass. Her posture stilled. “She wasn’t feeling great,” Jen said. Quiet. Careful.

Rio shifted. “She didn’t say anything, not even goodbye.”

“She tried,” Jen replied, tone a little more direct now. “You just might not have seen it.”

A pause stretched out between them. The music thumped overhead. Somewhere near the bar, someone dropped a shot glass and whooped like it was hilarious. A chorus of laughter followed.
But the air around their table had gone quieter. Thicker.

Lilia glanced over. “I know you said she’s been working really hard,” she said, voice low. “Just being here tonight took effort.”

Rio frowned. “Effort?”

Jen looked up. Her eyes were sharp now—not angry, but alert, like she was ready to shield something fragile if she had to. “Didn’t Alice tell you?” Jen asked.

“She said… she mentioned something when she told me she’d be here tonight,” Rio said. Her throat felt tight. “I didn’t really—” She stopped herself. Swallowed the rest. Didn’t really listen. Didn’t really think it mattered.

“She had a car accident,” Jen said, turning her glass slowly between her palms. “Eight months ago. A drunk driver hit her at an intersection blowing through a red light.“

There was a silence at the table. Real this time. No one interrupted.

“The car flipped,” Jen continued. “Twice. Crushed her in on the driver’s side. She was unconscious when they pulled her out. Woke up ten days later.”

Billy let out a slow breath, low under his breath. Even Lilia looked away, as though the weight of it needed space.

“She had multiple broken bones but thats all healed. The main thing was she couldn’t talk when she woke up,” Jen said. Her voice dropped a little. “Not even basic stuff. She could think. She could understand. But the part of her brain that lets words out—”

“Was damaged?” Lilia finished, gently.

Jen nodded. “Traumatic brain injury. It’s not always visible. People don’t get it because she can still walk and write and laugh at jokes, but… speech is different. It’s more fragile than people think.”

“She can write, though?” Rio asked quietly. She hated how dumb the question sounded the second it left her mouth.

“Yeah. And read. And she knows what she wants to say. That’s the cruel part.” Jen’s voice had turned gentle again, but there was an edge under it. “The thoughts are still in there. The sentences are perfect. But when she goes to speak, they fall apart somewhere between her brain and her mouth.”

Rio said nothing. She felt the beginning of something rising in her chest—a knot of guilt, or something shaped like it.

“She’s been in therapy ever since,” Jen said. “Speech therapy. Occupational. Cognitive. She practices every day. She’s stubborn like that.”

Lilia smiled faintly. “She’d have to be.”

“Some days are good. She can get a few words out,” Jen said. “Other days…” Her voice faltered. “It’s like trying to force a sentence through a wall.”

Rio stayed silent. She remembered Agatha’s face tonight—how she’d turned toward her, opened her mouth. That flicker of something behind her eyes. The tension in her jaw.

And Rio had read it all wrong.

Had decided it meant something else. Something easier to accept.

Alice’s voice broke gently into the stillness. “She wanted to come tonight. Even though crowds are hard. Noise is hard. Talking in a group is hard. She still wanted to try.”

“She was excited to meet you,” Jen added, gesturing towards Rio.

Rio looked up, startled. “What?”

“I told her you were smart,” Jen said, with a small shrug. “And funny. The kind of person who doesn’t waste time with small talk.”

Rio blinked at that. Her mouth was dry.

“She even wore that stupidly expensive jacket,” Jen went on. “The one she puts on when she needs to feel brave.”

Rio remembered it now—sleek, deep navy. Tailored to perfection. The kind of thing that made someone look composed, powerful. But Agatha hadn’t looked powerful. Not when you looked closely. She’d looked… tense. Like every breath cost something.

“She’s not cold,” Jen said, as if reading Rio’s thoughts. “She’s not uninterested. And she’s not rude.”

Her gaze sharpened. Protective again.

“She just doesn’t owe anyone an explanation the moment they want one. Especially not in a bar full of noise and strangers. Especially not when she’s barely holding it together.”

Rio’s fingers tightened around her glass. The condensation had soaked through the napkin beneath it, dampening her skin.

She didn’t speak. Because if she did, she might have to explain why she hadn’t given Agatha a second chance to try. Why she’d decided what the silence meant without even asking herself if it might mean something else.

“She’s not broken,” Jen said. “She’s just… carrying something you can’t see.”

And Rio knew better than most what that was like.

She thought of the checklist in her head that never stopped running. The invisible rulebook she needed to make sense of other people. The way unfamiliar expressions tied her brain in knots. How much easier it was to assume the worst than to deal with ambiguity.

Alice had warned her. Gently, over the phone that morning “Sometimes Agatha takes a while to speak. Don’t worry if she seems quiet.” And Rio had nodded like she understood, brushing it off. She didn’t have time for vague social puzzles. If someone wanted to talk, they would. If they didn’t—why pretend?

But it hadn’t been pretend.

And now, sitting in the booth, the music pounding above her and the others starting to talk again like the tension had broken, Rio felt it sit like a stone in her stomach.

She hadn’t meant to hurt anyone.

But she had.

And no one even knew.

Except her.

The conversation around the booth had started up again, low and easy. Billy said something half-funny about the bartender’s ponytail, and Alice gave a soft laugh that didn’t quite reach her eyes.

But Rio wasn’t in it.

She sat in silence, still staring at the place Agatha had been. The shape her absence left behind was louder than the music now.

Every few seconds, a memory rewound and played itself again: Agatha’s parted lips. That slight tremble in her jaw. The look in her eyes—wide, too bright.

And Rio’s mind wouldn’t stop parsing it. Looping it. Trying to re-interpret what she’d dismissed.
She wasn’t smug. She was stuck.

Lilia’s voice brought her back. “What kind of work does Agatha do again?”

“She owns and runs a publishing company,” Jen said, tone neutral but proud.

That made the whole table blink. “Wait, she owns it?” Billy asked, raising an eyebrow.

“Yup,” Jen said. “Started it herself a years and years ago. Small imprint at first. It’s grown even more since the accident, actually.”

Rio frowned, her voice slipping out before she had time to filter it. “How can she run a company if she can’t talk?”

The silence came fast. Tight. Jen’s gaze snapped to Rio, sharp and glittering—but it wasn’t cruel. It was the kind of look people gave when they were deciding whether to explain something or walk away.

Lilia cleared her throat, giving Jen space.

“She can write,” Jen said finally. “Emails. Memos. She manages staff through text and documents. She’s really precise.”

“You told me she’s always been a control freak when it comes to work,” Alice added with a fond smile.

“But meetings?” Rio asked. “What does she do in a meeting?”

Jen exhaled slowly. “Depends. Sometimes she has someone else handle the speaking. Sometimes she prepares slides. Sometimes she types responses in real time. Depends on the day.”

Rio nodded, slowly. “So she’s… disabled.”

Jen’s shoulders tensed, and Alice gave her a quick look—checking in. “She has a disability, yes,” Jen said carefully.

Rio’s fingers twitched against the condensation of her glass. Her brain had latched onto a thought it couldn’t let go of. She needed to understand. Needed to see the whole system, or it wouldn’t compute.

“Does she get help with things?” she asked. “Like—does she live alone? Can she drive now? Is it permanent?”

Alice gave a soft wince.

Billy made a face like he wasn’t sure if he should derail the conversation.

Jen didn’t flinch. She just looked at Rio, and something in her eyes changed—not angry, but assessing. “Are you asking because you want to understand,” she said, “or because you’re building a profile?”

Rio blinked. “I—don’t know. I just… want to get it right.”

There was a pause. Jen nodded, once.

“She lives alone,” she said. “Yes. She can drive but she doesn’t like to, but she’s working on it. We don’t know if it’s permanent. But that doesn’t mean she’s not still adapting. Doesn’t mean it won’t keep improving.”

“She’s one of the most competent people I know,” Lilia added, having met Jen and Agatha before introducing Alice. “Seriously. Her brain just… routes around the roadblocks.”

Rio sat with that. Turning it over.

“She didn’t look disabled,” she said, frowning. “I mean—I don’t mean that like it sounds.”

“I know what you meant,” Jen said, a little too quickly. “It’s just… you can’t see everything that matters.”

“I didn’t see anything,” Rio whispered. “Even when I looked right at her.”

Alice reached over and gave her wrist a light touch. “Most people wouldn’t. It’s not obvious. That’s part of why it’s so hard.”

Rio didn’t speak again. Her brain was buzzing—thoughts tangling and colliding. She’d asked the wrong questions. Said the wrong things. And she didn’t even know which ones until after.

It wasn’t malice. She wasn’t cruel usually.

She was just… off. Again.

Her tone. Her timing. Her questions. Her assumptions.

She didn’t know how to ask about people without sounding like she was dissecting them. Didn’t know how to leave room for what she didn’t understand. And now Agatha had walked out into the night, misunderstood and unheard.

And Rio had let her. Had helped make her invisible.

Rio sat rigid in the booth, trying to stay still—but her fingers tapped the side of her glass in uneven beats. She didn’t notice. Her gaze was fixed on Jen now, as if the right answer might unlock something if she just asked the right combination of questions.

“So she runs a publishing company?” she said suddenly, cutting through the lull.

Jen glanced at her. “Yes, like I said, for years now. Why?”

Rio’s brow furrowed. “But… she can’t speak.”

There was a pause.

“She can’t speak easily,” Jen corrected. “But that doesn’t mean she can’t communicate. Or manage people. Or run a business.”

“She looked at me like she wanted to say something,” Rio said. “But nothing came out.”

“She probably did try to say something,” Jen said, her voice firm. “It’s just that her speech is what got damaged. Not her thoughts. Not her intelligence.”

Rio was quiet for a beat, then said, “I didn’t mean to imply she wasn’t intelligent.”

“But you assumed,” Jen said, not unkindly.

Rio looked away. “I... yeah.”

“She’s actually brilliant,” Alice offered, more softly. “She has a master’s in comparative literature. Speaks French and Italian. Reads Latin.”

Billy whistled. “Alright brainbox Agatha, damn.”

“She’s always been sharp,” Jen said. “Scary sharp. The kind of person who finished your sentence in a way that makes you realize you didn’t know what you were about to say.”

Rio frowned. “But the way she sat there—”

“She was struggling with her output,” Jen interrupted gently. “That’s all. Her mind’s untouched. Just the connection between what she thinks and what her mouth can do.”

Lilia added, “She got hit hard. A full-side collision. The part of her brain that forms and initiates speech—that’s what took the trauma.”

“She sometimes gets tremors,” Jen added. “Mostly when she’s overtired or anxious. Or when she tries too hard to push through the speech block. Her nervous system kind of… rebels.”

“So she can talk, just not all the time?” she asked, voice low but intent.

Jen gave a slow nod. Her shoulders had drawn back, subtly braced. “Yes. Some days it’s easier. Some words come reliably. But stress, noise, fatigue—all of that makes it harder. It’s like her brain… scrambles the delivery system.”

Rio tilted her head slightly, processing. “So it’s not just language. It’s the conditions around her. The environment.”

“That’s part of it.”

Rio nodded once, quick. “Did the doctors give it a name?”

Jen hesitated. “Yes. But it’s complicated.”

Rio leaned forward slightly. “I’d like to know it.”

There was a pause. Alice gave Jen a glance—not quite warning, but cautious. Jen’s jaw worked, just once. Then: “She was diagnosed with aphasia. More specifically, expressive aphasia. Her comprehension’s intact, her writing too, but initiating speech is like trying to push through glue.”

Rio repeated the term under her breath. “Expressive aphasia.” She let the shape of the words settle on her tongue. “So it’s damage to the speech-output center?”

“Yes.”

“But she can understand everything?”

Jen nodded.

Rio’s brows furrowed. “That must be incredibly frustrating.”

“It is,” Jen said. “Painfully so.”

Lilia, watching from the side, interjected gently. “Rio, are you okay?”

Rio didn’t answer the question. She was still focused. Still building the internal framework.

“Does she use AAC?” Rio asked next.

Jen blinked, surprised. “Sometimes. She’s tried apps, but they can feel clunky in conversation. She prefers typing short responses on her phone if she needs to explain something. But she doesn't always want to explain.”

“Because of shame?” Rio asked. “Or pride?”

“Because she doesn’t want to be reduced to a diagnosis,” Jen said, a little more firmly.

“I wasn’t reducing her,” Rio replied, brow creased. “I’m just trying to understand. If I know how her brain works, I won’t make the same mistake next time.”

There was a pause. Then, surprisingly, Jen softened. “That’s… fair.”

Rio nodded again. “She must plan around it constantly. Anticipate things. Scripts, maybe? Rehearsals?”

“She has some cards from speech therapy,” Jen said reluctantly. “Words she’s most likely to need to use. Sometimes she flips through them like flashcards before events so she can sound the words.”

Rio’s hands stilled.

The idea of that—someone rehearsing social interaction down to the syllable—landed somewhere deep and familiar in her chest.

“She writes alot more than she speaks,” Jen added. “She says writing feels like breathing, but speaking feels like swimming in mud.”

There was something in the way she said it—almost a direct quote.

Rio absorbed that in silence. The image played itself out in her head. Agatha, sat at a table. Flipping through cards. Practicing words. Alone.

“What else does she do?” Rio asked. “To cope. To adapt.”

Alice gave a quiet sound—half-sigh, half-warning. “Rio…”

“No, it’s okay,” Jen said, holding up a hand. Her eyes hadn’t left Rio’s. “She’s not being cruel. She’s… parsing.”

Rio blinked. “Parsing what?”

“You’re trying to translate her,” Jen said gently. “Into something your brain understands.”
