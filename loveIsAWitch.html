The U-Haul Diaries

Rio leaned against countertop of their kitchen, sipping her coffee and trying to wake up.  She looked around her home—quiet now, in the early mornings like this.   She took a sip, letting the warmth spread through her, and stared out at the greenhouse through the patio glass. It was a rare moment of peace in her otherwise chaotic life. In the quiet morning like this—where it is just her, she couldn’t help but think how she never thought she’d be here— have this.    

She couldn’t help but marvel at how much had changed, at the life she’d built. To say it had turned out differently than she imagined would be an understatement. Growing up, she’d pictured herself in love, sure, but this ? This life, with Agatha Harkness ? It was beyond anything she ever thought she could have.  

Of course, like any self-respecting lesbian, Rio had always dreamed of being married to wildly hot, older woman. Someone sharp, and sophisticated.  The kind of woman who turned heads wherever she went. But Agatha? Agatha was so much more than she could have dreamed. Somehow, she managed to be both a MILF and a sugar daddy rolled into one—Rio's ultimate wet dream. 

And, God help her, Rio loved every stubborn, fiery, and frustrating inch of that woman. 

Not that it was all smooth sailing. Agatha could be a pain in the ass even on her best days.  And on her worst? Well...let’s just say The Witch of Wall Street wasn’t a nickname handed out lightly. That title alone should’ve been a giant, flashing neon sign for Rio, complete with sirens blaring, screaming: DANGER: DO NOT TOUCH. 

But Rio? She had never in her life wanted to touch something— someone —as much as she wanted to touch Agatha Harkness. 

She could still remember the first time she saw her. Agatha had swept into the conference room like a storm, commanding attention without even trying. Her tailored plum pants and white shirt had hugged her frame just right, and those sharp blue eyes seemed to pierce straight through Rio when they landed on her. 

Flirting with Agatha had been reckless. Dangerous, even. Rio knew it from the start. Agatha wasn’t just her boss—she was the boss. The one everyone both feared and idolized. A woman with the kind of power that could snuff out a career with a single word.    

Rio had always been smart enough to recognize danger when it was staring her in the face. But, as it turned out, being smart didn’t mean being smart.  And in a move that should have torpedoed her career, she somehow came out the other side not only with her career intact and thriving but also with the woman of her dreams.  

It wasn’t easy getting here, though.  

God, no.   

They’d been through hell and back to get to this version of happiness. The love they now shared wasn’t born from fairy tales or picture-perfect beginnings. It was messy, raw, and real. It started with the intention of just being casual— fuck buddies , nothing more—but somewhere along the way, they discovered they fit so well it scared the hell out of both of them. That fear almost tore them apart. 

And, because no love story between them could be simple, it took a good old-fashioned death threat, a gunshot wound (the scar of which still traced a jagged line across her arm), and the indignity of her eyebrow hair never growing back quite right after.  It wasn’t pretty—they barely survived— both figuratively and literally — but somehow, they’d made it through.  

They were happy now. Married . Living a life Rio had once thought was reserved for other people—for someone else’s daydreams.  Every day she woke up in Agatha’s arms—there wasn’t a single part of her that didn’t feel grateful for it.  But that didn’t mean it was perfect.  

Not by a long shot.  

Agatha was still Agatha —stubborn, sharp-tongued, and fiercely independent to a fault. And Rio? Well, Rio had her moments too. They clashed sometimes, the way two people with strong wills inevitably do. They both liked control, both liked things done their way, and neither was particularly good at backing down. But somehow, even in the middle of it all, they made it work. 

The hardest part, though? The thing Rio still had to navigate, even now? 

Agatha’s emotional walls.  

When they first got together, Agatha had the emotional capacity of a teaspoon. Maybe less. Expressing vulnerability just wasn’t in her wheelhouse, not after a lifetime of being taught to suppress it. Agatha Harkness wasn’t supposed to need anyone.  

Rio liked to joke that Agatha had upgraded from a teaspoon to a tablespoon over the years, but even now, their emotional capacity wasn’t exactly balanced.  But it was growth, and that's what mattered. 

Rio never held it against her. Not really. It wasn’t Agatha’s fault that she’d been raised by a woman like Evanora Harkness. Agatha’s childhood had left her emotionally stunted, and when Rio finally understood that, it stopped bothering her as much. Instead, it made her fiercely determined to make sure Agatha never felt unloved again. 

Rio never thought she would be the kind of person to say “I love you” too often. She had never said it before, but she always had this preconceived notion that if you said it too often, it lost its meaning.  B ut with Agatha?  She made an exception. Agatha needed to hear it. She needed to hear it every day, sometimes more than once, until the words started to settle into the cracks left by her mother’s neglect. 

And as much as Rio gave, she started to notice something beautiful in return: Agatha didn’t always say the words back. But her actions? They screamed “I love you” louder than words ever could. 

And thanks to her total recall, those memories were crystal clear, like snapshots frozen in time. There were so many—an endless collection of moments when Agatha’s actions whispered I love you without ever needing words. 

But her favorites?  

Those were etched into her heart and impossible to forget. The nine times Agatha Harkness’s love shined through her actions. And, because Rio loved hearing it too, there was the one unforgettable moment when Agatha broke through her walls and actually said the words out loud.

Rio loved New York. 

Professionally, it was her playground. The city’s relentless pace and cutthroat energy demanded everything she had every single day, and Rio thrived on it. She lived for the challenge and for the satisfaction of holding her own in a place that chewed up and spit out the unprepared. 

Personally, New York was freedom. It wasn’t just the diversity—it was the sheer indifference . You could be whoever you wanted, love whoever you wanted, and the city wouldn’t even blink. Gay, straight, bi, trans—hell, you could walk down the street in a full suit of armor and barely turn heads. The subway alone was proof enough of that. She’d seen things on the A train that would’ve caused a town-wide meltdown back in Texas, but here? Barely a raised eyebrow. 

But even with all the love she had for the city, there was one thing she couldn’t stand. 

The cold. 

Growing up in southern Texas, winter was a joke. If the temperature dipped into the low 40s, the town practically shut down. She was used to blazing heat, sunshine so intense it made the asphalt shimmer, and winters that could still pass for late spring. Tank tops in December? Normal. 

This, though— this New York winter —was different. It was the kind of cold that got into your bones, no matter how many layers you wore. It numbed her fingers, turned her nose red, and left her cursing every icy gust of wind. 

Rio avoided going outside in the winter at all costs. 

But here’s the thing—she had a weakness. A dangerous, glaring weakness for blue eyes. And earlier in the day, she had two sets of them staring her down. 

One pair—sharp and amused—belonged to Agatha, who was leaning against the doorframe with her signature smirk that said she already knew Rio was about to cave.  

The other? Those were the real problem.  

Nicky’s blue eyes had that pleading, wide-eyed look perfected to an art form, framed by a pout so devastatingly adorable it should be classified as a weapon. The kid could probably ask her to walk into oncoming traffic, and Rio would smile, step right off the curb, and let a New York transit bus run her right over, Regina George style.  

God help her, the little menace had her wrapped around his tiny finger—because here she was, voluntarily outside, freezing her tits off, just to watch some obnoxiously large Christmas tree light up.  It wouldn’t even matter if the tree started raining $100 bills. Nothing— nothing —was worth braving this kind of cold. 

But apparently, this was a “tradition” between Agatha and Nicky. A tradition that Rio was officially part of—or more accurately, dragged into like a hostage.  

She glanced back at Agatha, who stood perfectly composed, barely fazed by the biting wind, looking like a goddamn Vogue ad in her tailored coat and scarf. Meanwhile, Rio was three layers deep and still shivering.  

How the hell did they find this look enjoyable?  Rio was dying.   

Each second out here felt like an eternity, and she was willing time to move faster, silently begging the universe to hurry up so she could retreat to the warmth of literally anywhere else. She was miserable, every part of her screaming in protest against the cold, but she kept a brave face for Nicky. 

The kid was having the time of his life, dancing and jumping up and down, his smile so wide it could’ve powered the tree lights himself. Not a single shiver or complaint—just pure joy in the middle of this freezing hellscape. 

Meanwhile, Rio could literally feel her teeth chattering, the sound rattling between her ears like a goddamn jackhammer. 

“Oh my god.  You are pathetic,” Agatha scoffed behind her, so aghast and somehow teasing that it made Rio’s frozen nerves bristle. 

The snark hit her square in the back, and Rio was seconds away from unloading on her. She was cold, she was miserable, and she didn’t deserve Agatha’s commentary when she was out here suffering for her kid . 

But before she could whip around and give Agatha a piece of her mind, she felt it—hands sliding around her waist, pulling her back into a solid, warm body. 

The warmth spreading through Rio’s coat was instant, cutting through the icy ache in her bones. But it didn't compare to the fire blazing in her chest. Because if there was one thing Rio learned over dating her these past 3 months, it was this: Agatha Harkness did not do PDA.    Ever . 

Behind closed doors, Agatha was different. She loved touch—both giving and receiving. In private, her walls would come down, and she’d let Rio in, just enough to make Rio’s heart ache with how rare and precious it felt.  

But in public? The Agatha everyone else saw was untouchable. She didn't to be perceived as soft. New York was always watching, and Agatha had an image to uphold. Affection in public wasn’t just frowned upon—it was forbidden. 

Rio got it. She really did. Agatha’s no-nonsense boundaries weren’t exactly hard to respect most of the time. Rio wasn’t big on PDA herself, so it wasn’t like she was out here trying to hold hands in public or anything. Still, sometimes the urge would creep in—just a small touch, a quick brush of Agatha’s arm or the instinct to rest her hand on her back. 

But every time she did, she’d get the look.  

That sharp, cutting glare that made her feel like she’d committed some grand romantic spectacle—like sticking her tongue down Agatha’s throat in the middle of Times Square—when all she’d done was graze Agatha's back while standing next to her. 

It was maddening, but it was Agatha. And Rio had learned to live with it.  Adding it to the list of maddening quirks that came with loving someone like Agatha Harkness. 

But now? Here? Agatha’s arms were wrapped tightly around her, pulling her so close in the middle of Rockefeller Plaza, surrounded by people. It wasn’t just rare— it was unheard of.  

Rio stayed perfectly still, afraid that if she acknowledged it, even slightly, Agatha would pull away. So instead, she leaned back into her, letting Agatha’s heat seep into her frozen limbs and her heart at the same time. 

Agatha’s voice came a few minutes later, soft and low, just above a whisper by her ear.  

“Better?” 

Rio swallowed hard, trying to keep her cool, but curiosity got the best of her. Moments like this didn’t just happen with Agatha—they were rare, practically sacred—and Rio couldn’t help herself. 

“You know,” she started, her tone teasing, “this is totally against your rules.” 

Agatha huffed next to her ear, a quiet sound that was equal parts annoyed and amused. But her arms didn’t move away like Rio expected—they tightened just a little more. 

“Yeah, well—watching you shiver like a stray dog left out in the cold is borderline torture. Honestly, Rio, all you’re missing is Sarah McLachlan singing ‘In the Arms of an Angel.’ You could have your own ASPCA commercial. ‘For just 10 cents a day, you can save this poor Texas woman from freezing in the streets of New York...’”  

Rio snorted, fighting back a laugh, though her lips twitched into a smile anyway.  

“You’re such an ass,” she muttered, as the warmth crept into her body in more ways than one. She thought about elbowing Agatha for the mocking, but instead, she leaned her head back against Agatha's shoulder, letting herself sink into heat as much as the moment. 

“You did it because you love me,” Rio mumbled under her breath, just loud enough for Agatha to hear. 

Agatha chuckled, dark and low, the sound sending a pleasant shiver down Rio’s spine.  

“I love your fingers,” Agatha corrected, her voice dripping with that trademark sarcasm. “And frostbite attacks the extremities first. Can’t risk losing those bad boys.” 

Rio couldn’t help it—she laughed, a real, genuine laugh that had more warmth bursting through her chest. 

This woman was absolutely impossible . 

But Agatha didn’t let go. She stayed there, arms wrapped firmly around Rio, surrounded by hundreds of strangers, holding her like it was the most natural thing in the world—like it was something she did every day.  

And as the Christmas tree finally lit up, a brilliant burst of color against the dark sky, Nicky squealed with delight, his laughter echoing around them. 

Rio couldn’t help but smile, watching his little face light up brighter than the tree itself. It was impossible not to get caught up in his joy, even with the biting cold. 

Then, she felt it—Agatha’s lips brushing softly against her cheek, a quiet, fleeting kiss that sent another wave of warmth rushing through her. Agatha’s arms tightened around her, pulling her closer like she wasn’t planning to let go anytime soon. 

And just like that, all the cold, all the misery of the night just melted away. 

Rio didn’t have to wonder or second-guess. She felt it—clear, simple, and undeniable.  

Agatha Harkness, with all her rules and obsession with maintaining her image, had chosen this moment to break them. She’d rather hold Rio, here, in front of thousands of people, than let her stand there miserable and freezing. 

Because that was Agatha. Beneath all the sharp edges and perfectly curated image, when it really mattered, she’d toss it all aside without a second thought. For Agatha, this was love. No big speeches or dramatic displays—just holding Rio close, breaking her own rules, and keeping her warm because she couldn’t stand to see her cold. 

And standing there in Agatha’s arms, Rio didn’t just feel loved.  

She knew she was.

Look—Rio knew exactly who she was dating. She wasn’t naive. Five months in, she fully understood what it meant to be with Agatha Harkness, a woman who, quite frankly, had more influence in New York than the mayor. So yeah, Rio was well aware there were perks to dating someone like Agatha. 

They never had to wait for dinner reservations, even at places that had year-long waitlists. Broadway tickets for sold-out shows? Easy. And Rio now had a growing collection of rare, ridiculously expensive plants from Agatha—plants that, combined, could probably buy a decent car. 

Of course, dating a woman as powerful as Agatha had its drawbacks too. 

Rio wouldn’t say she was scared of Agatha. She knew Agatha would rather crawl inside herself and die than hurt someone she loved. But someone she hated ? That was another story entirely. Case in point: when Agatha broke her hand punching James Hughes in the face.

Rio had watched from the couch, sipping her drink and thinking, Please, God, let me never mess up that bad.  

And queue...Señor Scratchy’s entrance into their world. 

Rio stopped pacing in Agatha’s kitchen and stared down at the white and brown rabbit in question. He was happily munching on some lettuce in his pen, blissfully unaware that his presence alone had likely dug Rio’s grave.  His tiny black nose twitched as he glanced up at her, and Rio swore the little fluffball looked smug. 

This wasn’t how today was supposed to go. 

Agatha had been called away for some client emergency, leaving Rio in charge of taking Nicky shopping for his birthday. It wasn’t the first time she’d been left alone with him—they spent plenty of time together at Agatha’s home and Rio’s—but this was the first time they’d gone out in public without her.

“Don’t let him manipulate you into buying the whole toy store.”

“I can say no to him, you know.”

And to be fair, Rio had planned to take Nicky to the toy store and let him pick out something reasonable. But then, on the way there, they passed a pet shop. 

“Can we just go in and look ?” Nicky had asked, his big blue eyes wide pleading. 

And so they say...the rest is history—and now, here she was, standing in Agatha’s kitchen, staring down at Señor Scratchy while mentally preparing herself for the inevitable fallout his presence would likely cause.

But then the sound of the key sliding into the deadbolt made Rio’s stomach drop like a lead weight. Her head snapped up, and for a fleeting, desperate second, she actually considered making a run for it. But Agatha was blocking the only exit. 

Unless she wanted to leap out the window—32 stories up—that wasn’t exactly an option. Sure, she wouldn’t survive the fall, but was it really any worse than surviving Agatha’s wrath?  

Rio looked down at Señor Scratchy, and they made eye contact. 

“Well, buddy,” she muttered, “it’s been nice knowing you.” 

She stepped out of the kitchen and into the hallway, figuring she might stand a better chance if she explained everything before Agatha actually saw the rabbit. Maybe, just maybe, she could manage some damage control. 

And as luck would have it, the door opened just as she made it into the hallway, and there was Agatha, stepping through before stopping immediately, seeing Rio waiting for her. 

Their eyes met. 

“Hey,” Rio said, her voice a little too high-pitched to pass as casual. 

Agatha’s sharp eyes narrowed.  

“Is Nicky okay?” she asked, her voice tight with worry. 

Rio nodded quickly, too quickly.  

 “Yeah—sleeping, out like a light.” 

Agatha let out a breath of relief, setting her keys and purse down on the table as she walked toward Rio, her gaze locked on her.  

“Then why do you look like that?” 

Rio froze.  

“Like what?” 

“Like you’re about to confess to murder,” Agatha said, her eyes scanning Rio’s face like she could read the truth if she looked hard enough.

“Look—I’ve got to tell you something, and you’re not going to like it.”

“Did you cheat on me?” 

Rio blinked. Of all the things Agatha could have come up with, that was what she landed on?

“Cheat on you? That’s ridiculous!” 

But Agatha’s eyes didn’t soften. Instead, they narrowed further as she crossed her arms.  

“That doesn’t sound like an answer. That sounds like gaslighting.”

“Okay, fair point. I see how you could perceive it that way, but let me clarify.”

“I didn’t mean it like that . What I meant was that of course I didn’t cheat on you. I said it’s ridiculous because cheating implies thinking you can find something better than what you already have—and that’s impossible for me. Because I already have the best.”

“Nice save,” Agatha muttered, but her arms uncrossed, and she took a step closer, her posture softening ever so slightly. 

“Not a save,” Rio corrected, her voice steadier now. “Just the truth.” 

Agatha studied her for another moment, then sighed.  

“Fine. If it’s not cheating, then what’s the thing I’m not going to like?” 

Rio hesitated, searching for the best way to approach this. 

“First of all—I love you,” she said cautiously. 

Agatha raised a brow, her lips twitching in a mix of amusement and irritation.  

“Oh, so...weaponizing our love is back on the table? I must’ve missed that memo.” 

Jesus Christ. Agatha was on a roll tonight—and apparently, so was Rio.

And oh, did that backfire on her.

“It’s not like that,” she said quickly. “I’m not weaponizing anything—I just... I wanted to tell you it one last time in case I never got to say it again.”

"Someone is being dramatic."

“Okay, so… you know how I was supposed to take Nicky to the toy store?” 

Agatha’s brow lifted, suspicion already creeping into her expression.  

“Yes,” she said slowly, dragging the word out like it was a warning. 

Rio forced a smile.  

“Right. Well… on the way there, we passed a pet store, and—” 

“Rio,” Agatha interrupted, her voice sharp. “What. Did. You. Do?”  

Rio hesitated, her mind racing for a way to soften the blow, but there was no easy way to say it. She sighed, defeated. 

“He begged me to go in and look,” she said quickly. “Just to look . And then there was this rabbit, and Nicky was so excited, and he named him Señor Scratchy before I could even think, and…” Rio trailed off, gesturing vaguely toward the kitchen. 

Agatha’s eyes narrowed.  

“There’s a rabbit in my house right now?” 

Rio winced.  

“I mean… technically, it’s Nicky’s rabbit.” 

Agatha blinked, her expression unreadable for a moment, and then she pinched the bridge of her nose, exhaling slowly. 

“You bought my son a rabbit,” she said flatly. 

“To be fair,” Rio interjected, holding up her hands defensively, “it’s a very well-behaved rabbit.” 

Agatha dropped her hand, giving Rio a pointed look.  

“You realize you’ve just added a live animal to my already overwhelming existence, right? Do you have any idea what this means? The care? The mess?” 

Rio nodded quickly, biting her lip.  

“I know, I know. But Nicky loves him. Like, really loves him. I swear, you should’ve seen his face, Agatha. He was so happy. And I’ll take care of him. The rabbit, I mean—not Nicky. Well, Nicky too, obviously, but—” 

“Rio,” Agatha cut in. “Stop talking.” 

Rio clamped her mouth shut, watching as Agatha processed. Her arms crossed tightly over her chest, and she turned her head slightly, as though she were considering all the ways she might kill Rio without traumatizing Nicky. 

Finally, she sighed.  

“Where is this rabbit?” 

“In the kitchen,” Rio said sheepishly. 

Agatha shot her a sharp look as she passed by, and Rio stayed rooted in place, watching her go. She hesitated, her eyes flickering to the now-unblocked door. Freedom. It was right there. 

Then she heard it—Agatha’s low, muttered “Motherfucker.” It wasn’t loud, but it was enough.  

The door suddenly looked very tempting. She could run, avoid the fallout, and deal with the consequences later. But then again, if Rio was going to meet her end, Agatha’s hands wrapped around her neck wouldn’t be the worst way to go. 

With a resigned sigh, Rio turned and walked into the kitchen. She opened her mouth to say something— anything —but stopped dead in her tracks at the sight in front of her. 

Agatha was standing in the middle of the kitchen, the white-and-brown bunny cradled in her arms like a baby. She was gently stroking his fur, a soft, almost dreamy smile playing on her lips. 

Rio blinked, trying to reconcile the image of Agatha Harkness, New York’s most terrifying woman, holding a rabbit like it was the most natural thing in the world. 

“Señor Scratchy, huh?” Agatha murmured, still petting the bunny as though she hadn’t just called him a motherfucker thirty seconds ago. 

Rio blinked again, trying to find her voice.  

“Uh… yeah. That’s his name.” 

Agatha glanced up at her, the smallest hint of amusement in her eyes.  

“Nicky named him, didn’t he?” 

Rio nodded, a little unsure where this was going.  

“Yeah. He said the name just…fit.”  

Agatha let out a small, almost reluctant laugh, her gaze dropping back to the rabbit.  

“Well, he’s not wrong.” 

Rio stared, completely dumbfounded. She’d been ready for yelling, maybe a soul-piercing glare, or even a full-on lecture about responsibility and how much of a pain a rabbit would be. But this? Agatha standing there, holding Señor Scratchy like he was the crown jewel of New York? 

This wasn’t the reaction Rio had prepared for. 

Agatha looked up and caught Rio’s wide-eyed expression, shooting her a pointed look. 

“You’re lucky he’s cute,” she said, her tone sharp but not unkind. With a small sigh, she glanced back down at the rabbit and gently placed him back in his pen. She latched the door and turned, walking toward Rio like nothing out of the ordinary had happened. 

“He can stay,” Agatha said simply, her voice calm, like she was stating a fact. 

Rio just stood there, frozen, waiting for the other shoe to drop. Agatha stopped in front of her, so close Rio could feel the warmth radiating off her, and their eyes locked. Agatha didn’t say anything at first, just studied Rio like she was sizing her up. 

And then, to Rio’s complete shock, Agatha reached up and cupped her face, her hands soft against Rio’s cheeks.  A couple seconds later Agatha tapped her cheek lightly, almost teasingly. 

“You’re also cute,” Agatha said with a smirk, stepping past Rio and heading toward the bedroom. Over her shoulder, she added casually, “You can stay too.” 

Rio stood there, watching her go, as she disappeared through the door. For a moment, the kitchen was quiet except for the soft rustling of Señor Scratchy settling into his pen. 

And Rio just… smiled. 

Because if that wasn’t Agatha’s way of saying she loved her—even when Rio made decisions like impulsively buying a rabbit—then Rio didn’t know what was.

Rio became dimly aware of Agatha hovering over her, pressing soft kisses to her head and whispering words that barely broke through the haze of exhaustion and emotion.  

“You did so good, mi amor,” Agatha murmured, her voice steady but thick with awe. “Look what you made. She’s beautiful.” 

And Rio—honestly?  

She was overstimulated.  Completely, utterly overwhelmed. Her body ached, her vagina throbbed with a pain she was pretty sure would haunt her forever, and her newborn daughter was screaming on her chest like she had a personal vendetta against the air itself. 

And then there was Agatha, pressed close against her side, whispering all these incredibly rare, sweet, beautiful things that Rio loved to hear, but couldn’t even begin to process. 

It was everything, all at once. Too much and yet perfect in a way that felt almost unreal. 

Then Veda was taken away to be cleaned up and checked over, and Agatha went with her, leaving Rio alone in the hospital bed. The world that had been so loud just moments ago suddenly fell into a heavy, aching silence. Everything held its breath, waiting for the nurses to come back and tell her that her daughter was okay. That everything was okay. 

And as Rio laid there, acutely aware that this was another moment—a pivotal one. The kind where a single word could send her life careening down a new path. She had never prayed harder for one specific outcome in her life. 

And then, the quiet was broken. Agatha was walking back to the bed, their daughter bundled in a soft purple blanket she bought the moment she found out they were having a girl, her face lit up with a smile that spoke louder than words ever could. 

Rio’s heart clenched. She didn’t even need to ask. That smile told her everything she needed to know. Their daughter was healthy. The path ahead was clear. 

Suddenly, the world came rushing back—noise, lights, everything pressing play again after an unbearable pause. Rio’s breath hitched as Agatha sat down on the edge of the bed, lifting her arms to show Rio their daughter, all cleaned up and impossibly tiny. 

Her gaze caught first on the full head of dark hair, and Rio couldn’t help but laugh softly, tears welling up again. But when Veda’s eyes fluttered open, Rio sucked in a sharp breath. She’d secretly hoped for blue eyes like Agatha’s and Nicky’s—the donor had blue eyes, after all—but instead, she found herself staring into impossibly dark brown ones. 

Her eyes. 

Something deep in Rio’s chest shifted, tightening and softening all at once, leaving her breathless. 

“Ten fingers, ten toes,” Agatha murmured, her voice filled with quiet reverence. “Seven pounds, three ounces, nineteen inches long. Lungs and heart sound great.” Agatha’s gaze flicked to Rio, her smile softer now. “She’s perfect.” 

And in that moment, Rio believed it with every fiber of her being. 

They both just stared at Veda for a long, quiet moment, taking her in—the softness of her tiny features, the rise and fall of her breath, the little noises she made. Then Rio heard a sniffle. She glanced up to see Agatha wiping at her cheek, tears streaming down her face even as she smiled. 

Agatha caught her gaze and smiled even wider—and Rio’s heart clenched, hammering in her chest, because she had never seen Agatha look as beautiful as she did in that moment. Tears streaked her face, her eyes bright with joy, their daughter cradled protectively in her arms. 

“I love you, Rio. I love you so fucking much,” Agatha all but choked out, her voice trembling—not from hesitation, but from the sheer weight of the emotion threatening to collapse it entirely. 

Rio’s chest gave a happy, aching lurch, her own smile breaking through the tears she could no longer hold back. They spilled freely now, warm trails down her cheeks as her heart overflowed. 

“Language, small ears,” Rio said with a teasing smile, her voice wobbly with emotion. 

Agatha let out a laugh—a mix of a sob and a chuckle—as her shoulders trembled. She glanced down at Veda, her hand lingering to trace a finger gently along the baby’s surprisingly chubby cheeks before carefully passing her over to Rio. 

“She’s already heard me say a lot worse,” Agatha admitted with a soft grin, her eyes warm and full of love as she looked back at Rio. 

Rio laughed at that, a watery sound, because it was true. So very true. 

They sat in comfortable silence for a moment, the weight of everything settling in around them, when a soft knock broke through the quiet. 

Both Rio and Agatha looked up as Jen poked her head into the room, her expression caught between amusement and exasperation. 

“He is going to self-combust if I don’t let him in,” Jen said with a grin. 

From behind her, a familiar voice piped up, loud and insistent.

“Aunt Jen, let me in! I want to see my sister!” 

Rio chuckled, and Agatha let out a long-suffering sigh, but both of them nodded. Jen opened the door, and Nicky practically bolted into the room, his excitement radiating off him. 

“Easy,” Agatha said immediately, her tone firm but gentle. 

Nicky froze in his tracks, his wide blue eyes taking in the sight before him. His gaze landed on Rio in the hospital bed, and then slowly shifted down to her arms. 

Rio watched as he processed it, his little nose scrunching up for a moment in thought before he stepped forward hesitantly. Agatha reached out, grabbing him gently and grunting as she hoisted him into her lap, positioning him so he could get a better look. 

Nicky’s eyes went impossibly wide as he stared at the tiny bundle in Rio’s arms. He gasped, a sound of pure awe. 

“We have the same hair,” he said softly, his small fingers brushing at his own dark curls. 

Agatha chuckled, her smile warm and full of pride. Rio’s own smile grew as she nodded at him. 

“Come here, mijo,” Rio said softly, motioning for him to crawl into the open space beside her. 

With Agatha’s guidance, Nicky climbed onto the bed and settled in next to Rio, his movements slow and careful. 

Rio glanced up at Agatha, their eyes meeting for a brief moment. Fresh tears gathered as they shared a look before turning their attention back to Nicky. 

“Hold your arms out, buddy,” Rio said gently. “You have to be really careful.” 

Nicky’s eyes widened with a mix of awe and determination as he held his arms out, his little hands trembling slightly. Agatha steadied him, her hands gently putting them into position, as Rio carefully placed Veda into his waiting arms. 

“This is your sister,” Rio said softly, adjusting Veda so she was cradled securely against him. “Her name is Veda.” 

Nicky stared down at her, his face lighting up with wonder, his expression so tender it made Rio’s chest ache. 

“Hi, Veda,” he whispered, his voice reverent and soft. “I’m your big brother.  You can call me Nicky.” 

And just like that, Rio lost the battle against her tears. They spilled over silently, her heart overflowing as she watched her son meet his sister for the first time. 

Nicky noticed almost immediately.  

“Amá, you’re crying,” he said, his tone suddenly worried as he looked up at her. 

Rio smiled through her tears, glancing at Agatha, who was watching her with equal tenderness, before turning her gaze back to Nicky. 

“Happy tears, mijo,” she said, her voice thick with emotion. “They’re happy tears.” 

Nicky’s smile widened, the worry on his little face fading as his shoulders relaxed. He turned his focus back to Veda, as if nothing else in the world mattered but her. His gaze was so full of love it made Rio’s chest ache. 

She felt Agatha’s hands slide into her hair, her fingers moving gently, playing with it in the way she knew Rio loved. That familiar motion sent a wave of calm washing over her, and Rio leaned into it, letting herself melt into the moment. 

Her eyes moved over the three of them, taking them in. 

Agatha, the woman who had built a life for them brick by brick, and loved Rio so fiercely that she could feel it in every breath she took. 

Nicky, the boy who chose her to be his mom, who had given her a love she never expected but couldn’t imagine living without. 

And Veda, her daughter—so small, so perfect, a piece of her heart she never knew was missing until now. 

Her family.  

Her everything. 

Rio’s chest tightened as she thought about it. All the moments that had led to this—every twist of fate, every leap, every choice. Each one had brought her here, to this hospital bed, surrounded by the people she loved most in the world. 

She thought about everything still to come—the moments waiting for them out there in the unknown. The joys and the challenges, the hard days and the beautiful ones. Life with Agatha Harkness had never been easy, never predictable, and certainly never boring. Their future? It would be just as messy and unpredictable as the journey that had brought them to this moment. 

But as Rio looked at her family—Agatha at her side, Nicky holding Veda with a love so pure it stole her breath—she knew one thing with absolute certainty. 

Whatever came next, they’d face it together. 

And Rio couldn’t wait.
