The Strawberry Thief



Chapter 1

 

 

It was quite an ordinary morning, on an ordinary Tuesday in mid-April, when Rio noticed something entirely out of the ordinary in her garden. 

It wasn't the spinach leaves that had chewing marks, or the little hole that had been dug under her wooden fence, no, it was the missing strawberries on the bushes right next to the gate. Now one might say that she could have easily mixed them up with another particular set of berries, but the day prior, Rio had taken a good long look at them and decided they would stay on the bush for another sunrise to soak up a more deep, red color from the sun.

Squatting down next to them, she let the empty stalks glide through her hands. They looked too clean for the strawberries to have been chewed off by a door mouse or a bird. No, the fruits had been ripped off clean with the green hood still attached like only a human would. Which was odd - because there was no other house for miles, only prairie and forest and the old stream that contently rippled along all day in and out. The closest town was a day's ride away. And folk never wandered out here on a whim. Most people in town had little love for her since she had bought old Joseph Riddock's land to extend her fields and create even more distance between them and her. She was a county of one and she liked it that way.

Looking back at her stolen strawberries, Rio wondered if it could have been some vagabond that was wandering the lands, possibly heading westward to seek better luck out there. Whoever it was, they were long gone and likely not to return. So instead of fretting over it any longer, she took a white handkerchief out of her pocket and filled it with as many of the remaining strawberries as she could.

The day was still young and with her belly full, she decided to take her mare out to the small pasture to graze. Looking into the sky, she judged that the weather would hold for now, even though the breeze sang of an approaching thunderstorm. If it came with hail again, she needed to get some of the softer spring fruits picked while the sun was still out. She could always turn them into jams and marmalade for the winter, she reckoned. 

On her way back she checked in on her hens and collected the eggs they had laid since yesterday. They would make a fine second breakfast once she was done with the more time-critical tasks around her house.

She loved working under the sun, her old straw hat drooping into her face, shielding her eyes just enough to let her focus on the soil and the blooms. At this time of year, the land was brimming with gentle life, tiny butterflies and beetles emerging from the warming earth, her bee hives humming steadily as the first workers began gorging themselves on the golden pollen dusting her flowers. The air smelled of damp grass and sweet petals, and everything felt quietly alive.

Rio spotted one bee asleep in a half-open blossom, its legs tucked in like a child curled under a blanket. She smiled as she reached for a handful of early raspberries and mirabel plums, their skins warm from the sun. She paused for a moment, listening to the soft rustle of leaves and the distant chirp of birds, and thought about how good her quiet little life was - how lucky she was to be allowed to watch sleeping bees in her garden, to witness the earth’s ever changing seasons without rush or noise.

She hummed when she took lunch under her old willow tree, a few hard-boiled eggs, some home-made deer sausage, and rye bread. Wiggling her bare toes in the long, green grass, she listened to the ripple of her stream and wondered if tomorrow would be a good day to fish, in case the weather would calm down enough over night.

When she walked back to her cottage, she saw the first dark clouds claiming their place on her part of the sky. They were thick and grey - almost black - against the light blue of the firmament. Judging by the breeze that was slowly picking up and the swallows that were already flying lower than usual, she had about two to three hours left until it would start to pour. Rio put her hair into a tight bun before she started collecting all of her gardening tools, storing them in her shed and locking the door properly so the wind wouldn't open it. When she walked her mare back in from the pasture she wondered if she should do the same with her gate. It had once come loose during a windy fall night and hit against the fence all night while Rio had tried to find sleep. But then her glance fell on the strawberries. She didn't like the thought that someone was stealing them. But she disliked the thought of someone going hungry even more.

 

When she had packed up all of her things, bound the last plants together so that they would support each other against the wind, and locked up the chicken coop, she took a long piece of rope and tied several knots over the gate posts. If someone really needed to come in, they would manage to untie it.

Walking back up the path to her house, Rio felt the first thick drops of rain land on her bare arms, cool and sudden. The sky had deepened into a shade of blue so rich it was nearly purple, like ink bleeding across the horizon. A hush fell over the garden, broken only by the rising wind that rustled the trees. By the time she reached her door, the branches were shaking, and moments later the rain came in earnest: heavy, fast, and loud. A jagged flash bolted across the sky, followed by a low, rolling growl of thunder.

Rio paused beneath the small roof that extended over her doorway, watching the storm unfold with quiet awe. The air was thick with the scent of wet earth and crushed leaves, a perfume she had always loved. It reminded her of childhood summers and long-forgotten stories whispered under the sound of the rain. She lingered for a moment longer, letting the rhythm of the rain settle her thoughts, then stepped inside, content and calm. The house greeted her with its familiar stillness, and she decided to rest for the remainder of the day.

She replaced nearly burnt-out candles with new ones, fluffed up her bed and washed herself over her small basin to get rid of the day's sweat and grime. Face to face with herself, she pinched her left breast and sighed. When she had been younger, she had always hoped they would grow a little more, make her a little more attractive. It comforted her that no one saw her much these days and it therefore did not matter how big or flat they were. Combing her hair, she tried to compliment herself on her sharp jawline and big triceps. There was rarely ever a thing she was unable to lift or drag over her land. Perhaps that meant that one could never have it all: Feminine curves and strong muscles, independence and happy children in her garden. 

And yet she sometimes wondered if something was passing her by that she had no grasp on, no way to hold fast. It was of no use. Here she was, well beyond marriageable age, the sole inhabitant of her property. When she died, it would all go to some distant cousin she had never met. Or perhaps it would all be made into smaller plots of land to satisfy the heirs. She could live with that, she was but a visitor to the ground she stood on. And yet, it sometimes gnawed on her.

Instead of dwelling on it for too long she opened the old hatch that sat in the middle of her living space and led into the root cellar. She stored most of her fresh food down here, small bowls with harvested fruit and wicker baskets full of vegetables. There was still some leftover leek soup from the day before, so she took it with her to warm up on her stove. 

When it was steaming, lazy bubbles popping on the surface of the thick liquid, she took it over to the small dining table and looked out of the window and into the growing darkness as she ate. There was a slim line of yellow still visible over the horizon, a last goodnight from the sun.

 

Rio spent the rest of her evening fiddling with a small wooden contraption she had started working on weeks ago. She was mostly self-sufficient, harvested from her garden, fished in the river, and hunted in her forest. She had two tenants that tended to a few of her pastures and paid rent, but she had once been taught how to woodwork and she very much enjoyed filling her time with it. Sometimes she made chairs and tables, sometimes it was little toys like this, boxes that would jump open if one pressed the right, disguised button. Whatever it was, she usually had no issues selling them to a travelling salesman that passed by her house a few times per year. It paid for new clothes once a year and occasional visits to the doctor or her attorney, but most of it she saved for a rainy day.

Waiting for some of the wood glue to dry, she sat down on her padded bench, burying her face in one of the cushions to listen to the rain for just a second that quickly turned into an hour and then another. 

The glue was long dry and her candles burnt down to their stumps when she awoke again. Drowsily, she realized that both the rain and wind had only gotten stronger while she had slept. But then she heard what must have woken up. 

Hollow hammering.

Had the gate come open after all? 

No this was closer. This was her front door. 

Something shot through her - had the storm raged it open? She turned around only to realize it was still closed, the key still turned. She listened, waiting for further sounds, but there were none more.

She wrapped herself into a woolen blanket and tiptoed to the entry way. In the darkness she saw next to nothing through the small milk glass window. Carefully, she turned the key only to feel that something was pressing the door open. Unprepared for the weight of it, the handle slipped away from her fingers and the door flew open at once, sharp drops of rain hitting her as a body fell against her floorboards.

An arm stretched out, her hair wild on her face and chest, she looked pale and blue when a flash cracked through the sky. But that wasn't the worrying part. The worrying part was the thick, hot blood seeping from a long cut on her right calf paired with the fact that she was unconscious, eyes closed, plump lips slightly parted.

Rio kneeled down to her in an instant and reached for her face. She was cold too, too cold.

“Hey,” she tried to shake her, “do you hear me?”

The girl's mouth moved, but no sounds made it past her lips.

Rio sprung up again and pushed the door shut, saving her house from flooding. Then she was on the ground again, hand gently patting the girl's cheek. She reached for one of her lamps to see her better. Her dress was drenched, clinging to her curves like a second skin. Her left hand held a torn cape. Her feet were bare, dirty, and red. Where had she come from? At this hour and in this weather? Was she from town and had gotten lost on an afternoon walk? Nobody would have been able to walk far with that kind of wound, barefoot in the cold.

None of Rio's tries to shake her awake worked. And when she put a hand on her forehead, Rio felt how warm it was compared to the rest of her body. She was developing a fever.

Rio's hand shook when she unbuttoned the stranger's dress. She tried not to look when she peeled off her undergarments, discarding them all in the puddle next to her door. Taking off her own shirt, she pressed it against the wound until the blood flow finally stopped. 

It took her the better part of the night to get the stranger’s body dried off, her own arms aching from the effort. Rio wrapped her hair in a towel and hoped it would soak up the lingering dampness. The house was quiet except for the occasional crackle of the fire and the tiny, pained sounds that escaped the woman’s lips, soft whimpers that made Rio wince each time. She tore one of her linen sheets apart to dress the wound on the woman’s leg, working carefully, her fingers trembling slightly as she tied the makeshift bandage.

The stranger never stirred. Not when Rio lifted her from the floor with slow, deliberate movements, nor when she laid her gently in the bed, wrapping her in layers of her own sheets and blankets. She lit a small fire in the hearth to chase away the chill, watching the flames catch and grow, casting flickering shadows across the walls. The woman’s breathing was shallow but steady and Rio could see her eyes move rapidly underneath their lids. She was dreaming of something, and Rio only hoped it wasn’t a nightmare.

Exhausted and sweaty, she sat with her while the storm raged on, paying close attention to her breathing, feeling her forehead ever so soften to make sure she warmed up, but that the fever wasn’t getting worse. Outside, dark drops of rain were coating the window, the sky pitch black with anguish. She tried to think of all the things she would have to do in the morning, but none of them distracted her from the woman in her bed and her rosy cheeks, tiny vessels filled with blood underneath soft, translucent skin.

Rio had tried to avert her eyes, had not let them wander purposefully. 

But she had seen the swell of her belly. The way it was just a little rounder, a little more protruding than it should have been. Perhaps it was nothing. Simply the way she was built. Rio would not dare to ask her when she would wake up. She was too shy about these things. And it was none of her business. So she took a deep breath and decided to close her eyes for just a minute. Not long at all. Just so she could rest them. The wooden chair felt uncomfortable beneath her, sharp edges cutting into her skin and muscles. 

She still fell asleep and did not wake again until morning dawned.

Chapter 2

 

Rio awoke with a sneeze. 

The stuffy air was tickling her nose and her back ached from falling asleep on her chair, muscles all tight and tough and scrambled. She sat up straight and immediately smelled the sweat on herself. She needed a proper bath. 

Opposite her, on the bed, she saw her guests‘ eyelids flutter. Rio rose to check on the wound and heard her shoulder crack. She carefully lifted the sheet only to see that some blood had seeped through the dressings, but not enough to worry. Below her, the stranger stirred and flinched under Rio‘s touch. She looked at the leg again to feel whether it was more warm or cold than it had been the night before and the next time she looked up, irises as blue as the sea stared back at her. Rio almost jumped from surprise, but the girl was faster. She shook Rio‘s hands off her leg and scrambled back on the bed until she was all the way in the corner against the wall, thighs to her body, hands against the wallpaper and blanket.

„Y-you are the gardener!?“ she stated, though it came out more as a question than a fact. It made Rio frown in confusion. Nobody had ever taken her for or called her a gardener.

“I’m not really a gardener.“

“Well all I ever see you do is gardening!“

“How…” Rio began, then caught herself and corrected her quickly, “I should say, I also have a mare and a few chickens to tend to, actually.”

The stranger looked at her, startled, “well who is your master then? Who do you work for?“

“I don’t have a master, uhm, this is my house. The garden is mine too, I‘m the only one here.“

The confusion never disappeared from the stranger's pretty face. Her eyes darted down to Rio’s legs though.

“Is that why you wear breeches?”

Rio looked at herself. She was still in the same trousers from the day before.

“They… make it a little easier to move around,” she scratched the back of her head in abashment. People did not often see her when she was working. When she travelled she would take a skirt to put over her trousers as soon as she arrived. Absolutely nobody would ever get her to ride in a side saddle, just so she could look proper. 

Her opposite still looked bewildered.

“I am Rio,“ she tried and wondered if she should have introduced herself sooner, „and you are?“

The stranger swallowed hard, her eyes darting to the window and then to her leg, as if she was contemplating an escape. Rio watched as her shoulders sunk and she began nibbling on her lower lip. It sent a gush of warmth into Rio‘s chest.

“Uhm Agnes.“

„And where are you from?“

“That’s none of your business!“ she hissed, her face growing stern, and Rio raised her palms in defense. She had looked so soft and fragile last night, Rio had not taken her for a feisty spirit.

“A-alright… I just was wondering how you came here… I don’t normally get visitors.“

“Well I can leave at once if you’d like me to!“, she crossed her arms like an offended child. In the lazy light of the morning, she looked incredibly young. Rio's eyes darted to her midsection, now well hidden under the sheets. The courage to ask did not come to her.

“Fuck!”

Rio cleared her throat, “I tried to dress it as good as possible, but I'm no doctor… though I could send for one if you-”

“No!” Agnes shook her head, “no I'm fine, it looks fine like this!”

“If you are worried about the fee, I can-”

“I'm sure it will heal on its own,” she pushed the sheets aside and moved her legs over the edge of the bed, “I won't be bothering you for much longer, have my clothes dried?”

It all happened so fast Rio barely had a chance to react. Agnes tried to stand up at once, but instead whimpered in pain, reaching for her calf just before her feet gave out and she fell forward, thankfully right into Rio's direction, so that she could catch her. Sharp nails and clammy hands dug themselves into her skin when she helped her back onto the bed.

“Are you alright?” she asked, feeling Agnes push her off, “you need to be careful or it will open again…”

The girl stared at her leg with a deep frown on her forehead. Rio could only fathom what was going through it, which thoughts were chasing one another at the sight of her disability. She wondered if it could have something to do with the state she was in. Whether the father was waiting for her somewhere. Or if she had run from him.

“W-where are you looking to go? Do you need to be somewhere?” Rio tried, carefully.

“That's… nobody's business but mine,” she replied. Though it no longer sounded hostile and more like an excuse she was replaying.

Rio simply nodded.

“Well you can stay here until it is healed. It should only take a fortnight if we keep it clean and dry and if you try to rest the leg.”

The girl frowned.

“Why would you do that?” she asked, and there was something in her voice that almost sounded like disgust. It tore something open inside Rio. Like this, it felt like an accusation.

“I-I could not send someone that is hurt and injured out into the wilderness. I am not built that way.”

She swallowed hard. The stranger did not seem to believe her.

“I have no money, I cannot pay you.”

“I would not take it even if you had any.”

“I have nothing else to offer.”

“I know.”

“Don’t ask me for anything later. I do not want to repay debts I wasn't aware I was owing!”

Rio shook her head, “no I won't. I promise.”

Agnes scrutinized her head to toe. She probably saw streaky hair, sweaty circles on Rio's white linen shirt, and wrinkles in her trousers. But something seemed to convince her that Rio meant no harm, only to help. Mayhaps her disheveled looks afforded her a little advance on trust, because finally her opposite seemed to relax her muscles.

“Are you… hungry?” Rio tried next.

“No…” she replied, but Rio could already hear the rumbling in her tummy.

“I can make some porridge with berries if you'd like.”

More tummy-rumbling. Agnes moved her hand up as if to hide it.

“Well if you were making some for yourself anyway …”

Rio nodded and felt how a smile settled on her lips. 

The oats were quickly cooked and seasoned, a familiar smell settling around the house as she threw blackcurrants in, stirring them into the thick mush. When she took them back into the bedroom, Agnes sat propped up against the headboard, quietly glancing out the window.

“It is so nice today…”, Rio handed her the bowl. 

Upon the first bite, her face contorted, “mhm,” she murmured and swallowed.

Selfconsciously, Rio tried a bit of porridge from her own bowl, it tasted normal, the berries still a little sour, “is yours not good?”

“It’s perfectly fine,” the girl nodded and stuffed another spoonful into her mouth.

Smiling, Rio focussed on her own breakfast and tried not to scrutinize her guest too blatantly.

She checked on her wounds when they had both eaten, padding the angry red flesh dry while Agnes hissed. She let the wound rest for a while, opening the window so fresh air might come in, then she dressed it in fresh gauze. 

“How long will it take? You said a fortnight? What if I need to wash myself or… well step out.”

“If you are lucky,” Rio shrugged, fastening the bandages, “I would not advise you to put any weight on it for a few days, I can help you move if you want to use the outhouse.”

“Oh I think I am well enough to relieve myself on my own, thank you very much,” she cut her off with a snippy tone.

“My outhouse has a door,” Rio rolled her eyes, “you can close it and I can step away.”

Agnes crossed her arms, letting herself fall back again into the bed. It made a lock of her hair fall into her face, she puffed out a bit of air, but it just sent the hair flying back into her face. It looked somewhat adorable, her arms folded over her waist, her skin dry and soft now where it had been soaked and cold last night. Rio’s eyes darted down to where the bedding was hiding her guest’s belly. She wondered for a moment whether this was the right time to ask whether she needed any other sort of care, whether there perhaps was a husband that was potentially looking for her. But then what if she wasn’t pregnant and this was just the regular swell of her body? Rio had just built the tiniest bit of rapport now, she did not want to lose it again so quickly. Besides, would Agnes not speak of this herself if she truly were with child?

“You are staring,” her guest’s stern voice cut through the quiet, Rio rose to her feet almost instantly.

“I’ll get these washed,” she gibbered and quickly gathered both breakfast browns in her hands, “call for me if you need anything.”

The glance Agnes gave her was demeaning, she pretended not to notice. Instead, she took the wicker basket she collected her dirty dishes in down to the river. It wasn’t even half-full, but at least it provided a good excuse to leave for a bit. Outside, patches of ground were still wet, puddles on the grass had not drained yet. She made a mental note that she needed to check on the garden over midday so see if the storm had done any damage. When she ran by her chicken coop, she quickly let the hens out, counting them to make sure none had been stolen by a predator the night before.

When she returned to the house, Agnes sat on the bedside, Rio already saw it through the open door when she entered.

“You said you would help me to the outhouse?” she sighed, resigning herself to her fate.

“S-sure,” Rio nodded and put the basket down, “would you like a robe? It’s not warmed up yet.”

“I’m splendid thank you,” Agnes shook her head and waited until Rio had propped her up with one arm over her shoulder. Slowly they made their way through the main room until they stood in the bright, cold light of the day. Agnes squinted when the first rays of sunshine hit her face. She had freckles on her cheeks, tiny brown dots that formed constellations on her milky skin. Rio immediately looked back onto the ground.

“Unimaginable that just a few hours ago it was storming…” Agnes whispered and Rio nodded.

Barefoot, she took Agnes down the stone path that led to her outhouse further down the stream. She had built it herself over a brick-lined pit that drained by the river. It was a silly thing to be proud of, yes, but whenever she did make it into town she was reminded of how luxurious it was.

“I think I’m going to be quite alright,” Agnes cleared her throat when Rio tried to help her into the privy, so Rio quickly turned around, listening to the door falling shut. She stepped away looking down into the grass, observing how the rain had likely fueled it to grow. The stream had risen slightly, almost over its banks. It would go down within a day or two, she was sure, unless it would rain again - but then it did not seem like that.

When she had walked Agnes back to the house, she fluffed up the bed and properly opened the window to let more fresh air in. She filled the sink up for her so she could wash herself, placing a chair in front of it. She took great care to knock loudly once she wanted to come back in, but Agnes was already back on the bed in a fresh gown Rio had put our for her.

“I hope the water wasn’t too cold?”

“I didn’t mind much,” she answered, her feet covered by the sheet, her glance out the window.

“I have a few books in case you would like to read something to keep yourself busy?" she pointed to the shelf above the bed, "I need to look after the chicken and the crops.”

“I’m alright,” she nodded, not turning her head. From the right angle, something sad had settled in her face. She looked uncertain, as if she was still making up her mind about staying or making a run for it as soon as Rio would lay her head down tonight. Rio dearly hoped she wouldn’t. When her hands dug into the soft soil, she felt a strange kind of tickling below her right ear. Something inside of her liked the company. The change, that after such a long time there was someone in the house, someone she could be helpful to for once. Another soul that shared the space and spoke and breathed the same air as she. She just needed to take care not to grow too used to it. 

She sighed as she collected her hen’s eggs, five in number, all big and white. One of the girls was broody, sitting fast in her nest. She carefully petted her feathers, feeling how warm the hen was.

Upon looking at her eggs, she decided she would make an omelette tonight. A few leaves of chard had already grown big enough to be harvested. She could fry them up in a bit of lard perhaps. In case Agnes liked chard that was. If not, she could always look out for a bit of wild garlic or arugula up by the woods. They were less bitter and more flavorful, perhaps someone with Agnes' condition liked that better. She took a deep breath in when she caught herself overthinking and shook her head. Besides, she needed to let Viktoria out so she could graze and Rio could clean up her stable. 

The mare greeted her with a friendly whinny and Rio fed her a bit of yellow dandelion before petting her and letting her out into the small pasture by the house. She watched her for a few minutes, nibbling on some more early currants since she was still out of strawberries. She looked at the empty bushes and sighed, she had looked forward to those, the first ones of the year. She only hoped whoever had helped themselves to them had enjoyed them properly. Over her shoulder, she looked back at the house. Her eyes hadn’t even fully focused on it when she caught a flicker of movement behind her bedroom window, where the curtains swayed in the light. For a heartbeat she wondered if she’d imagined it - just a trick of the sun glancing off the glass, nothing more.

Huffing, she started to muck out Viktoria’s stable.

 

The sun was already past its highest point in the sky when she returned to the house. As she walked through the door, she smelled on her own armpit, surmizing that she best not enter the bedroom so her guest would not be spooked by her stench. She did not need to worry though, because when Rio lightly pushed on the open door, she found her guest fast asleep on the bed, her injured leg over the sheets, her face turned away to the wall. The late afternoon light lay over her in thick stripes, one of them illuminating her face. There they were again, the freckles - Rio could see them even from a distance. Her breath stilled for a moment, Rio barely noticed it. Because her every sense was captured by how the girl’s chest rose and sank in a slow, steady rhythm that seemed to determine the ebb and flow of every atom in Rio’s universe. Her feet felt like they had taken root on the dead wood below, they were heavy and yet tickling to run away, fast and far. She felt forever torn by it. 

What saved her was a cloud that seemed to shift between Agnes’ face and the sun. It stole the light from the room for a split moment, long enough for Rio to turn away and run down to the river. She dipped her face into the cold water of the stream, ripped her shirt away so she could wash herself on the stony river bench. Only when she was clean and odorless did she gather enough courage to walk back to the house, her dirty sheet in front of her chest, something new she would have to learn now that someone might be watching.

She found a new shift in her cupboard, along with a fresh pair of green trousers. Her hair was still partially wet where water had found it, but Rio knew it would dry quickly.

Agnes was awake now, her nose in a book. When Rio walked past her door, she looked up from it, seemingly waiting for Rio to say something.

“Uhm… I’d make dinner now,” she scratches her ear.

“Thank you… I guess,” she nodded and then seemed to remember her manners, “would you like help?”

“No,” Rio waved it off, “it’ll just be a few eggs and greens.”

“Maybe go easy on the salt,” Agnes suggested, and sucked in her upper lip.

It made Rio chuckle, the sheer ease with which she made demands would likely have thrown another host off, but it somewhat lightened Rio’s mood instead.

“Very well, Miss Agnes,” she nodded and went to her kitchen.

Chapter 3

 

 

Rio set up temporary camp on her padded bench once she had gotten Agnes back to the bedroom after dinner. She had fallen asleep on it often enough, it would serve her well for the time being, she thought. With the night came a bit of cold wind, so she lit the small oven in her bedroom as Agnes kept on reading by candlelight.

“I’d go to bed now, the wood will burn down and the embers will keep you warm all night, you don’t need to worry about the fire,” she nodded, standing in the doorway. 

Agnes looked at her for a second, the book in her lap, her fingers between the pages, “uhm thank you,” she nodded and it sounded as if the words did not come over her lips very often. Perhaps because she was seldom given a reason to be thankful.

“Rest well,” Rio bade her good night and closed the door behind herself.

It took her a while to find sleep. Even though she had labored all day and her muscles ached, she was suddenly very alert again in the dark. Her eyes would not grow tired and her mind kept jumping from one thought to the next. She resorted to watching her own little hearth burn, the flames a welcome theater before her eyes until they were just bright spots on the coal, like freckled constellations in the dark.

 

She woke when the first rays of sunlight peeked in through the windows. The door to the bedroom was still closed and everything else rested undisturbed in its usual place, no sign that someone had hastily left in the middle of the night. Rio felt wet between her legs but ignored it as she always did. She would wash it off after breakfast.

“How often do you get visitors out here?” Agnes asked when she served her the same breakfast as the day before, though this time she had boiled the oats in milk and sweetened them with honey. She seemed to like them a little better.

“Seldomly.”

Agnes nodded and chewed on her spoon.

Rio sucked in a deep breath, and gathered the little courage the morning afforded her, “which is why I was wondering how you ended up at my door.”

Agnes immediately stopped moving, her eyes fixated on the bowl, her lips half wrapped around the spoon. She did not move for what must have been some of the most uncomfortable seconds in Rio’s life, then she swallowed and looked up.

“I was just passing through.”

“Where were you headed?”

“Nowhere.”

“You can’t have been going nowhere? Were you on your way home in the storm?”

Agnes looked at her long and hard, before she spat, “I already told you that that is my business and mine alone, and if you cannot accept that, I am happy to leave at once!”

Rio raised both of her hands in defense, “no I-I was just worried! Is nobody worried for you? Wondering where you might be? Whether you got hurt,” she pointed to Agnes’ leg, “whether you are safe?”

A flicker of hurt flashed over Agnes’ face, quickly hidden behind a wall of nonchalance, “what is it to you? Huh? Already full of me?”

Rio gapes at her, “n-no that’s not what I meant I was just-”

“Rio, right? I am very grateful for your hospitality, but I do not like your nosiness and I would greatly appreciate it if you could stop asking me questions that I do not wish to answer!”

“I’m sorry,” Rio bit down hard on her lower lip. Alright then. She doubted that she would be able to circumnavigate the topic entirely for the days that lay ahead, but maybe this was enough to be discussed over cooling porridge and unmade beds.

Agnes asked to stay in the main room after she had eaten half her porridge and Rio offered to help her back over. She said her back ached from half laying half sitting up in the bed and opted to prop her leg up another chair.

“I can do this myself, you know?” Agnes murmured when Rio took the bandages off to look at the wound that was still oozing and angry, “you do not need to waste your time on me.” Rio just gave her a quiet nod and left her to it. When she caught a glimpse of the bandages a while later, she saw that they were messy and tied up with thick knots that inevitably must be pressing into the tender flesh. She retried them when she helped Agnes to bed at night.

 

A day passed and then another. It was a cumbersome rhythm that they slowly sat into. Conversations were kept superficial, there was never any meat to them. Rio did not dare ask more questions regarding where her guest had departed from or where she was headed. And then of course the pregnancy was completely off-limits as a topic. Rio often saw her touch her belly as she stared into the distance, but when Agnes caught her staring, she immediately stopped. At least the wound seemed to begin to heal, no matter the amateurish dressings of it. Agnes had only let her help one more time when the ends of the gauze were too short to tie them up. She had muttered a quiet thank you though and it had kept Rio going for the better part of the day. 

Other than that, Agnes sat by one of the windows most of the day, looking out when she wasn’t reading. Rarely did she start a conversation by herself, and usually it was just about Rio helping her to relieve herself or move somewhere else. She had tried putting weight on the leg, but alongside the open wound, her ankle was still swollen and thick, keeping her from moving about on her own. It made the air in the house feel heavy. Before sleep, Rio would try to quiet the voices that told her the girl would have already left if she could put one foot in front of the other, that her presence was mere convenience, and that her guest did not care for it other than when Rio was auxiliary.

And still.

Her garden needed tending after another day of rainfall. She weeded and let the chicken and ducks roam freely to pick up any snails that were munching on her vegetables. One morning, a few days after her guest hat arrived, she was loosening the earth on a small patch close to the back of her garden to plant carrots and radishes when she heard light steps approaching.

The sun was in her face when she looked up to see it was a young boy. As he came closer, Rio recognized him as one of her tenant’s sons, though she did not remember his name.

“Good mornin’ Miss Vidal,” he greeted her when he arrived at her fence, taking off his cap.

“To what do I owe the pleasure?”

“My name is Eddie, my father sends me,” he cleared his throat, “uhm, Todd Davis, we rent the North Helheim farm from you?”

“Aye, I know your father, is there something amiss?” She rose from where she was kneeling in the dirt.

“He saw last night that one of our fences broke in the storm a few nights past. He would like for you to have a look at it.”

“Alright,” she nodded and was finally close enough to see the boy’s cracked lips, “did you walk all the way here on foot?”

“Uhm yes,” he admitted.

“Wait here I’ll get you some water,” she turned around and walked up the path to the house. It did sound like Mister Davis to make his son walk for hours when he could have come himself on horseback.

Inside, Agnes sat at the table, looking at a sewing pattern in one of Rio’s books, she lifted her head when she heard Rio approaching.

“Are you getting on out there?” She asked when Rio reached for a clay jug and filled it with some stream water she had gotten earlier in the day.

“Yes, there is a boy down by my fence he says-”

“A boy?!” Suddenly Agnes' eyes blew wide. She stretched her head out only to jerk back the instant she realized she was in the open doorway’s line of sight, “fuck!”

“What’s the matter?” Rio looked at her in shock, leaning right to see if anyone was in the doorway, but she couldn’t even see the boy from where she stood. Agnes had almost sunk underneath the table.

“I-I thought you said no one rarely comes by?!”

“He is my tenant’s son, they have an issue with their fence,” she tried to explain at the sight of the panic in Agnes’s face.

“Just… is he gonna come into the house?”

“No… I am gonna get him some water and then take the mare to see his father. I might not be back until dinner time, it’s a bit of a ride.”

“How did he get here?” Agnes kept asking.

“He walked,” Rio stood in between the doorway and her guest’s line of sight in order to get Agnes to look at her, “he is no threat, he is barely even ten.”

It almost did nothing to calm her down.

“Why are you so nervous, he is just a boy?”

“I don’t like boys very much,” Agnes snarled.

Rio frowned, “well I got to get him this and get going,” she sighed, “will you be fine until I come back?”

“Will you be back before dark?”

“I shall try my best.”

“Can you lock the door?”

Rio gave her another bewildered look, “I would let you lock it from the inside but you cannot move there on your own, can you?”

“Lock it from the outside then,” she nodded, “I will be fine here.”

What on God’s green earth had gotten into her? The way she sat in her chair, like a scared little girl, it made something twist inside Rio’s stomach. She looked towards the door then back at her guest. She looked as if the grim reaper himself was waiting outside the door, ready to walk in and take her with him.

“Are you sure you will be fine on your own?”

“Well what are my options, that boy is not gonna leave on his own now will he?” Agnes rambled, swallowing hard.

She was right. Moreover, the broken fence would not fix itself would it? So Rio locked the door behind herself, handing the boy the jug before she picked up her tools from the shed, getting a saddle on Viktoria.

 

She lifted Eddie up onto her mare’s back before joining him. He didn’t notice the way she glanced back toward the house, nor the worry tightening her eyes. As they trotted down the path and then up onto the road that led to Helheim, she couldn’t shake her bewilderment. The encounter replayed in her mind, sharper now that the distance between them and the house was growing. Whatever had frightened Agnes so deeply about a boy not even in his teens? Was it a general fear of men, or a particular person she dreaded might have come? The thought unsettled her more than she wanted to admit. Rio could not imagine someone so feisty to get startled this easily; for her to react like that meant something had cut close to the bone. The look on her face lingered with Rio, haunting her for the rest of the ride.

She made Viktoria canter once they turned onto the wider road. On each side, blooming meadows brushed past them, the ones to their left all belonging to her. She let them lie idle to create distance between her and the next inhabitants, which were Eddie’s parents. The boy held onto Viktoria’s mane as best as he could and never complained. They only stopped once to let her have a sip of water under an old oak tree. In between the wheat, tall flowers were blooming on the other side of the road. As Viktoria grazed for a moment, Rio made a mental note to take home a few when she had looked after the fence. Absentmindedly, she let a few petals tingle her fingers. They would look nice on her kitchen table. Perhaps Agnes would enjoy them when she sat there and read, perhaps they would provide a little distraction. Shaking her head, Rio walked back to where Eddie was already waiting for her by her mare.

North Helheim farm came into view soon after.
It was a small, simple two-story house next to a large red cow barn. The Davises kept dairy cattle and a few sheep for milk and wool. Last year, Rio had helped Mister Davis build a chicken coop as well. She did not particularly like him and was glad when she did not have to deal with him for months. He had fought in the war, though Rio wasn’t sure on which side and she did not dare to ask. His wife’s family had already lived on the land when Rio had bought it and no matter how much disfavor she had for him, she did not want to chase the family off the land, they had never known another home. He was a shouty man, ordering his wife and kids about when he felt they were not following his orders. Rio reckoned it was like this for many families - and therefore the prime reason she had never even looked at a man twice. 

At the gate, she dismounted Viktoria and then helped Eddie to the ground before she bound her hair at the nape of her neck and closed all the buttons on her tunic. She had found that out here she would be respected more like that. Especially by her tenants. And even more so by Mister Davis.

He sat out on a bench by the house, smoking his pipe when Rio tethered Viktoria to a post. Immediately he yelled for another one of his sons to fetch water for the horse and some hay, remaining seated himself.

“Miss Vidal,” he greeted her, chewing on his pipe in a way that let her know she was but a nuisance here. 

“Mister Davis,” she nodded, “some trouble with the fence I hear?”

“Yeah, we had two of ‘em heifers run off last night, musta broken through it after the storm weakened the planks.”

“Well I sure am sorry to hear that, did you manage to capture them again?”

“Aye, we got them alright,” he cleared his throat, “but I must say Miss Vidal it is high time we get some wire between the posts, I’ve heard great things about it, keep ‘em cattle all in. They don’t like hurtin’ themselves.”

Rio sighed quietly. She had seen the barbed wires on one of her other tenants’ fences, he had installed it himself. Mister Davis, though, was not the kind of man who would pay a penny when he could try and get Rio to do it.

“I’m not taking a particular liking to those I gotta say, it sounds cruel to the animals,” she put a hand on her hip. He did not like the answer very much, Rio could see that in his eyes, though if he wanted to torture his cattle he’d have to put the wires up himself. She was not gonna help him with that, nor was she going to pay for them.

“Very well,” he put down his pipe, nodding to his son that was getting Viktoria dried up, “Philip will help you with the fence.”

They walked out over the pasture together in silence. The young man knew her, but still seemed to be confused about the way she wore her clothing every time they saw each other. Rio caught him stealing glances as they moved along the fence. She knew he had at least one sister, and that girl surely did not wear breeches. Rio had seen her once in a fine, blue prairie dress with braids reaching down all the way to her waist. She had waved to Rio and Rio had hurt for her when her father had dragged her away. Best not to let a young girl get any ideas. Best not to let her know trousers fit women just as well as men.

The air had warmed up considerably when they reached the broken part of the fencing. It was more fallen over than really damaged. A few planks had come loose, but other than that she would only need a few new nails to fix it. Arguably it was something Mister Davis or his sons could have had done by mid-morning. No need to send poor Eddie walking three or more hours her way only for Rio to ride back here.

“Hold it up please,” she pointed to the first fallen element and began to fasten the loose planks to the posts.

 

The sun shone hot as the hour passed. A slight wind picked up, darker clouds in the distance forecasting more rain for the night. The skin on her arms had turned slightly reddish when Philip helped her put up the last bit of fence before they were done. She tied the posts together with a bit of drawstring for extra support before she checked their integrity. Above her in a tree a bird sang when she sipped on her water flask, looking at her work and feeling her aching muscles. She dreaded the ride home already. There was barely anything she would get done today and if her body could make any promises at this hour, it would not let her move much tomorrow. 

She smelled the sweat on herself as they walked back over the pasture. The high grass that had gently brushed her legs on the outbound walk now itched her with every step she took. All she wanted was to dip into the river and cool off her overheating skin. Closer to the barn, she saw the swallows diving low and knew she did not have much time to return home before the rains would start.

“Have someone check the fence tomorrow, in case anything comes loose,” she told Mister Davis when they were back at the house. He simply nodded and beckoned Eddie to come over from where he sat in the doorway. He carried a brown woven bag.

“Missus Davis will not let you leave without some of her bread and milk,” he grumbled, “I need the glass bottle back next time you come this way.”

Rio peered into the bag and saw two packages wrapped in paper, likely loaves of bread, and a large bottle with fresh milk.

“Please give her my regards,” she looked up towards one of the windows, but did not see anyone there. 

He huffed, raising himself up on his cane, “we’ll see how well your fence holds.”

Rio gave him a nod and noticed that Eddie was already untying her mare, leading the patient animal over to where they stood. She bade him goodbye before climbing back into the saddle. As she rode past the yard, she couldn’t help glancing back once more, seeing if she could catch a final glimpse of them. But the space behind her was already empty, the doorway still, the yard quiet - as if the moment had folded in on itself the second she left.

When Viktoria started her trot, Rio’s thoughts were back with Agnes. She needed to find out what had bothered her about the boy. And perhaps the time had come to ask about the child she was carrying. She could deny it, but the lie would not hold for very long depending on how long it would take for Agnes’ leg to heal. Who in their right mind would prefer to be locked into a house when they just as well could have stayed inside on their own accord? It kept on puzzling her as she rode, so much so that she almost missed the oak tree.

She left Viktoria by the stream to drink as she took her knife to cut some of the flowers. Only when she heard the clopping of unfamiliar hooves did she look up. Apparently today had appointed itself her day for socializing, whether she liked it or not. She recognized the man on the horse. He was a councilman from one of the towns west of her home.

“Miss Rio, what a coincidence,” he greeted with his hat.

“Mister Collins,” she nodded and turned towards him.

“How do you fare these days? I don’t think I have seen you since the fall!”

“I’m very well, thank you very much,” she smiled.

“Are you not ever lonely up there in your cabin,” he nodded to the road that led to her home.

“Oh no, I am perfectly happy this way,” Rio assured him, cutting off another flower.

“Ah! Well that is good. Though you should really get yourself a dog. There is strange people coming through more and more now,” she shook his head. Yes, she had heard that before, and she had thought about it. But so far her shotgun had made her feel safe enough. She told him that.

“Now that I see you, you did not have any visitors recently? Or find someone trespassing on your land?”

The way in which he had phrased the question made Rio startle. 

“No. No one,” she lied. In the distance, she heard a low rumbling.

“Good good. I heard a young girl got lost about two weeks ago.  Ran off from home in Westwood. The town marshal there came looking around our parts. Said she is disturbed and… well dangerous.”

 

Dangerous.

A girl.

About two weeks ago.

 

Rio scratched her nose, breathing calmly, “no… she’s not come my way.”

“Huh… well I sure hope they find her,” he shook his head, patting his horse and straightening his back, as if he was getting ready to ride on.

“Does she have a name? In case I see someone,” Rio stopped him, the words leaving her mouth quicker than she could think.

“I did not catch her last name, but the marshal said she is twenty-four and called Agatha.”

 

Agatha.

 

Had it been any other name, Rio would have let it go. Had it been any other name, she would have brushed it off as coincidence, would have convinced herself her guest looked older than twenty-four even if it was just by a few years. Any other name and she would not have thought oh, Agatha - that almost sounds like Agnes.

She bade Mister Collins good-bye and wished him a nice week as he turned his horse back around and rode off into the distance. Standing in the field, Rio felt her mouth go dry. Behind her ear, her skin tingled again. She reached for it, scratched it, but it would not stop. She cast the flowers aside and whistled for Viktoria to come. In one swift motion, she hoisted herself up again and made her canter.

Ahead, the sky was dark and angry. Swollen purple clouds were gathering, forming another thunderstorm. The green fields gave them a stark contrast. Rio felt her tunic grow moist from the humidity and her own sweat. She tried not to think of it as Viktoria carried her down the road and towards her home. Tried not to think of the words Collins had used to describe the missing girl. She did not fear for her own safety, did not even think about needing to carry a knife back into her house. No, if Agnes was that missing girl, she was neither dangerous nor disturbed. But Rio needed to know what game she was playing and what had made her run away in the state she was in. 

The first drops of rain began to coat her skin as she finally reached the fork that led down to her house. She did not want Viktoria to trip so she made her walk instead of trot, no matter how much Rio felt like she wanted to rush her. She barely took off saddle and bridle before she ran inside, fumbling for the key in her pocket as the rain began to pour.

When she barged through the door, Agnes was still sat in her chair the way Rio had left her in the morning. She could hear her own labored breaths as she looked at her. She looked fragile, white, and delicate in her white dress. Her mouth was slightly open. A single candle illuminated the space around her. If Rio had ever felt a wish to yell or shout at her, it had long dissipated the moment she caught the girl’s glance. In fact, she wondered if it had ever been there to begin with. Because Rio could not remember her heart ever pounding in her chest like this, her insides twisting, her skin ever feeling like it was pulsing and about to split. Not by anger or wrath, but by worry. Worry for the broken soul that had found her way to her door and trusted her enough to let her cook her food, clean her wounds, and carry her to bed.

“Who are you?” she breathed and saw that a flash was cutting through the sky, momentarily brightening the room, “and I beg you, do not lie to me.”

Chapter 4

 

 

Her first glance went to the window. 

Realistically, how fast could she make it there even with the injured leg? Could she just push it open or would she have to smash the glass. If she cut herself how far would she get in this storm, at this hour? There was no way she would even make it to the next town, she would collapse on the road and her host would have to carry her back (if she bothered at all). No, she had a little dignity left. She would not humiliate herself like that.

Rio quietly closed the door behind herself, the rain splashing onto the floor. She had to shoot the bold so the wind would not pry it open again. Agatha gulped when she turned around, putting an additional lantern on the table and lighting it. Her arms were exposed, they looked darker than usual, the muscles flexing underneath her skin. When a flame began flattering in the lantern, it drew her attention to the dirty spots on Rio’s face and hands. They made her mouth go slightly dry. She had watched her work in the garden so many times, but Rio always kept tidy and arguably modest if one did not count the breeches she apparently wore everywhere.

“Hey, are you even listening?”

Agatha was not.

“Why were you so scared this morning?” Rio sighed.

Fuck. She wasn’t getting out of this was she? She wasn’t gonna get out of any of this after all. It had all been for nothing.

“Did he see me?” she asked.

“He didn’t, he didn’t even think anyone else was here.”

Agatha began chewing on her lip, trying to come up with another lie, a new one, that would keep Rio at bay long enough for her leg to heal before she could make a run for it. She had given it some thought already - in case there would ever be questions. After all, she had not had much to do, waiting for that stupid gash to close on her shin. That she had run away from a travelling circus would have been her eventual answer, had Rio ever pressed on about where she had come from. But arguably, this right here felt a little different, a little more like Rio knew something Agatha did not. Unable to look her in the eyes any further, Agatha lowered her gaze unto her fingers, fumbling with the book pages.

“Wh-what did you tell his family about me?”

Agatha heard some rustling, it was Rio shifting back in her seat. Behind her, a flash streaked past the window, the thunder came but a moment later.

“Nothing, we never spoke about you… but I met someone on the road who asked whether I had encountered a young woman…”

At once, Agatha’s heart began to pound. So they were looking for her even here. She had run so far, and it had all been for nothing.

“They said she was dangerous,” Rio added and Agatha had no choice but to look at her again. Tiny tears welled in her eyes, she could feel them coming, but there was no way she would let them run down her cheeks. She did not need Rio to see them, did not need her to realize how weak and soft she truly was.

“I wonder who they were talking about,” she spat, dripping of sarkasm. It was easier than showing dread.

Rio just looked at her. That woman was too good for this world, taking in a stranger, feeding her, waiting on her hand and foot. Either she had just never been exploited before, or she was the most starry-eyed person Agatha had ever met. No matter, in the short time they had spent together Agatha had begun to struggle not to lean on her too much, not to get too comfortable in her presence. After all, this was all temporary. People like her did not deserve plush pillows and fresh berries this woman had labored to grow out of the dark soil.

“I cannot protect you if you don’t tell me the truth,” she whispered and Agatha saw her hand flinch as if it had wanted to reach out across the table.

Protect her? There was nothing worth protecting anymore after all she had done, was there? Maybe the little one.

“What if it was true then? The part about me being dangerous and disturbed?” She heard herself mumble.

“Is it?” Rio asked and Agatha saw how single wet strands of hair were sticking to her sharp face, black vines of ivy on olive skin.

She sighed and closed her eyes for a moment. The wound on her leg hurt. “It’s Agatha. My name. Not Agnes.”

Rio nodded as if to encourage her to continue speaking. 

If she only knew. 

Agatha looked at the candle on the table. This was her last night in this little corner of paradise, was it not? Come morning Rio would surely chase her out the door if she knew the truth, or send word to the marshal herself. Goodness always had its ends. Grace never came without limits.

“I pushed my mother down the stairs. She uhm, there was a fight. She had me cornered and I pushed her. Then I ran,” she shrugged and suddenly felt like a much younger version of herself.

“Cornered?” Rio probed.

“There were things she wanted off me and I did not wish to comply.”

In her face, Agatha could see the frustration, that she wanted to know more, that she craved to understand. What if this wasn’t enough?

“I can leave as soon as the rain stops,” Agatha swallowed hard.

“Whatever are you talking about? How would you even make it up the path like this?” Rio shook her head.

“I can if it gives me a head start over the marshal.”

“What marshal?”

“The one you will undoubtedly call upon in the morning to collect me!” Agatha felt snot in her nose and wiped it on her sleeve. Fuck. She shouldn’t have come, she should have waited out the storm and built a new shelter or just moved past the area. 

She felt a gentle, cold hand on hers, still smeared in soil and rain water.

“I’m not calling on anyone to collect you,” Rio said with the softest voice, “I am not sure who you think I am, but I don’t traffic in defenseless women. I couldn’t live with myself if any harm came to you.”

Agatha registered the words, but her eyes stayed fixated on her hand. How long had it been since she had felt this kind of touch, one not administered out of necessity, but kindness and solace? She couldn’t remember. Maybe she could spare a little trust. A tiny advance on it.

“I-I can help!” Is the first response that came to her mind, “I can cook or mend clothing or help clean or-”

Rio shook her head, “Agatha this is not upon the condition that you contribute something.”

She listened to her name rolling off of Rio's tongue for the very first time and suppressed a warm shudder. Or any further thought about it for that matter.

“I cannot feel useless any longer, please, give me something to do!”

“We can see in the morning,” Rio raised both her palms, rolling her shoulders, “it’s late and I’d like to shed the wet clothes.”

For some reason the thought made Agatha’s throat go dry. Before she could question it, Rio walked into the little bed chamber and quietly closed the door. 

A long breath escaped through Agatha’s nostrils, one she had not been aware she had been holding. 

Rio helped her into bed as soon as she had changed into a dry tunic. There was an encouraging smile on her lips as she closed the door behind herself, her features only illuminated by the light of the candle she was holding.

 

The morning came fast and without mercy. Agatha had come to hate Rio’s rooster that alerted at first light several times so loudly as if he sat right underneath the window. She hid her head underneath her pillow and heard Rio stir in the other room. Agatha doubted that the padded bench gave her any kind of good sleep. Making a metal note to offer her to trade sleeping arrangement for the next night, she tried to ignore the noisy animal and fell back asleep.

The next time she woke the room was already fully lit with the sun gently shining through the window. Slowly raising her head, Agatha heard a loud grumble coming from her stomach. For a moment she dreaded Rio’s undercooked, salty oat pudding but then quickly scolded herself for it and felt like the most unthankful person to walk the earth.

At least her leg had finally ceased throbbing. It itched now, and she hoped it was a sign of the wound finally closing. Perhaps that meant she could try walking to the door on her own, after all it would just be a few steps from the edge of the bed to the door and it would finally save her the embarrassment of needing Rio every time she wished to move. The only problem was, as soon as she had half raised herself up, her hands holding on to the bed post, she began feeling dizzy. Her stomach rumbled once more though not in hunger now. She barely managed to reach for the big enamel bow underneath the bedside table until she violently began spewing sour vomit. Hot tears flooded her eyes as she retched into the bowl. She did register the sounds of steps coming closer, but only realized they belonged to her host when Rio’s warm hands reached for her hair, holding it back as she continued to throw up.

“Are you alright?” Rio demanded to know, moving the bowl a little closer so she could lean over a little less. Agatha tried to nod, but it probably wasn’t a very clear answer. Fuck this felt so bad, it had already felt bad when she had thrown up into the river, but at least then nobody had been watching. She fell back against the wooden bedframe when there was nothing left in her stomach. She could feel the salty traces of her tears on her cheeks and tried to breathe through her mouth so she did not have to smell the vomit. Only then did she begin to feel Rio’s hand on her arm, her thumb somewhat underneath her night shift that had moved during the ordeal. She immediately retracted it as soon as she saw that Agatha glanced that way.

“Do you-, has that happened before?”

“Yes it’s uh- Nelly Seamoure had it too in her early days,” Agatha mumbled. She could vividly remember the neighbor's daughter throwing up out of her window, her husband yelling at her for not aiming the right way. Poor woman. They had moved away after she’d had her third child.

“We should really get a doctor to look at you, somewhere where no one knows you?” Rio suggested and Agatha heard how tentative her voice was and knew it was her own fault.

“No, I’m fine,” she tried to be kind about it, “I don’t need no doctor.”

She could hear Rio sigh in subtle frustration, but she chose to ignore it, “I would kill for some breakfast though,” she tried to reassure her and simultaneously change the topic. She did not want more attention than Rio was already giving her. She did like the warmth of the blanket after sleeping without one for so long, but she was still afraid it might choke her, if she ever grew too comfortable with it.

Rio gave her a wet cloth to clean her face and a glass of water. Agatha marvelled at how it reflected the morning light. At home, she had only ever drank from tin jars or mugs. She had seen the glass on Rio’s shelf the first day she had sat at her table, but not dared asking what she used it for.

“Do you ever eat any bread at all?” Agatha asked when Rio helped her sit down at the table.

“Uhm, no it usually wastes before I can get to it all, so it’s rather a squander to make it.”

“What if I baked some?” Agatha took a spoon full of porridge to her lips, hoping it would neither be too sweet or too salty this time. It was bland, as if she hadn’t seasoned it at all. Agatha carefully sat the spoon down and reached for a plate of berries.

Rio looked at her in surprise, “you know how to bake bread?”

“Well,” admittedly, this was the point where Agatha should have likely confessed that she had only ever helped bake bread, but that she had never been entrusted with the entire process from start to finish, but Rio did not need to know that. Rio could very well believe that she was a master-breadbaker. Because if Rio believed that, she might trust her with it. Which in turn meant one less reason to chase Agatha out.

However, it all did turn the following two days into a nerve stretching ordeal: Rio had flour, but she lacked yeast; so Agatha determined she would make her own. She would check on it at least three times per day, sniffing if the mix of flour and water was turning sour, even taking the jar to bed so it would stay warm enough. Bolts of joy shot through her when she woke up on the third morning with the dough all bubbly and ready to rise a loaf of bread.

Rio eyed her curiously when she used one of her larger wooden bowls to combine the sourdough with more flour, water, and salt. Agatha did not mind one bit. Rio wasn’t supervising, she was observing. She was interested, she asked questions, and her face morphed into all kinds of funny shapes when Agatha answered them as well as she could. Only when Rio left again to let Agatha knead the dough in peace, did she feel how her shoulders had sunken for just a moment. 

Rio invited her to come outside and enjoy a bit of sun. She even offered to set up a chair for her in the garden, but the thought of someone passing by made Agatha hesitate. As much as she longed to feel the warmth of the first proper spring days on her skin, the idea of being visible, her presence being noticed, tightened something in her chest. Maybe tomorrow, was what she told Rio, and hoped she would feel different about it soon.

That night, she lay in bed awake for hours, watching dark clouds pass by the bright full moon. Rio had refused the offer to sleep in the bed, insisting she was perfectly fine on the bench. Agatha felt like it was more her stubborn sense of pride, than actually feeling comfortable on that wretched thing. Rio carried herself with that kind of quiet, old‑fashioned chivalry, she would not admit that her bones ached in the morning, even though Agatha watched roll her shoulders and stretch her limbs often enough now. Some part of her enjoyed the eiderdowns a little less because of it. The rest was still excited that she was no longer sleeping on the forest floor, even though she knew that that was what she had chosen, that that was what she would ultimately go back to once she could walk again. 

This one week had passed so quickly, how quick would the next one come and go? A fortnight, Rio had said, and she had been pleased with the way Agatha’s skin looked now, the scab closing off the cut nicely. 

She could pick at it. Scratch it a little and claim it must have happened in her sleep.

Agatha turned around and pressed her face into the cold of the pillow. If it got infected she would get a fever and that wouldn’t help anyone at all, would it now? It would only make her weak again, even more dependent on care. And that was the last she wanted. Sleep came quickly after that, her hand resting on her growing belly as she dreamed of a woman amidst the dawning of the morrow, holding a candle out into the fog.

In the morning, right after the damn rooster crowed, Rio got up to bake Agatha’s bread. 

It did not rise properly, and it did not scar the way nice looking breads did. But it tasted delicious when Rio cut it, still a little warm, and fried up eggs and spinach for both of them, drizzling it in home-made, salty butter. A tiny bit of crumb got stuck to her upper lip when they gorged themselves on it, Agatha kept thinking about it for the rest of the day.
