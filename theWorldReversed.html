Chapter Notes

Initially, this story was completely focused on Agatha and Rio. The idea for it was sparked by them, but it has grown into something much more beautiful. This work, much like Agatha All Along, is first and foremost about a coven. It tells the tales of powerful female friendships and bonds that transcend magic. The characters in it deal with loss, grief, trauma, and pain. They are flawed and imperfect but they also experience tremendous love. I hope you enjoy reading it as much as I enjoyed writing it.

Agatha Harkness hates the holidays.

She refuses to put up a tree in her apartment and never opens the door for carolers. She despises the bone-chilling snowfall and festive decor adorning the city, treating the happiest season of the year with complete disinterest.

When she opens her email one morning to reveal a holiday-themed invite from the head of the history department, she curses and immediately deletes it, even though he is her boss.

Agatha later discovers, thanks to some dreadful small talk with a colleague, that all of the department heads are working with the president to put on an elaborate holiday party and every professor is encouraged to at least make an appearance. The event doubles as an end of semester farewell, where the staff are meant to share their current research plans and most memorable stories from the past year. Agatha can’t imagine anything worse.

The administration likely wants them to bond over stubborn students and badly made eggnog , but Agatha is busy, grading essays and overseeing her graduate students’ research before the year ends. She tries to get out of the event, claiming that she has presents to wrap and songs to sing, but her colleagues see right through her.

Agatha Harkness is never busy during the holidays.

Unless, as her students sometimes joke, she sneaks out at night to deliver lumps of coal and eat misbehaving children. When she overhears that particular rumor, she always smirks.

Unable to lie her way out of it, Agatha begrudgingly drags herself to the event. She curses small talk and Christmas joy under her breath as she goes, flicking on her turn signal when she spots the president’s house, visible just through the trees. She pulls up to the entrance and her mouth nearly falls open.

The impressive house is, of course, professionally decorated for the upcoming season. Agatha shouldn’t even be shocked, really, but her eyes are overwhelmed by the lights and icicles and piles of fake snow. She briefly wonders why they need fake snow when the real thing will likely fall soon. The sky is already brimming with it, the potential for violent winter weather.

Agatha makes a mental note to find her snow boots; they’re buried somewhere deep in her closet. The elaborate, twinkling lights hanging around the property nearly blind her and she blinks rapidly, swerving to avoid a math professor who seems blissfully unaware of his surroundings.

When Agatha steps out of her car, her gaze dances up the side of the house. She can see people talking and laughing just inside the windows. Their silhouettes are slightly obscured by the fake icicles that hang from each edge of the roof, glittering in the colorful outdoor lights. The sharp acrylic daggers dangle precariously above Agatha’s head as she makes her way to the front of the house. She eyes them with distrust before pushing the door open, eying the large holly wreath pinned there, and descending into her worst nightmare.

Unsurprisingly, the entire affair is horrible (aside from the homemade pumpkin cookies). Agatha, squeezed into a corner of the sitting room while the president gives a toast to, “some of the brightest minds in the country,” resolves to escape as soon as possible.

After the toasts have finally finished, overplayed Christmas music pounds in Agatha’s ears as she engages in yet another conversation about an intriguing class she’s currently teaching. The Early History of Witchcraft, Folklore, and Magical Mythology is a new course she crafted for this semester, an enthralling introduction designed to lure students into the study of witchlore.

“Yes, all of my students are doing well,” Agatha says, for the millionth time. Her students’ performance is no surprise to her; an early expression of her tough grading style and strict demeanor leaves only the best and most dedicated scholars in her classes. The other professors are wary of her methods, but Agatha knows how to artfully nurture those who are truly willing to learn.

After a brief conversation with the president, a quick rundown of her research plans and future class listings, Agatha silently grabs another mug of apple cider and heads for the door. Just the idea of stealing one of the president’s mugs makes her chuckle. She’s sure he can afford a replacement; perhaps he could cut back on the fake snow.

Agatha smirks as she reaches for the door handle. When she glances down, getting a good look at the other side of the light blue mug clutched in her hand, and spots the obnoxiously joyful reindeer painted there, she fights the urge to throw it out the window.

“Professor Harkness!” Agatha jumps at her name being called, nearly spilling cider down the front of her suit.

One of her colleagues, an older lady who works in the English department, pokes her head out from around the corner. Agatha tilts her head. She’s only met her a handful of times, exchanging small talk in the library and at research symposiums. She can’t quite remember her name (she thinks it might be something like Mary or Marsha) but she knows the woman is a classic holiday-lover. She wears brightly colored Christmas sweaters with snowflakes and displays an intricately decorated tree in her office.

Agatha promptly sends a prayer to every god and deity that could possibly exist (thanks to her research, she knows quite a few) and takes a gulp of cider before spinning to face her current obstacle to escape. The cider isn’t intoxicating, unfortunately, but it’s the best she has at the moment.

It seems like her colleague senses Agatha’s attempt to leave early, moving almost uncomfortably close to her. She smells strongly of cranberries, and a strange look crosses her face as she gazes at her notoriously antisocial colleague.

“It’s so good to see you!” Mary-or-maybe-Marsha croons, and Agatha smiles tightly. She’s effectively trapped in the entrance now, the woman’s hand on her arm.

“I’m actually heading home,” Agatha replies, tilting her head towards the front door. She watches in confusion as her colleague’s smile drops from her face suddenly.

“Why are you like this?” she asks, words slightly slurred.

Agatha’s eyebrows rise. Her gaze falls to the cup clutched in the other woman’s hands (no doubt containing something that inhibits the senses and loosens the tongue) and she fights an exaggerated eyeroll.

“Excuse me?” Agatha replies, thinly veiled anger dripping from her lips. Usually people crack under that tone, her lashing words. That doesn’t happen here.

“Why do you hate it so much?” the woman asks, undeterred by Agatha’s tone.

She doesn’t clarify what exactly she is referring to, but Agatha knows.

She whispers a barely-audible goodbye and pushes open the door, her colleague’s hand falling away from her arm with a thud. The gravel crunches beneath her feet as she heads for her car, lifting an arm to shield her eyes from the obnoxiously festive lights.

The entire way home, Agatha fights the urge to scream.

Her colleague’s words won’t leave her mind. They swirl in her brain like the snow that will soon fall outside, chilly and uncomfortable. She finds it odd that defensiveness is rising up in her with such a vengeance; it’s a feeling she seldom experiences. Most years, she’s content to spend the winter season alone, locked in her apartment with a good book to read or a complex research paper to write.

She doesn’t explain herself to others; she doesn't care to.

Some incredibly dramatic and villain-esque part of her yearns to turn the car around, slam the door of the president’s house open, and regale that barely-familiar woman with awful tales from her childhood because, really, she hasn’t always hated the holidays.

As a child, her view had been completely different. At that point in her life, still young and innocent, she hadn’t yet disappointed her mother. The bright red and green adorning every inch of their home had filled Agatha with pure joy; she would’ve loved fake snow back then.

She used to sit in the kitchen, legs dangling off a chair that was too big for her, just to smell the incredible holiday dishes baking as her grandmother prepared dinner. She had looked forward to seeing her aunts and cousins at family reunions and Christmas parties. All of her friends had believed in magic, in the power of holiday joy and laughter, and Agatha was no exception.

The world, covered in snow, had sparkled in the way that it only does for children and the incredibly naive.

Then, everything had changed. Agatha grew up, the rose-gold lens she once viewed life through falling away and shattering at her feet. Once the weighty expectations of her family settled upon her shoulders, she found she could not stand under them. The holidays were miserable for her after that; a stark reminder of how different she was from the rest of the Harknesses.

To her mother, every move Agatha made was wrong. Everything she said was obscene. She could do nothing right and, during the holidays, there was an entire room of people present to jeer and laugh at her. Agatha wasn’t like them. She didn’t have the same uptight poise. She wasn’t concerned with her appearance or promising future; she just wanted to survive her mother’s home.

Occasionally, Evanora looked at her daughter with something much more complex than simple disappointment lurking behind her eyes, but Agatha could never quite figure out what it was. How was Agatha expected to celebrate and be merry when she was so incredibly hated?

She moved away from her hometown as soon as she could, slamming the door on her old life with the force of a daughter exiled by her own mother. The day she left still haunts her dreams, a dark mark on the home she vows never to return to.

Years later, celebrating and decorating feel pointless, only reminding Agatha of the hateful family she left behind, worlds away in Salem.

She has a few friends and colleagues she can tolerate in the city she now calls home, but no one she really cares about. Occasionally, one of her coworkers forces her to try dating again, but nothing sticks. Agatha tells herself she is completely fine with that; she works better alone anyways. The relationships she holds most dear are with the doctoral students she mentors, each one of them digging deep into the narratives and histories of witches in hidden places across the globe; places that have thus far been ignored by academia.

Often, Agatha’s students don’t celebrate the holiday season either. They’re too busy unraveling loose threads of history, guided by the steady hand of one of the most notable and, coincidentally, Salem-born witchlore professors in the country (who adamantly refuses to study Salem, much to her students’ confusion).

When it gets cold outside, many of them spend time in Agatha’s office, which is hidden away in a quiet corner of the History department. The bookshelves lining the walls are missing the most popular reading material in the study of witchlore. Instead, they hold dramatic recollections of myth and legend, first-hand experiences and stories passed down through generations. Some of them are written in other languages, their covers old and frayed. Agatha has spent years curating her collection, striving to have the most inclusive and nuanced view in her field.

Her office door, never adorned with a wreath, is propped open even on Christmas, which her most devoted students appreciate. Their other professors take time off during the holiday season. Agatha imagines they revel in holiday cheer, spend time with family members, and reunite with old friends. Professor Harkness does not, instead opting to face the season alone, aside from the occasional student who comes to her for advice.

Finally pushing the door to her apartment open, Agatha rubs her hands together and basks in the glow of her fireplace. The warmth seems to chase away her previous anger. Shadows dance up and down the walls as she unties her boots and slips her jacket off.

Agatha prefers it this way, far from insufferable office holiday parties and family dinners.

Anything else would be intolerable.

Or so she thinks.

The past that Agatha has spent years trying to bury comes gasping back to life just a few weeks before Christmas, when a crisp white envelope appears in her mailbox.

She stares at it for an impossibly long moment, the door of her apartment slipping closed behind her. It’s sent from a company she doesn't recognize, but the return address is in Salem. That alone is enough to make her nervous and she hates it, hates the constant reminders of her hometown found even miles away, even though she knows much of it is her own fault.

Following some unexplainable desire, Agatha had chosen to study witchlore at nineteen years old, which meant she could never quite escape Salem. The two hundred individuals accused of witchcraft in her hometown, their trials known far and wide, lurk dangerously in every corner of her life.

They hover just on the margins of her work and she encounters them, constantly, in the literature she reads and the research she conducts. She hears whispers of them in the halls of the History department, in new journals that have been uncovered or sites that are being investigated. They never leave her.

Agatha doesn’t look any closer, though.

She often wonders why she couldn’t have picked something else to dedicate her life to, like marine biology (or even regular biology would do).

She tentatively runs her finger over the word Salem , which is written in loopy cursive on the front of the envelope, before dropping the letter unceremoniously into one of her desk drawers.

After that, she tries very hard to ignore it, slipping her shoes off and pouring herself a glass of wine. She has work to do. Her eyes drift across her apartment, where articles and thick tomes on witchcraft and its history dominate every surface—including the walls. Her own diplomas and research awards are framed in the living room.

She pulls a stack of papers out of her bag, flipping through until she finds what she’s looking for. Several of her introductory students are taking advantage of her offer to review their final essay drafts before the end of the semester. The next one on her list, which she places in front of her with a flourish, is on the 1581 witch trials in Germany. It’s an intriguing topic but in just a few minutes, Agatha notices six slight spelling and grammatical errors and clicks her tongue. She grabs her red pen from behind her ear and circles the offending words.

She pushes the essay away for a moment and lifts her glass to her mouth. A quick glance at the window reveals that it’s starting to snow outside, little flecks of white sticking to the windows. She rolls her eyes at that, dreading the freezing air and cold feet she’ll be forced to endure for the foreseeable future.

After finishing her revision for the night, and exhausting her last excuse, Agatha finally retrieves the strange envelope from her desk drawer.

She moves to the couch, wine glass and letter opener in hand. She doesn’t get much mail these days, aside from the occasional bill or pamphlet encouraging her to purchase some fancy new credit card. The universe also thinks she needs a new couch, if the junk mail she receives is anything to go on.

She slices the envelope open and lets the letter opener fall from her grip and clatter into the glass dish perched on the side table. Flipping the envelope over for the first time, Agatha’s gaze is drawn to the deep blue wax seal, stark and obvious against the light cardstock. The slightly melted symbol impressed on it looks a bit runic; certainly an interesting choice. She drops the envelope onto the coffee table and unfolds the letter.

Her eyes drift down the piece of paper clutched in her hand.

Agatha's mouth falls open when her mother’s name appears, imposing and unexpected. She reads the entire thing quickly, her hand beginning to shake as she nears the end of the unnecessarily long final paragraph. Blindly reaching out, eyes still on the cream-colored paper, she slides her wine glass onto the coffee table. She barely notices when it tips over and shatters against the hardwood.

The letter is signed by the executor of Evanora Harkness’s estate.

Her mother is dead.

The words roll around in Agatha’s mind like rocks, slamming and pounding painfully against her skull.

The letter says she is the sole beneficiary; everything is left to her. She rereads that part a few times, eyes flitting back and forth quickly. Agatha hasn’t spoken to her mother since she left Salem at eighteen and she has absolutely no idea why she would be included in her will. After she left, Evanora cut her off from the family and refused to acknowledge she had ever existed.

Agatha imagines that the moment she left, all photos of her that once hung in the hallways of their home were ripped down. Her childhood bedroom was probably an office now, presentable and boring and everything Agatha wasn’t ; everything her mother had wanted her to be.

She flips the page.

Stapled to the back of the cover letter is a list of Harkness properties and assets that now belong to her. Her childhood home is the most notable bullet point. At the sight of the address, Agatha groans and throws the letter across the room. She nearly knocks over a vase.

Unfortunately, it doesn’t make her feel any better.

Tears gather in the corners of her eyes but she blinks them away, climbing to her feet and grabbing her phone. Denial rises inside of her like a tidal wave, fast and violent. This must be a mistake. It has to be a mistake. That, or she’s fallen into some alternate timeline in which she had been a perfect child, obedient and proper and not a lesbian.

Agatha retrieves the offending letter from the floor, glaring at it like her gaze can set it afire if she tries hard enough. She dials the numbers printed at the top of the page quickly, desperation building in her. She will not allow this to happen. She has worked too hard to distance herself from her family, to leave it all behind.

In her frenzied state, she only briefly notices the strange date at the top of the page. Where the year should be printed, there’s only a string of odd characters; certainly not numbers. Agatha shakes her head. It’s probably just a printing error.

This firm, which she has never heard of, is certainly incompetent.

The phone rings twice before a bright voice filters into Agatha’s ear. The woman on the other side of the phone quickly introduces herself as the executor’s assistant, but her voice is far too cheery for Agatha’s mood. She imagines her sitting in one of those horribly decorated office buildings, fir trees in every corner and silver snowflakes dangling from the ceiling. She shakes her head at the image.

Agatha rattles off the information she knows. Her mother is dead and she’s the sole beneficiary of her will, despite a lack of contact in decades. Some intern must have shredded an important document or overlooked an addendum somewhere. Her mother must have chosen someone else; someone who isn’t her .

She tells the assistant exactly this. The poor woman listens in silence until Agatha ceases her angry rant.

“Ms. Harkness—” she begins.

“It’s Dr. Harkness, actually,” Agatha snaps, cutting her off. She may despise her last name, and the family it ties her to, but she worked hard for her title; it’s the one part of her name she chose for herself.

“Oh! Of course, so sorry about that,” the assistant apologizes, her voice traveling an octave higher. She takes a deep breath and starts again. “Dr. Harkness, just one moment.”

Agatha hears pages turning on the other side of the phone. She drops her head onto the back of the couch, phone still pressed against her ear. She stares at the boring ceiling above her, plain and solid and uninteresting. She thinks about the textured ceiling of her childhood bedroom, tall and covered in slats of dark wood. If she really tries, she can hear her neighbors laughing next door. They’re always laughing. She hates it.

“Ah, here!” the assistant exclaims, apparently finding what she was searching for. “You are correct. Your mother named only you.”

Only her.

“What do I—” Agatha starts, her voice suddenly strained. “Do I have to do anything?”

“Nope! You are free to do as you see fit,” she answers cheerily.

“As I see fit?” Agatha questions, her heart pounding.

“With the estate,” the young assistant says, her voice still high and positive. Agatha isn’t sure if it’s real, or a figment of her own insane imagination, but she swears she hears Christmas music start up in the background.

She hangs up, tossing her phone down on the couch.

Her fingers shake as she reaches for her wine glass, trying to drown the feelings swelling inside of her, but it’s in pieces on the floor. Of course it is. The wind howls outside, loud and intrusive, as Agatha kneels down next to the couch, her knees colliding with cold wood.

She doesn’t care about her mother dying; she’s always hated her.

She doesn’t care about her stupid, elaborate, miserable childhood home either.

Agatha carefully picks up the shards of glass, each one a strange shape, and fights the tears burning in her eyes.

Later, restless and unable to succumb to sleep, Agatha stands at the window of her apartment and watches the snow fall. A glass of hot chocolate warms her fingers, the rich smell filling her senses. Despite being a self-proclaimed hater of the holiday season and the adjacent desserts that come with it, Agatha has an incredible sweet tooth; she always has.

She furrows her brows as tiny snowflakes melt against the thin barrier between her and the cold, intricate designs ceasing to exist as they collide with something sturdy. She observes each of them, their edges and swirls and shapes so unique; a phenomenon of nature. Water trickles down the outside of her brick building, thousands of tiny snowflakes returning to the ground; back to their beginning.

They seem drawn to the glass, despite it promising their end.

Agatha knows then, hot chocolate burning on her lips and snowflakes melting just inches from her face, that she will return to Salem.

Mercy and Divinity

This is, quite possibly, the worst idea Agatha has ever dreamed up.

She’s back in Salem, hand trembling as she points out another road to her driver. She still remembers how to navigate the area; she isn’t sure she could ever forget. She watches, heart pounding, as the forest slowly thins outside her window.

As they get closer.

When she was younger, the forest outside town was the only place she could escape her mother’s burning, judging gaze. She never lost her way; not out there.

Her driver turns and Agatha’s eyes widen as she gets a glimpse of the town she hasn’t seen in so long. Colors flash by her window quickly and she raises a hand to rest against the cold glass. There are cafes and stores she doesn’t recognize lining the streets, brick buildings with brightly colored doors that span the entire rainbow.

Glowing lights dangle overhead in an array of festive colors, leaping from one streetlight to the next. They’re not particularly visible against the bright sky, but Agatha notices them nonetheless. One wooden sign, hanging above the entrance to a cafe, advertises witch-themed desserts and coffee creations. Agatha scoffs, but she’s secretly curious.

She also sees a particularly jolly man dressed as Santa, children gathered around him as he dances in a shop window. The town is busy, countless tourists milling through the streets. They step out of the museums in coordinated tour groups, hats pulled low over their ears.

Most of them are easy to spot, walking slowly and aiming their cameras at everything in sight. Agatha watches someone take a photo of the Starbucks across the street and fights a laugh; that certainly wasn’t there in the 1600s.

She’s quietly glad she didn’t end up back here in October. Even as a kid, she had dreaded the thousands of people who descend on the town every fall; Halloween enthusiasts begging to hear stories of “real life” witches, to press their hands against brick buildings and chipped memorials.

Occasionally, a daring tourist had knocked on the front door of the Harkness house; its dark, antique exterior making it a target. The unwelcome visitors often asked questions about their family history and wondered “if they knew any witches.” Evanora always slammed the door on them, her face full of rage. Agatha never asked why it made her so upset.

Perhaps it's that experience which drove Agatha to become a historian, some deep curiosity urging her to speak to the dead instead of just at them. Living in Salem, she learned that sometimes, if you listen close enough, they speak back.

Agatha tears her eyes away from one particularly busy museum and spots a hotel down the street. She fights a groan when she recognizes the name. Desperate to not stay overnight in her childhood home, she had tried to book a room there.

She had tried to book a room at any hotel in Salem, actually, but it was no use. She couldn’t find a single one available on such short notice. Perhaps it has something to do with the impending holiday season or maybe it’s always like this.

Either way, Agatha is annoyed.

“Home for the holidays, are you?” her Uber driver asks with a smile, and Agatha wonders if he can sense the tension radiating from her clenched fists and stoic expression.

He glances at her in the rearview mirror, slowing down to let a family cross the street in front of them. Agatha contemplates him quietly. His name is Devon and he has five stars on the Uber app. He seems like a decently nice person.

Agatha’s only complaint is that he asks too many questions. Anyone else would likely appreciate that but, shockingly, Agatha isn’t the chattiest of passengers. Especially now, finding herself back in the town she swore she would never return to. Devon’s small talk interrupts her silent contemplation of her awful choices.

“Something like that.” Agatha dodges the question, pressing her thumb against the condensation on her window.

She watches, eyes wide, as they leave the center of town and draw closer to the home her family has owned for generations; the land she grew up on. Dark trees with miniscule foliage fly past them and Agatha blinks rapidly to keep them in focus.

“The holidays can be a real hard time,” Devon replies knowingly, his eyes scanning across the road.

Agatha hums noncommittally; perhaps her distress is evident. They drive past one of witch hunt memorials and Agatha observes the tourists holding cameras and trinkets, flowers and letters. She turns her head to watch the scene grow smaller behind them.

They drive on, the car quiet aside from the gentle hum of the engine and the low music playing in the background.

Suddenly, painful familiarity barrels straight into Agatha’s chest. The land in front of her seems to shift and reappear in her mind like an old, faded photograph. She recognizes the trees, the subtle dips in the road, the ditch that’s always been slightly deeper on the right side.

“This one here,” she directs, pointing out a brick driveway on the left. It’s almost completely hidden by trees and bushes, vines climbing up through the iron gate which is, oddly, open.

The car jerks to a stop in front of Agatha’s childhood home, the ancient wood paneling nearly black after centuries of weathering. The windows are just as dark, curtains drawn. Agatha can’t see inside; can’t anticipate what might be waiting for her. She steps out of the car and thanks Devon, dark purple suitcase in hand.

“Happy holidays!” Devon calls from the front of the car. Agatha smiles weakly at him and pushes the door shut.

She turns, her eyes finally settling on the house in front of her. It looks older, drooping under the impossible weight of so many memories. The front garden looks like it hasn’t been tended to in years. Vines spiral around the columns, overgrown and reaching for something.

In some twisted way, Agatha knows they’re reaching for her.

Dozens of dead or soon-to-be dead flowers dot the landscape. Agatha kneels down next to one of them, still on the edge of the property. She wrinkles her nose in confusion when the delicate petals break apart and slip through her fingers. When she was younger, the front garden was her mother’s pride and joy, a bright and manicured statement to the neighborhood that the Harkness family was one to respect.

Agatha’s eyes widen as she takes in the formerly vibrant grass, patches of it now brown and trampled under her feet. It’s so devoid of life, so different from how she remembers it. She expected the property to be in pristine condition, upheld and managed by her mother the same way it had been in the past. Apparently, the opposite is true.

When Agatha goes to step forward, lifting a boot from one decorative stone to the next, it feels like her feet are rooted to the ground. She imagines that the earth recognizes her, the wayward Harkness daughter, and is trying to pull her back into the fold.

Salem has always felt like that to her; as if some deeper force is constantly trying to suck her back in. At eighteen years old she had physically fled to get away from it. Perhaps it’s her flair for the dramatic or her constant study of witches, magic, and fate, but Agatha tends to err on the side of caution when it comes to feelings like that.

She makes a conscious effort to resist the pull of whatever divine force is tugging on her strings. History tells her that blindly following a path like that can be dangerous. Awful, catastrophic choices have been made by those who think fate is urging them forward.

At the heart of it, though, the idea of not being truly in control is what terrifies Agatha the most. For that reason alone, she’s seriously debating calling another Uber and fleeing back to the airport. Perhaps Devon is just down the street, taking a break at one of Salem’s many coffee shops before he picks up another passenger.

Agatha squeezes her eyes shut, trying to center herself. The wind burns the back of her throat as she forces air into her lungs. She reminds herself why she’s doing this: she wants answers.

After the initial shock and denial had dissipated, after she had reread the executor’s letter dozens of times, Agatha was left completely clueless about her mother’s death. Unsure what else to do, she had locked her feelings away in some far-away corner of her mind and tried desperately to view the situation through an entirely academic lens.

She’s comfortable in that position, sorting through bits and pieces of history to understand what transpired and why, and it really doesn’t make sense that she was included in Evanora’s will. Agatha spent a few days investigating, shut behind her office door, but she could find no answers from miles away.

Every official she called in Salem refused to speak with her over the phone; they were weirdly paranoid. She even contacted the local mortician and received no answer. She left a rambling, dramatic voicemail instead (or maybe four, but she would never admit to that).

It seemed that Evanora had simply disappeared, no mention of her death appearing online or in the local paper. Agatha wasn’t satisfied with that; her entire career as a historian was built on the stories of the long dead. People didn’t just disappear. Her mother wouldn’t just disappear.

Despite Agatha’s urge to sell her childhood home and never return to Salem again, her academic curiosity got the best of her.

It so often did.

She booked a plane ticket, deciding that she would return to her hometown just long enough to discover what happened to her mother. In the absolute best case scenario, it would only take a few days. Then, after everything was settled, Agatha would sell the property and close this chapter of her life for good. After that, she would never have to come back; never have to step foot in Salem again.

It would be easier to arrange everything from her hometown anyways, she reasoned. She imagined managing an entire estate over the phone—and from hundreds of miles away—wasn’t easy.

It certainly would be less emotionally taxing, though.

Agatha is reminded of the advice she often gives her students, gently pushing them towards excellence. Perhaps another day.

That voice.

Anchors

Well, most of the time.

It’s the last time she sees her.

Alice, for the first time in years .

Alice, who has haunted her dreams for decades .

Agathos .

Rio Vidal.

Swallowing Light

“Damn it.” Agatha grumbles, burying her hands in the soft pockets of her robe. She stands on the front porch of her family’s abandoned house, obnoxiously large sunglasses shielding her eyes from the glare of an entirely white yard.

Snow.

Agatha thinks it’s horrid.

Last night, after her former-friends-turned-married-couple left, Agatha did some research. She opens her laptop and clicks into the tabs she was looking at before she gave up, deciding she was too exhausted to keep working. She eventually gave into fitful sleep in the eerie living room, her dreams full of dark lips and teasing eyes.

If she happens to feel some strange, unexplained pull towards the obnoxious woman (Rio, she reminds herself), it’s unrelated.

Then again, Agatha isn’t sure she knows what a mortician is even supposed to look like. A vibrant memory featuring a skull ring perched on one of Rio’s fingers jolts into Agatha’s mind and she fights a morbid chuckle.

Perhaps it was obvious.

From what Agatha found during her midnight scrolling, Rio didn’t exist on the internet until recently. She started working in Salem a couple years ago and began posting a blog about obscure plants found in the area and then…

That’s it.

Agatha shuts her computer with a sigh and drops her head onto the back of the plush chair. Rio’s lack of online presence isn’t weird, but it is annoying. Many individuals don’t have large digital footprints, herself included.

Agatha actively avoided having an online presence for years, fearful that her mother or some other angry family member would track her down and drag her back to Salem, back to her shattered life. At that point, with her classes filling up and her name gaining recognition, Agatha was hopeful that not even the wrath of Evanora Harkness could drag her back to Salem.

Apparently her death could, though.

Of course, her mother has actually been dead for a decade and Agatha could’ve safely appeared on the internet much sooner. That knowledge makes her unreasonably angry. Agatha sighs, climbing to her feet and sliding her laptop away. She has bigger problems to deal with than her former lack of online presence. She slips into the bathroom, desperate to steer her thoughts away from her dead mother.

Her robe falls to the ground behind her and she tests the faucet, turning it both ways until warm water rushes over her fingertips. As a kid, she could never remember which direction was for the hot water and which would startle her with freezing cold spray.

The scalding rivulets bead down her arms and shoulders as she rests her head against the tile.

Agatha just can’t wrap her head around her.

Rio .

She knows there are more important things to be thinking about: dead mothers and abandoned homes, but she wants something else, a distraction from the insanity that is now her upturned life.

She wishes Jen and Alice would hurry up so she could interrogate them about her newest distraction instead of just scrolling for hours through unrelated Google results and dead ends.

Agatha would call Jen and badger her to drive faster but she no longer has her phone number. She has her self-imposed thirty years of solitude to thank for that. All of her contacts from Salem are gone, lost when she wiped her phone and bought a new one in a different city. She didn’t want to speak to any of them again.

Well, she did, but she knew staying in contact would make everything harder. Some small part of Agatha, a tiny fragment of her heart that is just now beginning to breathe again, wishes she had done it all differently.

Maybe if she had, her mother wouldn’t be dead and she wouldn’t be here, alone in an empty house, wondering what a plant-loving mortician does for fun.

“Agatha!” Alice’s voice is far too bright and cheery for the weather outside but Agatha is still surprisingly glad to see her.

She stands on the front steps with a huge smile plastered on her face. She’s bundled in a large coat emblazoned with the Salem Police Department logo. An orange scarf is tight around her neck.

Agatha spots the snowball in her hand just in time to swing the door back in front of her and dodge. Her stomach growls from her hiding spot and she desperately hopes the duo made breakfast plans. The blueberry muffins she consumed yesterday had been a rare find in the empty Harkness house.

“Alice! Hurry up!” Jen’s voice travels from the car and Agatha is suddenly grateful for her impatient nature.

Agatha peeks around the door and Alice holds her gloved hands up in mock surrender.

She slides into the back seat of Jen’s fancy car and is overwhelmed by long-forgotten memories—a summer full of chaos. Jen’s father taught her to drive as soon as she turned sixteen, but Alice and Agatha weren’t as lucky.

Evanora said she had better things to do than teach an annoyed and stubborn teenager to navigate and steer, so she hired Agatha a tutor. He was a horrid man from Boston, uptight and rude. Unsurprisingly, Agatha despised him. She imagined Evanora had called in some favor with him because he certainly didn’t seem to enjoy teaching. Alice’s mom, on the other hand, didn’t realize that she even needed to be taught, too caught up in an upcoming album release to think about anything else.

And so naturally, Jen taught both of them, relaying the knowledge that her parents had passed along. She screamed and laughed maniacally as Agatha swerved down back roads and got lost in the woods. Alice even ran into a ditch once, yelling that they were all going to die as the car rolled to a stop.

As Alice opens the door and slides into the driver’s seat, Agatha hopes that her skills have improved since then.

“So, where do we start?” Agatha asks carefully, hands fiddling in her lap.

“I made a list of people who might know something,” Jen says, and Agatha fights the urge to roll her eyes. Jen always makes a list. “Since we have you with us, a legal relative of Evanora, you can probably find out more than we ever could before.”

Agatha ignores the unwelcome guilt that settles over her. She wasn’t here. The words jolt through her.

Alice turns off Agatha’s street, flicking the turn signal with her hand. “I already tried the police station, obviously. They don’t have any record of her death.”

“What does that mean?” Agatha asks quietly, even though she’s pretty sure she knows the answer.

“I have no idea” Alice grumbles, her fingers tight on the steering wheel. “Most of the time, if someone dies at their home, the police are called, but we have no record of it.”

“Who pronounced her dead, then?” Agatha asks.

“Someone in the coroner’s office,” Jen replies, and Agatha's stomach drops. She quickly reminds herself that Rio didn’t work in Salem back then, even if she does coronary work now.

“That’s our first stop,” Alice quips. “I did a little digging and found out that Rio’s predecessor organized Evanora’s memorial. He would’ve processed her body, too.”

Agatha rests her head against the glass and tries to talk herself out of booking the first flight home.

“I’m sure she still has the files,” Jen adds.

“Who is she to you?” Agatha asks, her voice loud. She winces at her tone. Alice peers at her in the rearview mirror, a confused expression on her face.

“A friend,” Jen replies with a smirk. Agatha squeezes her hands together. Jen always enjoyed this when they were teenagers, riling her best friend up. Particularly when it came to Agatha’s (uneventful) love life. Not that Rio has anything to do with that. It’s not like her strange appearance on Agatha’s lawn, on her bench, has been stuck in her brain since yesterday.

“I met her through the police department,” Alice offers, when the silence grows tense and heavy. “She does coronary work for us, sometimes. Murder investigations and the like.”

Agatha supposes that makes sense. It lessens the rapid pounding of her heart, at least. Salem is a small town, after all. Of course they would all know each other.

“How did she know you were even here, Agatha?” Jen asks. “I mean, usually I don’t question her weird knowledge but this was strange, even for her.”

Agatha scoffs. “She was at my house, Jen . Just showed up”—she waves her hands, a bundle of emotions flaring up in her chest—“and sat on my bench.” She crosses her arms across her chest with a huff.

“Your bench?” Alice inquires, obviously fighting a laugh as her eyes flick to the mirror and take in Agatha’s expression. “Does it have your name inscribed on it or something?”

Jen draws her hands through the air in front of her. “I can see it now: Property of Agatha Harkness. ”

“Shut up,” Agatha grumbles.

“Can we get breakfast?” she asks after a moment, swiftly changing the subject to distract from her flushed cheeks. Jen definitely notices but, thankfully, keeps her mouth shut.

She totally smirks, though.

Eventually, Alice pulls into the parking lot of the funeral home. Agatha drops her head against the back of her seat and wants to scream, instead balling up the green and red paper that had once held her breakfast burrito.

Thankfully, Alice and Jen had introduced her to a diner she didn’t recognize from her childhood, all wood tones and warm lighting. They had grabbed their food in styrofoam takeout boxes, eager to get to Rio’s office. The owner, a charming lady in a Christmas sweater named Sharon, had shoved peppermint milkshakes into their waiting hands as well.

“Best thing on the menu, a tiny slice of joy.” She had assured them when three questioning looks were directed at her. Agatha briefly wondered if it was insane to drink a milkshake in December.

“Every day has joy in it, if you know where to look.” Sharon had also said, winking in the trio's direction as they rushed back to the car. Alice and Jen thanked her with familiar smiles.

Agatha had scarfed down her food despite the uneasy churning of her stomach. She had rolled her eyes at it at first but milkshake is good; she can’t deny it. The bright flavors burst across her tongue and she silently thanks Sharon.

She still isn’t sure about the joy thing, though. Agatha has a hard enough time finding the joy on a normal day, one that’s not spent searching her haunted hometown for scraps of information regarding her mother’s strange and mysterious death.

Plus, she really doesn’t want to be here —not with her.

Rio.

Agatha spots her through the window before Alice even pushes the front door open. She downs the last of her milkshake, tossing the Christmas-themed cup in the trash with perhaps more force than necessary and steals another glance in the window.

Rio’s hair is pulled up, tight around her ears. A few strands of dark hair have fallen loose and now frame her face. Her brow is furrowed, like she’s concentrating on something.

The scene is a stark contrast from their first meeting and it has Agatha reeling. Rio looks so normal, like an average person doing their job in an average office. This Rio looks like she hadn’t burst into Agatha’s life—and property—and proceeded to help turn her life upside down.

She’s dressed differently, too. Her long sleeves and ripped jeans have been swapped for black scrubs, except, when Agatha squints a little she notices something that the scrubs aren’t black.

They’re green, impossibly dark green.

To make matters even worse for Agatha’s blood pressure, Rio’s arms are on display. The long sleeve shirt she has on underneath her scrubs is pushed up past her elbows. She twirls a pen in her hand and Agatha fights to pull her eyes away from the tattoos that climb up her pale arms, now completely visible as the door flies open in front of her.

Agatha is awful at keeping plants alive and as a result, she can’t name a single plant or flower dancing across Rio’s wrists in dark ink but suddenly, she wishes to know all of them; to roll their names around in her mouth and trace the fine lines of their leaves. She rips her eyes away.

The office is devoid of holiday decorations aside from a tiny ceramic Christmas tree perched on the front desk. It looks older than Agatha, the colors faded and dim. The lights still burn bright, though, their light casting half of Rio’s face in warm hues.

There’s someone else there, too, who Agatha only notices once she steps fully inside. She thinks he’s a kid for a moment, before she notices his smudged eyeliner and long limbs. He leans back in a chair at the front desk, dressed in similar dark colors to Rio. His ears drip with silver jewelry and a blue notebook is held tight in his hands.

He continues doodling as the three of them enter, not bothering to look up as he drags his pen sharply against paper. A name tag pinned to his chest reveals his name is Billy. Intern is scribbled underneath in a teenage scrawl that is all too familiar to Agatha.

She often sees it in her introductory classes. Once, a few years ago, when she still had her students handwrite their essays, an entire class of clueless first years with awful handwriting was enough to make her regret her career choices.

“Alice!” Rio’s voice fills Agatha’s ears with a rush and her gaze is drawn away from the distracted intern. Rio tosses the clipboard she was holding aside and steps forward. She sounds so friendly and casual, albeit confused at their sudden arrival.

When Agatha first interacted with her, Rio was teasing and guarded, flirty and mischievous. Now, that’s all missing, replaced with a warm and friendly smile that Rio directs at Alice as she pushes her way inside and moves towards the front counter.

“What are you doing here? Jen and—“ Rio continues, her voice dying in her throat when her eyes flick to Agatha.

Rio’s eyes silently consume her; darkness swallowing light without a sound.

Agatha can’t think of anything to do, anything to say. It seems foolish to offer her hand to a woman who might not know her name but still seems to see right through her. Agatha’s mouth opens but no words come out. She's normally not this bad at flirting, but she’s not quite sure why that would matter because she’s certainly not—

Then, she feels it again: that pull.

“I’m Agatha. We’re here to arrest you,” she deadpans before she can stop herself, dropping her name just in case Rio doesn’t know it yet. She moves forward without a second thought, before her brain can catch up and stop her from doing something reckless.

The teenager in the corner looks up now, his notebook snapping shut, but Agatha barely notices his confused gaze on her.

“For trespassing on my property,” Agatha finishes in a dramatic whisper. She wishes she had a set of handcuffs to twirl in the air, but she smirks anyway.

Rio smirks back.

“Are you now?” Her mood shifts, the blaze that Agatha had been expecting to find in Rio’s eyes returning as quickly as fire catches. Her gaze seems warmer than before, Agatha notices, and then quickly banishes the thought.

Alice jumps in between them, waving a hand. “No, we’re not here to arrest you, Rio. Besides, Agatha doesn’t have the authority.” Alice shoots a glare in Agatha’s direction.

“We just have some questions about a case your predecessor caught nearly a decade ago,” Jen finishes for her wife, shooting another glare at Agatha. She rolls her eyes.

The strange tension quickly diffuses and Agatha lets out a breath she hadn’t known she was holding, a rush of air that Rio seems to notice. Her eyes linger on Agatha for a moment, narrowing slightly before she turns to face Alice and Jen.

“Who?” Rio asks shortly.

“Her mom.” Alice points a finger towards Agatha, and Rio’s eyebrows twitch, just a bit, but she doesn’t turn her head. Agatha wouldn’t have noticed if she hadn’t been staring.

She hates herself just a little, for noticing.

Rio doesn’t ask any other questions and Agatha reasons that Alice must show up with strange requests like this all the time, acting on secret police business and solving complicated mysteries. Alice waves a hard at the teenager lounging in the corner, Billy, before they can leave the room.

“Staying out of trouble, kid?” she asks.

Alice’s words spark something in the back of Agatha’s mind, bits of a whispered conversation floating into her brain, but she’s too preoccupied by Rio to follow the train of thought any further.

Billy smirks up at Alice, long eyelashes fanning across his cheek.

“No way.”

He ducks his head, flipping his notebook open on his lap. Agatha briefly glimpses sharp blue lines, the shapes they form oddly runic and familiar, before turning back to Rio.

She leads the three of them through the office quietly, stepping down a dark corridor and into a small room. It smells old, not dissimilar to Agatha’s now-abandoned childhood home. Filing cabinets line the walls, handwritten and alphabetized labels plastered above each metal handle.

“Why so old school, Rio?” Jen asks with a laugh, pointing to one of the peeling labels. Alice leans against the doorframe next to her. She seems unsurprised by the ancient state of the mortuary‘s recordkeeping.

Agatha runs a finger over one of the labels, pressing her hand against the cold metal and the weathered paper. She fights a smile, casting her gaze around the room. A single lightbulb fizzes and buzzes above their heads.

The room is stuck decades in the past.

Just like Agatha.

Each box is meticulously labeled and Agatha’s heart sings at the recordkeeping, the easy access to years of information. She wants to keep her nerdy pleasure to herself but the words slide out of her mouth as if some barrier—which usually keeps her thoughts inside—has been removed.

Agatha pretends she doesn’t know why.

“I get it. There’s comfort in the physical,” She says, moving in front of the cabinet labelled G-H in spidery cursive. She wonders briefly if it’s Rio's handwriting.

“The physical, huh?” Rio appears next to her and Agatha fights the urge to jump out of her skin at her sudden appearance, even though she knew she was there. Agatha slides the drawer open with a creak.

“History professor,” Agatha replies quietly, waving a hand towards herself. Agatha’s fingers dance across the organized folders with practiced ease. She flips through them quickly and waits for her mother’s name to appear.

”Really?” Rio’s eyebrows lift in Agatha’s peripheral vision and she watches her expression change from teasing to curious. “What do you study? Teach?”

Agatha ignores the way her heart stutters in her chest when she glimpses Evanora’s name just inches away. She opens her mouth to answer Rio but hesitates. She wonders if she even should. Perhaps she should take her folder and leave, slamming the door behind her like she had after their first meeting.

This time, though, she isn’t in her own yard. She’s standing inside a dark mortuary office, no longer in control. She’s holding a folder labeled Evanora Harkness, desperate for someone in Salem to understand her. Suddenly, Agatha finds that there are worse things than telling Rio, a virtual stranger, more of her personal information. She already has her address, after all. If she was a serial killer, Agatha would probably be dead by now.

“Witches.”

She pulls the folder of information out and turns to face Rio, stopping when she sees the expression on her face. She looks surprised. It’s a new look, a new expression that Agatha catalogs and slides into the back of her mind. She slips it into her own mental system of filing cabinets, not unlike the ancient ones around her.

“You study… witches?” Rio asks, a single dark eyebrow raised.

“Yes.” Agatha slams the cabinet drawer shut with too much force. It rattles the ground under her feet. Rio doesn’t jump.

“Billy is obsessed with witches.” The statement is so unexpected, so soft and knowing, that Agatha's mouth nearly falls open.

Silence stretches between the two of them, and Agatha lets her eyes dance to the doorway.  Alice and Jen are noticeably absent.

When it’s clear Agatha is not going to respond, Rio speaks again.

“Why?”

The simple question catches Agatha off guard. She doesn’t think anyone has ever asked her why before. Researchers have flocked to her presentations, have bought her books and endorsed her findings, but no one has ever bothered to inquire at her motivations or wonder why she’s the only professor in their department who researches witches, such an obscure and often discarded part of history.

“I was born here.” Agatha turns, meeting Rio’s eyes again. She waves her hands around like merely being in Salem is an explanation all on its own. “I left as soon as I could but…”

Agatha steps close to the sole window in the room and watches the snow fall outside, each fleck home to infinite patterns and repeating crystals. The folder with her mother’s name on it feels heavy in Agatha’s hand but she can’t be bothered to try and stop herself from speaking. Something deep in her chest makes it feel pointless.

“It always calls me back.”

The words spill from Agatha’s mouth like a never-ending fountain. Her explanation rushes to fill the space between her and Rio, the two of them standing tensely in the barely-illuminated room, trapped between endless records of death.

“Have you ever thought that maybe it’s you?” Rio asks from behind Agatha. Her voice is low and cautious but it’s genuine. It’s the only thing that keeps Agatha from running, that hint of sincerity.

“What?” Agatha shoots back, her tone beginning to sharpen. She doesn’t turn around, though, keeping her eyes firmly planted on the dirty glass in front of her, on the land beyond. The sun burns her eyes and leaves spots in her vision.

“Maybe it’s you calling out to Salem,” Rio says. “Not the other way around.”

Agatha exhales. Gasps, really.

She turns to face Rio then, leaving behind the specks of snow that seem to fall faster the longer she watches them.

“You think so?” she asks, her voice betraying a rare uncertainty. The wind bangs against the thin wall behind her, demanding to be heard.

“I think that you wouldn’t be back here unless you wanted to be.” Rio seems certain in this knowledge but Agatha isn’t sure how. They don’t know each other.

After a moment of silence, Rio speaks again.

”You don’t seem like the type of person who is called— more like someone who does the calling.”

I came to meet you. Her words from yesterday suddenly seem much more important.

Agatha opens her mouth to say something witty, something that will make Rio’s mouth twist and her eyes blaze, but she has nothing. She glances down at the file in her hands, turning it over against her palms. Memories seem hidden in its surface, dancing in the dark shadows of the room and nestled in the quiet breaths of Rio next to her.

Agatha recalls the day she walked into a lecture on the Salem witches and felt her blood heat up in her veins.

Is it possible that the tendrils rooting her to the front yard of her childhood home didn’t belong to some foreign entity determined to suck her back in, but to her?

Something seems to wake up inside of her, something terrifying and freeing. She brushes past Rio and into the hallway, gasping for breath that seems suddenly stolen from her lungs.

Distraction

Turns out, Alice and Jen had escaped the ancient files, layers of dust, and Agatha and Rio’s strange tension to have a hushed conversation in a room down the hall. Agatha assumes it’s used for meetings and consultations, dimly lit with a wooden table in the center. She casts her eyes up and down the walls, noting the oil paintings of plants and flowers adorning the space. They’re all antique, with yellowed paper and dates going back decades.

Rio likes plants, Agatha already knows. She also seems to have an affinity for the antique. Agatha may not understand Rio’s fascination with the natural (she famously kills everything lush and living that she touches), but she does understand her appreciation of the old—the often forgotten.

Billy is sprawled in one of the chairs, his feet draped over the side in a display of effortless teenage athleticism. It makes Agatha think of a younger Alice, who was always vaulting over the backs of couches and kicking her socked feet up on tables. Alice and Jen sit opposite him at the table, their hands twined together.

Agatha finds the three of them there moments after she leaves Rio. Her heart is still thudding dramatically in her chest, Rio’s words reverberating painfully in her skull. She extends a hand to Billy without giving much thought to it, but something changes in his body, in his expression, the moment he sees her.

He jumps from his relaxed position and shakes her hand with far too much enthusiasm for the way she feels on the inside, like a fire has just started burning in her chest.

“I’m Billy!” he chirps. “Sorry I didn’t introduce myself earlier. I didn't realize you were, well, you.” Agatha doesn’t understand what he’s rambling about but she writes it off as nothing and moves on. She’s never quite understood teenagers, not even when she was one.

“Agatha.” She offers her name up as well, already tired of introducing herself even though she’s only done it twice today.

“I know. Jen and Alice told me all about you,” Billy nods.

“Did they?” Agatha raises an eyebrow in their direction, tossing the folder into the center of the table. “Found it.”

Alice’s eyes light up and she reaches out to grab it, but as Agatha sinks into the chair at the head of the table, she has a sneaking suspicion they won’t be finding much in the suspiciously thin file.

“Rio!” Jen snaps after a few tense moments of silence. Her voice is loud from where she’s peering over Alice’s shoulder. It carries down the hallway, certainly reaching Rio.

Speak of the devil.

She appears in the doorway and Agatha steals the folder away from her friends to avoid looking at her. She scans the little information available there. Her breath catches as she notices the exact date of Evanora’s death, the forever immortalized moment in which she ceased to exist. The date isn’t familiar to Agatha; why would it be?

She didn’t feel a great pain in her chest or experience the painful severing of a once-cherished bond. She probably spent hours immersed in a book or reading essays that day, completely unaware that her mother was dying.

“Did she have an autopsy?” Jen asks, her voice sharp.

Rio settles into the chair next to Agatha, much to her dismay, and pushes right into her personal space. She tucks her legs up underneath her and leans over the pages, sliding her finger down lines of text.

Agatha tries to ignore their closeness, dragging her index finger over the bold letters of her mother’s name instead—in Latin it means light giver. Agatha thinks that this is painfully ironic.

Rio leans closer.

“No.” Rio shakes her head. “I mean, the mortician wouldn’t have done one anyway, that’s the coroner’s job, but it would still be indicated on the report here.” She slides her finger over an unchecked box and taps twice.

“Why not? Why didn’t she have one?” Agatha whispers, her voice tight.

“If there was nothing to indicate foul play and no family to push for one, they probably just didn’t do it.” Rio shakes her head and Alice’s face gets redder, her fists curling tighter on the table.

“Why was the casket closed, then? At the memorial?” Alice eventually asks, just as Agatha flips the final page of notes over and finds a few more lines of scribbled information.

Her eyes flick over them and her stomach churns uncomfortably.

“Strange, unexplainable discoloration on the body,” she reads aloud even as her voice threatens to crack and shatter. “No family present, community advised to proceed with no viewing.”

No family present.

The words are another nail in Agatha’s coffin. They push her further underground, deeper into soil saturated with guilt and pain.

“What does that even mean?” Billy asks suddenly and Agatha’s head shoots up. She had nearly forgotten he was there. “Unexplainable discoloration?”

“Billy,” Rio says almost placatingly. “Note-taking wasn’t as rigorous or detailed as it is now and my predecessor wasn’t exactly the most meticulous.” Rio’s tone, that of a mentor imparting useful wisdom, makes Agatha want to scream.

She wonders briefly why her mother’s file has to be the subject of Rio’s depressing teaching moment. She wonders morbidly if Billy, who seems to be some sort of mortician-in-training, will learn from this; from her mother’s cold case. The despair of it all feels astoundingly similar to that of the endless dead ends Agatha and her students often face in their research of long-forgotten individuals.

They sometimes encounter dozens of obstacles before finally landing on something concrete, something they can build upon. Typically, Agatha can handle it; she can face the disappointment head on and channel it into something useful, like determination.

Now though, she doesn’t feel that way at all. Perhaps because it’s not just a mysterious historical figure she’s investigating but her own mother. Perhaps it’s the Salem air around her, thick with old memories and crushed dreams.

”That’s it?” she asks, a roaring anger starting up behind her eyes. It dances at the edges of her mind, just waiting to be let in. She shoves her chair backwards so she can stand, flipping through the pages again. The edges are frayed with age and dipped in a thin layer of dust. Rio’s warmth next to her disappears in a rush.

Her mother’s cause of death, listed as simply natural causes, makes Agatha feel sick. She barely notices as Alice points Billy out of the room with a shake of her head. He quietly slips out, eyes wide and curious.

“Where is the mortician? The one who wrote this?” Agatha lifts the folder and turns to Rio, her eyes blazing.

“He’s dead.” The words leave Rio’s lips quickly and easily, like she hadn’t considered they would be devastating to Agatha’s ears.

They are.

Agatha slumps back in her chair, the roaring in her head fighting to absorb any coherent thought. She knows logically that this isn’t the end, that there are more leads to follow and stones to turn over, but she feels suddenly, absurdly, like the world is ending.

She blames Salem, the place she grew up.

She blames Alice and Jen, the closest friends she has ever known.

She even blames Evanora, her own (dead) mother.

Anything to keep from blaming herself.

“ Agatha .” Rio’s voice is low enough that Jen and Alice don’t notice, too preoccupied rereading the report and debating their next steps. They are as devastated by this lack of information as Rio—presumably because they gave up hope long ago.

“Look at me.” Rio speaks and Agatha’s eyes fly open. She is commanded by her and it is glorious; to give up control, to defer to someone else for the first time in her life. Agatha thinks, briefly and dangerously, that she would do anything Rio asked her to. The roaring anger in her mind quiets a bit.

Distraction.

Rio’s ringless hand hovers just inches from her arm, like she yearns to touch but thinks better of it. Agatha wonders if her touch would set them both afire, consuming and burning and ending . The snow outside would melt away like a mere annoyance . The thought is not as terrifying as it should be.

It certainly feels like Rio could do something like that with her hands, something grand and unfathomable. Agatha wonders if their slight collision would burn the building down—the entire town.

Rio tilts her head, a smirk dancing on her lips and Agatha only briefly thinks that it’s absurd, to be smirking in a situation like this, to be wondering about Rio’s electric touch as she gazes at the record of her mother’s death, but then Alice stands abruptly.

She slams her palms against the wooden table.

“We’re not done just yet.”

Jen nods, Rio withdraws her hand, and Agatha sighs.

The three of them do eventually leave Rio’s small office, the mortician not-so-subtly kicking them out because she has work to do . It’s not like their visit was much help, anyway. Regardless, Alice had pulled her phone out and scanned the mortician’s report quickly before they left, ensuring they had a copy if they needed it.

“We should talk to Lilia,” Jen says, her heels clicking against the pavement. “She’s the next person on my list.”

“Lunch first?” Alice asks, her eyes going comically wide. Agatha nearly laughs at the sight. Jen just nods indulgently and climbs into the driver’s seat.

Agatha turns, watching Rio shake the hand of an older man, his eyes red and his face splotchy. She gently pats his arm and leads him down the hallway, her eyes briefly flicking up to meet Agatha’s through the window, apologetic and searching. She can’t even bring herself to be mad at Rio. It’s not her fault the person who worked here before her kept awful notes and then died.

“Who is Lilia?” Agatha asks, when her brain finally moves on from Rio. Her form disappears down the dark hallway and Agatha reluctantly pulls the car door open, dragging herself away from Rio’s magnetic pull.

“She runs a business across town. She’s sort of a multitalented psychic: palm readings, tarot, fortune telling, that kind of stuff. She’s very popular with the tourists.” Alice supplies, a grimace crossing her face when she speaks the last part. Agatha isn’t surprised by it.

Growing up in Salem, particularly during the fall months, the three of them often felt like unwilling participants in an insatiable circus. They were ogled at and photographed by crowds of tourists who wondered if they were secretly witches, seconds away from cursing everyone in a ten mile radius. Agatha sometimes wished she could have.

“Did Evanora know her?” Agatha asks, wondering why her mother would associate with a tourist-loving psychic.

“Neither of us really spoke to your mother after you left, but we did see her at Lilia’s from time to time,” Jen says.

“Maybe she was having a reading done,” Alice adds with a snort.

Agatha rolls her eyes. The image of her mother, never one to believe in fate or destiny, having her palm read sparks a bubble of laughter in her chest. It seems extremely out of character but, then again, Evanora had lived for many years after Agatha left—perhaps she changed. The idea is an insane one.

“Lilia is great,” Jen says fondly. “She’s one of my best customers.” She smiles brightly, perfectly white teeth contrasting against flawless warm skin.

Agatha knows from a quick internet search she did upon her arrival in Salem that Jen pursued the natural skincare business she’d always dreamed of as a teenager. Decades ago, Alice and Agatha were her test subjects for new—and certainly flawed—products. Once, Alice’s skin had been accidentally dyed purple for a week after Jen’s newest ingredient proved too pigmented.

“She helped the police out with a case once, a criminal who confessed everything to her under the guise of a fortune telling. She had just moved here and didn’t know anyone. We’ve been friends ever since,” Alice says with a soft smile.

“She’s like your pseudo-mom,” Jen adds with a chuckle. “She’s even done tarot readings for the both of us.”

“She predicted our wedding before we even started dating!” Alice smiles over at Jen and Agatha’s heart twists in her chest.

She feels like she’s stepped into something that she no longer has any claim to. Salem may have belonged to her once, when she was young and as joyful as anyone could expect her to be, but it no longer does. Alice and Jen have moved on, contact with each other and their new friends. They’re happy , which is more than Agatha can say for where she ended up. For a moment, she allows herself the freedom to imagine what could have been.

In another world, in an alternate timeline, she would have met Rio the moment she moved to Salem. They would’ve danced around each other for weeks or months, teasing and flirting and eventually crashing together.

She would have met Lilia and Billy, learning to love the fortune teller and the strange kid that interns with Rio. She would have become familiar with Jen’s endless facial serums and stood at the front of the venue during her two best friends’ wedding, maid of honor for whichever friend claimed her first.

She would have been here for her mother’s funeral, to investigate her death and lay her to rest, even if Evanora didn’t quite deserve it.

Agatha shakes her head, pushing the depressing thoughts away as they arrive at a brick building. White letters above the door advertise its name as Witchside Tavern. Agatha rolls her eyes and Alice sticks her tongue out in response.

“Trust us, Agatha. It’s good.” Jen says, ending the fight before it can begin.

Agatha, as much as she would like to disagree, can’t. The food is quite good, despite the overdone and obviously witchy references. The place is warm and cozy and Agatha finds herself charmed and a bit more at ease than she was earlier.

Nearly an hour later and their trio has resumed their mission. Turns out, Lilia’s business is in an old strip mall. Between a nail salon and pool supply store, Madame Calderu's Psychic Readings is printed on a bright yellow sign.

Most of the mall is dull, all brick and neutral tones, but Lilia’s storefront has been painted light blue. Bits of color jump out all across the facade and small plants are stacked up outside. Multicolored garland, made of vibrant paper and fabric, hangs just below the sign. It advertises tarot, palm readings, and a crystal ball.

“Alice! Jen!”

Success, happiness, radiance , her brain supplies.

Lilia does not take her offered hand.

She stares at Agatha instead.

Suddenly, the quietest sounds around Agatha seem magnified: the buzzing of the lights overheard, Alice’s footsteps as she paces the room, and the chatter of teenagers just outside the glass door. Agatha isn’t a big fan of fate. She typically refuses to believe that anyone or anything holds that much power over her, but she can still hear the faint echoes of Lilia’s words, as if they’re sinking into the walls around her and the wood beneath her feet.

There’s a collage of artwork surrounding them in the narrow hallway, ancient paintings of witches, magic, rituals, and sacrifices. They’re all done in vibrant colors and hung in ornate frames. Each one stands out amongst dangling strings of beads and flowy fabric.

“Why not? There are much weirder things in Salem.” Jen shrugs. Lilia and Alice’s voices get louder as they turn a corner. “Like you,” Jen quips after a moment. She smirks gleefully at Agatha, who punches her arm in response.

Suddenly, she understands why people believe it—Lilia’s charade. It’s all very convincing. Her table is covered in crystal balls and stacks of tarot cards. The lighting, all warm and shadowy, casts her face in various hues of orange. Lilia’s large golden jewelry glows and she seems perfectly content when she rests her hands on a crystal ball and glances directly up at Agatha.

“I’m here because—”

“I did.” Her words nearly take the wind out of Agatha, squeezing her lungs and heart like a vice. Lilia did know Evanora.

Alice and Jen were right—but why? What use would Evanora Harkness have for a maybe-psychic? Agatha’s mother didn’t befriend people without reason. She didn’t even speak to anyone who couldn’t be of some use to her, including her own daughter.

“What advice could you have possibly given her?” Agatha throws her hands up. “My mother wouldn’t have been caught dead with a psychic!” Her voice rises and rises, her attempt at a calm and unflappable facade obviously fruitless. Lilia suddenly looks much older, the wrinkles in the corners of her eyes more pronounced. Her shoulders dip and when she looks back up at Agatha there is no malice there—there is something more akin to pity haunting her expression.

“Perhaps, my dear, you did not know her as well as you thought,” Lilia whispers. Agatha’s mouth drops open with a click.

She hears Alice stand up, but she never sees what she does.

She’s already gone.

She saw a therapist once, right after she left Salem, who said she had problems with transference. As much as she had hated him and his infuriating opinions, she knows he was probably right. She’s angry at her mother for being awful and, quite frankly, for dying, and she’s taking it out on everyone else. That doesn’t make any of it less real, though, or less painful. Agatha digs her heels into the snow outside and sets off down the street.

Regardless of her own issues, she does think Lilia really is quite infuriating.

Why couldn’t she just be helpful? Why did she have to imply that Agatha, who lived with Evanora for eighteen years, who bore the brunt of her hatred and her love, didn’t truly know her? Agatha thinks that perhaps she knew her better than anyone. A daughter who knew the real her, the woman who appeared when the doors were locked and the prying eyes of their neighbors were gone.

Agatha breathes harder, barely noticing the familiar buildings she passes as she walks: the bookstore she used to curl up in on the weekends, the Chinese restaurant where she would pile into a tiny booth with Alice and Jen after school, and the large stone church that her mother dragged her to every Sunday. The sky isn’t dark yet, the sun hovering still above the horizon, and Agatha briefly wishes it would drop lower and plunge everything into darkness.

She glances up and down the street. The center of Salem is almost eerily quiet, the only noise coming from a few teenagers standing outside a coffee shop. Large takeout cups are clutched in their hands as they laugh and gossip. Agatha also sees a couple pressed together on a bench, their cold hands intertwined. She supposes most people are bundled up indoors, enjoying a weekend inside with their families.

When Agatha slips past the iron gate, ducking under the large metal chain, she’s reminded of dozens of late nights growing up when she did exactly the same thing. She would sneak back from Jen’s house in the middle of the night, creeping up the stairs to avoid waking her mother. Evanora couldn’t care less where she had been but would certainly yell at her anyway.

Now, though, Agatha doesn’t have to be careful or quiet or moderated. She lets her feet slam against the driveway, each step sending pieces of her rage into the earth. It takes her a moment to get the key in the lock, her hands shaking from either the cold or anger—Agatha isn’t sure which. She yanks the front door open, letting it slam against the wall as she steps into the house, and then she freezes.

Rio.

For a moment, Agatha isn’t sure what to say, but then Rio raises her eyebrows slightly and the words are there as if they always have been, tumbling out to land at her feet. It doesn’t feel like her walls are being lowered or her armor is being pierced, but more like there was never a wall to begin with—like there never has been.

“She said that I…” Agatha swallows. “That I didn’t know her.”

Rio nods. It seems like she knows this information already but she doesn’t say anything. She just nods, stepping forward to close the door behind Agatha and slip her coat from her shoulders. Then, she draws Agatha’s hands into her own.

Agatha feels their warmth spreading from her palms and fingertips up her arms, warming her to her core. The touch sends sparks up Agatha’s entire body, her anger burning away with each rub of Rio’s thumb against her wrist. It cracks something open inside of her, something frozen and jagged and painful.

“I did,” Agatha says, her lip trembling dangerously.

She wishes the anger would come back. She misses the violent rage that has kept her upright and determined for so much of her life, is suddenly desperate for it. No one has ever taken it away before, not like this.

Rio is quiet for a long time, standing still and silent as Agatha’s rage leeches from her slowly and the tears in her eyes are blinked back. It all melts away, unable to exist in the presence of Rio’s gentle touch, of her warm breathing so close to Agatha’s neck.

“I believe you,” she eventually says, once Agatha’s breathing has reduced to gentle puffs instead of great, labored gasps. “That you knew her.”

That is something. Agatha looks up at her, tilting her head just enough so their eyes are level.

“Did you break in?” She asks suddenly, never withdrawing her hands from Rio’s grasp. She continues to run her fingertips along Agatha’s skin, even though they’re no longer cold.

“Yes.” Rio smirks, her eyes crinkling in the corners and Agatha nearly gasps at that revelation, at the tiny details of Rio’s face that she can see from this close. “Is that okay?”

Agatha laughs then, really laughs. She forgets about the stupid mortician’s report and Lilia’s infuriating words and Alice’s pleading gaze. Her head drops forward, landing on Rio’s shoulder. If anyone ever asks, she will swear it’s an accident.

It’s not.

Ghosts of Flowers Blooming

Agatha Harkness loves a good distraction.

She isn’t sure she could’ve survived this long without them, honestly. When she left Salem, the only thing that kept her sane was burying herself in her research, spending hours scouring over niche documents and digging through forgotten archives.

Every year when the holidays roll around, when Agatha’s old-school calendar tragically flips to December, she continues this pattern. She distracts herself with her students and her work, shutting out the unwelcome world around her.

She hates and hates and hates , drawing it over herself like a protective shield. Then, she proceeds to distract herself from the subject of her hatred.

Unfortunately, there’s a problem with distractions: they don’t work forever. Eventually—inevitably—they fall away, and there’s nothing you can do to stop it.

Agatha wakes slowly.

Sounds reach her first: the dull humming of the heater that she grew familiar with in childhood and the sharp click of the clock down the hall. She had hated that thing when she was younger, tossing and turning restlessly at night as it ticked away and taunted her.

Memories of the previous day begin to float back to her, pulling through the fog of sleep. Agatha recalls the failed searching for clues regarding her mothers death, her angry trek through the aftermath of Salem’s most recent snow, and—

Oh.

Gradually, Agatha realizes where exactly she is and, more importantly, who she’s with. Her hair is falling over her eyes, her chest pressed against something warm and solid and… breathing?

The memories she’s searching for return to her in a rush, slightly fuzzy and magnified.

Rio, wrapping her arms around Agatha’s shaking form. Drawing her in and whispering in her ear, pressing warm lips against her temple.

Rio, guiding her to the couch and shoving a takeout box in her hands, turning on some mindless Christmas movie that Agatha had despised.

Rio, lips stained with red wine, falling asleep against her side.

Agatha cracks her eyes open and comes face to face with the subject of her memories, fast asleep and snoring lightly. Her eyelashes flutter as Agatha exhales. She’s gorgeous. That’s all Agatha can think, all her brain can manage. She fights the urge to lift her hand and trace Rio’s face, run her fingertips along her sharp jaw and soft lips.

She wonders what they would feel like.

That would certainly be inappropriate and so Agatha ignores the urge, glancing at the clock on the mantle—it’s nearly nine. She should probably get up, have a shower, and call Alice and Jen. Apologizing for leaving them at Lilia’s would be a nice thing to do, even if Agatha hates the idea of it. An apology to Lilia is likely in order as well, but Agatha hates the idea of that one even more. Apologizing isn’t exactly her forte.

All of that is nearly impossible, though, because she’s practically on top of Rio. The two of them are twined together on the couch with Agatha’s head planted on Rio’s chest. Agatha briefly contemplates trying to get up, extricating herself from Rio’s warm arms which circle her completely, but it only takes a few seconds for her to give up.

She resigns herself to her fate and does her best to ignore the sharp panic that burns in her stomach as she comes to that realization. The last calming effects of sleep melt away. Agatha isn’t sure how they ended up like this and the proximity is enough to make her nervous, even though she has no real reason to be.

The last thing she remembers is watching Rio fall asleep and then turning the movie off. Apparently, she fell asleep too, and the two of them somehow gravitated together in their sleep. Empty wine glasses are balanced on the coffee table along with discarded takeout boxes that Rio had brought with her.

Agatha didn't ask Rio why she showed up at her house, too pleased with the outcome to question it, but she’s smart enough to know that it was Jen, or Alice, or perhaps both of them. They always looked out for her when they were younger. It seems that even decades later, this hasn’t changed.

This time, though, it was Rio they sent to coax Agatha away from the darkness.

Rio, who is currently waking up. Her eyelids flutter softly and then fly open. She seems alarmed for a moment, her body tensing, until she meets Agatha’s gaze and smiles sleepily.

“Morning,” she murmurs, her voice low and raspy. Agatha bites her lip, waiting for Rio to realize what’s happening and push her away, to reject her proximity.

She doesn’t.

Her eyes flicker shut and, if possible, she seems to pull Agatha closer, her hands reaching out to run up and down her back. Agatha shivers, actually trembles at the contact and then immediately wishes she hadn’t, pressing her burning cheeks against Rio’s chest.

Agatha has been alone for years; she’s used to it. Occasionally, she gives into her coworkers’ lousy attempts at peer pressure and goes on a blind date or two, but she’s never felt anything like this, a need that could burn her from the inside out with its intensity.

Her ex-girlfriend Wanda is the closest she’s ever gotten to the fairy tale and although Agatha thinks that anyone else would have been content with what they had, she just wasn’t. They met in college, all cautious looks and breathless firsts, and stayed together for years.

Wanda was comfortable and safe and after a while, Agatha wasn’t in love with her anymore. She honestly isn’t sure if she ever was. Sometimes (oftentimes) the thought keeps her up at night.

Perhaps she’s just cursed to abandon everyone she loves.

But Rio is different—she’s full of racing pulses and challenging smirks. She feels like standing at the edge of a cliff and staring at the bottom, like hovering your hand inches from a raging fire.

Agatha is terrified and Rio is, unhelpfully, doing awful things to her blood pressure.

“Relax, Agatha.” Rio’s voice is lighter now and Agatha internally curses herself for being so obvious, for letting her anxiety show so clearly. “I’m not gonna stab you.”

Rio chuckles when she says it, which sends a rush of relief through Agatha’s mind—Rio isn’t freaked out. Agatha forces herself to smile back despite the mention of stabbing. She finds it charming, actually, that Rio knows exactly what to say to soothe her racing mind.

Agatha Harkness is charmed. Isn’t that something?

She would very much like to avoid thinking about how charmed she is by Rio whilst on top of Rio.

“I do.” Rio smiles brilliantly. Being the subject of that joy, that perfection, makes Agatha’s head spin and she has to abandon her study of the ceiling. “I have a garden.” Her fingers move up Agatha’s back, sliding into her hair unannounced.

“Oh.” Agatha gasps. She rushes to push her thoughts away from Rio’s touch, from the soft movement of her hand as it cards through her hair and the feeling of her legs wrapped around hers. “What kind of… what do you grow?” She stumbles over the words.

Rio opens her mouth to reply, a smirk already ghosting across her lips, but she’s rudely interrupted. Agatha immediately recognizes her ringtone and wants to scream. The ringing is coming from somewhere below the two of them, slightly muffled but still annoying.

Her quick movements blow up a layer of previously undisturbed dust that leaves her, unfortunately, a sneezing mess. She can’t hear much from her position half underneath the couch, but she’s pretty sure Rio is laughing.

She blinks a couple times, trying to clear the dust from her vision, and is left staring at an obviously used piece of white chalk. One end is worn down from use, cracked and unevenly shaved. She rolls it between her fingers, soft powder falling on her palm.

Weird.

“We’re on our way!” Alice’s voice is loud in her ear and Agatha winces.

Rio laughs from her position on the couch, obviously able to hear Alice through the phone. She rolls the piece of chalk back and forth in her palms.

“What?”

Agatha curses every god she’s ever known and every deity that’s ever been worshipped and Salem itself for throwing her into the orbit of Rio Vidal.

“Alice, I don't need a tree,” Agatha corrects sternly. “I don’t even really celebrate Christmas.” She pinches the bridge of her nose as she contemplates how exactly she’ll get out of this.

Once, when she was a kid, she got lost in the maze of impossibly tall firs and screamed for what felt like hours until a poor employee discovered her. Her mother was probably off somewhere charming an employee and securing the most picturesque tree in the entire lot.

Evanora hadn’t put a lot of stock in the “childish value” of Christmas, presents and joy and miracles, but she did care about making sure their house was the most elaborately decorated one in all of Salem. Agatha is, therefore, not a huge fan of Christmas tree farms, but she finds that Rio’s smile, and the promise of making her smile even more, is enough to make her want to visit one anyways.

She struggles to put her feelings into tangible words, struggles to say that when she goes to a Christmas party or sees the first hints of snow falling outside, she feels thirteen again, unable to please her mother when all she wanted was her love.

Agatha doesn’t like to think about the holidays, about happy families and ornate trees and snow angels—all of the things she never had. It’s too painful and so she hates it all instead. She wears her hatred much like her anger, armor standing between her and the emotions she most fears.

There’s nothing to do, nothing that can heal eighteen years worth of scars. Agatha knows she could refuse to answer Rio, could turn away and leave but perhaps that would hurt even worse than this.

The memories are there without intention, they are breaking through the surface despite Agatha’s insistence they stay buried. Evanora, changing her hair and clothes and teaching her to hate herself all in the name of fixing her.

“Toothbrush?” Rio asks again, trying and failing to smooth down her hair in the mirror above the sink.

“Oh, yeah. In the cabinet.” Agatha mumbles, pressing a hand against her eyes as the bathroom door clicks shut. She hears the shower start.

Agatha knows she should appreciate what Rio is trying to do but, suddenly, the room seems much smaller. She isn’t built for this—for holiday joy and festive cheer. Those things are for people who had happy, normal childhoods. She can pretend all she wants, wake up with Rio and throw pillows at her and laugh with her friends, but it will never be real.

She hears Evanora’s familiar voice in her head, always lurking in the depths of her darkest thoughts. It wraps around her insides and squeezes; it sits on her chest and weighs her down like a ton of bricks.

You don’t deserve it.

You were born evil—cursed.

Agatha’s head jerks up, her gaze immediately narrowing at Jen’s words. Her stomach drops out with a swoop.

Difficult.

“Why won’t Lilia just tell us what she knows?” Agatha asks, doing her best to project a facade of anger despite the roaring pain inside her. She balls her hands into fists at her sides.

“Lilia has a… way about these things,” Jen says carefully. “She doesn't tell you anything until you’re ready to hear it.”

“And I’m not ready?” Agatha asks. She tries to sound angry, she really does.

“Apparently not,” Jen whispers, and Agatha swears she looks upset by that, too. “But that doesn’t mean we’ll stop trying. We will find out what happened to her.” Jen looks up, her eyes suddenly full of fire.

Agatha nods, unsure what to say, and then Jen is pushing past her. Her heels clack against the wooden floor, Just as the sound begins to recede, Alice’s head appears from around the side of the car.

“Is it safe?” she asks with a laugh, balancing several boxes and bags in her arms. When Agatha nods, unable to force herself to smile, Alice lets out a comical sigh of relief. She starts to follow Jen into the house but pauses when she sees the look on Agatha’s face.

“Are you alright?” she asks tentatively, pulling her scarf closer to her neck as a gust of wind hits her. Agatha debates not answering, pushing the emotion out of her eyes and pretending to be fine, but she knows Alice will keep pushing.

“Do you think that I’m…” Agatha takes a deep breath, choosing her words carefully. Images flash through her mind. Rio, laughing and assuring her that she can rewrite her memories and Jen, telling her that Lilia’s words must have been difficult to hear. They push down on her like a slab of ice, cutting and freezing and suffocating. “Cursed?”

Agatha expects Alice to laugh fondly, to tell her that she’s being irrational and that of course she’s not cursed, but Alice doesn’t. Her eyes seem to darken, like someone has sucked the life out of them. It suddenly seems much colder outside despite the already low temperature.

“No, Agatha.” Alice’s voice is suddenly thick with unshed tears and Agatha’s breath catches in her throat. “Why would you think that?” Alice looks up at her and her expression, so devastated, compels Agatha to speak.

“I’ve spent so long running from happiness, Alice.” She gulps. “Because I didn’t…”

“You didn’t think you deserved it,” Alice finishes and Agatha nods, thankful for the words that she doesn’t have to say.

“But now I think that I could have it. With you and Jen, with….” Agatha just barely stops herself from saying Rio’s name but. “I’m terrified to give myself back up to this place. It feels like defeat.”

Alice shakes her head firmly. “It wasn’t Salem that hurt you, Agatha. You never did understand that.”

Something seems to change in her expression, a resolution appearing that Agatha doesn’t yet understand. Alice clenches her jaw and reaches around Agatha, pulling the door shut. She drops the boxes she was carrying and with a deep breath, keeps talking.

“I remember that day, the week before you left. You told me that this town would eat you alive but you were wrong—it wasn’t this place . It was never the soil or the trees or the sun that caused you pain.” The trees seem to shake around them, the wind howling against their ears. Agatha swears the ground moves beneath her feet.

It's as if Salem can hear Alice’s words.

Agatha opens her mouth to respond, anger flaring in her chest, but Alice keeps going, plowing ahead with a rush of hurriedly spoken words.

“I understand why you thought that, I really do. It’s easier to run from a place than a person. You thought that you could leave all of your problems behind in Salem, turn your back on them and move on but, Agatha , that’s not how it works.”

Agatha collapses into one of the porch chairs. She reaches out with a cold hand to one of the bushes beside the porch. There were flowers here, once, when she was a child. They’re all dead now. She closes her hand around one.

“When you left, you just took your ghosts with you,” Alice continues. Her words are harsh but somehow also kind and Agatha knows, suddenly, that she’s imagined this conversation before. “Do you know how scared I was last night? That you left again. ”

Agatha squeezes her eyes shut and wishes that Alice would stop talking, would stop her quick and violent examination of her most desperate actions.

“I didn’t know, Alice,” she chokes out. She gasps for air even though Alice is the one panting and raving, her hands gesturing wildly around them. Alice had been scared, terrified that her friend would abandon her again, and the thought nearly splits Agatha open.

“I know that if you want to leave, I can’t stop you, but don’t leave under the assumption that your pain will stay here,” Alice says as she stops pacing. Her hands close tight against the railing of the porch. “You think that if you solve your mother’s death, if you find out what happened, it’ll hurt less but it won’t. You can’t love people, or even hate them, and then expect to erase them from your life.”

Alice starts speaking faster, her breath fogging up the cold air in front of her.

“You can lie and tell me that you forgot us, that you never thought about me, or Jen, but I won’t believe you. Because I thought about you every day .” She’s nearly crying now, tears brimming in her eyes. Agatha can’t bring herself to meet them, staring at her boots instead.

Alice kneels in front of her, forcing her to make eye contact.

“Agatha, we love you,” Alice whispers reverently. There is no room in her tone for disbelief or disagreement, but Agatha shakes her head anyway, fighting against the burning in her eyes. “You’re allowed to be happy—even here. Evanora is dead.” Agatha hears the unspoken words, the silent implication that if Salem wasn’t what hurt her, Evanora was, and Evanora is gone.

“This place,” Alice continues, glancing up at the house. ”It’s not hers anymore. It can be yours, if you want it.”

Agatha wants to argue, to yell that Salem is cursed and awful but she can’t. She remembers Rio’s words from yesterday, her suggestion that perhaps Agatha has been yearning to return to Salem all this time and she knows, with a rush of clarity that makes her dizzy, that they’re right.

She’s been running for years in the name of freedom, fleeing from her mother and her family and her worst mistakes, except she wasn’t really free at all. They were there still, in broken glass on the floor and in snowflakes falling outside. They lived in the shadows of her apartment and the flames of her fireplace.

Alice is right. She took her ghosts with her.

The two of them sit together, shivering on the porch as Alice’s breathing slows. Agatha stares at the dead flower still clutched in her hand, so tight her knuckles are white. She twirls it around and jerks back when the wind catches it, ripping it from her grasp and tossing it across the melting snow. Finally, she speaks.

“I don’t know how.”

“To be happy? To not let the past destroy you?” Alice asks, sounding even more heartbroken than she had before. Agatha nods. “Just try. Stay and face your demons—don’t run from them, from us .”

“I can try,” Agatha replies, and the effort of speaking moves her even closer to tears. She rubs a hand over her chest and finds that it physically hurts, like someone is trying to rip her heart out. “I’m sorry, Alice.”

They are words Agatha never imagined she would say, never thought she would have the opportunity to speak, and they threaten to slice open her tongue. She forces them out anyway. Abruptly looking away from Alice, she brushes the tears from her cheeks with the back of her hand. She is sorry—that she left, that she came back, and that she wants to leave again.

Before she can turn back and gauge Alice’s reaction, there are arms around her, strong and sure and trembling.

“I know,” Alice says, her voice muffled in Agatha’s hair. “I don’t blame you, I really don’t. I know it was awful for you here, but it doesn’t have to be anymore.”

Agatha tries to believe her. Against all evidence and better judgement, she wraps her arms tighter around her friend and tries to believe that she might be happy, that she can be. When Alice pulls away, her eyes are red and her face is splotchy.

Agatha turns her head, avoiding the strong, persistent sun looming over them (and the bright spots it will inevitably leave against the back of her eyelids). She spots the flower lying amongst the snow, the one the wind took from her hand just moments before.

It’s blooming.

To Have Perfect Faith

Rio drives a truck.

This shouldn’t be shocking news, really, but Agatha is fresh off the most gut-wrenching conversation she’s had since Jen slapped her and told her that Evanora was dead, so she thinks she’s allowed a little shock.

Absurdly, she wants to laugh; wants to throw herself down upon the snow and freeze away the ache in her chest at the revelation. That’s not quite possible, though, because Rio is looking at her with this expression on her face. Her eyebrows are drawn and her mouth is tilted down—like she’s sad.

Agatha doesn’t understand it and wouldn’t even know where to start, so she just pulls open the passenger door of Rio’s dark green pickup truck and climbs in.

They get about five minutes into the drive when Rio, who’s been tapping her hands nervously against the steering wheel, finally speaks.

“What did you and Alice talk about?” She doesn’t look at Agatha, like she’s afraid of her reaction.

“Salem,” Agatha responds simply. She’s so used to giving one word answers, to concealing her true feelings, that it comes naturally. She traces circles in the condensation on the window, watching as the familiar buildings and shops fly past.

Only when Rio doesn’t say anything else, just continuing her anxious drumming, does Agatha realize she’s waiting for her to say more. It goes against all of her self preservation instincts and raises her heart rate just a bit, but Agatha thinks about Alice, pleading with her to trust them, trust someone , and she continues.

“When I grew up, I thought that everything bad in my life was because of this place,” she whispers. “That it was fundamentally evil, or bad, or something.” The words hurt less than they would have an hour ago. Agatha is already scraped raw, all of her worst words drawn out and exposed.

“That’s why you left?” Rio asks, eyebrows dancing higher even as her eyes stay glued to the road. Agatha cringes internally; of course Rio knows about that. She’s certainly been close with Alice and Jen long enough to hear about their legendary friend who fled the state at eighteen and never looked back. Agatha imagines it’s quite a story—she’d probably like to hear it if it wasn’t about her.

“It’s why I did a lot of things.” It feels like an understatement, but it’s certainly true. “And now it’s coming back to bite me…” Agatha scrubs a hand over her face, remembering her earlier conversation with Jen. She had assured her that they would find out what happened to Evanora, but Agatha isn’t feeling confident.

“You do the calling, remember?” Rio quips, and it takes Agatha a moment to realize what she’s referring to, what conversation she’s referencing.

You don’t seem like the type of person who is called—more like someone who does the calling.

“I can call all I want, Rio.” Agatha jokes unconvincingly. “I can scream into an empty void for hours but what if there’s nothing? Nothing else to find? What if I can’t—”

Agatha dips her head, dangerously shielded insecurities bursting to life on her tongue, flowing from her lips unobstructed: that she will leave Salem with fewer answers than she arrived with, that all of her training will fail her here, that even in her mother’s death, she is not enough for her.

“Agatha, do you truly fear what will happen if you find nothing?” Rio’s words are soft, but they are still deadly. “You’ll go back to your old life, professorship and legacy in tow. You’ll forget about all of this.”

Rio waves a ringless hand in the air, the other tight on the steering wheel. Agatha stares. The mysterious woman in front of her, the infuriatingly attractive mortician who implied that she was called to meet Agatha, may be right about a lot of things but she is wrong about one: Agatha could never forget this.

“I wonder”—Rio smirks, and Agatha nearly dies—“what will happen if you do find something?” Agatha considers with a jolt that there are things here, buried six feet deep in the haunted soil of Salem, that she might not want to find.

The dead don’t often tell you what you want to hear, after all.

“If it’s worth anything, I think this place is magical,” Rio continues.

Agatha fights the sudden urge to laugh, stomps it down inside of her when Alice’s tearful voice resounds in her head: it wasn’t Salem that hurt you. She wonders about Rio’s word choice; magical isn’t a word used lightly in Salem, not with its history.

“Tell me, then.” Agatha turns to face Rio, even though she can’t look back. She admires her side profile distractedly. “Tell me why it’s magical.”

It’s a plea, of sorts. Agatha wants to understand—she wants to see Salem as something other than what Evanora made it. Rio slows for a stop light and turns to face Agatha.

“It’s where I met you,” she says lightly, that smirk that Agatha has grown so familiar with twisting on her mouth. Her eyes seem to glow, all warm and hazel and Agatha swears she can see a bit of green pooling at the edges of her irises. Rio looks away after a few seconds, turning onto a sidestreet as the light switches.

They don’t speak for the rest of the drive. Every time Agatha attempts to say something, opens her mouth and tries to form syllables, she thinks better of it and bites her tongue. There is no response, really, not to that; being told that you make a place magical, a place you’ve hated and despised for decades.

Agatha jolts out of her thoughts as the truck slows. She peers out the window at a large side, wooden and decorated with dozens of tiny trees. It reads: Seven Sisters Christmas Tree Farm. There’s a little stand outside serving cocoa and cookies and the entire place looks like it appeared straight out of a holiday rom-com. As Agatha climbs out of the truck, Rio appearing at her side, she notices that the cookies are decorated as witch hats adorned with wreaths.

She rolls her eyes and drags Rio in the opposite direction, not even realizing that she’s grabbed her hand until they’re disappearing into the rows of tall trees. She tries to pull away, to flush with embarrassment and pretend it didn’t happen, but Rio holds tight.

When Agatha turns to glare at her, Rio doesn’t meet her eyes, only smirking and making a bee-line for the hot cocoa stand that Agatha had just attempted to avoid. Agatha groans and pulls against her hand, but she doesn’t put any real force into it.

Moments later, a beaming Rio shoves a stack of cookies and a cup of hot chocolate into her hands.

“Okay, stick with me here,” Rio says once they’ve secured their snacks. She excitedly points between two different display trees. “ Abies fraseri or Pseudotsuga menziesii ?”

Agatha shakes her head and can’t even be shocked. Of course Rio knows the scientific names of Christmas tree species.

“They look the same to me,” Agatha deadpans, clutching her cup of cocoa like a lifeline.

Rio tosses the reminder of her cookie in her mouth and snatches Agatha’s hand. She then proceeds to excitedly drag them around the entire farm, stopping and considering each impressive tree they stumble across.

Agatha gives up on trying to keep track of their location about twenty minutes in, when Rio leads them through a row of trees because she thought she saw a bunny. Agatha thinks there are still needles in her hair. She also gave up on trying to get her hand back a while ago, secretly happy to let Rio keep it.

“How can I help the happy couple today?” An employee appears out of nowhere, his red polo displaying the name Eddie. He smiles at them, his eyes seemingly stuck on their joined hands.

“Oh we’re not—” Agatha starts.

“Looking for help!” Rio interjects. She smiles and pulls Agatha closer, wrapping an arm around her waist. “I have it handled. I’m an expert.”

Eddie seems skeptical but he nods anyway, leaving them alone and heading towards a family with two small children.

Agatha steps out of Rio’s arms with a scowl, ignoring the twinge of loss when she does. “You’re an expert?”

“Mortician by day, plant enthusiast by night,” Rio answers quickly, and Agatha just knows she says that often.

Agatha bites her lip to keep from laughing. She decides then to take their forced proximity as an opportunity to do some information gathering of her own; level the playing field after Rio so carefully extracted all of her worst fears. It’s not like she has much else to do as she follows Rio’s zigzagging pattern through rows of fir trees.

“What’s the connection there? Between dead people”—Agatha winches at her own crass language, which only evokes reminders of her dead mother—”and plants.”

“Life, Agatha,” Rio says, like it’s obvious. “They both live and die and breathe.”

She steps forward, running a hand over one of the trees like she can feel it breathing, like she can sense it living under her fingers. Her eyes slip closed, and Agatha rushes to change the subject.

“So, why did you move here? To Salem?” she asks casually, trying to ignore the strange, transfixed look on Rio’s face.

“I don’t know, really.” Lines appear across Rio’s forehead as she scrunches her face up as if she’s trying to solve a particularly difficult puzzle. “I went to graduate school a couple hours from here and always loved it. The eerie vibe really entranced me.” Rio waves a hand like she’s casting a spell, fingers fluttering on the wind, and Agatha smirks. “One day, I showed up and never left. It just…”

“Felt right?” Agatha blurts, unable to keep the words to herself.

“Yeah, exactly.” Rio’s head bobs up and down excitedly. “You get it?”

“I do,” Agatha replies, dipping her head so Rio can’t see the blush that spreads across her face like wildlife. “I’m familiar with the feeling.”

Rio pauses and then lifts a hand, her palm brushing lightly against the side of Agatha’s face. She feels like a raging fire, every inch of her skin burning and warming all at once, even in the cold. Agatha’s eyes slip closed of their own accord, and as much as she curses internally and screams at her body to recoil, it leans into the contact.

“Yeah, you would be,” Rio whispers. She shakes her head slightly and lets her hand drop away. The sudden loss of warmth forces Agatha’s eyes open but Rio is already gone, staring up at another tree. “It’s this one.”

“Whatever you say, sweetheart,” Agatha quips before she can stop herself. She falls into it easily, the banter they’ve been developing and reveling in for the past few days.

As soon as the words leave her, she presses a hand against her mouth and wishes a sinkhole would emerge beneath her feet and swallow her up. She hadn’t meant to say it, too caught up in the moment to bite her tongue. Rio’s touch and Rio’s words always seem to unsettle her. She makes the walls that Agatha so lovingly maintains willingly lower. Rio turns slowly, her eyes bright.

“ Sweetheart ?” she muses, stepping forward and smirking like she’s won some impossible prize.

“I was just teasing,” Agatha corrects sternly, trying to put her best professor face on. Rio tosses her head back and laughs, light and perfect.

“I don’t believe you”—Rio steps forward and leans in slightly, tipping forward until her face is inches from Agatha’s—“Professor.”

Agatha gasps, Rio’s lips so close to hers that she could simply lift her face and press them together. Then, Rio abruptly pulls away, Agatha’s hand once again tight in hers. She heads straight for Eddie, the young boy they spoke to earlier.

“I hate you,” Agatha grumbles, as Rio drags them away.

“You could never,” Rio whispers in her ear. She sounds completely certain.

Agatha drapes her arm out the window of Rio’s truck, tipping her head back and letting the cold air fill her lungs. She glances at the tree in the side mirror, an impressively large fir tied to the bed of Rio’s truck. Eddie helped them, making comments about how great of a couple they were the entire time. Agatha had briefly wanted to strangle him.

She’s desperately trying to convince herself it’s because he was wrong —and not because she wishes he was right.

“We’re almost there,” Rio informs her, turning the music down. Agatha tilts her head back and forth, squinting out the window at the blurry buildings disappearing behind them. Rio has a surprise for her; she told her so after they left the Christmas tree farm. Despite Agatha’s pleading and prodding, Rio hasn’t budged.

When the lighthouse comes into view, Agatha’s first thought is to wonder how Rio knows; how she knows that, as a child, Agatha would escape here to this quiet place on the water. She would sit, with a book or her thoughts, and stare at the waves. It comforted her when no one else could.

Rio pulls right up next to the lighthouse and turns her truck off.

“This is one of my favorite places in Salem,” she says, and Agatha thinks if it was possible for her to fall any harder for Rio than she already has, this is when that would happen. “I wanted to show you.”

Agatha’s heart skips a beat. Rio doesn’t know that she loved this place, that it was her sanctuary all those years ago. She just loves it, and wants to share it with her.

Rio climbs out, opening Agatha’s door and smirking at her—there’s something else, Agatha realizes. Rio has another surprise planned. Agatha doesn’t try to guess, certain that she could never figure it out, only following Rio up the path and rubbing her hands together in anticipation.

When they get to the base of the lighthouse Agatha’s mind whirls, briefly caught in between decades.

The time she spent here as a teenager is blending into her consciousness, along with memories of her tears and her laughs. The last time she was here was the night before she left Salem. She is certain of that, but when she pulls at the memory, it falls away like it doesn’t wish to be seen—like it’s hiding. It makes Agatha briefly uneasy but she pushes the feeling away, her eyes drawn to the familiar view.

She moves to sit on one of the rocks, the one she had always loved as a kid because it was the closest to the water. Birds soar and leap across the surface, twirling and twisting without a care. The sky is dark despite the early hour. Grey clouds are flitting back and forth across the sun, and it’s like a mosaic of light against the rocks, sunrays dancing and disappearing.

Agatha turns to look at Rio, her eyes widening when she presents Agatha with a picnic basket.

“Fancy lunch by the water?” Rio smiles shyly, like she’s afraid Agatha will turn her down. Agatha shakes her head; Rio has absolutely no idea, does she?

“Depends on what you brought,” she shoots back with a smirk, and Rio collapses onto the rock next to her. They sit in comfortable silence, sandwiches in hand, and watch the water. Rio even pulls out one of those battery-powered candles, resting it in between them like a makeshift dinner.

Agatha stares at Rio, at the soft smile on her face, and knows she can’t deny it anymore. The realization washes through her with a rush of clarity.

She’s falling in love with Rio Vidal.

She has been, she thinks, since the moment she first laid eyes on her, all sharp lines and soft skin, lounging in her garden and crashing into her life like she belonged there; Rio unapologetically entered her life with a smirk and a flower and Agatha doesn’t think she’ll ever be the same.

Eventually Agatha breaks the soft silence, unable to stop herself from requesting clarification, from confirming what she already knows. She’s always done well with more information, after all.

“Is this a date?”

Rio’s head shoots up. She returned the picnic basket to the truck a while ago, the two of them continuing to stare at the water like a scene from a cheesy romantic comedy. Agatha would roll her eyes if she wasn’t so captivated.

“Not yet,” Rio replies slowly.

She stands, Agatha’s eyes tracking her intently, and walks up to the door of the lighthouse; the one that's always locked to the public. Agatha knows this, because she tried to break into it once. She was young and clueless and had no luck. Rio doesn’t break it, though. She slips a rusty key into the lock. The door clicks and swings open easily.

She lifts her head to look at Agatha then, a shy smile on her lips. “I know a guy. A girl, actually.”

Rio leads Agatha up dozens of stairs, sliding the key back in her pocket and holding her phone flashlight out in front of her. Agatha follows silently, certain that any words she could say wouldn’t be enough.

When they finally reach the top, and Rio pushes the door open, Agatha feels like the world has shifted underneath her, like she’s stepped into an alternate universe—one where someone cares about her enough to give her this.

The view from the shore is beautiful but from up higher, it’s breathtaking. The water seems so much more alive and kinetic; the swaying of the trees is impossible to ignore. There are lanterns on the side of the lighthouse, warm light spilling from them in the overcast weather. Rio’s face looks dipped in sunlight, each sharp line of her face made that much more intense.

Agatha can hear their breathing, so much louder and more intrusive this close together. Everything else in the world seems completely silent. The crashing of the waves slows, the howling wind quiets, and the clouds stop moving.

The calm before the storm.

“You still don’t believe me, do you?” Rio asks, her eyes dark and narrowed. She tips her head back and closes her eyes, somehow completely unaffected by the cold air that whirls around them.

Agatha doesn’t know what she believes.

She rubs a hand over her eyes and stares out at the harbor, captivated by the waves that silently and violently crash against the surface.

“Believe what, Rio?” Agatha whispers. Her voice is quiet. Speaking loudly would feel wrong, like shattering the bubble of serenity that they’ve stumbled upon.

“That this place is special.” Rio waves a hand out over the water and Agatha briefly notes that her fingertips are trembling. “Magical, even.”

Agatha takes a deep breath. She turns her head, allowing herself to really look at Rio. Her eyes are shut, her eyelashes occasionally fluttering in the wind. She leans against the railing with no fear, like she’s certain it won’t give out and send her flailing to the ground. Agatha doesn’t know how to have that kind of faith—she’s certain she’s never had it before.

“How do you trust it?” She gestures to the railing, to the flimsy white fence that keeps them safely suspended above the ground, but she means something different entirely. Rio’s eyes open and she shifts, turning to face Agatha. All of her hair blows to one side and it’s incredibly charming, even though it has no reason to be.

For a moment, Rio seems unsure. She tilts her head side to side, drumming her fingers against the wood she’s no longer resting against.

“Can I show you?” Her voice has dropped lower than Agatha’s ever heard it and she knows what Rio is asking, like she knows the shade of her eyes and which ring she wears on each finger. Agatha knows, and there is no world, no timeline, no dimension, in which she says no.

She nods, hears Rio’s startled exhale, and then Rio is all she can see, all she can feel .

Her hands, somehow burning hot, immediately find their place at the base of Agatha’s skull, pushing and pulling at the same time. She steps closer and closer until there is no room left between them, until Agatha’s back is pressed into the railing of the lighthouse. It creaks and groans under the sudden weight but Agatha doesn’t care.

Rio’s mouth is chasing hers like she’s drowning and Agatha gives her life.

When their lips meet, crashing together like planets colliding, Agatha understands. She gets it now, how Rio can have such perfect faith, because how could she ever doubt this? Rio grips her hand wildly, never moving her lips away or opening her eyes. She grabs Agatha’s freezing hand and presses it to the center of her chest—Agatha lets her.

When her fingertips land there, only muscle and bone separating her from Rio’s pounding heart, she gasps. Something inside of her that has been cracking and splintering since she arrived in Salem roars up again, screaming like a caged animal. There is a rushing in her veins, in her head, which has to do with more than just Rio’s fiery touch as she continues to devour Agatha, all teeth and whimpers and desperate movements.

Rio moves slightly, slotting her legs between Agatha’s, and she can’t stop her head from tossing back against the wind. Her back is pressed so hard against the railing she’s sure she’ll have bruises tomorrow. She gasps at the contact, Rio’s entire body melding into hers.

Her ribs seem to bend and crack, like they’re making room for something new to grow. They collapse and rebuild and it is not painful—it is dizzying. The sky fractures along with them, freezing snow and rain suddenly pelting down against the two of them.

For a moment, Rio doesn’t even notice, too busy dragging her lips down Agatha’s neck but then, the lighthouse rattles dangerously around them and her head flies up, pupils blown wide open. Agatha lets Rio pull her inside, the door of the lighthouse slamming shut behind them. Some part of her expects Rio to keep going, to render her gasping and boneless on the floor of the lighthouse, but she doesn’t.

She just presses a hand against her heart and stares at Agatha, her eyes full of an emotion she can’t name, a humor she doesn’t understand. After a moment, her fingers fall away and she leans back against the wall, a laugh falling from her lips like she can’t quite stop it.

“Believe me now?”

Agatha doesn’t hear the knocking, not at first.

She’s too busy fighting against the infuriating stinging in her eyes as she stares up at the haphazardly decorated Christmas tree in the living room. The entire house is cleaner, more warm and lived in. Dust no longer coats every surface and hangs in the air.

There are framed pictures on the mantle that certainly came from Jen and Alice’s house and candles in every corner. Agatha even spotted a few music posters in the hallway along with the now-uncovered art, which she assumes is Billy’s doing. The three of them were still here when Agatha and Rio returned, eager to give them a tour and show them what they had done.

If Agatha closes her eyes, the sounds of Alice and Jen talking in the corner mixing with Rio’s soft laughs and the classical music Billy insisted on playing, she can almost pretend it’s not her mother’s house. She presses her mug of tea against her lips and breathes through the pressure in her chest, the strange feeling that’s been there ever since Rio kissed her.

For a moment, she forgets that she despises the holidays, hates Christmas trees, and thinks that ornaments are stupid. The wind continues to howl outside, the insane snowstorm that they had driven home in continuing with a vengeance. It had been quite the job to even get the tree inside, everyone soaking wet and laughing hysterically afterwards.

It all falls away, lost in the warmth of the fireplace and in the smile pulling at the corners of Rio’s lips as she adds a star to the very top of the tree. Agatha can’t remember the last time she felt like this in the entire month of December. She hesitates to even name the feeling—certainly not jolly or festive.

Content, perhaps.

Evanora had made the holiday season a nightmare, pulling and prodding at Agatha’s every move, dragging her to dinner at her grandparents, and forcing her to play hostess for dozens of family friends. Their perfect Christmas tree, full of perfectly plain silver and blue ornaments, had only served as a reminder of what Agatha wasn’t.

Gazing up at this tree, though, Agatha stares at the countless ornaments of mismatched colors and funky shapes, the ones that Alice and Jen had dragged here from their basement. Billy had helped decorate, trying to make sure that each color was evenly distributed across the tree, but working with Alice meant he hadn’t gotten much balancing done.

Agatha sinks lower into the couch. Her body suddenly feels impossibly heavy, full of missed chances and complicated grief. She wonders why no one told her the holidays could feel like this, instead of being dominated by fear and pain and sadness.

Rio grins adorably as she stands back to admire her work, gazing up at the gold star. She’s been just as engaged in decorating the tree as she was in picking it out. Apparently satisfied, she flops onto the couch next to Agatha, like she can’t imagine being anywhere else, and drops her head onto her shoulder.

“Having fun?” Rio’s voice is low and teasing, nearly fading away in the soft sounds of the burning fireplace.

“Absolutely not”—Agatha shakes her head—“That tree is atrocious.” She waves a hand at it.  Some of the branches are bent and crooked from their haphazard dragging of it inside. The wind and freezing rain and snow certainly hadn't helped either.

The words are expected, they are Agatha’s familiar shield, her protective armor made of syllables and jabs but this time, there is no malice behind them. They are teasing and sweet and fond. Rio grins.

“Hey, you love it.” She throws an arm around Agatha’s shoulder and Agatha can’t even bring herself to scoff or roll her eyes because Rio is right. She leans in closer, revelling in the fact that she can.

She barely even notices Jen get up, her socked feet padding against the floor as she pulls the front door open. The wind gets louder and Agatha shivers, wondering briefly what insane thing Jen is doing this time. It’s been storming for hours, everyone agreeing to crash in Agatha’s many guest rooms to avoid the worst of it.

“Lilia?” Jen asks incredulously.

Agatha’s heart sinks. She turns around at the word. Jen stands in the doorway, unshed tears in her eyes. She gravitates to her wife—in a way that Agatha understands now—and wraps an arm around her back. Alice nods.

“Family curse,” she croaks. “No one can leave Salem.”

Agatha feels betrayed only briefly. She wonders why Alice kept this from her, why she hid something so monumental and important, but it doesn’t take long for her to figure it out. Alice protects them; she always has. She would never have wanted to share this burden, even if Agatha wouldn’t have viewed it that way.

Rio appears, then, slipping in through the open door. “On danger of what?” she asks. To anyone else, she would certainly appear unfazed, but Agatha notices the tension in her shoulders and the nervous tapping of her hand.

“What do you think?” Jen snaps. She drops her eyes to the floor.

“Death,” Alice whispers. “It’s impossible to break. Believe me, my family has tried for decades.”

Agatha’s mind races. After ending up back in Salem, discovering that magic is real, and lifting her own awful curse, it’s hard for her to believe that anything is truly impossible. She takes a deep breath, trying to distance herself, but this isn’t a theoretical discussion on magical theory. This is Alice; her lighthouse in the dark, the first person who believed that her power could be used for good; that she wasn’t evil.

Agatha hasn’t given much thought to it: what her power might be able to do beyond the dangerous and feared legends that live in her mind and haunt her mother’s house—but she does now. She wishes she had access to Evanora’s hidden library. It holds the only book she’s ever discovered with any information on her particular brand of magic.

The Taking of Power.

She realizes something with a jolt. The book didn’t specify what kind of power.

Agatha takes a deep breath, forcing the words out before she can talk herself out of it. “What if I could break it?”

She’s already coming up with a plan, her brain working overtime trying to recall everything she’s ever read on historic bloodline curses. There’s not much, but she’d guess that her power would only work if the curse was separate from Alice’s own magic, layered over it or intertwined.

“I just told you, Agatha. It’s impossible.” Alice shakes her head. “My mother tried everything. It drove her insane.”

“Your mother didn’t have a witch with abilities of legend to help her,” Rio practically sings, and Agatha would probably blush if her heart wasn’t pounding so hard.

“I think I could“—Agatha struggles for the right words—“absorb the curse magic and leave your magic alone.”

“You’ve never done anything like that before,” Jen snaps. Agatha is surprised when no anger rises up in her. She knows that Jen is scared, can see it in the set of her jaw and the furrow between her eyebrows. Agatha can imagine how much research she must have done on this very topic, trying desperately to free the woman she loves.

Agatha steps forward, eyes locked on Alice. “You always told me that my power wasn’t evil—that it could be used for good. What if this is it?”

Alice blinks. She only looks afraid for a moment, and then the expression is gone. She lifts her chin.

“Let’s try it.”

“Alice,” Jen whispers. “Are you sure?”

“I trust her,” she says, and Agatha’s heart nearly explodes.

Alice trusts her—even now.

“You’ll need to blast me.” Agatha steps back and Rio seems to understand what she needs, sliding in behind her and resting her hands on her waist.

“I’ve got you,” she whispers in her ear.

Alice shakes her hands out, chest rising and falling quickly. “This feels wrong,” she says.

“I know, but you won’t hurt me,” Agatha reassures her, even though she has no way of knowing that.

Alice lifts her hand, orange magic that resembles flame erupting from her skin. Agatha flinches, images of her mother’s painful and sharp power pressing into her mind, but she forces herself to stay put.

Alice’s magic reaches for Agatha, but there is no violence in its motion, only familiarity. Agatha reaches out, too, even though that hadn’t been part of her hastily compiled mental plan. Sparks and threads gather in her hands, responding to her call.

Her fingertips do not meet scorching heat as she feared. Instead, there is just endless warmth. It seems to sink into Agatha’s very core and she gasps, tears jumping to her eyes at the depth of it. Alice’s love, loyalty, and protection are woven into the flames. Agatha tilts her head. If she listens closely, she can hear the curse. It buzzes in her ear, but she doesn’t know how to find it.

They stand there for a few minutes, the two of them suspended in a sort of trance. Agatha is careful not to tug on Alice’s magic any further. She doesn’t want to hurt her. Agatha doesn’t move until she feels Rio’s hand brush against hers, her fingertips golden and full of light.

“Let me help.” Rio’s hand rests on top of Agatha’s, a rush of light spiraling through the orange magic in front of them.

Suddenly, Agatha can see the power in Alice’s body—the magic that helps her blood rush through her veins and allows her lungs to explain. Rio is illuminating just Alice’s magic, the power within her that is lush with life.

Agatha can see the curse, then. It’s darker than Alice’s magic and burns when it touches her fingertips. She wraps her hands around it regardless, yanking it towards her chest. Alice cries out, but Agatha isn’t surprised. The curse is ancient and rooted deep. She lets her own instinct take over, overwhelmed by that same selfish drive that her mother had been so very afraid of.

Her power wants to take . For the first time in decades, Agatha lets it.

The curse builds in her palms, sparks flying up her arms like tiny jolts of electricity. It pushes toward her ribs, seeking a new connection. Alice slumps over, collapsing into Jen’s arms as the connection snaps abruptly. Agatha turns on her heel. She doesn't know how to destroy it, but she has a feeling Rio might.

Rio presses her hands into the distorted, swirling magic. It changes colors, shifting to green, and shatters like glass in Agatha’s hands.

“Back to where you belong,” Rio whispers, her voice almost fond. Agatha doesn’t have any time to consider what she’s done—the impossibility of it all—before Jen’s arms are wrapped around her.

“Thank you, Agatha,” she gasps in her ear.

Agatha pulls back with a small smile. “Of course.”

Alice is leaning against the counter, eyes sparkling. She doesn’t say anything, only presses a hand against the center of her chest and smiles. Agatha knows the feeling.

Agatha Harkness doesn’t hate the holidays. She never truly did. When she opens her eyes on Christmas morning—in Salem—it’s Rio that she sees.

“Hello,” she murmurs, pressing her lips to Agatha’s shoulder. “It’s snowing.”

The house is toasty warm, Rio’s carefully hand-stitched stockings hanging from the fireplace. The two of them have plans with Alice and Jen later and, if Rio has her way, they’ll spend the day ice skating and then tucked away in a booth at the local diner. It’s not what Agatha imagined her holidays would look like this year, but she’s decided to roll with it.

She would never admit it, but she really can’t imagine being anywhere else.

“I can see that,” she whispers, glancing over at the window. Countless snowflakes stick to it and then melt away, each of them complex, unique, and gone in an instant. They seem drawn to the glass, despite it promising their end.

Or maybe, just maybe, it’s not the end at all.
