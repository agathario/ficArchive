Chapter 1

Agatha banged on her bedroom wall yet again at 3 am. She felt bad for whoever was on the other side, but hearing their screaming, crying baby every night for the last week through the world’s thinnest apartment wall was some type of medieval torture method brought back from the dead just to punish her.

After a few minutes, she heard her neighbor grow quiet, and Agatha finally settled in bed, weighted blanket atop her duvet, eye mask settled firmly over her head, before the screaming started up again. “Oh for the love of-”

She shoved her covers away and ripped the mask off before standing, taking a second to grab her purple floral robe and loosely draping it over some threadbare shorts and her black tank top. She stormed out of her home in nothing but fluffy slippers, knocked harshly on the door next to hers, and waited. She huffed out, exactly once, half out of annoyance and half to blow her messy hair out of her face without sacrificing her slouch.

A woman opened the door, with warm brown eyes, legs for days, leading up to boxer shorts that suddenly made Agatha’s mouth go dry, and an apologetic look as she held the culprit against her hip. “Hi. Can I-”

“Yeah, hi, Agatha. Also, that baby is big enough to sleep through the night,” Agatha interrupted.

“I’m Rio,” tried the other woman, extending the hand that wasn’t holding a wriggly child, who couldn’t have been a year old yet. After a beat of Agatha not shaking it, she dropped it. “And this is Nicholas.” As if on cue, Nicholas began to whimper when he heard his name. “He’s having some sleep regression issues,” Rio continued, trying to sound cheerful as she stared at Nicholas.

“Well,” Agatha said, moving closer to Rio, “I’m now having sleep regressions too. I can hear you guys every single day of the week.”

“Do you have any kids, Agatha?”

“No, but I know that Nicholas should be a good sleeper by now.”

As she looked closer, Agatha could see the cracked edges of Rio. Her ponytail was haphazard, her socks mismatched, and the old basketball t-shirt she wore definitely had spit up on the sleeve. “He’s…It’s complicated.”

Before Agatha could reply, Nicholas began to wail, yet again. Rio winced at his ear-splitting screams and just walked back into the apartment. Agatha took that as a sign to follow her in.

As she shut the door behind her, she took in her neighbor’s apartment. It was a layout that mirrored Agatha’s own, and she wondered if the reason she’d been hearing Nicholas’s cries so clearly was because they had to share a bedroom wall.

But, beyond the layout, there wasn’t a lot of similarity.

Agatha had her creature comforts and a lived-in space, yes, but Rio’s…felt less like a home and more like a garden center. Between the plants taking over the space, and low lighting, and the mismatched baby toys and supplies, Agatha felt slightly disoriented.

“Please,” Rio whined from the living room. Agatha saw her sit with Nicholas in her lap. “I fed you, I changed you, I bathed you, I am one step away from putting sleepy time tea in your bottle. C’mon, Nicky.”

Ignoring her words, as if he could understand them anyway, the baby kept crying.

Agatha looked on and, in her delirious, sleep-deprived 3 am brain, did the only logical thing she could think to do. She crossed the room in three large steps and picked her strange neighbor’s baby up. She cradled him against her collarbone, head held just above her heart, and began to rock him back and forth as she paced the room.

She wasn’t sure what exactly Nicholas would like, but she had a hunch. He seemed shocked to have another human hold him, his dark brown eyes going wide and curious as they stared up at Agatha.

Then, his cries quickly went from loud to whimpers, to absolutely nothing at all until Nicholas was sleeping peacefully in Agatha’s arms. She hummed nonsensically as she kept pacing, eyes on the tiny hand that was curled into her shirt.

“How’d you do that?” Rio asked, mesmerized.

“Sometimes babies just want to be walked,” Agatha replied, still looking down at the child in her arms.

“But how?”

Agatha shrugged. “I just know. Can’t we just leave it at that?”

They were quiet for a bit, Agatha continuing her motions around the living room while Rio watched on from the couch.

When Agatha was certain Nicky wasn’t going to wake up, she asked Rio where his crib was.

“Oh,” Rio replied with a blush. “I haven’t had a ton of time to set things up for him, but it’s back here.”

“Time to set up? This baby looks like he’s at least seven months old?” Agatha was confused as she followed the other woman down a hallway and into what was obviously Rio’s bedroom.

“Wow, you’re good. He’ll be eight months in two weeks.” Rio pointed to a blue pack and play that was empty except for a stuffed Snoopy in one corner.

“That doesn’t answer my question. Also, he needs a crib.”

Rio grimaced. “I know, I know.”

“So…?” Agatha tried.

“I’m taking care of it. There was a mix-up and we got the wrong one delivered but I promise a real crib is coming tomorrow,” Rio replied quickly. “Can’t we just leave it at that?”

“Rio,” Agatha replied, knowing her own words had been used against her as she tried not to wake the baby while chastising the other woman.

“Listen,” Rio said, now whispering, before she moved about the room to turn on what looked like an incredibly fancy baby monitor. “I’m still half convinced you’re a figment of my sleep-deprived imagination.“

“All the more reason to wonder why you’re unprepared to have this baby?”

“It just…It just happened.”

“Rio.”

“Okay, let’s make a deal. You accept I’m Nicky’s legal guardian, and I won’t ask any questions about why you’re so good with kids. Deal?”

Agatha frowned but nodded. “Deal.”

A week later, Agatha heard the crying again. Earlier, at least, closer to eleven than three in the morning this time.

Agatha stood, running through the same motions she had the first time, and walked up to Rio’s apartment.

“He needs a bottle,” Rio said, once she was certain he’d handed him off correctly. “I think.”

“Easy enough.”

Agatha watched as Rio measured and made a bottle, almost with scientific precision. Once finished, she passed it over to Agatha, who shifted the baby, nestling him in the crook of her arm, before beginning to feed him.

Rio tilted her head towards the bottle of wine on the counter, eyebrow quirked up, and Agatha nodded once in silent agreement.

Agatha didn’t want to think about how natural it all felt. She couldn’t. It was just one of those fluke things; she happened to be good with babies, and her neighbor had a baby. Big deal. It was the right thing to do. The price of living in a community or whatever, Agatha thought.

“He needs to be burped after,” Rio said after sliding Agatha’s wine glass across the kitchen counter.

“I know.”

“Right, right.” Rio watched as Nicholas greedily drank his milk. “So…what do you do?”

Agatha’s eyes snapped up, and she turned to stare at Rio. “What?”

“I can’t ask about the whole,” Rio made a vague gesture at Agatha with her hand and a noise that sounded like a growl mixed with a yawn, “that thing, so I figured we’d talk about something else.”

“And that something else is my job?”

“You seem very concerned with your beauty sleep for someone who looks this hot at midnight. And I saw you last week with a bunch of papers in hand, so I assumed you’re important somewhere.”

Agatha blushed and turned back to Nicholas. “Shut up.”

“You’re right,” Rio shrugged. “Probably the wine talking.”

“You haven’t taken a sip yet.”

“Hmm, has good observation skills,” Rio said, pushing off the counter to walk around Agatha, sizing her up. “Are you a P.I.?”

Agatha’s brow furrowed. “No?”

“Maybe you do some type of design work? Skilled and meticulous and weirdly sensitive about your art?”

“Meh, it’s not that creative,” Agatha answered, curious to see what else Rio might come up with.

“Alright, attention to detail but not that creative…lawyer?”

“I’m good at arguing if that’s what you’re asking,” she replied with a sly smile. “But I’m mainly focused on defending myself.”

Rio shrugged. “Business lady? Of some kind?”

“I do wear suits often, but no.” Agatha tried to hide her smile.

“I…give up,” Rio acquiesced.

Agatha sighed, staring at Rio for another beat before giving in to her warm gaze. “History professor.”

“Ahh,” Rio said, with the click of her tongue. “Good with babies because you like shaping the minds of tomorrow.”

“Something like that,” Agatha acquiesced. “What about you?”

“I’m a mortician, currently on leave though,” Rio added, “obviously.”

“You’re…a mortician?”

Rio raised an eyebrow as Nicky gurgled, breaking out of his feeding stupor. “Is that so surprising?” Agatha gave her a look. “Oh. It is.”

“Well, kind of.” Agatha placed the bottle on the counter and moved Nicky until his head was hovering just over her shoulder. “Burp cloth?”

Rio grabbed the first one she found, off the kitchen table, and brought it to her. “Why is that surprising?”

Agatha shrugged, once again situating herself until she could properly burp the baby. “You’re…my neighbor.”

“I hate to tell you this, but people in Westview do have to live somewhere. Even me.”

“Right,” Agatha said in a no-nonsense tone. “And isn’t that somewhere usually the funeral home?”

“I…it’s…it’s a team effort. I’m not the only one involved, so, no, I don’t live at the home. My…coworker does.”

“Would you ever? At least, while Nicky is still a child?” Agatha questioned after Nicky let out a particularly loud burp.

Rio snorted at the sound. “Maybe. But I’m not really the kind of person to. I kinda like the separation of that work and…everything else.”

”Is that why your apartment looks like a jungle? Can’t have your own private, indoor garden at the…there?”

“Alright, fair,” Rio said with a chuckle. “But when Nicky came around, I suddenly had a lot more time at home and a lot less of an irregular schedule. And he naps a lot, so…idle hands, and all of that.”

“He takes a lot of naps?”

“Yeah, like three-ish, sometimes four?”

Agatha sighed. “No wonder he can’t sleep. Seriously, how did you become a parent?”

Rio went quiet for a moment. “We agreed not to talk about it, right?”

Agatha bit her lip, knowing she’d crossed the line. So she stared instead, tracing the lines of Rio’s face with her eyes, nearly saying a prayer for the gap between her teeth, thought about what her pink lips would feel like against her own, and it felt…terrifying.

Had she followed Rio’s gaze, she would’ve seen the other woman doing the same to her.

Agatha passed a well-fed and sleepy Nicky over. “Here. Yours,” she said, before practically running out of the apartment.

Once the door was closed behind her, Agatha took a deep breath. She heard Nicky whimper as Rio shushed him gently, but tonight was not the night to explore…whatever that was.

She walked away, heart heavy and tears threatening to spill.

Two days passed, and, after a particularly satisfying bath, there was a knock at her door. Agatha opened it to see a pumpkin spice latte on her doormat and another note. Sorry. Better?

Don’t apologize for what you didn’t do. A latte is closer, but this morning was affront to pumpkins worldwide.

Rio, however, either didn’t have the same issues or was making up for her lack of appetite during her illness by ordering something called The Lumberjack Special, with a million questions following it. Wheat. Scrambled. Two- No, three pancakes. Sausage and extra crispy bacon. Home fries were fine.

“Wanda,” Agatha sighed out in disdained agreement.

Her ex-wife was calling to ask if Agatha had seen that a new position had opened up in UCLA's history department, looking for someone with her skills and field of study. Oh, and that she needed someone to go to her 8-week checkup with her.

Rio’s brow furrowed. “The husband-“

“Was an assshole. Is still one, if I’m being honest. He’s…one of those guys that acts like a hippie and then you find out his grandfather was a lord or duke or someone who is definitely responsible for some atrocity that made his family a ton of money. Honestly it’s a miracle the twins won’t be raised the same way.”

“And they’re still married?” The shock was evident in Rio’s tone.

Agatha shrugged. “Haven’t gotten there yet but, yeah. Wanda insists that he’s changed and is a good dad or whatever, but I try not to talk to him too much.”

“Fair enough,” Rio replied.

Agatha leaned forwards, playing with the paper napkin on the table. She mentioned flying out to LA for the very first time, worried, because despite how things ended with Wanda, there was still a part of her that felt connected to her. She was someone who understood a part of Agatha’s life no one but Lilia would ever witness. Looking back, it didn’t take a lot for her to stay.

Wanda needed help and she begged for Agatha of all people to stay with her.

“It was my biggest mistake,” Agatha said, slowly and calculated. “But brought some of my happiest memories and my favorite people into my life.”

Rio gulped just as the waitress began to set the plates down. It was quiet, neither really digging in now. “So…”

“So,” Agatha explained. “I…interviewed at UCLA and they liked me enough. And…I felt like I’d somehow gotten a wife again. And kids.”

There was never any formal reunion. No marriage certificate resigned or divorce filings torn up. Agatha quietly resigned from her work on one coast and began at the other, much to Lilia’s chagrin. But they both agreed she needed a change of pace. She rented out an apartment for the grand total of two months before Wanda called her, uncomfortable as hell, in the middle of the night saying that if Agatha didn’t come home with a Taco Bell nacho cheese and a gas station hot dog she’d bite her head off.

Agatha chose to move in the next day. It took a grand total of just one more week before they’d ignited what was left of their romantic relationship.

“I thought okay, you know, we hit some type of midlife crisis early,” Agatha said, watching as Rio began digging in. Her chest warmed as she realized Rio was trying to look at her while eating, and failing to get any real food on her fork. “And I dunno, maybe, selfishly, I liked that she needed me.”

Agatha was there throughout the entire pregnancy, through twelve hours of labor, through both boys coming into the world. It didn’t matter that her name wasn’t on the birth certificate, she was their mother. Agatha still remembers, clear as day, driving them all home from the hospital, the twins on either side in the back while Wanda sat in the middle, looking at them in awe and murmuring sweet words every time one of them shifted.

They rented out a small condo with a yard at that point. Agatha thought about how it only took her seven long strides to get from her and Wanda’s bedroom, to where the twins slept. She’d learned a lot by doing, and more so by just watching her two boys interact with each other. Wanda was always the nervous and careful parent, so Agatha felt like she had more freedom.

Three blissful years passed by. Agatha was there for it all, raising her twin boys alongside Wanda.

Which is why it felt like ice in her veins when, on a random Sunday morning, when Wanda was busy with the boys’ breakfasts, Agatha decided to pick up her partner’s ringing phone with the caller ID Work V, and hear an English man’s voice say, “Hello darling, how are the boys?”

“Oh, Agatha,” Rio gently whispered. Her big, brown eyes shone with pity and Agatha felt another wave of anxiety coarse through her.

Agatha nodded. “Turns out, he had…a change of heart a few months after the boys were born. They’d been sending pictures and texts and calling each other when I was asleep or thinking I was doing the right thing by taking them out to the park, giving Wanda a break. For years. She didn’t think I’d find out because I’d been so focused on our sons.”

“That’s awful.”

“Separating was worse that time around. I…felt like I changed my whole life for her and she couldn’t...”

Rio waited for a beat. “That’s…a lot.”

Agatha let out a watery snort. “Gets worse. I flew out to Lilia, basically collapsed on her doorstep, then tried to go back for the kids. Wanda wouldn’t do that, saying Vision was their real dad and I wasn’t a part of their family. Flipped on me so fast I had to hire a lawyer and then Wanda claimed I abandoned the three of them. I was trying to sue my own family so I could at least see my sons. That’s the part I hated the most,” she finished, whispering ashenly.

Rio sat back in the booth. “That’s why you freaked out at the park. That lady thought you were with us but-“

“I know I…I want to be a part of…whatever I can get from you and Nicky. But I’ve been kicked out of my family before.”

“I wouldn’t-“

“That’s what everyone says,” Agatha replied, sounding defeated. “And then you’re sitting in a windowless room, agreeing to see your kids for two, maybe three weeks of their summer vacation every year, because it’s all you think you’ll ever get.”

“Fuck.”

“Yeah.” Their waitress came by to clear their plates and ask if they wanted anything else, but they both waved her off. “I’ll get more time with them soon, though. Wanda’s been on a mommy blogger guru kick and her and Vision are…having their differences. And, I got word that the court approved the change for the custody agreement, so they can’t really take it back now.”

“That’s great.” Rio offered a small but genuine smile.

Agatha leaned over her half of the table. “I am so sorry I didn’t tell you. I wanted to, I swear. I was just…”

“You’ve gone through a lot.”

“That’s not an excuse.”

“But you have.”

“Whatever. I’m still sorry for…for not telling you about them. I should’ve since like the second time I helped Nicky. And…sorry for leading you and Nicky along. Like I said, it was so good, I just wanted to take whatever I could get.”

“Agatha,” Rio said gently. “That boy isn’t yours.”

She huffed, looking down at her empty coffee mug before glancing back up. “I…I know.”

“I don’t say that to be mean but…a secret family.“

“I wanted more time. With both of you,” Agatha added.

Rio looked down at that. “You…I…You could’ve had more. I would’ve given you more.”

Agatha’s eyes closed, in a feeble attempt to stop her tears from springing. “Don’t.”

“There’s a part of me that wants to let you back in,” Rio began, causing Agatha’s eyes to snap wide open, mouth agape, and just staring across the table. “I won’t lie to you. Despite, everything I…I like you. I don’t think you’re a bad person. But,” she continued on, toying with her fingers, “there’s another part of me that does feel betrayed. You, well, you didn’t exactly lie, but you also weren’t truthful. You hid a part of yourself from me. Hid things that, yes are yours, but…I feel like there’s nothing stopping you from doing it again.”

Agatha shook her head. “I swear, those are the only skeletons in my closet.”

Rio gave a half-huff as her tongue poked against the inside of her cheek. “I have a lot in mine.”

It took Agatha a second and then she groaned. “Really? A dad joke right now?”

Rio shrugged, chuckling as the two let some levity pass between them. “I don’t get to make a lot of jokes as a mortician.”

“I’m serious though,” Agatha replied. “I’m sorry.”

Rio sighed. “I know. I think…maybe, could we start again?”

“Okay,” Agatha whispered. “Clean slate.”

“Though, to be honest, I want to move slowly and clearly.”

“Whatever you need.”

“No more of the talking or whatever. I need to understand what we’re doing.”

“I get it. It was honestly confusing for me too,” Agatha grumbled.

Rio shook her head. “This has to be mutual. I want to know you and your kids as much as you want to know mine. I want you to be someone I can rely on, without fear that you’re going to drop another bomb on me.”

“Like I said, that’s really the big one.”

“Well that,” Rio said, “and Lilia.”

“She’d never-“

“She didn’t tell me anything,” Rio replied, following Agatha’s train of thought. “But she did saythat you’d have a rough go at it. Admittedly, I was a bit more lenient with her than Jen and Alice, but there was something about her I knew I could trust.”

“I know the feeling,” Agatha acquiesced.

“I didn’t realize she’d known you for so long.”

Agatha inhaled sharply. “One trauma at a time, yeah? I promise that’s not as bad. But maybe I need time before you fully diagnose me.”

Rio rolled her eyes, but her smirk gave her away. “I’m not trying to psychoanalyze you.”

“Could’ve fooled me,” Agatha replied, with no real bite in her words.

Their waitress came up to the booth once more, asking if they needed anything else, before wordlessly dropping the check on the middle of the table, and a final smile at the two.

“I got it,” Agatha said.

“Let’s split it.”

Agatha shook her head. “Let me, please. I’m the one responsible for all of this.”

Agatha paid up front before they headed out.

“So,” Rio began.

“So,” Agatha echoed, feeling lighter now that she had confessed.

“I’ll…I’ll see you around?”

“Yeah, yes,” Agatha replied.

Rio gave her a look before leaning into her space, slowly as if not to spook her, and pressing a kiss against her cheek, hand warm against Agatha’s side. “Te veo,” she whispered.

Agatha felt like she spent an hour stuck to that exact sidewalk corner, thinking about the feeling that maybe, maybe she could have something good in her life again.
