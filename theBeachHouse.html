The beach house stood resolutely against the backdrop of the picturesque coastline, nestled near the water’s edge. It was just after dawn when Rio Vidal stood in her oversized worn hoody gazing out at the rolling waves, the salty breeze tousling her black hair. She had planned this weekend meticulously, drawing her closest friends together at the place they had spent most of their summer’s during and after college.

Memories flooded back as she stood there; the house held countless summers of laughter and love. Her thoughts drifted wistfully to bygone days when they were younger and care free. If she closed her eyes she could still see Alice singing on the coffee table with a hairbrush while she and Jen skipped around the table, drinks raised in the air. She would see Lilia practicing tarot on Billy while Agatha sat rolling her eyes at every prediction.
Those were the best chapters in Rio Vidal’s life.

Alice may have looked terrifying to anyone at first meeting, red streaks through her black hair, winged eyeliner sharp enough to kill a man, but Rio had never met anyone as caring as Alice.

Jen followed suit, her embrace gentle and lingering. “We’ve missed you, Rio. This was a wonderful idea.”
Jen exuded poise, her demeanour having softened in her 30s, which made her much more approachable, provided she liked you.

As if on cue, another vehicle pulled up, immediately apparent by the slow speed of the car that Billy was the driver of said vehicle.

Agatha.

“I told you I had a work thing I couldn’t get out of.” Flicking her hair and pursing her lips, she snaked her hands in the pockets of her slacks as she tilted her chin defiantly.

Rio’s eyes flickered briefly, anger flashing through her amber eyes before they dipped down to look at the floor letting out a small hollow laugh.

Agatha could see the hurt radiating from Rio, her usual full and broad shoulders now hunched slightly and her collarbones more prominent. Lilia had called it out earlier but now as Agatha observed Rio she was noticing the subtle physical changes in the other woman.

“Im sorry Rio. Really, I am.”

Rio raised her head to look at Agatha, she could see the sincerity in her blue eyes.

“I miss you.” Rio’s voice sounded smaller than Agatha had ever heard it.

The resolve she had built over the years crumbled at the sound of Rio’s broken voice and owl eyes staring at her pathetically.

She stepped forward and pulled Rio by the back of her head towards her into a crushing hug, cradling her head as Rio rested her chin on Agatha’s shoulder, her arms wrapped tightly around her waist, gripping Agatha’s shirt at the back.
Tilting her head, Agatha breathed in, inhaling the familiar scent of Rio’s natural earthy scent, her eyes closing as she suppressed a hum from escaping.

Agatha's entire body tingled with a warm, electric sensation as she wrapped her arms around Rio, her heart fluttering wildly at their closeness. She had missed Rio profoundly, yet had convinced herself that if she kept her distance, Rio wouldn't notice her absence.

Now, feeling the tightness of Rio’s embrace and hearing the heartfelt confession of how much she had been missed, a wave of regret washed over her as she cursed herself internally.

The pair stayed clutching at each other, neither one making any attempt to detach until voices approaching pulled them apart reluctantly just as Alice and Lilia appeared in the kitchen.

Agatha turned herself away from the newcomers as she smoothed her hair and swiped at a stray tear she hadn’t noticed had escaped.

Knowing that she wouldn’t be able to hide the array of emotions on her face in time to greet the two women, she quickly dipped out of the kitchen, ignoring Lilia’s sarcastic quip about interrupting.

As soon as the door to her room was firmly closed the damn of emotions collapsed and a sob tore from her throat as she brought the back of her hand up to her mouth to muffle the noise.

When Rio had invited her to this trip she promised herself she would be able to hold it together for the entire trip. Now here she was an hour in and falling completely apart.

She knew it would be hard, seeing Rio for the first time in over a year. They had spoken a couple of times since then but nothing like they used to. They were inseparable all through high school and college, their friends would tease them relentlessly, insinuating that they should just hurry up and get married as they asked so much like a married couple.

Agatha had realised in college that she was in love with Rio.

Rio had shown up in the middle of the night at Agatha’s dorm and dragged her outside to watch a meteor shower, forcing her down until they were both sprawled on the campus lawn.

Lying on the damp grass, Agatha turned her head to take in Rio’s features as the lights from the shooting stars illuminated her face, her adorable gapped tooth smile melting her heart, she had reached out and grasped the other girls hand in her own looking back up at the night sky. That was the night Agatha knew she was in love with her best friend.

Wiping her face she made her way to the window, pushing it open to inhale some sea air to calm her rapid breathing.

The sounds of voices from outside pulled her attention as she watched as someone was exiting out a newly parked car.

Agatha spotted the red hair immediately before the woman had fully emerged from the car, reigniting Agatha’s sharp breaths and increased heart rate as Rio approached the woman, wrapping her in a tight embrace, not as close as she had just held Agatha but still intimate enough to show affection.

Wanda.

Rio’s wife.

The woman who married the love of Agatha’s life.

Rio padded in barefoot, hoodie sleeves long over her hands, her hair tangled from sleep.

She was surprised to find the light already on.

Wanda stood at the stove, back turned, stirring something in a small pan. She wore one of Nat’s flannel shirts over her tank top, the sleeves rolled up, her hair knotted high on her head.

“You’re up,” Rio said softly.

Wanda turned, smiled a little. “So are you.”

Rio shrugged, rubbing a hand over the back of her neck.

“Didn’t sleep long.”

Wanda studied her for a moment. “You never do when you think too much.”

Rio offered a crooked smile. “That obvious?”

Wanda raised a brow, then turned back to the pan, eggs, by the smell. “You slept with Agatha.”

Rio opened her mouth to deny it out of reflex, and then stopped.

“Yeah.”

Wanda said nothing for a beat, just stirred slowly.

“I’m happy for you,” she said, eventually.

Soft. Real.

Rio blinked, caught off guard. “You are?”

Wanda turned to face her fully this time.

“Yeah. Don’t get me wrong, I don’t want to know what happened, how it happened, or whether she still hums in her sleep—”

“She does,” Rio muttered, then winced.

Wanda groaned. “Boundaries, Vidal.”

Rio laughed, relieved. “Right, sorry.”

Wanda leaned back against the counter, arms folded loosely across her chest.

“You look better,” she said, eyes scanning Rio’s face.

“Softer. Not like you’ve figured everything out, but like… you’re not holding your breath anymore.”

“I think I’m scared it’s going to disappear,” Rio admitted. “Like I’ll blink and she’ll be gone again. Or I’ll be gone.”

Wanda nodded slowly, then reached over and flicked the kettle off before it could whistle.

“I get it. You don’t trust the good things to stay.”

Rio looked down, fingers curled tight around the edge of the counter.

“But they can,” Wanda added, gentler now. “Sometimes they do. And I think… Agatha’s one of those things. She always was. You just never believed it.”

Rio looked up at her, eyes glassy.

“You really don’t hate me for this?”

“Hate you?” Wanda repeated, eyebrows lifting.

“Rio, I loved you. I probably always will, in a very complicated, exhausted kind of way.”

She stepped closer, voice dropping.

“But I love you enough to want you to be happy, even if I’m not the one giving it to you anymore.”

Rio swallowed hard. “Wanda…”

Wanda reached out and gently tucked a piece of hair behind her ear.

“I’m not saying it doesn’t ache a little,” she said. “But that’s not your fault. It’s just grief. Old love turning into something else.”

Rio’s throat tightened.

“You’re still the person I trust most,” she whispered.

“You don’t have to stop trusting me,” Wanda said. “I’m not going anywhere. You’re stuck with me, remember? Vows or not.”

They both smiled, a little sad, a little full.

Wanda pulled her into a hug.
Not romantic.
Not longing.
Just tight and steady and real.

“You’re my best friend,” she murmured into Rio’s hair. “You always will be.”

Rio squeezed her eyes shut.

“Thank you. For being here. For taking care of me when I couldn’t. For pushing me toward her when I was too scared.”

Wanda pulled back just enough to meet her eyes. “Just don’t make me hear the details, alright? I can root for you two without a full soundtrack.”

Rio laughed wetly. “Deal.”

Wanda gave her one last squeeze, then turned back to the stove. “Now sit your ass down. I’m making eggs. Then you can have your smoothie.”

Rio sank into a chair, pretending to gag but smiling softly.

Wanda plated the eggs with more force than necessary, setting one in front of Rio before sliding into the chair across from her. Her tea steamed between them, untouched now.

She didn’t speak right away, just watched Rio tuck her hoodie sleeves back and start picking at breakfast. The quiet stretched. The air was heavy with things neither had quite said.

And then Wanda exhaled hard, setting her mug down with a soft clink.

“I keep trying to tell myself it’s not my business,” she said. “That you have Agatha now, and I’m just supposed to, what? Be supportive. Smile. Stay in my lane.”

Rio looked up, startled by the shift in her tone.

“But the truth is, I’m terrified, Rio,” Wanda said, her voice catching. “Not because of Agatha. Or us. But because you’re walking around like maybe this is all the time you get. Like maybe the end is already written.”

Rio’s shoulders dropped. “It might be.”

“It might not,” Wanda snapped, not unkind, just scared. “And I hate that you’ve made peace with dying before even trying to live.”

Rio blinked. “I’m not—”

“You are,” Wanda said. “You’re acting like you only get one more chapter, and you’re trying to cram all the good stuff in before the lights go out.”

She scrubbed a hand over her face, frustrated tears welling now.

“I can’t… I can’t do it, Rio. I can’t sit here and watch you give up because it’s easier to say goodbye on your own terms than risk staying.”

Rio stared at her, throat closing.

“I know it’s not fair for me to say this,” Wanda continued, her voice softer but trembling, “but I love you so much, and the thought of you being gone, really gone, makes me feel like the floor’s going to fall out from under me.”

“I’m sorry,” Rio said quietly. “I’m scared of surviving and not being the same. Of losing pieces of myself. Of being too broken to come back to her.”

Wanda reached across the table, grabbing her hand, not gently, not delicately. Just firm.

“Then let us help put you back together. Me. Her. Everyone. You don’t have to come back the same, you just have to come back.”

Rio swallowed hard. “You really want me to do it?”

“I want you to try,” Wanda whispered. “I want you to want to be here.”

The silence between them went still. Heavy. Raw.

And then Rio nodded. Just once.

“I told Agatha I’d talk to the surgeon again.”

Wanda’s shoulders slumped in quiet relief. She stood and rounded the table, pulling Rio into a hug so tight it hurt.

“You’d better,” she murmured into her hair. “Because I’m not ready for a world that doesn’t have you in it. And I don’t think she is either.”

Rio clung to her, heart pounding. “I don’t think I am either.”

And for a few minutes, there was no pain, no past, no fear. Just two people who had once built a life together holding tight to the one thing they’d never stop being to each other:

Home.

Agatha woke slowly, the kind of sleep-drunk haze that made time feel untrustworthy.

Her body was warm, her limbs tangled in the covers, and the pillow beside her was indented, still holding the shape of another head.

She reached instinctively toward it.

Empty.

Her eyes snapped open. The other half of the bed was vacant, the sheets cool.

Panic bloomed instantly, low in her stomach.

No.No no no no

She sat up sharply, hair falling around her face, heart pounding too fast.
The last time she’d woken like this was five years ago.

Only then, she was the one who’d slipped out of bed in the early light.

Who’d grabbed her shoes with trembling hands and run before Rio could wake up.
Before she could say anything real. Before she could ruin the fragile joy they’d touched the night before.

And now… for one jagged second, Agatha thought maybe she’d ruined it again, even if she hadn’t moved this time.

Maybe Rio had woken before her and remembered the ache. The hurt. The hollow she left behind.

God, what if she left? What if I did it again just by being me?

But then, from somewhere downstairs, the faint sound of laughter.

Muted. Familiar.

Rio.

Agatha froze, listening. The voice wasn’t sharp or angry. It was tired, amused. Still there.

Still here.

She let out a breath she didn’t know she was holding, her shoulders sagging.

Rio hadn’t left.

She hadn’t either.

Agatha leaned back against the pillows, hand still pressed against her chest like she was holding her own ribs together.

Her mind was already racing, thinking about coffee, the smell of eggs, the possibility of a kiss in the hallway.

Of how she might reach for Rio and not be pushed away. Of mornings… plural. Not just this one.

She blinked up at the ceiling, watery-eyed.

You stayed. And so did she.

A different kind of panic rose now, gentler, but no less sharp- what if I get used to this? What if I let myself want it, and then I lose her for real?

Because loving Rio wasn’t a crush anymore.

It wasn’t a moment. It was a future she was starting to picture in terrifying detail.

And now that she’d finally held it again, skin, breath, laughter, safety, Agatha didn’t know if she could survive losing it all over again.

But maybe, she thought, clutching the edge of the blanket, this time I stay. And maybe she does too. And maybe we get more than just one morning.

Agatha didn’t move from the bed.

Not yet.

She just let the light wash over her, hand on the empty pillow beside her, a pillow that wasn’t cold anymore, just lived-in.

And for once, she didn’t run.

She waited.

Because she wanted Rio to come back to her.

And she had a feeling she would.

In the kitchen, a pan sizzled gently on the stove, Wanda at the helm, focused and precise in her movements.

Lilia hummed under her breath from the floor, still braiding tiny wildflowers into a chain. Jen had taken up her usual perch by the window, one long leg crossed over the other, her matcha clutched like a shield against anything earnest.

Rio sat curled on a stool, bare feet hooked on the rungs, hoodie sleeves swallowed around her hands.

Her hair was still damp from the shower, her cheeks slightly flushed, from the heat or something else, no one asked.

The bottom step creaked softly.

Agatha stepped in without fanfare, dressed in one of Rio’s sweatshirts, sleeves pushed up, the collar lopsided in a way that felt intimate even without context.

Her eyes moved straight to Rio like they always did now, quietly, instinctively and she smiled.

Small. Real.

Rio’s smile bloomed instantly in response.

Neither of them said anything. They didn’t need to.

Agatha moved toward the counter, brushing gently past Rio’s back, the kind of touch that could’ve been an accident but wasn’t. Rio leaned ever so slightly into it, just enough for Agatha to feel.

Wanda saw the whole thing, of course.

She didn’t react.
Didn’t let her hands falter. She flipped the eggs with practiced calm, the sizzle giving her something to anchor to.

She was happy for them. She was.

She’d meant every word she said the night before. But watching it, this, was harder than she expected.

There was something too familiar in the way Rio tilted her head when Agatha murmured something low. Something that pulled at old muscle memory.

Wanda exhaled softly through her nose.

Kept her eyes on the pan.

Agatha took the mug Rio handed her without looking, their fingers brushing. Her hand lingered a second longer than necessary, thumb grazing across Rio’s palm like punctuation.

No one said anything.

Jen turned a page in her book.

Lilia kept humming.

Wanda plated the eggs.

The silence felt… fine. Not tense. Not heavy. Just thick with things unspoken, as all quiet love is.

She brought the plate over, setting tit down in front of Agatha with a simple, “Eat while it’s hot.”

“Thanks,” Agatha said gently but didn’t quite meet Wanda’s gaze. She didn’t need to. The gesture had already said enough.

Wanda sat across from them, folding her hands around her mug. She didn’t look at their joined knees under the table.

She didn’t glance at the way Agatha’s pinky hooked softly around Rio’s. She just drank slowly and let the warmth of the mug fill the space the silence left behind.

And then-

The screen door creaked again.

“Hope I didn’t miss all the eggs,” came a familiar voice.

Wanda’s head jerked up a little too fast.

Nat stood in the garden doorway, sunglasses perched on her head, hair windswept and sleep-rumpled. She was wearing one of Wanda’s flannels unbuttoned over a tank top, and grinning like she belonged exactly where she was.

“You’re late,” Wanda said, aiming for neutral but landing somewhere between fond and flustered.

Nat stepped inside with the kind of ease that meant she’d done this before. Many times.

“I made peace with my sins. Thought I’d repent with breakfast.”

Agatha smiled into her tea.

Rio lowered her gaze, biting back a grin.

Wanda didn’t say anything at first, just stood, suddenly restless, and grabbed a plate.

“I saved you some,” she said without turning around.

Nat came up behind her and touched the small of her back, nothing dramatic, nothing for anyone else. Just enough to say: I see you.

Wanda’s shoulders loosened.

She handed Nat the plate, their hands brushing. A faint blush rose up the side of her neck, betraying her composure more than she liked.

Nat pretended not to notice. Or maybe she did, and just didn’t mind.

Wanda sat back down at the table, this time angled ever so slightly toward Nat as she joined them. Not a shift away from Rio and Agatha, just a shift toward something new.

No one mentioned the warmth blooming in the room now, different kinds, different roots. But all of it quietly, deeply real.

And for a while, no one needed to talk.

They just ate.

Together.

The sun had burned off the last of the fog, and the beach behind the house shimmered like something half-remembered from a dream.

The tide was low, the sand warm underfoot, and the sound of crashing waves stitched its way between easy laughter and the rustle of towels being shaken out.

Alice and Billy had arrived midmorning in a flurry of sand and enthusiasm, Billy carrying two surfboards under one arm like it was nothing and Alice already peeling off her shirt with a whoop.

“Waves are good today,” Billy called over his shoulder, already jogging toward the surf.

“Better be,” Alice said, kicking off her shoes.

“I need to erase every dumb thought I’ve had this week via saltwater.”

“That’s what therapy’s for,” Jen muttered, reclining deeper into her beach chair.

“Saltwater is cheaper,” Alice shot back, grinning.

Agatha was still fussing with the blanket she and Rio had set down closer to the dunes, far enough from the tide to stay dry, close enough for Rio to hear the ocean.

Agatha kept adjusting the umbrella, nudging it this way and that like it might shield Rio from the sun and the sky and the fragility of the world itself.

Rio sat cross-legged on the blanket, sunglasses perched on her nose, a soft smile tugging at the corner of her mouth. She was letting her head rest back, the breeze tugging gently at the ends of her hair.

“I’m fine, you know,” she said, as Agatha tucked a water bottle beside her and refolded the spare towel for the third time.

“You say that like I haven’t seen you unconscious twice this weekend.”

Rio laughed, quiet and warm. “You gonna hover the whole time?”

“Yes,” Agatha said simply, kneeling beside her now. “I am. You’re not allowed to complain about it.”

“I’m not complaining.”

Agatha glanced over, her gaze lingering, lips soft, eyes scanning her face like she was memorising it for the hundredth time.

“Good,” she said.

She reached out, not quite thinking, and brushed a loose strand of hair from Rio’s cheek.

Her fingers lingered just a second too long. Rio caught her hand and held it there, resting it lightly against her jaw, then turned and kissed her palm.

They both froze for a heartbeat, not out of uncertainty, but reverence. Like the moment was holy.

Across the beach, Lilia lay stretched out on a striped towel beside Wanda and Jen, a wide straw hat covering her face.

Jen was flipping lazily through a magazine, sunglasses hiding the fact that she was mostly watching the others.

Wanda had her eyes closed, arms behind her head, trying to soak in the sun without letting her thoughts wander too far toward the blanket near the dunes.

She heard the laugh before she opened her eyes. Rio again.

And then Agatha’s voice, low and fond and sharp at the edges.

Wanda smiled to herself, small, tired, true.

She could feel Nat nearby without looking.

The occasional glint of sun off wet shoulders. The sound of her board skimming across the surf. Wanda didn’t have to watch her to feel anchored.

Still, when she did turn her head and saw Nat paddling back toward the shore, hair slicked back and mouth curled in a satisfied grin, Wanda sat up straighter.

Just a little.

She hoped no one noticed. Especially not Jen, who definitely noticed.

Alice was shouting something to Billy from out in the waves, something competitive and deeply unserious.

He responded with a whoop and a splash that drenched them both. Lilia lifted the brim of her hat briefly and called out a warning about riptides. Neither of them listened.

“God, they’re like puppies,” Jen muttered.

“They’re happy,” Wanda said, without thinking.

Jen tilted her head, eyes on her. “You okay?”

Wanda nodded once. “I’m getting there.”

A few yards away, Agatha leaned back on one elbow beside Rio, still watching her like she was the sun and the sky and maybe also the reason the ocean stayed where it was.

“You warm enough?” she asked softly.

Rio arched an eyebrow behind her sunglasses. “You’ve asked me that four times.”

“Fifth’s the charm.”

“I’m fine, Aggie, today is a good day.”

Agatha swallowed. “Tell me if that changes.”

“I will,” Rio said. Then reached out and took her hand. “Promise.”

They lay there in silence for a while after that. Just holding hands, side by side on the blanket. Letting the waves talk for them. Letting the salt settle on their skin. Letting themselves believe this might be one of many days like this.

And it could be.

Maybe.

If they let it.

Farther down the beach, Nat dropped her board and walked up the sand, water streaming down her legs. She passed Wanda, still reclining, and gave her a slight nod.

“You gonna join us?”

Wanda opened one eye. “And risk you all seeing me fall off the board like an uncoordinated giraffe? No thanks.”

Nat grinned. “You say that like it wouldn’t be adorable.”

Wanda flushed. Actually flushed.

Nat kept walking, smirking.

Jen tilted her head behind her sunglasses. “Oh. That’s dangerous.”

Wanda rolled her eyes. “Shut up.”

Lilia just giggled.

The wind had picked up just enough to make the umbrella tilt suspiciously every ten minutes, and Jen was on her fourth attempt to reposition it without cursing. She was failing.

Wanda lay beside her on a towel, one leg bent, nose in a paperback thriller, sunglasses on, pretending not to be invested in Jen’s war with gravity. Nat was out swimming again, effortlessly graceful in the waves like she belonged there. Lilia was nearby, standing on a half-buried cooler for signal. And a few feet away, Agatha was stretched on a towel, one hand shielding her eyes, the other flipping lazily through a magazine.

Her gaze drifted over the group every few minutes, but she was mostly focused on the waves, until a suspiciously long silence made her glance toward the house.

Rio was gone.

That, Agatha thought, is never good.

Ten minutes earlier, Rio had “gone to get more water,” which in Rio-language meant plotting something suspicious while pretending to be helpful.

She’d found an old glitter-infused sunscreen in a basket under the sink, the kind meant for music festivals and wild college beach weekends, and paired it with a mischievous glint that spelled doom for someone.
Maybe two someones.

Targets acquired.

She tiptoed barefoot across the sand, bottle in hand, sunglasses pushed low on her nose like she was sneaking into enemy territory.

Wanda was reclined, deeply focused on her book. Jen had finally wrangled the umbrella into place and was now flat on her stomach, soaking up sun like a solar-powered plant.

Agatha, returning her gaze to the group just in time to spot Rio crouching low behind the towels, frowned deeply.

“No,” she muttered to no one, setting her magazine aside.

But it was too late.

With expert speed, Rio squirted a thick line of shimmering sunscreen directly down both of their backs, one bold stripe each, followed by a dramatic squeaky “ta-da!”

Jen jolted up. “WHAT, was that a jellyfish?!”

Wanda twisted around and froze. “Why am I sparkly?!”

From halfway down the beach, Rio was already sprinting backward, holding the glitter sunscreen bottle aloft like a trophy, cackling.

“You’re welcome!” she shouted. “Now you shine with inner AND outer light!”

Jen stood, inspecting her shoulders in horror. “Rio. What did you do?!”

“Custom makeover!” Rio called. “You’re both ready for Coachella now!”

Wanda looked down at her shimmering chest and arms. “I’m going to kill you.”

Agatha sighed heavily and stood up, brushing sand from her thighs. “Every time,” she said under her breath. “Every single time.”

Jen turned, wide-eyed. “She glitter bombed me!”

“She glitter bombed both of you,” Agatha said dryly. “At least it’s not permanent dye this time.”

“I sparkle,” Wanda said. “I look like a disco ball during Pride Month.”

Agatha pinched the bridge of her nose. “And somehow she’s the one I fell in love with.”

Jen blinked. “Seriously?”

Agatha gave her a flat look. “You’re surprised now?”

Across the beach, Rio stood with both arms in the air like a victorious Olympian.

“Do I regret nothing? YES. Would I do it again? Also yes!”

Wanda was already marching toward her, sunscreen bottle in hand like a weapon.

“Revenge is a dish best served sticky!” she shouted.

“I’ve made worse choices!” Rio yelled, laughing as she bolted down the beach.

Jen followed, shrieking. “I’m going to rub this in your HAIR, RIO!”

Lilia shouted from her cooler perch, “Someone film this!”

Nat finally looked up from the waves, smirking. “Do I even want to know?”

Agatha just sat back down with a long sigh, reaching for her abandoned magazine.

“She’s a menace,” she muttered, but she was smiling, just barely.

A little fond, a little tired. The kind of smile reserved only for someone who’s broken your peace and also holds your heart.

Behind her, Rio dodged a tackle and yelled, “You sparkle because I CARE!”

And Agatha murmured, “I need a drink.”
