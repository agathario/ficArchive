Central Park in the fall was practically showing off. The trees were dressed in every shade of red and gold, and the air smelled crisp. Couples strolled hand in hand, tourists paused for selfies, and somewhere, a saxophonist played a soulful tune that sounded almost too cinematic to be real.

But it wasn’t —it was simply Manhattan. Across the park, the West Side hummed with its own rhythm: Broadway was busy as always, its sidewalks lined with people darting between boutique gyms, shiny stores with even shinier price tags, and movie theaters advertising the latest superhero sequel no one had asked for. Delivery bikes zipped by in tight packs, dodging pedestrians with precision.

A few streets further, the noise dulled to a buzz. Through one of the brownstone’s windows, the morning light spilled lazily, illuminating the cozy world of the thirty-eight year old Rio Vidal.

She was still curled up in bed, her dark hair fanned out across the pillow, one hand flung dramatically over her eyes as if warding off the inevitable pull of the day.

She wasn't a morning person.

Her bedroom was an explosion of comfort and character: a queen-sized bed, a desk cluttered with notes and a computer, and bookshelves that seemed to groan under the weight of their contents.

When Rio finally stirred, it was with an exaggerated stretch.

She got up and padded to the bathroom in her pajamas—an ancient, oversized T-shirt that might have once belonged to an ex.

The living room greeted her. In the corner, her trusty espresso machine waited like a soldier at its post.

Cup in hand, she tiptoed to her laptop, her movements almost giddy. This was her secret morning ritual.

She opened the lid, fingers hovering over the keyboard, anticipation lighting her face.

The screen blinked to life.

"You've got mail."

A smile tugged at her lips. The familiar chime never failed to delight her, even if most of her inbox was cluttered with promises of miracle wealth and dubious self-improvement schemes. With the efficiency of a woman on a mission, Rio deleted the junk— “Big Cash Op,” “Oil Mkt,” and a particularly enthusiastic “THIS REALLY WORKS!” —until only one email remained.

"DTWR: Señor Scratchy."

She clicked it, settling back as she began to read.

“Señor Scratchy is my rabbit. I can’t believe I never told you about him. He loves the streets of New York as much as I do, but I can't let him enjoy it aside from through the window ....”

On the other side of town, Agatha Harkness was pouring herself a cup of tea, wearing a silk robe trailing. Her long, brown hair fell in waves over her shoulders, and her kitchen—minimal but chic—was bathed in the soft light of mid-morning.

At her feet, lounging on a purple pillow embroidered with his name, was Señor Scratchy, her rabbit and self-proclaimed ruler of the apartment.

Agatha smiled as she glanced at him, setting her mug down on the counter. She sat at her table, laptop open, the gentle hum of the city in the background. As her fingers danced over the keyboard, she thought of her unlikely pen pal, her Green Witch, and smiled.

"Don’t you love New York in the fall? It makes me want to buy school supplies. I’d send you a bouquet of newly-sharpened pencils if I knew your name and address,” she typed. “On the other hand, this not knowing has its charms."

The unmistakable sound of tiny feet pounding down the hall shattered the quiet.

Agatha barely had time to lift her mug before her eight-year-old son, Nicky, burst into the kitchen, his dark hair bouncing with the kind of energy reserved for children.

“Mom,” he declared, his tone serious, “we need to talk about breakfast.”

Agatha raised a perfectly arched brow. “Good morning to you, too, Nicholas.”

She gestured toward Señor Scratchy, still splayed on his pillow. “Perhaps you’d like to take a page from his book and try lounging for once?”

Nicky crossed his arms, unconvinced. “Scratchy doesn’t have homework. Or a science fair project. Or a mom who forgets to buy cereal.”

Agatha let out a dramatic gasp, clutching her robe as if he’d wounded her. “I bought cereal last week. That organic, non-GMO, prebiotic thing you begged me to try.”

“And it tasted like cardboard,” Nicky shot back, unimpressed. “We had to feed it to Scratchy, remember? He didn’t even like it.”

Agatha glanced at the rabbit, who flicked an ear as if in agreement.

“Fine,” she relented, leaning back in her chair. “What’s your brilliant solution?”

“I was thinking pancakes,” Nicky said, climbing onto a chair across from her. “But only if you don’t try to sneak in chia seeds this time. I’m wise to your tricks, Mom.”

Agatha couldn’t help but laugh. Nicky had inherited her wit, her sharp mind, and, much to her occasional annoyance, her stubborn streak. He was the kind of kid who could charm his way into—or out of—almost anything.

“Pancakes it is,” she said, standing up and stretching like she was preparing for a marathon. “But don’t get used to this. I have emails to answer, a rabbit to placate, and a life, you know.”

“Right, your ‘pen pal,’” Nicky teased, smirking. “Are you sure this isn’t just a catfish situation? Or, uh, I guess a rabbitfish ?”

Agatha froze mid-step, turning to give him a look of mock outrage. “Who taught you what a catfish is?”

“The internet,” he said casually, then added with a shrug, “and probably you. You teach me everything, Mom.”

It was moments like these that made Agatha’s exasperation dissolve into something warmer. She leaned down and kissed the top of his head, even as he squirmed and protested.

“Keep it up, wise guy, and you’re making the pancakes.”

Nicky grinned. “I’ll grab the whisk.”

The corner of 83rd Street greeted Rio with the soft chaos of the West Side waking up: parents ushering kids toward school—some by hand, others from behind the tinted windows of SUVs (some even in SUV’s by personal drivers, as was the case for Nicky)—dogs pulling on leashes, and delivery trucks parked with hazard lights blinking.

Rio paused at the newsstand on her route. The vendor greeted her with a nod, and she returned it with a soft smile, reaching for The New York Times . Her fingers lingered briefly on the cover as she scanned the headlines before tucking it under her arm and continuing her walk downtown.

Behind her, a block or so away, Agatha moved with her usual blend of purpose and flair.

She wore sunglasses big enough to suggest she might have been a celebrity—if not for the fact that she was juggling a pile of newspapers. She scooped up the Times , The Wall Street Journal , The Post , and The Daily News with the efficiency of someone who thrived on knowing everything.

The West Side unfolded before them as they both made their way down Broadway, unaware of each other.

Rio stopped to buy a black coffee at her usual spot. Across the street, Agatha lingered just long enough to pick up a bouquet of flowers—a mix of roses and eucalyptus—before vanishing into a side entrance of a building wrapped in plywood and scaffolding.

Meanwhile, Rio turned onto Columbus Avenue, coffee in one hand, her paper in the other. The city moved around her in its noisy, unpredictable symphony: cabs honking, construction hammering, and the distant chatter of pedestrians.

When she reached the corner of 73rd Street, she stopped in front of her store.

The Shop Around the Corner was exactly the kind of place that could make someone stop and smile. Twinkle lights framed the large glass windows, which were filled with displays of stuffed animals posed with books as if they’d been caught mid-read. A plush bunny was nose-deep in Goodnight Moon , a lion flipped through Where the Wild Things Are , and a teddy bear, wearing a charming pinafore, sat proudly with The Stupids Step Out .

Despite her slightly mysterious demeanor, this delightful bookstore was all Rio’s doing. She loved it as much as she pretended not to.

“Hey, Alice,” Rio called to the young woman waiting by the door, hands shoved into her pockets. Alice worked part-time at the store.

“Good morning” Alice replied, unsure, perpetually confused by Rio’s moods. She glanced up at the sky, which was crisp and clear, then back at Rio, as if searching for confirmation that this was, indeed, a beautiful day.

With a horrible metallic screech, Rio cranked up the grate over the storefront. The noise was loud enough to compete with the two cabs that nearly collided in front of the store, their drivers immediately launching into a shouting match that only New Yorkers could deliver with such poetic profanity.

Rio glanced at the chaos with a shrug. “Don’t you just love New York in the fall?” she grinned.

She flipped the sign on the door from CLOSED to OPEN , booted up the system, and sipped her coffee with the satisfaction of someone settling into their favorite routine.

Inside The Shop Around the Corner , the atmosphere was as warm and inviting as ever. The wooden shelves, lovingly scuffed with use, stretched from floor to ceiling, filled with books that beckoned readers of all ages. A cozy reading nook for kids, complete with beanbags and tiny chairs, nestled in one corner. Posters of classic children’s book covers hung on the walls, lending a playful touch, while a glass case near the counter displayed treasures: first editions of The Wizard of Oz series and Alice in Wonderland.

The highlight of the store was Rio’s pride and joy: a whimsical witch-themed display framed with pumpkin-shaped twinkle lights. A sign reading “The Ten Best Witch List” floated above an artful arrangement of books: The Lion, the Witch, and the Wardrobe , The Wizard of Oz, The Witches , and of course, The Legend of the Christmas Witch.

The counter held a glass jar brimming with green apple and blackberry lollipops, a siren call to children and adults alike.

Rio hung up her black coat in the back, her hand pausing mid-motion as a dreamy smile played on her lips.

“Okay, what’s going on with you?” Alice’s voice snapped her out of her thoughts. She leaned against a bookshelf, arms crossed and an eyebrow raised.

“Nothing,” Rio said, far too quickly to be convincing.

Alice smirked. “You’re in love.”

Rio’s head whipped around. “In love? No. That’s crazy. Do you think you could get the Christmas mailers out this week?”

“By Monday, I promise,” Alice said breezily. “But I have a paper due Friday. Now spill. What’s going on?” She narrowed her eyes, tilting her head in a way that made it clear she wasn’t moving until she had answers.

Rio sighed, rubbing her temples. “Ugh, fine. Remember the woman I told you about? The one I’ve been emailing?”

Alice’s face lit up with mischief. “Have you had sex?”

“No!” Rio spluttered. “I don’t even know her!”

“I mean Zoom sex, or phone sex,” Alice said, unbothered.

“No, Alice, what the fuck.”

Alice shrugged like it was no big deal. “Well, don’t do it. The minute you do, they lose all respect for you.”

Rio groaned, running a hand through her hair. “It’s not like that. We just... email. It’s nothing. And I’ve been thinking about stopping because it’s getting—”

“Out of hand?”

“Confusing,” Rio admitted. “But also not really. Because it’s nothing .”

Alice gave her a knowing look. “Where’d you even meet her?”

Rio hesitated, then smiled faintly. “The day I turned thirty-five, I wandered into one of those Over Thirty chat rooms for a laugh. She was there, and we started talking.”

“About what?”

“Books. Music. How much we both love New York.” Her voice softened, and a small smirk crept across her lips. “Bouquets of sharpened pencils.”

Alice blinked. “Excuse me?”

“Forget it.” Rio waved it off quickly. “We don’t talk about anything personal. That’s the rule. I don’t know her name, what she does, or exactly where she lives. So it’s easy to stop seeing her, because... I’m not, technically.”

Alice raised an eyebrow, unimpressed. “She could literally be the next person to walk through the door.” She glanced toward the entrance. “She could be...” She paused dramatically as the bell above the door jingled. “...Jen.”

Jennifer Kale strode into the shop with her usual confidence. In her thirties, Jen had a sharp tongue, a kind heart, and a wardrobe that made her look like she’d stepped out of a trendy catalog.

“Morning,” Jen said, setting her bag down behind the counter.

“Have you ever had an online relationship, Jen?” Alice asked, skipping pleasantries entirely.

Jen gave her a flat look. “Hello to you, too. And no. The internet is just another way to get rejected by women.”

Before anyone could respond, Lilia bustled in. At seventy-two, Lilia had an air of delightful chaos about her, from her salt-and-pepper curls to the jangling bracelets that never seemed to stay in place. She had been part of the shop longer than anyone and could outsmart them all without breaking a sweat.

“Good morning, Lilia,” Rio said warmly.

“What are we talking about?” Lilia asked, dropping her bag on the counter.

“Phone sex,” Alice deadpanned.

Lilia didn’t miss a beat. “I tried that once, but I kept muting myself.”

Jen laughed, and even Rio cracked a smile.

The bell jingled again, and this time, two West Side moms entered, each pushing a stroller.

Rio turned to greet the kids. “Jess and Maia, how are you today?”

Before they could answer, the rumble of a garbage truck outside caught Rio’s attention. She excused herself and stepped out the front door.

“Hey!” Rio called out, arms crossed as she watched the two garbage men hoist trash bags into the truck. “You forgot to pick up the garbage last week, and I got a ticket. Now you’re late today, and I could’ve gotten another.”

The first garbage man looked up, clearly unimpressed. “We were here. There wasn’t any garbage.”

“Yeah,” the second one echoed.

Rio gave them both a pointed look, clicking her tongue.

“What do you think?” the first man continued, exasperated. “I don’t want to pick up garbage? You think I go up and down the street picking up garbage and just skip yours for fun?”

Rio raised an eyebrow, her stance unwavering.

The second man nudged his partner, muttering something under his breath. They hurriedly finished the job, throwing the last bag into the truck as Rio watched, satisfied.

Back inside, the shop hummed with life again, the comforting chaos of books, laughter, and the city moving just as it always did.

The site was a whirlwind of activity. Electricians balanced on ladders, their tools clinking as they worked on tangled bundles of wire. Masons hollered instructions as they mixed mortar, and a faint hum of machinery underpinned it all.

Agatha’s assistant, Billy, clipboard in hand and looking perpetually stressed, ducked around a pile of drywall to meet Agatha. She stood at the center of the site, sharp-eyed and poised. Still dressed impeccably, of course.

“The electrical contractor called,” Billy began, barely taking a breath. “His truck hit a deer last night, so he’s out until tomorrow. The shelves are delayed because the shipment of pine had beetles. And there’s, uh, some debate about whether the stairs are going in the right spot—”

“That sounds fantastic,” Agatha interrupted breezily, her tone as dry as the dust in the air.

Billy frowned, his train of thought thrown. “Testing one, two, three, four.”

Agatha tilted her head. “Is the electrician here?”

Billy blinked. “I just told you—his truck hit a deer!”

Agatha didn’t seem to register his exasperation. Instead, she looked up at the exposed beams. “I hear nothing. Not a sound from the city streets. Just the beat of my own heart.” She paused, her gaze distant. “I think that’s how it goes... Something like that.”

Billy’s face shifted, suspicion dawning. “Have you met someone?”

Agatha shot him a sharp look. “What do you mean?”

He smirked. “Well, I’m getting a certain vibe from you—”

“I don’t give vibes,” she snapped, immediately pivoting to business. “Are we still on schedule?”

Billy let it go, falling back into his comfort zone of logistics. “We’re set to open two weeks before Thanksgiving.”

“Then we should announce ourselves soon,” Agatha said, her voice firm. “Let people know we’re coming.”

Billy chuckled. “This is the Upper West Side of Manhattan. The minute they hear, they’ll be lining up to picket the big bad chain store—”

“—that destroys everything we hold dear,” Agatha finished. Her lips quirked into a wry smile as she leaned toward Billy.

“But we’ll seduce them, won’t we? With our square footage, our deep armchairs, our lightning-fast checkout lines, our unbeatable discounts, and our...”

“...oat cappuccinos.”

Agatha folded her arms, satisfied. “They’ll hate us in the beginning, but we’ll win them over in the end.”

The sleek, corporate office hummed with quiet efficiency. The PurpleBooks logo, modern and imposing, dominated the wall behind Agatha and her colleague Herb in her glass-walled office.

“I am a little concerned about the neighborhood response,” Agatha began.

Herb leaned back in his chair with a smirk. “I have some very sad news,” he said, feigning gravity. “City Books on 23rd Street is going under.”

“Another independent bookstore bites the dust,” Agatha said, her tone casual as she made a note on her tablet.

“On to the next,” Herb replied with a grin.

“And I’m buying their entire inventory,” Agatha added, not missing a beat. “Architecture, New York history—it’ll make a perfect addition to the new store.”

Herb raised an eyebrow. “How much are we paying for that?”

“Whatever it costs,” she said, waving off his concern. “We’re also adding a section on West Side writers to appease the locals.”

“Right,” Herb said, unbothered. “Readers.”

Agatha tapped her pen thoughtfully. “There’s one mystery store—Sleuth, on 86th and Amsterdam. And a children’s bookstore. The Shop Around the Corner. I always went there as a kid... It's been there forever.”

Herb smirked, leaning forward. “Not for long.”

Agatha didn’t respond, but a flicker of something crossed her face. She quickly pushed the uncomfortable feeling down.

Rio looked up from her phone as something caught her eye—a butterfly. In the gritty subway car, it seemed impossibly out of place, its delicate wings a stark contrast to the metal and grime. “Once I read a story about a butterfly in the subway, and today I saw one. I couldn’t believe it. It got on at 42nd—”

The subway slowed, screeching to a stop. The butterfly hovered near the door before gliding out into the open air.

“—and got off at 59th, where I assumed it was going to Bloomingdale’s to buy a hat.”

She tucked her phone away, grinning.

On the corner by H&H Bagels, the flour truck rumbled to life, its engines loud enough to break the quiet of the early morning. The chute stretched from the truck, pouring a steady stream of fine white powder into the ground, like some strange ritual. The air filled with a soft, yeasty haze. Agatha paused as she rounded the corner, breathing deeply. The scent of baking bread surrounded her.

She drafted in her notes: “Did you know that every night a truck pulls up to H&H Bagels and pumps about a ton of flour into the ground? The air is absolutely amazing.”

Agatha stood in the kitchen, absentmindedly stirring her tea as she watched the scene unfold in the living room.

She had intended to get some work done, or at least read a few emails, but the sound of laughter kept pulling her back to the present.

The warmth of it, the pure ease with which Rio and Nicky interacted, made it impossible to focus on anything else.

It was an intricate cityscape—one of Nicky’s favorites—but tonight, it wasn’t about finishing the puzzle.

It was about the way Nicky kept trying to dramatically explain the ‘architectural importance’ of each piece, a skill he had clearly picked up from a documentary he’d been obsessed with last week.

Rio, playing along, would nod with exaggerated seriousness, occasionally offering completely incorrect facts just to make Nicky argue with her.

“You see, this tower is actually made entirely of spaghetti,” Rio said, holding up a piece that definitely did not look like pasta.

Nicky scoffed, hands on his hips. “That doesn’t even make sense! That would never hold up in real life.”

Rio grinned. “Ah, but what if it was magic spaghetti? Ever thought about that, genius?”

Agatha covered her mouth to keep from laughing. Nicky rolled his eyes, but she could see the way he was biting back a smile.

“You’re impossible,” he said, shaking his head before turning back to the puzzle.

“And yet, here I am, in your home, invited ,” Rio teased, tapping a piece into place. “Guess you’re stuck with me now.”

Agatha felt something deep in her chest pull tight. It was a strange thing, watching someone fit into your life so seamlessly. She had been cautious, hesitant even, about letting someone get too close to Nicky.

But Rio had slipped in with the kind of ease that made it seem like she had always been here, kneeling on the floor, challenging Nicky’s eight-year-old logic with her own ridiculous arguments.

And now she was never going to let her go, obviously.

She found herself gripping her mug a little tighter, overwhelmed by how much she loved this—loved them .

As if sensing her watching, Rio turned and caught Agatha’s gaze.

Her grin softened, her expression shifting into something warmer.

“Hey, Ms. Harkness,” she called. “Wanna join, or are you just going to stand there being all mysterious?”

Agatha raised an eyebrow, pretending to consider it. “Oh, I wouldn’t dare intrude on such serious architectural discussions.”

“She’s just scared she’ll lose to me,” Nicky said smugly. “Rio already lost twice.”

“That is slander!” Rio shot back. “I let you win. It’s called being a gracious opponent.”

“Sure, sure.”

Agatha let out a soft sigh and shook her head, walking over to settle on the couch. “I’m fine right here, thanks. But do continue—this is highly entertaining.”

Nicky beamed, and Rio shot her a wink before dramatically cracking her knuckles and focusing back on the puzzle.

Rio blinked. “Uh. No, actually. Do you ?”

Nicky nodded proudly. “Yeah, Sharon taught me. But only with, like, small things. Wanna learn?”

Rio’s face lit up in exaggerated enthusiasm. “Do I? Obviously.”

He scrambled to his feet and ran toward his toy box, rummaging through it before returning with three small beanbags. “Okay, so you start with just two, like this—” He tossed them up, catching them with ease. “Then you add the third one.”

Rio attempted to copy him, but as soon as she tried to throw the third one, all three dropped to the floor in a spectacularly uncoordinated display.

Nicky howled with laughter. “That was so bad .”

Rio clutched her chest dramatically. “Wow. The betrayal. I thought you were my teacher .”

“I am! But you have to actually listen.”

Agatha, now leaning against the armrest of the couch, watched the exchange with a kind of quiet amusement she rarely allowed herself.

Eventually, the juggling attempt dissolved into a mini pillow fight, and by the time they settled down, Nicky was curled up in Rio’s lap, yawning.

Agatha rose from her spot and walked over, brushing a hand over Nicky’s hair.

“Bedtime,” she murmured.

Nicky groaned but didn’t resist as she helped him up. He glanced sleepily at Rio. “Can we finish the puzzle tomorrow?”

“Obviously,” Rio said. “We still have to decide if the spaghetti tower is real.”

The morning was crisp, with a gentle breeze carrying the scents of the city, mingling with the sweet aroma of fresh coffee wafting out of the café across the street.

The buzz of excitement filled the air around The Shop Around the Corner, and a long line of familiar faces had already gathered outside.

As the ribbon-cutting ceremony neared, Rio stood by the counter, trying to ignore the fluttering in her chest.

The shop had never looked better—its storefront gleaming with a fresh coat of paint, shelves stacked with books, and soft lights casting a cozy glow over everything.

The first to arrive was Alice, predictably carrying two steaming takeout cups.

“I brought caffeine reinforcements,” she declared, sliding one across the counter.

“You look like you’re about to faint, and we can’t have that on your big day.”

Jen arrived soon after, sliding her arm around Alice’s waist with practiced ease.

“I’ll admit it looks good,” she said, her tone begrudging but playful. “Even if it has the faint stink of PurpleBooks,” she winked.

Alice nudged her. “You are the one that worked there.”

“Did she tell you she barely slept last night?” Jen said to Rio, quirking an eyebrow at Alice. “Too busy making sure you had ‘optimal caffeine levels’ today.”

Alice shot her a look. “You’re just mad I didn’t get your order.”

“I am personally victimized by that decision, yes,” Jen deadpanned, stealing a sip from Alice’s cup anyway.

Rio blinked at them. “Are you guys still hard launching at one point, or are you just waiting until everyone notices?”

Jen and Alice exchanged glances, before Alice grinned. “We are together. Boom.”

Lilia entered just in time to witness the outburst, a delighted grin spreading across her face. “Oh my God. You finally admitted it?”

Alice rolled her eyes. “Why does everyone act like this is such a big thing?”

“Because it is ,” Rio muttered. She and Agatha had been theorizing and making fun of them for a while—they at least deserved a proper confession.

Before Rio could respond, a burst of chatter at the entrance signaled the arrival of Agatha and Billy, the latter carrying a clipboard and looking thoroughly amused.

Agatha herself was dressed impeccably, her dark blazer sharp enough to cut glass, but her expression surprisingly soft as her gaze swept the shop.

Her eyes met Rio’s immediately, sparkling with affection.

A quick, almost imperceptible smile flickered across her lips.

"You look right where you belong," Agatha said, her voice warm as she stepped closer, fingers brushing Rio’s.

Billy cleared his throat from behind them. “If you two are done with the romantic gestures,” he said, “we’ve got an audience.”

Agatha shot him a glare before stepping up to the front of the shop, taking her place at the makeshift podium for the opening speech.

As the crowd quieted, Rio felt the familiar presence of her friends surrounding her, each person in the room a crucial part of this story, this moment.

Alice and Jen stood close together, hands brushing as they tried to act casual.

Lilia practically vibrated with excitement.

Nicky stood next to Rio, his gaze darting between his mom and the stacks of books he was obviously itching to explore.

“Thank you all for being here today,” Agatha began, her voice carrying easily across the room, “and for being part of this incredible journey. The Shop Around the Corner is not just a business—it’s a community. It’s a place where stories are shared, friendships are made, and where every book on the shelf has the potential to change a life. This shop, the heart of this neighborhood, has a history that deserves to be celebrated, and I am honored to be part of its future.”

Agatha paused and looked toward Rio, her voice softening.

“I’ve had the pleasure of seeing this shop through Rio’s eyes—through her passion for books, for this place, and for all of you. When I first met Rio, I didn’t expect that our paths would cross like this.”

It was true, in more ways everyone could know.

”But over the months, I’ve come to admire not only her unwavering dedication to the store, but her kindness, her ability to make this place feel like home. I can’t think of a better person to run this shop.”

“And I can’t think of anyone better to steal Rio’s heart,” Alice added softly, but audible enough to receive a few snickers.

Rio felt her cheeks warming. Agatha, though, only smiled more, her gaze locking with Rio’s.

Rio nudged Alice. “You’re one to talk, Miss Never Date Inside of the Friend Group.”

“Let me have my moment,” Alice whispered back, barely containing a laugh.

Agatha cleared her throat and suppressed a smirk.

“Okay, okay. I should mention that this shop’s place in our larger community of bookstores is possible because The Shop Around the Corner is now part of PurpleBooks. We’ve come together, and together we’ll make this shop a place that continues to thrive for years to come.”

The bell above the door jingled again. Rio turned, and her eyes widened slightly.

Wanda Maximoff. Of course.

And she wasn’t alone.

Beside her, standing comfortably close, was a tall woman with long brown hair.

“Oh, this is going to be fun,” Jen whispered, barely containing her amusement as she nudged Alice, who only hummed in agreement.

Agatha barely missed a beat, her expression flickering for only a second before she continued.

“Anyway, I want to thank everyone who’s made this moment possible. From Billy and Alice and Jen and Lilia to the rest of our amazing team, to all of you for showing up today, I’m so proud of everything we’ve accomplished. I think we can all agree this is just the beginning.”

At that, Nicky piped up from Rio's side, his voice high and clear.

“And don’t forget—the cookies! Mom said there are lots of cookies!”

“I knew you’d remind me about the snacks,” Agatha said with a proud grin.

She turned to Rio then, who was still standing by the counter, smiling softly.

As the speech wrapped up, Wanda and the woman sauntered over, Wanda’s eyes twinkling with amusement. “Place looks good, Rio. Agatha.”

“Wanda,” Rio said, nodding with a warm smile. “Didn’t expect to see you here.”

It wasn't necessarily that Wanda was unwelcome. She was just.... Well, Wanda.

“I had to see it for myself,” Wanda said smoothly. “It might make a good article. This is Kate, by the way.”

Kate offered Rio and Agatha a friendly nod. “Wanda’s mentioned you.”

Agatha raised an eyebrow. “Has she?”

Kate grinned. “Oh yeah. You two really left an impression.”

Rio couldn’t help but laugh, the absurdity of the situation settling in.

Agatha smirked, finally relaxing, and leaned toward Rio. “Well, at least we know she stays true to her type.”

“I can hear you,” Wanda deadpanned, though there was a glint of amusement in her eyes.

As the crowd slowly began to move toward the back for coffee and snacks, the space alive with conversation and laughter, Rio found herself standing next to Agatha, Nicky, and the rest of her friends.

The weight of the moment wasn’t lost on her—this was something that would stay with her.

Agatha, sensing the quiet shift in Rio’s mood, leaned in close, her breath warm against Rio’s ear.

“I’m proud of you,” Agatha whispered, her voice low, full of soft admiration and affection.

Rio’s heart swelled at the words. She turned toward Agatha, meeting her eyes with a smile that felt like it came from somewhere deeper than just her lips.

“I’m proud of you,” she murmured back, her words wrapped in all the love and gratitude she felt.

It was as if everything they had gone through together had led to this moment—a beautiful, quiet kind of victory that needed no explanation.

Nicky, oblivious to the tenderness between them, tugged excitedly on Agatha’s sleeve, his face lighting up with pure excitement. “Mom, Rio, what do you think is the biggest book in here? Can we look for it?”

Agatha’s smile softened even further, her eyes crinkling at the corners.

She crouched down a little to meet her son’s gaze. “Soon, sweetheart,” she said with a gentle laugh, brushing a stray curl from his face. “We’ll find the perfect one, I promise.”

The bookstore buzzed with energy—the soft clink of coffee cups, the hum of voices exchanging stories, and the lighthearted laughter of people rediscovering the magic of a place they loved.

As Rio stood there, feeling the warmth of Agatha’s hand just barely brushing against hers, she couldn’t help but think that the most beautiful stories were still to come.

DTWR .

"Don’t you just love New York in the fall? It makes me want to buy school supplies. If I didn’t already live with you, I’d send you a bouquet of newly sharpened pencils as an invitation to move in. Lucky me, huh? Billy says hi. I’ll bring home Thai for dinner.”
