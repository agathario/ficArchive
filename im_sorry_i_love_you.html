Summary

Rio Vidal’s first year of teaching elementary school comes with many challenges - challenges that she believes she is well prepared for, or at the very least, able to manage on the sly (or in the worst case scenario, be able to ask her teaching mentor): rowdy students, helicopter parents, classroom management - what have you.

This, however, she’s woefully unprepared for.

Her degree program did not meet this requirement; no training covered this either. She does not know what protocol dictates; her colleagues don’t have the experience either.

For you see, a first grade teacher shouldn’t have to attend the funeral of one of her sweet, sweet little students: Nicholas Harkness.

--
OR: First year elementary school teacher Rio Vidal falls in love with one of the parents of students as a medical diagnosis turns all their lives upside down.

Chapter 1

Chapter Notes

“You had the whole day to do this,” Alice grumbles, daring to stand on her tiptoes on the plastic student chair to better lift and straighten the green paper border of the pinboard - one of three that still needed to be decorated before tomorrow’s start of the school year. “ How are you so behind ? Ah, there! How’s it look?” 

 

From the opposite side of the classroom, Rio tilts her head and wrinkles her nose, inspecting the newly framed pinboard. “I don’t know if it’s completely straight,” she comments, mostly in jest. 

 

“What do you know about straight?” Alice retorts back, hopping down from the chair.

 

“Just about as much as you,” Rio quips back as Alice crosses the room to inspect the work as well. “Thank you for coming to help out - best roommate ever.”

 

Alice snorts as she leans against the wall with her arms folded over her chest, “Don’t try to talk me up, Vidal - I’m already here and I’m not going anywhere. Plus, I know you’d do the same if, you know, I was forced to decorate my own highschool classroom. I could never do elementary..”

 

“ I don’t think I could ever do high school,” Rio replies in earnest. She barely tolerated the experience the first time around. She couldn’t imagine having another go at it. “I’m also your ride back to the apartment, so you can’t go anywhere even if you wanted to.”

 

Rio grabs a dark green poster board from the small pile spread across a group of four student desks. The room is filled with 24 of them. Twenty-four , she’s counted an embarrassing amount of times, as if each count could numb her to that fact. 24 empty desks that tomorrow morning - bright and early - would be filled with 24 students.

 

She isn’t a stranger to a classroom. Her student teaching went swimmingly well, but from tomorrow on, she will be a teacher teacher. No safety net. It would be all on her. She will be the adult in the room with twenty freaking four children that belonged to families that cared about them and that will entrust Rio with them.

 

The thought sits as a nervous pit in her stomach, one that has been present for the last few weeks for one reason or another - mostly work related.

 

Mostly . 

 

Well, Rio guesses one could argue it was all work related, just not all related to the same job. 

 

After graduating at the beginning of the summer season, Rio gifted herself a trip backpacking through different countries in Latin America, one that brought her many cherished memories and, well, a considerable amount of debt that would not wait for her first teaching paycheck that she knew would be abysmal. 

 

So, she applied - and was hired - at one of the local lgbt+ cafes that doubled as a bar during the evening hours. The range of shifts also meant she could maybe balance it with the teaching, or that is what Rio hopes, anyway. It would be a shame to give it up so soon, especially when she needs the money.

 

And maybe it would also be a shame to give up seeing one of her morning regulars: a dark-haired woman always impeccably dressed with the most piercing blue eyes who refuses to give up her name. The first time Rio asked for it to scribble on the cup, the woman simply replied, “No.”

 

Rio’s interest had been piqued. Not to mention, watching the woman smirk as Rio actually did call out “No” in a crowded morning cafe did make Rio rather proud of herself. The confusion from the other customers had been worth it.

 

The order itself is simple enough - one that Rio memorized right away: four shots of espresso with a splash of oat milk (Rio doesn’t judge the amount of caffeine consumption among her customers, not when her tips relied on said caffeine). She could easily have it made before the woman reached the counter, but what fun would that be? She’d miss out on all the not-her-actual-names that Rio kept asking for.

 

“ In a hurry.”

 

“You think you’re funny.” 

 

“This is ridiculous.”

 

“ You’re ridiculous.” 

 

“That witch.”

 

“Scratchy.” 

 

“Señor Scratchy.” Rio commented on the lack of originality and then was impressed the woman didn’t jump the counter to strangle her. Her glare had revealed that intention to Rio. 

 

Yesterday was Rio’s last day until the following Saturday. Jen, the owner, gave her the time off so she could adjust to her teaching schedule. They would meet before her next shift to determine what her shifts would look like in the future.

 

So, this also meant that yesterday would be the last day Rio could see this mystery woman until next week. Rio didn’t wait for her to reach the counter to put in her order. Instead, it was ready when she approached. Rio didn’t write down a name - she didn’t have one to write.

 

In its usual place, Rio wrote down her number. The woman paused when she saw it, making Rio’s heart drop. Rio couldn’t read her, but there was no emotion conveyed to be read - a complete neutral expression. 

 

But Rio couldn’t help but smile when she realized that this woman left her double the amount of tip than usual. Whether it was for the impeccable service or the number, the verdict was out. She hoped it was a bit of both.

 

Well, a bit more of one than the other, if Rio were to be completely truthful with herself.

 

Rio was met with the first text when she woke up today. That adrenaline rush was better than any cup of coffee she’s ever drank.

 

Missed you this morning. Coffee was burnt.

 

Oh, I’ll let Jen know the roaster’s on the fritz.

 

Personally, I think it’s the lack of a beautiful barista .

 

The text throughout the day. It distracts Rio from the self imposed task she reserved for this Sunday: finish decorating her new classroom at Westview Elementary. By sundown, she realizes she needs to call in reinforcements - hence Alice currently helping her cover the last bulletin board with the dark green poster board. 

 

“Plus, considering your track record with setting your classroom on fire, it’s probably for the best there’s less inflammable objects in that science lab,” Rio comments, taping the last of the poster boards in place.

 

“ Listen , it wasn’t the classroom - it was just my grade book,” Alice defends herself with a huff. “And it only happened twice! Also, I should really get credit for ensuring the district’s fire extinguishers are not expired.” 

 

“It shouldn’t have happened once!”

 

“Don’t try to stifle young science innovation, Rio.”

 

“And who still uses a physical grade book anyway?”

 

“You know, I don’t think the walk to the apartment is actually that long.” 

 

“No, no, no, I’m sorry!” Rio apologizes, looking over her shoulder towards Alice who was staring back at her with her hands on her hips. “Those freshmen love you.” 

 

“Those freshmen are budding arsonists - do you know how easy it is to turn a bunsen burner into a flamethrower?” Alice asks. 

 

Rio bites back the comment she wants to make, that Alice is probably the one who showed them all how to do it. Sensing the thin line she’s already walking, Rio, instead, gestures towards the canvas bag on her desk at the front of the room. “The stuff for the welcoming board is in there,” she directs.

 

“Ugh, a welcoming board , how elementary,” Alice groans, dragging herself over to the desk. “I could never .”

 

“It’s going to be a flower vase,” Rio continues, knowing it would further Alice’s displeasure. “They’ll make flower petals tomorrow - paper cut outs of their hands. Ain’t that cute ? What are you doing tomorrow?”

 

“Setting my grade book on fire,” Alice answers nonchalantly. “Gotta start my third year memorably, you know?”

 

“ Alice .” 

 

“Hey, a Señor Scratchy is texting you?” Alice says with a hint of confusion, making Rio immediately turn around toward the other woman hovering over her desk with the canvas bag in hand. Rio left her phone next to it, apparently (honestly, she had lost track of it). “They wanna know if they’re seeing you tomorrow morning? Who the hell is Señor Scratchy?” 

 

In the blink of an eye, Rio is at her desk and snatching her phone to tuck away into her back pocket. She will text back later, explaining that her next shift isn’t until the weekend. “That’s.. It’s - she’s,” she struggles, grabbing the canvas bag from Alice. 

 

The stutter is enough for Alice to piece the pieces together. After all, Rio had informed her of yesterday’s plan. 

 

“Oh, my god , is that the mystery woman from the cafe?” Alice asks. She adds after a beat, “Oh, my god, is that why you’re behind with this decorating? Rio . Spill.” 

 

“Please, shut up and help me,” Rio begs. She walks back over to the board and sets the canvas bag on the floor before fishing out the necessary materials to create the craft she envisioned (or, rather, stole from one of the first year teacher social media accounts she had mass followed).

 

Alice follows at her heels, “I’ll help if you tell me everything . What’s her name?”

 

“I don’t know,” Rio admits, handing her the pieces of construction paper they would soon structure into a 2-D vase.

 

“You still don’t know?” Alice gasps in genuine shock. “What do you mean you still don’t know-” 

 

“There seems to be a lot of commotion in here for school after hours.”

 

The sudden new voice causes both of them to jump. In said commotion, both failed to hear the footsteps coming up the hallway and pausing outside the open doorway to Rio’s classroom. The older woman leans against the doorframe. How long had she been standing there? How much did she hear?

 

Rio would never get those answers; she didn’t want to ask.

 

“Ms. Calderu,” Rio says, breathless, as she pulls herself up onto her feet. “I’m sorry, we didn’t mean-” 

 

“Rio, you are allowed to address me by my first name now, since we are officially colleagues and all.”

 

“Lilia,” Rio corrects herself. Her former first grade teacher’s name still feels and sounds very foreign to her. Part of her believes it will never feel natural. The fact that Lilia is also her teaching mentor doesn’t help either. Isn’t that just another student-teacher relationship with more complicated layers? For the record, however, Rio wouldn’t want anyone else as a mentor.

 

“What are you doing here?” Rio asks.

 

“My dear, I could ask the same of both of you,” Lilia answers, straightening up. On the day before the  start of the school year, it is a very unnecessary question. All three teachers know why they are here. Well, Lilia probably has to guess why one of the high school science teachers is here, but there’s not enough wonder to earn an actual question. Lilia takes a step inside the classroom, gives it a glance over, and then offers Rio a nod of approval before handing her a sheet of paper.

 

“What is this?” Rio asks with a frown, giving the sheet a look over. It looked like her classlist - like the one she has on her desk, already printed.

 

“Your new classlist.”

 

“My new what?” Rio’s head snaps up. “What do you mean ‘new?’ A new one again ?” 

 

Lilia sighs, shoulders falling slightly, as she shakes her head, “Welcome to public education, Rio.. I tried to reason with her, but there is no reasoning with Agatha Harkness.”

 

“With who?” Rio asks, eyebrows furrowing in further confusion. Who is this Agatha Harkness and why should Rio care? Why is she messing with her classlist? 

 

“Her son, Nicholas, was registered in my first grade class,” Lilia explains. “But we both know how that situation went.” 

 

Ah, yes. 

 

At the rather last minute, the new kindergarten teacher decided not to move to Westview. They didn’t even provide a reason, nor did it seem they were going to inform the district either. Admin only reached out when they failed to show up to the mandatory district wide inservice training, leaving Principal Davis to scramble up a back-up plan.

 

The initial solution involved moving Rio, but Lilia put her foot down. It would be inhumane to do such a thing to a brand new teacher, Lilia defended before offering herself to teach kindergarten - she’s done it before and the prep wouldn’t take her much. The students in her own first grade class were divided among the others, leaving Rio, on top of her new teacher training, and the other first grade teachers to change their classroom set up. 

 

Honestly, it was more preferable than trying to work a whole new grade level curriculum.

 

“But what does that have to do with this?” Rio asks, waving the classlist. “I thought the kids were divided equally.” 

 

“They were, but.. when Agatha noticed I was no longer her son’s teacher, she demanded to know why, asking how admin could allow just anyone to teach him.” 

 

“Oh, helicopter parent,” Alice whispered. “Those are the worst.”

 

“Then why assign him to a literal brand new teacher ?” Rio questions, the panic rising in her voice. She did not need a helicopter parent right out the gate. Couldn’t she get through her first week? Hell, even her first day of school? “The others all have at least five years on me,” she says with more of a whine than she’s proud of. 

 

“Although that is true, I was the one who recommended you, Rio,” Lilia reveals.

 

“Lilia-!” 

 

“I know you, Rio,” Lilia states. Rio can hear the pride in her voice and she would be lying if she says it didn’t pull on her heartstrings. “How hard you’ve worked and how hard you’ve persevered in life. I know you are going to be a great teacher, especially under my own mentoring.” 

 

A smile tugs on the corners of Rio’s lips. She lowers her head in a poor attempt to hide her blush. “Forever humble as always, Lilia,” she mumbles, shaking her head, but it snaps up immediately when the realization hits her.

 

Twenty-five.

 

Not twenty-four.

 

“I have to find another desk,” she states. “I have to change the room-” 

 

“No,” Alice interrupts. “No, absolutely not. Not again. Just add a fifth desk to one of the groups of four and call it a day. Or night, rather.” 

 

“It won’t be equal!” Rio protests.

 

“Groups of five,” Lilia says, nodding. “Unfortunately, the desks won’t be even, but you’ll make it work.”

 

“I thank God everyday I’m not in elementary,” Alice murmurs under her breath.

 

Both Lilia and Rio choose to ignore the comment. 

 

“Wrap up the bulletin board, kiddo, and do call it a night,” Lilia says, finding herself agreeing with Alice. “The room doesn’t have to be perfect, but you should be well-rested.” 

 

Rio gives the classroom one more glance over, already planning how to rearrange the desks, which she will tackle in the morning. “Honestly, I think I’m too nervous to sleep,” she admits.

 

Lilia can’t help but smile. “I will let you in on a little secret - one veteran teacher to a new one: you will always be nervous about the first day of school. The nerves prove that you care.” 

 

—

 

As predicted, sleep evades Rio for most of the night, leaving her staring up at her ceiling for much longer than she cares to admit. She manages maybe a couple of hours, but not enough to classify herself as ‘rested,’ let alone ‘well rested.’

 

She gets to school early. It’s enough time to put up the finishing touches of the decorations, an attempt to really pull the overall theme of plants and growth together to match with their first science unit. She’ll change it as they proceed with their units, but for now it was.. mostly perfect. A part of her wants to set up a line of plants along the window seals.

 

Then again, Rio would find any possible reason to buy herself new plants - debt be damned.

 

She manages to finish rearranging the desks and creating Nicholas’ nametag to tap on his own just a few minutes before the twenty-five children walked into their new classroom, toting along backpacks that were far too big for their little bodies. 

 

Rio watches them and offers them her best, welcoming smile as her heart flutters both in excitement and a bit of anxiety. This is the moment - the moment - she worked towards for four years. Longer, technically. She knew for a while that she wanted to be in education - especially elementary.

 

Lilia Calderu had been her driving force for it, too. 


She owes a lot to Lilia, but Rio refuses to think about it now. She needs to concentrate on this day; she needs to concentrate on these students.

 

They take the time to go over introductions - each and every one of them. All twenty-six of them, Rio included. They tell her what they are most excited about; they share what their summers were like. Rio shares pictures of her own summer adventures - mostly pictures from Amazonia. It’s a good transition into the start of their plants unit.

 

But not all the transitions were.. decent. Lilia says it’ll come with practice and that sometimes there just isn’t a good way to switch subjects. It reassures her.

 

And honestly, the day goes by in a blurr. 

 

Before she knows it, the final bell rings and they say their goodbyes. Whereas the majority of them immediately head towards the door with shouts of ‘bye Ms. Vidal,’ a few linger behind to give her hugs.

 

One child doesn’t move from his desk, Rio realizes when she turns away from the classroom door.

 

With a smile, Rio crosses the room over to him, where he is still sitting. She kneels down next to him, an action that summons his attention. He turns towards her.

 

“Is everything alright, Nicholas?” she asks.

 

Nicholas tilts his head slightly, as if pondering the question, before looking back at his name tag - the one that Rio set up just that morning. He taps his finger against his written name. “Can this say ‘Nicky?’” he asks. “No one calls me Nicholas.” 

 

“Oh! Of course it can, Nicky,” Rio answers, purposefully using the preferred name he presents.

 

“Mama says it’s my nickname,” Nicky explains. “But she uses it way more than my actual name. She calls me ‘Nicholas’ when I get in trouble,” he reveals.

 

In a slight dramatic fashion, Rio gasps slightly, “You don’t get in trouble often, do you, Nicky?” 

 

He immediately shakes his head. “Nope!” he says proudly. “And Mama says that ‘Nick’ is too grown up. I’m not a grown up yet.” 

 

Rio wrinkles her nose and nods, “I think your mom is right. ‘Nicky’ suits you very well.” 

 

“Do you have a nickname, Ms. Vidal?” 

 

“I mean.. Rio’s already a pretty short name,” she explains, standing up straight. It leads Nicky to stand as well. She helps him sling his backpack over his shoulder. “But sometimes people try to call me ‘Ri.’”

 

This time it’s Nicky who wrinkles his nose as he looks up towards her. “I like ‘Rio’ better, Ms. Vidal,” he states. 

 

“Me too,” Rio agrees. “I hope you had a good first day today, Nicky. I’ll change your name tag - you’ll have a new one for tomorrow.” 

 

“Thank you, Ms. Vidal,” he grins. “Did you have a good first day, too?” 

 

Placing her hand on his backpack, Rio begins to gently lead the boy towards the door of the classroom. She can still hear Alice’s comment clearly in her mind: helicopter parent . She didn’t need his mom wondering where in the world her son is. 

 

Before they could reach the door and before Rio could answer Nicky’s question, the door is pulled open. Rio doesn’t know what to expect, but the thought of… her standing there in the entrance to her classroom isn’t in the realm of possibility. 

 

Four Shots of Espresso and Oat Milk - no, wait. No. Señor Scratchy – no.

 

Agatha Harkness steps into her room.

 

Both women stand with the same frozen expression, one mixed with shock and confusion. Both tried to stand a little straighter, tried to get their bearings after having the wind knocked out of them. In those eyes that made her chest tighten for weeks (not that this moment is any different), Rio catches a hint of.. Amusement?

 

Nicky throws himself against his mother, wrapping his arms around her middle in a tight hug. “Hi, Mama!” he greets enthusiastically. He pulls away and turns his attention towards Rio. “This is my teacher! Her name is Ms. Vidal!” 

 

“Ms. Vidal,” Agatha repeats, offering a hand which Rio takes. “It’s nice to.. meet you. Officially.” 

 

“The pleasure is all mine, Mrs. Harkness-” 

 

“Ms.,” Agatha corrects. “Or Agatha, I guess. If you prefer.” 

 

“So, it’s no longer Señor Scratchy?” Rio dares to ask, raising an eyebrow.

 

From the corner of her eye, Rio sees Nicky frowns in confusion. “Why does Ms. Vidal know our rabbit’s name?”

 

Immediately, Rio’s eyebrows shoot up as amusement now graces her own features. “You used your rabbit’s name?” she asks, offering Nicky no context. She also wants to ask if they really have a rabbit (as well as to see pictures, but Rio holds back).

 

“No.”

 

“You used that, too,” Rio points out. 

 

“Adults are weird,” Nicky murmurs, the conversation now lost on him.

 

“Oh, baby, you have no idea ,” Agatha agrees, taking his hand.

 

“Goodbye, Ms. Vidal! See you tomorrow!” Nicky says, now pulling his mother along. He probably wants to get home to that rabbit of his, Rio notes.

 

“I’ll see you tomorrow, Nicky,” Rio repeats before meeting the other woman’s eye one final time. “Agatha,” she says with a nod.

 

“Ms. Vidal,” Agatha replies before stepping out of the classroom.

 

Rio decides to not change Agatha’s contact information in her phone. Instead, she asks for a picture of the bunny. Agatha only sends an emoji of a rabbit. Rio rolls her eyes.

 

–

 

She’s able to sleep better that night. The exhaustion of the first day caught up with her nearly as soon as she crossed the threshold of the apartment she shared with Alice who did not come home until much later. There’s a knock on her bedroom door after she’s collapsed onto her bed nearly immediately after dinner.

 

“Come in,” Rio groans.

 

The creaking of the opening door is followed by her roommate’s cackling laugh. The sight of Rio in pajamas planted face first into pillows and limbs spread must be quite the amusing sight? Rio’s too tired to care.

 

“Long day?” Alice asks.

 

Rio feels the edge of the foot of her bed sink. She rolls towards the movement, onto her side, so she can meet Alice’s eye. She nods, before adding, “You?”

 

Alice nods too and then tells her, “Caught that grade book on fire. Already have them eating out of the palm of my hand.” 

 

“You set the fire on purpose?” Rio questions.

 

“Gotta get them interested somehow,” Alice remarks, shrugging her shoulders. “Give them something to talk about, you know? Might as well be science , right? Anything exciting happening in the land of first grade? How many shoes did you have to tie?” 

 

“Five,” Rio answers, automatically.

 

“Riveting,” Alice replies. “You know what, though? Honestly, I thought it would be more.” 

 

Rio pauses for a moment, the thought just now crossing her mind. “Me too,” she agrees. She waits another second before revealing, “Señor Scratchy is one of my students’ mom.” 

 

When Alice doesn’t respond right away, Rio forces herself to sit up. She sits just in time to meet Alice’s shout of, “Excuse me, what!? I’m sorry, why didn’t you lead with that ? What does that mean ?”

 

With a groan, Rio throws herself back against the bed. Her back meeting the mattress knocks the wind out of her a little bit. She pulls one of her pillows over face. “I don’t know,” she admits. “Do you think I might’ve pissed off a cosmic entity in a previous life?”

 

“Have you seen you ?” Alice remarks. “You probably were the cosmic entity and now you have to live among us mere mortals.”

 

Rio can’t help but snort.

 

“But, girl, I don’t know either,” Alice admits, referring back to Rio’s question, not that she has the intention to offer a potential solution. “However, this sounds like it’s going to be interesting. What are you going to do?” 

 

“I’m too tired to even try to think about it,” Rio says into the pillow. It must’ve muffled her voice because in the next second, it’s pulled from covering her face. Rio lets out an annoyed sigh. “But she knows I’m at the cafe on Saturday.” 

 

“Well, didn’t that other first grade teacher quit and abandon her life?”

 

“ Alice ,” Rio warns. “Not funny.” 

 

“Sounds like we’re waiting to see what happens on Saturday then?” 

 

“Why are you invested in this?” 

 

“Rio Vidal, I’m offended . Of course I’ve invested in your happiness,” Alice retorts, standing. “Or the good story that comes out of it that we’ll drink over later.” 

 

“ Goodnight , Alice,” Rio huffs. 

 

–

 

The second day of school, Rio arrives at the standard thirty minutes prior to the first bell of the day with the intention of ironing out the wrinkles of yesterday’s lesson plan, adjusting today’s, and fixing Nicky’s name tag. The name tag is the first thing on her to-do list lest Nicky arrive early to still see his old one. 

 

She peels off the old one with relative ease and just as she finishes taping the new one to the desk, the classroom door opens. Anticipating some students arriving early, Rio takes a half step back when instead she finds Agatha once again in her classroom.

 

“Ms. Harkness, good-” 

 

“Agatha,” she corrects and then holds up her to-go cup. “I mean, you know my coffee order. Might as well.”

 

“Agatha,” Rio repeats, nodding. 

 

“I should’ve put it together when my son’s teacher and my barista have the same first name,” Agatha comments. “There can’t be too many ‘Rio’s in Westview. Didn’t expect them to be the same person.” 

 

Rio shrugs as she teases, “You probably haven’t had your insane amount of caffeine yet that morning.” 

 

“You’re ridiculous - don’t ,” she warns, pointedly, earning herself a laugh from Rio.

 

“What name did you give Jen this morning?” Rio asks, curious.

 

“One that I probably shouldn’t repeat in an elementary school,” Agatha answers without missing a beat. “Coffee’s still burnt.”

 

Grinning, Rio doesn’t press, despite being curious. She looks down at the old name tag still in her hands. Agatha’s gaze follows. Rio offers it to her. “In case you’re one of those sentimental parents,” she explains.

 

Agatha takes it from her, her touch gentle as she does so, as if its the most precious treasure in the world. The way she looks at it, the warm, genuine smile it gives her.. Rio can’t help but smile herself, too.

 

She also can’t help but think how beautiful Agatha is.

 

Catching herself in the middle of the thought, Rio quickly straightens and clears her throat. “Is there anything I can help you with, Agatha?” she asks, remembering that it was the older woman who sought her out in the classroom. “Is Nicky okay?” 

 

“Oh, he’s fine,” Agatha instantly replies, now fully present in the moment. She sets her coffee down on a random student’s desk, allowing her to tuck away the name tag into her purse as well as pull out a familiar little ziplock baggie. Rio sent each student home with one, filled with a singular bean just starting to sprout wrapped in a paper towel.

 

“Homework on day one? Of first grade?” Agatha protests.

 

Rio tenses. 

 

Helicopter parent .

 

“Jesus, relax, I’m teasing,” Agatha backtracks. “I know - the science unit. It’s just.. the stem of ours kind of.. Shriveled? I’m not a plant person. I mean. I know what a living plant looks like, but this is not that .”

 

“And I’m the one who needs to relax?” Rio teases right back, reaching out for the bag. She explains it for a moment and then turns towards the window seal. “I have extras just for this situation - let me grab you another one,” she says.

 

“Was it anything we did?” Agatha asks, following after.

 

Rio shakes her head, “No, sometimes bean sprouts just die.”

Chapter 2

Friday comes around soon enough, much to Rio’s delight (and salvation, not that would openly admit it to any of her colleagues). Though she stresses that she does enjoy the job - because she absolutely does enjoy the job, she looks forward to this evening’s program of becoming one with the living room couch watching some questionable television with take out from one of her favorite hole in the wall Chinese restaurants.

 

Student teaching did not prepare her for this utter exhaustion.

 

However, that evening is still a few hours away, unfortunately. She still has half a work day to tackle and get through before releasing these munchkins back to their parents for the entirety of the weekend.

 

And, fortunately, her daily break is scheduled right in the middle of the day, giving her a moment to breathe: the coveted prep-hour/break schedule of back-to-back the kids’ lunch and recess, followed by one of their specials classes (music, art, and physical education). Today was physical education, for the second time this week. So far, it’s Rio’s favorite solely because they come back to her after letting nearly all of their energy out with one activity or another.

 

Just a few minutes after the end of the kids’ recess, while they are probably already eagerly dividing themselves into teams with their PE coach in the school gym, there’s a knock against Rio’s propped open classroom door.

 

“Did you eat?” she hears Lilia ask, her voice laced with a slight concern pulling Rio’s attention away from her computer screen and lesson planning for the following week. She doesn’t want to stay after the students leave for the day, not when she has a very desirable date this evening with the sofa and television.

 

“Yes, Mom ,” Rio teases, smirking ever so lightly. It earns her a soft, affectionate eye roll from Lilia. Pen still in hand, paused from scribbling down notes, she then gestures towards the potted fern in Lilia’s arms. “What’s that?”

 

“Well, sorry for being concerned over you missing our usual lunch date,” Lilia responds with feign offense as she walks up to Rio’s desk where she sets the plant down. Upon discovering they shared the same lunch period and prep period (something Rio suspects was Lilia’s doing, though Lilia refuses to admit), Lilia insisted on Rio joining her for lunch all week. It was their little check-in: a moment for LIlia to see how things were going and a moment for Rio to ask any questions, or for both of them to simply talk about their day. 

 

Today, however, Rio decided to skip to finish preparing for next week.

 

Again, nothing is going to keep her after work this afternoon.

 

Rio is determined to become one with that living room couch.

 

God help whoever dares to stop her.

 

(Her money is on Alice.)

 

“This, my dear,” Lilia begins to explain. “This is your ‘you survived your first week of teaching’ gift - granted, I am assuming that you’ll make it through the next few hours and that you’re bolting as soon as that bell rings. I wouldn’t hold that against you either.” 

 

Plan instantly discovered and attention now diverted from the lesson plans on her desk, Rio throws her head back in a laugh, bringing herself to her feet. She comes around the desk to pull Lilia into a quick hug. “You didn’t have to do that, Ms. Cal-” 

 

“Ah!” 

 

“ Lilia ,” Rio corrects, stepping back from their quick embrace. “Thank you, that’s very sweet of you. And not at all necessary. Truly.”

 

Not having it, Lilia waves a hand in the air as if to dismiss Rio’s comment physically. “Please, as if we shouldn’t mark important milestones and we both know how much this week - the moment - means to you, Rio. I just hope you still enjoy plants. Perhaps I should have checked before assuming.”

 

The sentiment isn’t lost on Rio; she knows what Lilia is referring to, even though neither of them put them into words (they don’t have to): the car accident and drunk driver that left her an orphan at such a young age, her own long recovery from the crash, the lost memories - some of which she never remembered, the less than stellar foster homes throughout the years, putting herself through school entirely by herself, landing this job - the one she hoped would be available after her graduation, starting this career..

 

No, Rio doesn’t give any of those memories a voice. She doesn’t need to open the floodgates at work (or, rather, at all, for that matter), not with half a day to get through with twenty-five smiling faces. Plus, with the heavy silence lingering between the two of them, Lilia already knows how Rio feels. 

 

After all, Lilia was there through everything.

 

So, instead, Rio says reassuringly, unable to hide the thick emotion in her voice, “ Love them still. Can’t get enough.” 

 

As if an attempt to bypass the heavy air between them, Lilia clears her throat and dares to change topics altogether. “The first year teachers get together is this evening,” she states as if the social invitation for all district employees hasn’t been sitting in her calendar all week. “Will you be attending?”

 

Rio’s furrowed eyebrows and wrinkled nose is all the answer Lilia needs. Even Alice has been nagging her to go all week, but with an early morning shift at the cafe tomorrow and the growing level of exhaustion complying all week, Rio officially declined the invitation end of day yesterday.

 

”I’ll try for the next social event,” she promises. “There’s one at the end of the calendar year, right? Before the winter break?”

 

Lilia opens her mouth to comment, but before she can, she is interrupted by a loud chiming from Rio’s computer that makes them both jump to attention out of habit.

 

“Sorry, that’s-” Rio replies, returning to her office chair and scooting herself closer to her monitor to check the instant message from the school’s system waiting for her. “That’s the nurse,” she finishes with a frown. Her heart drops into her stomach.

 

“Is everything alright?”

 

Shaking her head slowly, she clacks away at her keyboard and sends off a quick response to the sender. Rio looks up at Lilia as she returns to her feet. “Nicky fell during PE,” she reports, concerned.

 

“Nicholas Harkness?” Lilia asks for clarification.

 

With a sigh, Rio stands from her desk once more and nods, gently correcting, ”Yeah, Nicky. The nurse is saying he split his lip. I want to go check on-” 

 

“Don’t apologize, my dear - go,” Lilia says.

 

–

 

It’s a short walk from her first grade classroom to the nurse’s office - one that Rio hoped she didn’t have to make so soon, but yet here she is quickly taking long strides. Rio finds Nicky sitting in the corner of the small office, ice-pack to his mouth. The desire to comfort overcomes her and she talks a step towards him, but a sudden hand on her shoulder stops her in her tracks in the middle of the room.

 

“Ms. Vidal,” the nurse greets. Embarrassingly enough, Rio struggles to recall the woman’s name. They briefly met last week when Rio was bombarded with information during training. “The bleeding’s finally stopped, but we’ll want to keep the ice pack on for a little longer to help reduce the swelling. I don’t think he should return to PE today.”

 

Rio nods in agreement, taking the few remaining steps that separate her from Nicky in the corner of the room. She leans forward slightly and holds out her hand towards the sniffling boy, tears still welling up in his eyes. “He can come hang out in our room for the rest of the time, no problem,” she offers with a gentle smile. “I could use the company.”

 

Lesson plans be damned; she could stay after work a little while if it meant making him feel better.

 

Nicky, trying to furiously blink back those tears brimming on the corner of his eyes, reaches out to grab Rio’s hand - his touch tender and meek. He doesn’t look up, shuffling towards her in silence acceptance of her invitation. She gives his hand a small squeeze as if to silently reassure him that everything is okay. 

 

Everything will be fine.

 

“According to the coach, he tripped over himself as the kids were all running around and fell face forward,” the nurse states. “Fortunately, he didn’t hit his head - little miracles. I will go ahead and contact his mother to let her know what happened, that everything is alright.”

 

It’s a knee jerk reaction that escapes her instantly. “Oh, no,” Rio finds herself quickly replying before she can stop herself or even think the idea through. “I can call Ms. Harkness.”

 

The last time they spoke was Tuesday morning before the start of the school day when Agatha showed up to pick up Nicky’s replacement bean sprout for their plant unit. The last text exchange was still that rabbit emoji from Monday. Many times over the last few days, Rio found herself debating whether to reach out - whether to continue their previous conversation from Sunday. To continue the playful flirting and bantering that left her smiling all day that previous Sunday - the distraction that caused her to frantically decorate her room last minute before the first day of class.

 

Rio also found herself hesitating more times than she cares to admit on their text exchange, becoming a far cry from that bold barista who wrote her number on a to-go cup of coffee in hopes to continue the banter her week away from the cafe. The recent developments - discovering that Nicky, her student, is also Agatha’s son, gave Rio pause.

 

She also found her roommate shouting ‘ just send a message already ’ a handful of times across the apartment - something about the aura of their living quarters feeling too nervous and anxious.

 

Sometimes, those shouts were followed or led with ‘ you only live once - don’t the youths still say that?’

 

They, for the record, do not.

 

Alice is also quick to point out that Rio was her barista first before she ever was her son’s teacher - by a few weeks, even.

 

Despite her best interest and her wants, Rio never did reach back out and, well, she could blame work - the first week being chaotic and all that. She can easily behind the excuse instead of admitting that she didn’t know how to proceed forward.

 

Honestly, a part of her wishes that Agatha would break the silence and force her own hand - make the decision for her.

 

Instead, Rio relies on this moment, presented to her in the form of a swollen lip and a defeated little boy missing out on dodge ball. 

 

Or maybe kickball? 

 

Either way..

 

“Come on, buddy,” Rio says gently, leading Nicky out of the nurse’s office and into the hallway to make their walk back to their quiet safe haven.

 

He follows Rio silently and lets go of her hand once they are back in the classroom, making a beeline straight for his desk opposite the room from her own. Rio, grabbing her phone  and a tissue from her desk, walks over to him and kneels down to his level.

 

“You okay?” Rio asks gently, tilting her head to the side, offering him a kind smile.

 

From behind the ice pack that Rio suspects he’s also just trying to hide behind, he nods his silent response.

 

“Good, I’m glad you’re okay,” she says, using the tissue to tab at the tears in his eyes. She places it on his desk. “That was probably quite the shock, huh?”

 

Again, he nods and Rio fears this would be the extent of their conversation, but then his tiny voice asks, “Does it look bad, Ms. Vidal?”

 

She reaches out for the ice pack and carefully lowers it, revealing the cut running down the left side of his puffy lower lip. She smiles warmly again at him when he meets her eye and shakes her head. “Not too bad. It’ll heal up quick,” she promises. “Do you want to talk to your mom?” 

 

“Yeah,” he answers, his voice still so small. She wants to reach out and pull him into a hug. Instead, Rio pulls up Agatha’s contact information and Nicky tilts his head in confusion that echoes in his words. “Why is my mom Señor Scratchy in your phone?”

 

“Uhh,” Rio hesitates for a second, willingly herself to quickly find a believable answer, one that doesn’t reveal the truth of Agatha being a smartass a couple weeks ago while ordering herself coffee. “It’s.. well.. It’s to remind me that she still hasn’t shown me your pet bunny,” she explains, begging to any listening deity that the excuse is convincing enough to a six year old.

 

“ Rabbit ,” Nicky corrects with an air of offense, leaving Rio to question if there is a difference between rabbits and bunnies. She’s fairly certain there isn’t, but this child, with all his sudden bold confidence, is making her second guess her entire education. 

 

“..rabbit,” Rio decides to correct herself. Honestly, she is grateful that Nicky isn’t asking why she had his mother’s contact information to begin with, though she probably could scrounge up another excuse, need be. She’s his teacher, after all. Of course she has her contact information.

 

Small victories.

 

She dials the number; there’s an answer on the third ring.

 

“Ms. Vidal? What’s wrong?” Agatha says quickly. Direct mixed with confusion and concern. Of course she is. Rio hasn’t contacted her in days and now she’s calling during the school day, no less.

 

“Hi, Agatha,” Rio replies, meeting Nicky’s gaze. “I have you on speaker and Nicky’s sitting with me-” 

 

“What’s happening?”

 

“I’m okay, Mama,” Nicky speaks up, sensing the rise of his mother’s panic.

 

“Hi there, baby..” There’s a shift in Agatha’s voice. It grows immediately warm, full of the love that she has for her son. Rio can’t help but smile at the endearment. For a moment, the panic disappears. There’s even a small sigh of relief.

 

“Nicky is okay now,” Rio reassures. “But he did have a tumble in PE just a little bit ago that landed him in the nurse’s office-“

 

“There was so much blood, Mom, but I didn’t get any on my shirt!”

 

“Nicky, what ?” 

 

The panic instantly returns in full force. 

 

Wrinkling her nose, Rio closes her eyes tightly. Damn. Next time they would have to go over their tack. Rio hopes there isn’t a next time, though. She doesn’t want to call Agatha like this again (though she wouldn’t mind calling Agatha again, if she were to be completely honest with herself - courage, where art thou?)

 

“He cut his lip in the fall,” Rio explains. “But it’s all under control. It’s fine; he’s fine.” 

 

“Does he need to go to urgent care? Do I need to come over?” Agatha asks. “I’ll come over. I can leave work-“ 

 

“You do not need to come over, Agatha, and he does not need medical attention - the nurse cleared him,” Rio reassures again, glancing over at Nicky. “But the school was going to inform you regardless of the incident. I figured it would sound better coming from me. Err, from us.” 

 

There’s a pause. For a split moment, Rio thinks the line died, but Agatha speaks up and offers a soft, “Thank you, Ms. Vidal, for the call then. Nicky, baby, I’ll see you after school, okay?” 

 

“You’re welcome, Agatha,” Rio responds. 

 

“Bye, Mama!” Nicky sneaks in before Agatha actually does hang up the line. He puts the ice back on his lip. “She’s really worried, I can tell. Her voice got really high.”

 

“Your mom really loves you,” Rio grunts and stands up, wincing slightly from the amount she spent kneeling. She really should’ve pulled up one of the student chairs.

 

She walks back over to her desk and sits down. Nicky’s gaze is still on her, watching intently. He points to the plant on her desk. “Ms. Vidal, what’s that?”

 

“Oh, this?” Rio asks, picking up the pot containing her new fern and lifting it to give Nicky a better view of the plant for a moment. “Ms. Calderu gifted me this little guy. Isn’t that sweet of her?”

 

Nicky’s eyebrows furrow. “Little guy? That’s a plant, Ms. Vidal,” he points out, bypassing Rio’s question.

 

“It is,” Rio nods, watching Nicky stand up and wander over to her desk. He stands next to her; they’re almost eye level. She pulls the pot closer to the edge of the desk. “I tend to think of my plants like people. Or pets, I guess. They’re living, too. Like your.. rabbit.” 

 

“You almost said bunny , didn’t you?” he accuses, eyes narrowing.

 

Rio holds her hands up as if she were caught in her terrible, terrible crime. “You caught me,” she admits. “But, anyway, your rabbit has his own special name, doesn’t he?”

 

“Señor Scratchy,” Nicky says proudly.

 

“Señor Scratchy,” Rio repeats, nodding. “I like to give my plants their own names, too.” 

 

“What are you going to name this one?” Nicky asks, curious.

 

“I don’t know,” she says honestly with a small shrug. “We just met, so nothing’s come to me yet. I got to give it some more time, you know? See what kind of personality he has first.” 

 

Nicky nods, but his face lights up after a moment, graced with an idea. “Ms. Vidal, do you know how we have our bean sprouts? And the bean sprouts we keep here in class, too? Can this be the class’ too? It could be our classroom pet. Our classroom plant.” 

 

The idea makes Rio smile. She tilts her head, giving it a thought (as if she didn’t make her decision automatically). “That sounds wonderful, Nicky,” she answers. “Then maybe we need to give him a name as a class. We’ll do some brainstorming when everyone comes back. How does that sound?”

 

“ Amazing !”

 

–

 

Rio looks up towards the clock when the final bell rings and then diverts her attention back to the twenty five students shuffling in their seats to grab their backpacks. “Alright, my little noodles,” she announces from her own desk. “Have a good weekend and I will see you on Monday, bright and early!” 

 

There’s a mix melody of “bye Ms. Vidal”s and “have a good weekend”s as they lift their small chairs onto their desks and head to the classroom door. Just as the last of them filed their way out, Agatha bolts her way in and Rio realizes that Nicky lingered behind.

 

”Hello, Ri- Ms. Vidal,” Agatha says quickly, correcting herself, as she makes a beeline to Nicky hovering at his desk. She doesn’t glance her way as she walks past and Rio doesn’t fault a worried mother wanting to see her child. “Nicky, baby,” she cooes, kneeling down to his level. “Oh, look at your lip, sweetheart. Does it hurt?” 

 

“Mama, did you know that today I’m spaghetti?” Nicky states, bypassing all of her concern nonchalantly.

 

Agatha is taken aback, confused. “What?”

 

”What?” Rio echoes from behind her own desk.

 

Nicky nods in confirmation, as if such a little notion could explain anything with any sense of certainty. “Today, we’re noodles.” 

 

“Oh!” Rio gasps, covering her mouth. Agatha, still kneeling, shifts on her feet to turn towards Rio, confusion ever present.

 

”That’s what Ms. Vidal called us today. Noodles,” Nicky explained. “So I decided I’m spaghetti. Billy and Tommy are probably macaroni. Yesterday, we were nuggets.” 

 

Struggling to keep a straight face, Rio explains, “The day before, they were cinnamon rolls.”

 

”What’s with all the processed foods?” Agatha asks, standing straight as she helps Nicky put on his backpack.

 

”Yeah, maybe I should call them my little bean sprouts on Monday,” Rio muses. “I was considering pizza rolls.” 

 

Nicky’s face lights up at the mention. “Mama, can we have pizza rolls for dinner? It’s Friday .” 

 

“Should I be afraid that you share the same diet as a six year old boy?” Agatha questions.

 

”You heard the kid, it’s Friday . He clearly deserves pizza rolls,” Rio says, siding with Nicky. “Plus, I think that’ll help with the whole cut lip thing. I know it would make me feel better.”

 

Nicky beams up at his mother in excitement, taking her hand into his. “Yeah! It’ll help!”

 

Agatha lets herself get pulled towards the windows of the classroom, but she glances over her shoulder back towards Rio. “Don’t encourage him,” she playfully hisses, as if she hasn’t already caved at the idea of giving the boy exactly what he asked for.

 

”Besides, you have to celebrate completing your first week of first grade - that’s a rather important milestone, if I do say so myself,” Rio, indeed, continues to encourage him.

 

Nicky stops at the window seal lined with growing bean sprouts and the new potted fern. He tilts his head to look back up at Agatha, agreeing, “Yeah! We have to!”

 

“You know, that isn’t a milestone I remember reading in all those parenting books.”

 

“Oh, really? Then you don’t know how you celebrate the end of the second week?” Rio questions.

 

“Enlighten me, Ms. Vidal.”

 

With a mischievous smile, Rio meets Nicky’s eye. He matches her grin and mouths the words ‘chocolate cake’ at her.

 

Rio leans back in her office chair and responds, “Obviously, it’s a slice of–”

 

“A whole one!”

 

“A whole chocolate cake,” Rio immediately corrects herself.

 

“Like the one Aunt Wanda made for my birthday over the summer!” Nicky exclaims with a little bounce.

 

Agatha looks between him and Rio, narrowing her eyes as she shakes her head, mostly at Rio. “I don’t like this little team up you two have going on here,” she states. “All this talk about celebrating - how’s Mama supposed to celebrate these so clearly real milestones?”

 

“I’ll share the pizza rolls and the cake,” Nicky says, matter of factly. “And you can have wine,” he adds nonchalantly.

 

Rio tries to hide her laugh, tries to play it off as a cough and covers her mouth. It’s not convincing.

 

Agatha opens her mouth to respond, but Nicky pulls on her hand again. He points at the fern and explains, “We have a class plant, Mama! I don’t think Ms. Vidal can be trusted with an actual class pet. She doesn’t know the difference between a rabbit and a bunny.” 

 

“Oh?” Agatha’s eyes narrow slightly, meeting Rio’s with a mischievous and playful glint. “You’re right, Nicky. That simply won’t do.” She then mouths the words, carefully enunciating ‘there is no difference.’ 

 

Ensuring that Nicky isn’t paying her any attention, Rio makes a grand gesture of rolling her eyes and shaking her head. Her smile never leaves her face - if anything, it only grows due to this ridiculous interaction she’s quite enjoying. It’s one that won’t last for much longer, she fears. “I promise, I’ll do some research this weekend!” 

 

“Mmhmm, I’ll check your homework on Monday then,” Nicky remarks. “Mama, we named it Ferny. As a class!”

 

“Ferny?” Agatha repeats, raising her eyebrows.

 

“It was a unanimous decision made by a room full of first graders,” Rio quickly follows up in her own defense. “I immediately was out voted.”

 

“It’s a good name , Ms. Vidal!” Nicky defends, puffing out his chest as he defended his classmates.

 

Agatha throws her head back in a laugh, one that only ensures that Rio keeps smiling (she likes the sound of that laugh; she wants to hear more of it). This time, it’s Agatha who gives Nicky’s hand a gentle tug before heading towards the classroom exit. “Come on, my love. We don’t need to keep Ms. Vidal away from her own weekend any longer.”

 

Nicky walks in step with his mother. “Bye, Ms. Vidal!” he calls back over his shoulder as they step into the hallway.

 

“Bye, my little meatball!” Rio calls after him.

 

From down the hall, although it is nearly overshadowed by Nicky’s loud bemused giggles, she hears Agatha groan, “ Stop that! ”

 

Rio can’t help but chuckle to herself.

“One last final plea,” Alice's voice comes from behind Rio lounging comfortably on the middle of the couch in oversized pajamas and wrapped in a fuzzy throw blanket (the very definition of lazy comfort, if she says so herself). The cushions on either side of Rio’s head sink under her roommate’s hands pressed firmly into them. She looks up to see Alice’s face hovering above her own, pouting as if that would work against Rio at all - as if she did not face twenty-five different daily versions of that multiple times this week alone.

 

Alice would have to try much harder to get her to cave because currently? There isn’t a force strong enough to separate her from this couch.

 

And Rio, determined, is not going to cave, not when she has been looking forward to this very evening - this very moment - for the better part of three days now. There are no words to describe the level of exhaustion aching throughout her entire body. She didn’t anticipate first grade to be so relentless - how could they have so much energy?

 

“Why do I feel this isn’t the final plea?” Rio questions as she narrows her eyes accusingly at her roommate, using chopsticks to grab a potsticker out of the takeout container she holds. As she takes a bite, she tilts her head to the side and raises an eyebrow, not breaking their shared gaze as she chews. “My answer hasn’t changed from the thousand of other times that you asked, Alice,” she enforces. “Hasn’t changed since we got the invitation. Which I replied to, by the way. Nope.” 

 

Dramatically, Alice throws her head back and releases a guttural sigh. “Come on,” she whines, looking down at Rio again. “Aren’t you even the least bit tempted to see Calderu throw back some flaming shots? Or Davis wine drunk?”

 

“Aren’t you even the least bit tempted to have me pay rent on time?” Rio retorts without missing a beat. “Plus, I can’t even picture Calderu in the same vicinity of a shot glass. Davis and wine, however, I can definitely see. That woman’s definitely too..p erky . She’s hiding something.” 

 

“You’ll never know if my quest to get her to was successful or not - I won’t tell you,” Alice grumbles, childishly. “Besides, the rent for September was due last week. You still have plenty of time to get October’s in order–”

 

An eruption of screaming voices from the television set across the living room jerks both of their attentions towards it. Alice waves a dismissive hand at the screen displaying shaking night vision footage of men freaking out at each other, yelling at the top of their lungs. “I can’t believe I’m being stood up for trash ghost hunting shows. You hate ghosts,” she points out in despair.

 

“ Hate them,” Rio concurs, nodding as she pulls the throw blanket closer to herself in an attempt to signal that she isn’t changing her mind. She is officially tucked in and she has no plans to leave this apartment - hell, or even the boundary of the living room for that matter. Would she even drag herself to her bedroom later? Who knows? Not even she does.

 

“Doesn’t getting appropriately smashed with your colleagues throughout the district sound more fun than whoever they are?” Alice gestures towards the tv. Somehow - somehow - the camera is still shaking and the group of men are still yelling at each other about who knows what.

 

“That’s not as strong of an argument as you think it is,” Rio remarks, taking a stab this time at another potsticker.

 

“ Fine ,” Alice says, shifting gears and changing tact. “How about drinking some liquid courage, watching said colleagues get appropriately smashed, and maybe - maybe - you get the nerve to actually text your crush back.” 

 

“If I wanted to get drunk and text Agatha, I know where your cheap ass bottle of tequila is,” Rio grumbles. “But I really don’t want to drunk text her. And it’s not about nerves , Alice..” 

 

It is a little bit about nerves.

 

But it is mostly the circumstances Rio finds herself in.

 

..and nerves.

 

“So, strictly for clarification’s purposes, we are finally admitting that Agatha is your crush now?” Alice asks. “Good to know. Hey, can I have a dumpling?”

 

The rush of heat to her checks causes Rio to tilt her head down, not wanting to give Alice any sense of satisfaction for the blush now present. There’s also a tiny part of her that doesn’t want to give her a potsticker either, but does so reluctantly and refuses to look up to give her any gratification.

 

“Thank you!” Alice replies, chipper, plucking the potsticker from between the pair of chopsticks and taking a bite. “I’ll be sure to give you a play by play if anything scandalous goes down - don’t want you to feel excluded from unofficial district nonsense. Or for you to hear it from anyone else.”

 

Rio chuckles lightly - smiles even, but it fades a tad as Alice steps away from the back of the couch and towards. Rio sits up and turns herself towards Alice who is already unlocking the door. “Hey,” she calls out, tone now incredibly serious as the television continues playing, forgotten.

 

Alice turns around and gives Rio a reassuring nod. “I know and I will if I have to, promise,” she says for added reassurance, her own tone matching that of her roommate’s. It’s not a new conversation; it’s practiced and memorized. Frequent. Regardless, ever since their friendship solidified, it is one that Rio insists on having, even with fewer and fewer words as time goes on. The reassurance keeps her anxiety at bay, so it’s one Alice doesn’t mind having whenever it raises its head.

 

It’s important to Rio, so it’s important to Alice -  sometimes, it truly is as simple as that.

 

“But-” Alice tries to begin.

 

“No but’s,” Rio immediately interrupts, shooting her a warning glare. 

 

“I’m a butt person,” Alice deadpans. “You know this, Rio.”

 

“You’re a freaking idiot,” Rio states, her smile returning as she once again shakes her head in disbelief.

 

“Unfortunately, your idiot, though,” Alice grins. “What I was going to say is that it’s technically a work function and I have my limit - I don’t need those stuffy admin stooges knowing anything they shouldn't know about me.”

 

“Like you being a budding arsonist?” Rio guesses. “And that you’re encouraging future budding arsonists as well?”

 

“ Exactly. Don’t wait up for me - enjoy hate watching ghosts,” Alice says, pulling their front door open. Before she takes a step out, she looks over her shoulder. “And, you know, just ask her how the kid is doing - probably the easiest way to start a conversation tonight-” 

 

“ Bye , Alice,” Rio calls out, returning her attention and self back to the television.

 

Hearing the closing of the apartment door and the lock clicking itself back in place, Rio allows herself to relax back into the soft couch cushions for what she assumes would be for the rest of the evening. Rio wastes no time gathering her belongings from the back office - not that she has much to gather beyond the tips Jen indicated and her messenger bag which she swings over her neck and shoulder. Rio runs her fingers through her hair before deeming herself presentable (as if she hadn’t just spent the last however long conversing with Agatha, looking just fine - that portion of her day wasn’t this portion where she’s moments away from a happy hour leading to who knows what). In a perfect world, she would run back to the apartment for a quick shower. She doesn’t think Agatha would mind if she asked. 

 

But she also doesn’t want to prolong anything.

 

With minutes still to spare, Rio wizzes through the length of the coffee shop, giving Jen a small wave after her boss gives her a cautious laced ‘good luck.’ She chooses not to read into it. 

 

Stepping outside, she finds Agatha hovering along the edge of the sidewalk, phone to her ear and smiling sweetly. Rio doesn’t have to guess who is on the other line; she knows immediately.

 

“I’m glad you’re having fun, sweetheart, but please be extra careful. I’ll see you later. Love you, too.”

 

Nicky.

 

Even after the conversation ends, Agatha stares lovingly at her phone (Rio, if she were a betting woman, would place money on Agatha’s background being a picture of her son - that sounds like a winning bet, if she ever heard one). 

 

“Everything okay?” Rio asks. 

 

Agatha jumps at the sound of her voice, taken by surprise. She glances at Rio and then quickly back to her phone. “You technically still have two minutes,” she grumbles, as if caught red handed in a moment of private weakness. 

 

“You told me not to be late,” Rio reminds her, shrugging. 

 

Rio smirks when Agatha mumbles something under her breath about not having to take things quite so literal. Agatha tucks her phone away into her pants pocket and then confirms, “Everything’s okay - the twins taught him to do a backflip on the trampoline, the little menaces, and he had to share.”

 

“Backflips are cool,” Rio deadpans.

 

“Don’t encourage them.”

 

“Okay, one: not a single musketeer is in our presence to be encouraged,” Rio points out. “And two: I am not on the district’s clock on the weekend - I don’t have to be a fully responsible adult.”

 

Agatha rolls her eyes in a mixture of amusement and, based on the smile tugging at the corners of her lips, only some minor annoyance. “Come on, it’s a short walk.” 

 

Both women fall in step with each other, walking along the sidewalk that runs along Westview’s mainstreet, leading up to the town’s little square, marked with a quaint stage and gazebo used primarily for holiday functions or whatever community event the local city government deems fit. They’re never big, never too extravagant, but it would be Westview without it. Rio often found solace in those events growing up, especially after–

 

“Here,” Agatha announces, reaching out and grabbing Rio’s wrist to pull her along inside. 

 

The gesture, however small and unserious, makes Rio’s heart skip a beat. The feeling of Agatha’s skin against hers causes a pit to form in the middle of her stomach. Although the touch doesn’t linger long (Agatha releases her grip as soon as they are inside), Rio’s nerves continue to shoot off for moments to come. They don’t settle until they’re both seated in a booth in the back of the dimly lit, modernly decorated pub - until their first round of drinks arrive.

 

Whisky on the rocks for both of them (Rio may have panicked and just ordered whatever Agatha did).

 

“Have we decided on any of the appetizers? The ones listed under happy hour are currently half off regular menu price,” their waiter reminds them.

 

“Would it be weird to order for no parmesan on the garlic parmesan fries?” Rio asks, looking up from the happy hour menu. “With the aioli on the side?” 

 

Rio catches Agatha peering over at her. Rio tilts her head to the side and smiles lightly. “You get your coffee with oat milk every morning,” she explains, as if it were the most natural thing in the world. 

 

“That’s rather attentive of you..” Agatha mumbles, hiding her own smile behind her hand as she returns her attention to the menu.

 

The waiter confirms Rio’s order, “Garlic fries, hold the cow - got it. And for you, ma’am?” 

 

Agatha’s eyebrow twitches; Rio smirks. She guesses it’s the ma’am . She doesn’t bring it up.

 

”I’m going to be a bit of a rebel and order from the regular menu,” Agatha warns, holding the happy hour flyer towards the waiter who takes it. She looks back at Rio and picks up her glass of whisky, leaning back against the cushioned seat of the booth opposite of her. “Because I know the Brussels sprouts here are to die for.” 

 

The smirk vanishes off of Rio’s face and her mouth hangs open as the waiter repeats Agatha’s order back to her. Agatha confirms with a nod and takes a sip of her glass when the waiter steps away.

 

”Is this a ruse to get me to try Brussels sprouts ?” Rio questions in disbelief. 

 

With a shrug of her shoulders, Agatha sets her glass down. “Two birds, one stone,” she answers. “When’s the last time you ate a literal vegetable, Rio?” 

 

“I just ordered fries!”

 

”Potatoes are a starch.”

 

”A starchy root vegetable .”

 

”I’m trying to keep you from getting scurvy,” Agatha deadpans. 

 

Rio leans forward, over the table. “Let me remind you, I have a whole class of bean sprouts.”

 

“Ah, so that’s your grand plan: spend all term growing maybe - maybe - one serving of vegetables. Great scheme there, Vidal,” Agatha smirks, bemused. 

 

Rio wants to kiss that smirk right off her face.

 

…what ? Where did that come from?

 

Suddenly, Rio is glad she decided to sit across from the woman and not next to her, for both of their sakes. Rio grabs her glass and takes perhaps too large of a sip. She swallows hard. She tries to ground herself.

 

”Actually,” she says, clearing her throat as she tries to reel in her thoughts. “I do want to propose a school vegetable garden in the future. I think it’s an important skill set for the kids to have and, I don’t know, maybe the town will get on board with it - start a community garden space.” 

 

Arm laid across her chest, propping up her elbow, Agatha nods along, resting her chin against the back of her hand. “So, why not? Propose it, I mean?” she asks with genuine curiosity. 

 

Resting her arms against the tabletop, Rio shrugs, “I’m still trying to find my footing here. I want to have a little bit more experience before I try anything of that caliber. I think I’m still a bit too… green .” 

 

“Did you just..?” Agatha’s voice trails off. 

 

Rio offers a goofy smile (she’s rather proud of herself, she won’t lie). They both take a sip of their respective drinks.

 

“So, why education?” Agatha asks, leaning forward. Glass in hand, she swirls the amber liquid around the melting ice. “Why teaching?”

 

It’s a potential loaded question, one that Rio knows she will have to dance around for now. She doesn’t want to lie; she wants to be honest, but there are some things she isn’t willing to offer so readily just yet.

 

”Lilia,” she settles on an answer.

 

” Really ?” Agatha instantly replies, but immediately backtracks. “Sorry, I didn’t mean it like that. I just.. she’s that inspirational, huh?” 

 

Rio nods slowly, “She was there for me during a pretty big transitional moment in my life.”

 

“In first grade..?” Agatha seeks clarification, eyebrows raised slightly.

 

“Mmhmm,” Rio confirms, biting the inside of her cheek. She takes a moment to piece together what she wants to say next. Agatha doesn’t interrupt or offer any comment - she waits for her to continue. “I had a good childhood until.. I didn’t and Lilia was there for that every step of the way. She continuously checked in on me over the years and offered me a small refuge whenever I needed it - the woman made me a garden in middle school in her own backyard. I want to be that kind of teacher - hell, I want to be that kind of person .” 

 

Out of pure reaction, Agatha reached across the table with her free hand to grab Rio’s.

 

Rio’s breath hitches.

 

”You are that kind of person, Rio,” Agatha states with a strong sense of certainty and conviction reflected in those piercing, blue eyes that hold such determination, as Agatha is ready to convince her if Rio tries to say otherwise.

 

Which..

 

“That’s.. really kind of you to say,” Rio begins, her voice now barely above a whisper. “But I’ve only been in this position for a week.”

 

“Well,” Agatha counters. “I have one six year old who will vouch for you. Probably three if I could get my godsons’ attention for more than two seconds.”

 

A small laugh escapes Rio as she lowers her head for a moment. When she looks back up, Agatha still has her gaze firmly locked on her. She squeezes Rio’s hand gently, earning herself a soft smile from the younger woman.

 

“Nicky really adores you, you know,” Agatha says. “You and that little bean sprout. He takes it with him to bed every night - has a special little spot for it on his nightstand and everything. He uses a tissue to tuck it in .”

 

Rio’s eyebrows shoot up in disbelief. “Stop, no way.”

 

“Oh, it doesn’t end there,” she carries on. “He brings it back downstairs to the living room in the morning because when that second bean sprout came home, he was so determined to find the best spot in the house that gets the most sunlight because this one isn’t going to die this time.”

 

The laugh that escapes Rio this time is louder - genuine and real.

 

“You’re laughing? I’ve had stress dreams this week about Señor Scratchy eating the damn thing, Vidal, and you’re laughing?”

 

Rio pulls her hand away from holding Agatha’s to cover her mouth in an attempt to stifle her laugh from growing into a full on cackle. Tears prick at the corners of her eyes, daring to stream down her face, but she dabs them away with her other hand.

 

“It’s funny,” Rio manages to choke as she tries to gather herself with a couple of deep breaths. “It’s also endearing.”

 

“You find me having stress dreams endearing?”

 

Rio shakes her head, “No, no, no - it’s endearing how amazing of a mom you are to him. Really warms the heart, you know? You’re good, Agatha.”

 

Agatha hums. As quick as a soft smile appears on her lips, it vanishes. She lifts her glass to her mouth and drains the rest of the whisky. “Trust me, not everyone would agree with you there,’ she comments, her eyes growing dark. 

 

“What do you mean?” Rio asks, confusion growing.

 

“Ask me what type of law I practice,” Agatha provokes.

“You’re an infant,” Agatha groans, throwing her head back.

“I’m twenty-three,” Rio corrects before stage whispering. “And if you can sneak over a Smirnoff Ice for m’lady, that would be great.”

 

“I’m in my 30’s, Rio - not ancient. I know what getting Iced is,” Agatha declares.

 

“Did you know that frats throw 90’s parties now?” Rio asks without skipping a beat and, well, rather boldly. “And that the 2000’s are considered vintage?”

“I’m with her,” the waiter agrees, pointing at Agatha. “And I’m leaving before this conversation causes any more emotional damage. What are you having, you bearer of false news?”

“I don’t care what the bill comes out to be, I’m tipping that man one hundred percent,” Agatha states firmly, unraveling her silverware set to find her fork. “You are a menace.”

 

“Like the Maximoff twins,” Rio nods, pushing her basket of fries towards the middle of the table. She keeps the aioli closer to herself. “Seems like I’m in good company - maybe we can talk Nicky onto our side,” she muses, picking up and taking a bit of a singular garlic loaded french fry.

 

Agatha stabs a brussel sprout with her fork and gestures the silverware towards her to punctuate each word, “Don’t you dare corrupt my sweet, innocent bean sprout caretaker.”

 

Rio wrinkles her nose at the dreaded vegetable, as if its very existence has caused her great personal offense. “You should really consider getting him an actual plant of his own,” she suggests. “Especially if he’s this partial to a little bean.” 

 

“I have been known to kill succulents - that may not be a good idea,” Agatha remarks, bringing the brussel sprout to her mouth.

 

Shaking her head slowly, Rio rolls her eyes and teases, “Fortunately, I’m talking about Nicky and not you, plant killer - set some reminders on your phone.”

 

Agatha huffs and stabs at the dish of veggies once again. The waiter returns with their second round of drinks - nary a bottle of Smirnoff Ice insight.

 

“A golden pothos might be a good one to start with,” Rio continues, ignoring her reaction. “It can handle a missed watering - doesn’t need too much light. You can also propagate them, if you’re feeling confident enough as a plant parent, and they grow out in a trailing vine which makes for some cool decorations.”

 

For a moment, Rio hesitates and then admits, “I’m not sure if they’re safe for rabbits, though. One second..”

 

As Rio fishes her phone out of her pocket, Agatha reaches across the table for Rio’s silverware set to unravel. After a quick search, Rio frowns, shaking her head. “Toxic to rabbits,” she sighs, setting her phone down. “I’ll think of something else,” she states, determined, but the determination disappears in an instance upon noticing her silverware across the table. “What are you doing?” 

 

Agatha pushes the napkin and knife aside, as they won’t be needed. She pierces two pieces onto the end of the fork and holds it towards Rio. “I don’t think an explanation is necessary.”

 

“ Those are toxic to Rio Vidals,” she says firmly, eyes narrowing at the shriveled monstrosities at the end of the fork.

 

“If you want to see the rabbit, you’ll taste the Brussels sprouts,” Agatha states, just as firm and eyes just as narrowed.

 

Rio meets her gaze. ”I don’t negotiate with terrorists.” 

 

“He’s a real fucking cute rabbit, Rio.”

Rio can’t complain.

 

”The fluffiest white fur,” Agatha describes. “Speckled with brown spots. The floppiest ears and the bushiest little tail.” 

 

The tip of Rio’s tongue pushes against the side of her cheek. Her eyes narrow a hair further as she questions, “What about your other rabbit, hmm?” 

 

It’s faint, but it’s there: a small flush across Agatha’s cheeks, a sign of Rio’s tiny victory. Rio bites back a smirk, not daring to provoke her any further. This would do just nicely.

 

Agatha clears her throat, trying to find her grounding once more, and shrugs her shoulders. The tip of the fork is still waved in Rio's general direction. “Guess there’s only one way to find out,” she teases. 

 

Stares never break, Rio reaches out and grabs the damn fork. Reluctantly, she brings it up to her mouth and seductively runs the tip of her tongue against the metal prongs and veggies before pulling them free with her teeth. 

 

There’s a sudden loud bang, the table jerks, and Agatha hisses in pain as she leans over to rub her shin, breaking their gaze.

 

Rio sits up straight, using her free hand to cover her mouth as she chews against her will. “Are you okay?” she mumbles.

 

”Peachy,” Agatha groans, still wincing. “Found the damn table leg.” 

 

“Should I go get some ice-“

 

”No, no,” Agatha interrupts. “Let’s not embarrass myself and wound my pride further. It’ll be fine in a minute. So, how is it?”

 

Swallowing, Rio answers, “I’d rather have scurvy.” 

 

“Oh, come on! They’re cooked in bacon fat!” Agatha defends. “ And there’s a toasted pistachio crumble!”

 

”What a waste of bacon and pistachios,” Rio sighs heavily. She takes a few fries in her hand and dunks it into the aioli - anything to get rid of the wretched taste in her mouth. 

 

“You’re ridiculous,” Agatha grumbles. “More for me.”

 

”Oh, yeah, they’re definitely all for you,” Rio confirms, shoving more fries into her mouth. A beat of silence passes between them before she answers, returning to their previous conversation. “So.. what type of lawyer are you?”

 

Agatha’s lips thin as they press together, giving the answer some thought before giving it voice, “I’m a criminal defense lawyer. I haven’t been known to always represent the best people and that.. really does some great things to one’s reputation.” 

 

Another moment of silence falls between the two women. Rio slowly nods, taking in the information and really processing what Agatha said. “So, for example..” she says, carefully piecing her words together. Her eyes are fixated against the tabletop. She doesn’t want to look up at her; she doesn’t want eye contact. Part of Rio fears Agatha would see right through her. “You’d potentially be the lawyer representing a drunk driver charged with vehicular manslaughter?”

 

There’s another silence, one that lasts a little bit longer than the last. The swirl of Agatha’s glass eventually breaks it.

 

”Bingo.”

 

”Why did you get into that type of law?” Rio asks, looking up from the table.

 

Agatha shakes her head and reminds them both, ”No tragic backstories on the first date, remember?Let’s not ruin the mood.”

 

Rio nods slowly  in agreement and then proposes, ”I think we need to do a couple rounds of shots.” 

 

“You don’t have to tell me twice,” Agatha agrees, turning herself to look over the edge of the booth and away from Rio to catch their waiter’s attention. He takes a couple paces towards their back corner, but stops when Agatha holds up two fingers. She must’ve mouthed the order over to him since Rio doesn’t hear a word. 

 

Agatha shifts back in her seat, returning focus to Rio. “We’re doing double shots of tequila,” she confirms. 

 

“Whoa, okay, then, umm..” Rio surveys their miniscule amount of food. “Then we should maybe order something a bit more substantial to eat.” 

 

“Nicky swears by the chicken tenders here,” Agatha suggests. “You know, considering you two have the same palate.”

 

With a roll of her eyes, Rio counter suggests, “I recall seeing a prosciutto and goat cheese flatbread with a hot honey drizzle. Would you split that with me?”

 

“Sure.”

 

A beat.

 

“Is that why you wouldn’t tell me your name?” Rio asks, bluntly, returning slightly to their previous conversation. “When I first started at the coffee shop?” Although she did agree to not delve into tragic and complicated pasts and in that aspect, Rio wouldn’t dare push, not with a boundary so clearly set. However, it didn’t stop her from putting some pieces of the puzzle together. 

 

“I didn’t want the cute, new barista looking me up and writing me off,” Agatha replies, shrugging her shoulders before stealing a couple of the french fries from the middle of the table. “Though, if we’re being truthful, I also didn’t expect me to actually write and then call out whatever nonsense I said that morning for nearly three weeks .”

 

Rio smirks, rather proud of her ridiculous antics. “I was determined to wear you down until you finally told me, but then.. I took a week off and kinda had to speed up the process a little bit - didn’t want you losing interest,” she shrugs and takes a sip of her drink. “Would you have though? Told me eventually?”

 

Agatha steals another fry, takes a bite, and chews slowly - whether it’s to buy time or mull over a response, Rio can’t tell. Her expression is stoic, unyielding to whatever emotion the older woman is experiencing.

 

And Rio? 

 

She finds herself holding her breath in anticipation.

 

Finishing off the rest of the fry, Agatha leans back against the back of the cushioned booth seat. She folds her arms over her chest. Confused, Rio furrows her eyebrows and purses her lips. Is it that difficult of a question?

 

Then, she feels the side of Agatha’s leg slowly moving up between her legs, running along her inner right. The breath Rio is holding escapes her in a surprised huff of air. With her prize earned, the corner of Agatha’s lips tug into a light smirk.

 

“Yes,” Agatha says simply, confidence radiating off every inch of her.

 

The waiter delivers them each their double shot of tequila rimmed with salt and a saucer of lime slices. “Anything else, ladies?” he asks. 

 

“Yes,” Rio squeaks.

 

Agatha chuckles, “We’ll take the prosciutto and goat cheese flatbread.”

 

“Coming right up.”

 

As the waiter walks away, Agatha pulls away from Rio from under the table, much to Rio’s disappointment. When Agatha scoots closer to the table and picks up one of the shot glasses, Rio mimics her every movement through throwing the shot glass back.

She’s fluent in Spanish and picked up a little Portuguese, but definitely not enough  to confidently speak despite Agatha’s insistence (the Spanish, however, makes Agatha a bit flustered - Rio will keep this knowledge in her back pocket). It made the most sense to come back to Westview.

 

They pick away at the fries and brussel sprouts - yes, even Rio, earning her some more teasing from Agatha (“ admit it , they’re growing on you.” ‘That sounds like a very serious condition, I should get it checked out”). They sip on their drinks until empty, both feeling the intended buzz.

 

Agatha reveals she grew up outside of Salem, Massachusetts (and suddenly the that witch coffee cup name makes much more sense) until elementary school when her mother suddenly moved them to Westview. She left for Boston for law school and never looked back. She did a small stint in New York City, but couldn’t imagine raising a child there. She had always wanted to be a mother and being on her own wasn’t about to stop her from achieving that goal.

 

They share the flat bread once it arrives and continue their delicate little dance of learning, engaging, and teasing until nothing but crumbs remain on their respective plates. They agree on one last round of shots - singles this time, if only to keep the tipsiness going. 

 

When they realize that they’ve lost track of time, Agatha pulls out her phone to text Wanda an apology, but Wanda doesn’t mind. Nicky asks if he can have a sleepover and Agatha grants permission without a second thought. 

 

Just as she earlier insisted, Agatha pays the bill, only so she can tip the waiter one hundred percent - both for his service and, well, for Rio making both him and her feel like they have one foot in the grave (“seriously, what are you? The grim reaper?”). 

 

The sun is setting when they finally leave the establishment and step onto the sidewalk, happy hour long behind them.

 

“Did you drive..?” Rio asks. “You definitely shouldn’t drive now.”

 

“Walked,” Agatha answers. “I’m not too far from the main square.”

 

Rio nods and insists, “Let me walk you home, then.”

 

“That’s not necessary-”

 

“I wanna be chivalrous - let me be chivalrous,” Rio interrupts, puffing out her chest in defiance and only sways slightly. She’s not taking no for an answer.


It earns her a small laugh from Agatha who quickly looks her over before nodding. “Alright then,” she agrees. “ Vamanos , my knight in shiny armor.”

”Just in case what ?” Agatha challenges, smirking as she takes the offered seat.

 

Rio flushes and clears her throat, “You didn’t have to do that - bring breakfast.”

 

Agatha shrugs, “It’s just bagels and not even great ones at that. It’s also the least I can do after making you go home at 3 in the morning.” Pulling out a wrapped bagel from the bag. Agatha offers it to her. “Plus, it gives me an excuse to see you again.”

 

Unwrapping the plain bagel and cream cheese, Rio lowers her head in an attempt to hide her sheepish smile. “Well, then, thank you,” she murmurs. After a beat, her head snaps up as the realization hits her. “Hang on, wait-“

 

”Wait?” Agatha repeats, leaning back in the office chair, confused as she unwraps her own bagel. “For what?”

 

”What’s the difference between a rabbit and a bunny?” Rio questions, dead serious. “There is no difference, right?”

 

Agatha throws her head back and laughs.

 

”No, seriously, Agatha,” Rio speaks over her, fighting back a giggle over her own. Agatha’s laugh is infectious. “Nicky will ask me. He gave me homework .” 

 

“And Ms. Vidal failed to complete the homework? Sounds like that means no recess,” Agatha teases, licking the excess cream cheese running along the edge of the bagel. 

 

The sight makes Rio’s mouth grow dry; a pit forms in her stomach. She swallows hard and shakes her head, willing the not so work appropriate thoughts away. “There’s no difference,” she states again.

 

”Right, no difference,” Agatha confirms, crossing her legs. She rips off a piece of bagel. “But ‘bunny’ sounds like a word a baby would use and he, as he will very adamantly tell and remind  you, is not a baby.”

 

”So, what do I tell him?” Rio asks, frowning. “I can’t imagine that’s an answer he wants to hear, especially if potentially surrounded by some of his own peers.”

 

Agatha shrugs, popping a bit into her mouth and speaking around the piece, “You’ll figure it out, I’m sure.”

 

”Thanks for the help,” Rio mutters in disbelief. She will come up with something, that much she knows, and it might as well be on the spot - something that she will not overthink. Something is bound to come to her. 

 

“Happy to offer it - speaking of which,” Agatha begins. “The PTA - or the school - I don’t remember. The emails are very similar. Anyway, whoever sent out a message about the first grade field trip in two weeks.”

 

Ah, yes, their trip to Westview’s little botanical garden - a tie in to their current science unit. The kids were already buzzing with excitement for their excursion outside of school during school hours. It is mostly because they get to ride the school bus like the big middle school kids for once. 

 

These kids.. always wishing to be older, to be more grown up. Rio wishes they could hold onto their own innocence for as long as they can; there’s no need nor rush to become an adult. It’s not as cracked up as they make it out to be while young.

 

Instead of voicing a response, Rio nods in confirmation.

 

”And it states that parental chaperones were needed,” Agatha continues. “Usually, I would never offer my services. Frankly, there are only three kids that I willingly keep an eye on, but.. considering that they’re all three in the first grade and that I am.. quite fond of a particular teacher. I’m thinking of throwing my name into the ring - with Nicky’s permission, of course.” 

 

“I..” Rio’s voice trails off, her mind still stuck on quite fond of a particular teacher . She knows this about Agatha - her desire to make these little comments that knock the wind out of Rio. Rio, usually, can stand her ground, even if the flushing out of her facial features gives her away. 

 

But here at school? It’s an uneven playing field, one that Agatha recognizes and uses in her teasing nature and advantage. She knows Rio will try not to react.

 

“Umm, do you want me to put your name down? Or -“

 

”I’ll email whoever once Nicky gives me the green light,” Agatha answers, taking another bite. 

 

Rio’s is still on her desk, untouched. She’s too distracted to eat.

 

”Thanks - I think Mrs. Maximoff is helping out, too.” 

 

“Of course , Wanda is,” Agatha finds herself grumbling as she finishes off the top half of the bagel. 

 

Frowning, Rio tilts her head and asks, “Are you sure you two are friends?” 

 

“Best of, unfortunately,” Agatha sighs dramatically. “Seriously, though, when push comes to shove, I know Wanda will out mamma bear any one of those other assho-“

 

”Language.” 

 

“Dicks-“

 

”That’s not any better, Agatha,” Rio groans with a chuckle. “See, this is why I locked the door.” 

 

“And here I thought I was getting a private session with Ms. Vidal,” Agatha sighs, wrapping the rest of the bagel for later. “Though, as a second thought, I don’t really mind the reprimanding.” 

 

Agatha is a mystery, one that Rio wants to solve, but there’s a complexity behind her that leaves her, well, mystified.

 

”You’re incorrigible,” Rio says, folding her arms over her chest as she shakes her head.

 

”Come on, don’t you prefer me horizon-“

 

”Don’t finish that thought,” Rio interrupts.

 

”Why?” Agatha asks, leaning forward and looking up to meet Rio’s gaze. She now hovers just above Rio’s lap. “Afraid I’m right?”

 

Rio isn’t afraid; Rio is well aware that Agatha is very much right.

 

Before Rio can respond, there’s a knock on the classroom door, followed by the sound of the locked doorknob turning, but refusing to grant access. The sound makes both women jump slightly, but only Rio hops up onto her feet. 

 

There’s a second knock just as Rio grabs the door knob and pulls it open for Lilia who takes a step inside. Her lips thin when she spots Agatha pulling herself up onto her feet from Rio’s office chair.

 

”Ms. Vidal, our doors are to remain unlocked during school hours,” she chastises. “You know the rules.”

 

”Yes, Ms. Calderu, I know - must’ve forgotten to keep it unlocked on my way in,” Rio quickly explains. “Honest mistake.”

 

”Let’s ensure it doesn’t happen again, hmm?”

 

”Always a stickler for the rules. Technically,” Agatha begins, making her way over to the two women. “School hours start at 8am. It’s a little before 7:30. My client is innocent, Honorable Judge Calderu.”

 

Lilia looks Agatha over and shakes her head, “You haven’t changed one bit, Ms. Harkness.”

 

”And neither have you, Calderu,” Agatha replies, inching towards the door. “I was just on my way out. Ms. Vidal, thank you for listening to my concerns.”

 

”Yes, you're welcome,” Rio murmurs. “Let me walk you out.”

 

” Chivalrous , this one,” Agatha comments. “The district should be very proud of this hire - give her a raise immediately .”

 

Biting back a smile, Rio rolls her eyes, shaking her head.

 

If only the promotion process were that easy.

 

“Hurry back, Ms. Vidal - we have our Monday morning meeting,” Lilia reminds. 

 

Rio nods and then follows Agatha out into the hallway, retracing the steps she took this morning on her way in. They step off the curb together and cross into the parking lot, with Agatha fishing her keys out of her blazer pocket.

 

”Thank you for the breakfast,” Rio says, breathlessly, once they reach the familiar black Mercedes. 

 

Agatha waves a dismissive hand and unlocks the door with her other, “No need to thank me, really.” She pulls the door open. “Though, I am sorry it was rushed - there’s more that I wanted to talk about.”

 

“Oh? What about?” Rio asks, trying not to sound surprised.

 

”Not here, not now,” Agatha answers with a finality that does not allow for further questioning, especially now that their time has no short. “Nicky has soccer practice at 6 this evening at the high school, if you’re willing to join. It usually lasts an hour.”

 

A soft ‘oh’ escapes Rio once again as the invitation takes her by surprise. “Um, yeah, I can be there, but would it be a little odd if I showed up..?” 

 

“In the conquest of the Kingdom of the Dust Bunnies yesterday, it.. may have come up that we are..” Agatha’s voice trails off as she glances Rio up and down. “That we’re friends .” 

 

“Friends,” Rio repeats, slowly as to enunciate the word. A smile threatens to tug at the corner of her lips once more this morning. “Now, why do you say that with such displeasure?” 

 

“Because I don’t necessarily make it a point to fuck my friends,” Agatha remarks without missing a beat.

 

The forceful, blatant response chokes Rio. She coughs roughly into a clenched fists and immediately looks around their surroundings for any potential eavesdroppers. The parking lot is still relatively empty, thankfully.

 

Agatha smirks rather gleefully because of course she does . 

 

“Are you feeling alright there, Ms. Vidal?” Agatha asks, bemused. “You know, I was just thinking: the morning air is starting to get a little crispier, isn’t it? Wouldn’t want you to catch anything now.”

 

The joke’s on Agatha - Rio’s already caught something, indeed, but Rio keeps that particular feeling to herself.

 

“I’ll see you this evening, Ms. Harkness,” she manages.

 

–

 

Alice lends her the car for the evening; it didn’t take much convincing, not after Rio willingly reveals her invitation for the soccer practice. Alice claimed that she was doing her part “in getting this ship sailing” - those were her direct words - right before insisting that Rio could have the car whenever, if it meant updated details. 

 

She was too invested in this.

 

The soccer field is behind the school, Rio drives along the perimeter of the grounds until she reaches the sports fields’ parking lot. The spots near the fence entrance are already taken, claimed by parents and their children who arrived early and not at 6:05 (Rio lost track of time, unsure what to wear; the clock pressured her into a simple pair of dark jeans and her alumnae university sweater - the laundry situation is dire, she realized).

 

Rio parks a row over, kills the ignition, and scrambles out of the car. Agatha’s words from their date echoing in her mind: I don’t tolerate tardiness.

 

Yeah, but she didn’t say it this time, Rio points out to herself. It doesn’t stop her from jogging up to and squeezing through the gate. She scans the group of parents standing on the sidelines and then over to the ones sitting on the bleachers. 

 

Not a Harkness in sight.

 

Fallen shoulders spring back up hearing a voice - an adult, rather unexpectedly - call out, “Ms. Vidal!”

 

Rio turns her attention back to the parents standing along the edge of the field. A redhead in black leggings and red flannel waves her over. 

 

“Mrs. Maximoff,” Rio greets, approaching the woman. She reaches out for a handshake. “It’s good to see you again.”

 

“Agatha and Nicky are running late,” Wanda explains, keeping her eye on the twins running among the children on the field. “They were having issues with Sca– boys!” Wanda raises her voice, addressing her children in a language that Rio doesn’t recognize.

 

“ Mom !” Billy shouts back in surprise.

 

“We’re not allowed to push them!” Tommy condons. 

 

“At least try to practice speaking Sokovian,” Wanda grumbles under her breath, offering an apologetic smile to the women standing within earshot to their right.

 

Rio chuckles, recalling Agatha’s colorful, near complete sentiment regarding the woman deemed her best friend: if push comes to shove, Wanda will out mamma bear the rest of the other parents.

 

“I was the same way their age with Spanish,” Rio shares. “Understanding was no problem. My parents talked to me consistently in Spanish. But speaking? I stubbornly refused until I was older.”

 

“Yeah?” Wanda laughs dryly. “What changes between now and then. Give me something to look forward to, please?”

 

“They’ll realize how important it is to keep a connection like that with their family,” Rio answers softly with sincere honesty, eyes focused on the kids trying to steal the ball away from each other in small groups.

 

“And what do they think of your Spanish now?” 

 

Rio pauses, caught off guard by the question she doesn’t know how to address. Before she thinks of a way to evade answering all together, a blur of a small child runs between her and Wanda, dropping a water bottle on the grass at their feet.

 

Wanda calls out to him, “Nicholas Harkness! Is that how you tell your favorite aunt ‘hello?’”

 

“You’re my only aunt and I’m late , Aunt Wanda,” Nicky yells back, not bothering to look as he continues towards Billy and Tommy. “Scratchy hates the car!”

 

“Scratchy..?” Rio repeats, eyebrows furrowing.

 

Wanda turns towards the parking lot and calls out to the woman making her approach towards them, “Did you seriously bring the rabbit, Agatha?”

 

Rio follows Wanda’s lead. The moment her eyes land on a very dressed down Agatha (a completely different version from this morning), the wind is knocked out of Rio’s lungs, especially when she spots the brown spotted, white fur little creature curled against her chin. Was he wearing a harness? And, oh god, he had a leash ?

 

It takes everything in her willpower to not self combust.

 

“Of course I did,” Agatha confirms, stopping a couple of feet in front of them. With a coy smirk, as she continues to rub small little circles against Scratchy’s side, Agatha meets Rio’s gaze. “I am a woman of my word after all. Also, don’t take that tone with your god-rabbit,” she adds, turning to Wanda, now frowning.

 

“Oh, Christ ,” Wanda sighs, exacerbated. “There is no such thing as god-rabbits.”

 

“That’s what you think,” Agatha snaps back, tilting her chin down to touch her chest so she can place a small kiss on the top of Scratchy’s head. He ducks a bit from the sudden movement, but escaping is not an option. His nose continues to twitch and his eyes dart around - Rio could die right here and right now from the cuteness overload. Whether it would be from the rabbit or Agatha is anyone’s guess. 

 

“But if anything happens to me ,” Agatha continues. “You’re getting both Nicky and Scratchy, Wands. I have an ironclad will and you’ll be stuck with both of my boys.”

 

“You’re ridiculous,” Wanda murmurs, shaking her head. The loud screech of a whistle pulls her attention back to the practice at hand. She yells out something once more in Sokovian.

 

“So I’ve been told,” Agatha mumbles before turning to Rio. With the tilt of the head towards their left, she explains, “Let's go to the other end - people make him nervous, especially Maximoffs.”

 

Wanda doesn’t return her attention to the two other woman, but regardless she defends, “Tommy pulled his ear one time on accident- ”

 

“And one time was enough - the little heathen. Isn’t that right, Scratch?” Agatha asks the rabbit for confirmation. Scratchy only continues to wiggle his nose. “Come on.”

 

Rio doesn’t need to be told twice. 

 

She would follow Scratchy into battle, not to just the opposite end of the field’s bleacher. Agatha is the first to sit down on the first row - a good choice, considering Rio knows the rabbit doesn’t mind launching himself from at least the height of Agatha’s bed. Agatha shimmers the tote bag off her shoulder and onto the bench; Rio takes a seat next to it.

 

“You’re going to have to bribe him with treats,” Agatha explains. She uses one hand to hold Scratchy in place against her chest and the other to blindly fish around the tote bag. “This asshole has been known to bite - he bit me yesterday morning after I realized I forgot to feed him dinner Saturday night.”

 

She finds the small plastic bag containing thin apple slices and holds it out to Rio.

 

“And he’s the asshole?” Rio asks, pulling the bag open and grabbing one of the slices between her finger tips. She leans forward towards Scratchy and holds it up to him. The rabbit pushes away from Agatha’s chest and inspects the treat carefully before he begins to nibble. “I would have bitten you, too.” 

 

“You did bite me!” Agatha reminds, carefully moving Scratchy towards Rio, who cups him into her free, appleless, arm. 

 

Scratchy immediately leans into Rio’s body, confirming one very important thought: Rio would die for this god damn rabbit.

 

“That same night,” Agatha continues. “You’re the reason I forgot-”

 

“Don’t blame me !” Rio’s head snaps up at the unfinished accusation. She tilts her head back down towards the rabbit. “Scratchy, don’t listen to her,” she speaks now in a baby voice. “She’s already trying to destroy our friendship.”

 

Agatha scoffs, “Don’t speak to him like a toddler - he’s more dignified than that.”

 

“You’re right,” Rio agrees with a chuckle, fishing out another apple slice. “He’s a true gentleman.”

 

A silence lulls between the two of them - well, three of them. They watch as Scratchy truly relaxes, nearly flops against Rio, as he finishes the second apple slice. Content, Rio gently begins to stroke the rabbit’s soft fur. 

 

Agatha was right: he is a really fucking cute rabbit.

 

“I owe you an apology,” Agatha speaks up, breaking the comfort of both the silence and the moment.

 

“For trying to turn Scratchy against me?” Rio asks, too distracted to hear the seriousness in Agatha’s tone. “For calling him an asshole?”

 

“No, an actual genuine apology,” Agatha clarifies.

 

Rio looks up, confused.

 

“I wanted to say this to you this morning,” Agatha continues. “But Lilia’s intrusion made me realize it wasn’t the best time. You had a whole work day to get through and it wouldn’t have been appropriate. Not my most brilliant move.”

 

“But apologize for what, exactly?” Rio asks.

 

“I.. “ Agatha begins slowly, carefully searching for the words as she looks Rio in the eye. Rio holds onto Scratchy a little bit tighter, trying to soak in all the grounding comfort the little guy is providing. ”While we were enjoying ourselves Saturday - at the bar, specifically - you alluded to something I couldn’t stop thinking about. I should have let it be, but.. I didn’t think anything would turn up because what are the odds?”

 

Rio’s voice grows incredibly soft as she pushes again, “What do you mean..?” 

 

Blatantly and to the point as to no longer continue beating around the bush, Agatha blurts out, “I found the crime scene report from the accident.”                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 

And there it is: the rug being pulled from underneath her (she’s glad she’s already sitting down); the stomach punch that leaves her breathless; the immediate stinging of her dark eyes. Rio immediately turns her head away from Agatha. She focuses on the kids now running up and down the field - no soccer ball in sight; she focuses on the soft fur underneath her finger tips; she focuses on not suddenly drowning.

 

“Rio,” Agatha says gently. “I’m sorry. Truly.”

 

A dry, short laugh escapes Rio before she can bite it back, “Sorry for losing my parents and sisters or sorry for looking it up?”

 

There’s a beat of silence, a moment before Agatha asks in return, “Can it be both?”

 

Rio nods slowly, keeping her eyes forward - keeping her gaze on the practice.

 

“I didn’t go beyond that first page,” Agatha insists. “I know it wasn’t my right to find out this way. I won’t, however, apologize for also looking up the verdict after the fact because I needed to know-”

 

“How good his criminal defense lawyer was?” Rio knows she sounds more bitey than intended; she knows none of this should be directed at Agatha. That, however, doesn’t stop her voice from being curt, laced with sharp edges as the scared little girl from years ago lashes out in a dormant, now reawakening fright.

 

“That he got what he deserved,” Agatha states immediately and firmly. “There are plenty of times where you don’t want these people winning, Rio, but then you remember the oath you took.”

 

“So then why do you do it?” Rio questions, an answer seeks from their shared Saturday together.

 

“For the people who are truly innocent. Wrong place, wrong time, or things gone astray. I do it for the people who are good and need help getting others to see it,” Agatha explains. Rio still doesn’t look at her - she can’t. It’s taking every last ounce of herself to hold everything together.

 

“But people like this guy?” Agatha continues. “Who leaves a six year old completely abandoned and orphaned? I’m glad to see he served out his life sentence.”

 

The last sentence hangs in the air. Rio sits up a little bit straighter, eyebrows furrowing as she struggles to understand that string of words tied together. Frowning, she finally forces herself to turn to Agatha. There can be no trace of confusion.

 

There can’t be any confusion.

 

“Served out..?” she says, slowly.

 

Agatha hesitates, desperately looking over the younger woman’s tense features as if meticulously trying to figure out how now to further cause her any harm. Instead, Agatha offers only the truth, “Rio, he passed away two years ago.”

 

The dam immediately breaks, but Rio refuses to allow herself to be overcome right here, right now. Not in front of these kids and their parents - some of which were her own students and their parents. No, she wouldn’t permit it.

 

“Rio,” Agatha tries again.

 

Rio holds Scratchy out to Agatha who instantly scoops him back into her arms.

 

“I need to go,” Rio all but whispers as she stands up and beelines for the parking lot. If Agatha calls after her, she doesn’t hear it; she can’t hear anything over the loud ringing in her ears, mixed with the sound of her rapid heart beating so painfully against her ribs, feeling as though it could explode at any moment.

 

She doesn’t register her movements; she’s functioning on autopilot. She couldn’t tell you how she got into Alice’s beaten up Subaru, or how she managed to roar the engine into life, or pulling out of the sports area parking lot. 

 

No, the next moment Rio realizes where she is, she’s in front of the high school drop off area - at least even not in control, she has the good sense to not try to drive home.

 

The loud, watery sob that escapes her, takes her by surprise. Rio crashes a hand against her mouth, trying to silence herself as tears cascade down her cheeks. With a shaking free hand, she grabs her phone from her pocket - nearly drops it into the abyss of the nooks and crannies of the console. 

 

Trembling fingers find the contact she seeks. Her call is answered on the second ring. She knows she can’t hide the emotion thick in her voice, so she doesn’t bother. All she says is, “Alice..”

 

“ Don’t you dare move. I’ll be right there .”

“Where is she?” Alice demands, out of breath, as she jogs up to the car that Rio is leaning against. “I swear to god, Rio, I’ll knock some sense into her right here, right now - ow!”

 

Alice grabs the stitch that radiates a sharp ache from her side and doubles over, wincing.

“Are you ?” she pants, trying to catch her breath. “Did she hurt you?”

 

“Did you run the entire way here?”

 

“Rio, did she hurt you?” Alice repeats in between breaths, evident that yes, she did run all the way here from the apartment. She stands up as straight as she can and stares at Rio, waiting for an answer - waiting to see what her next move should be. 

 

Oh, Alice, always the protector - her protector. 

 

Ever since their first group therapy session as rebellious preteens - both hurting in their own unique way, both pushing the limits of the boundaries set for them instead of by them.

 

Swallowing the lump in her throat, Rio slowly shakes her head. Her bottom lip trembles and tears collect along the brim of her eyes once again. She tilts her chin down, trying to hide the visibility of being upset, but she knows it’s all for nothing. Alice, who reaches out and places a hand on Rio’s shoulder, already knows. There’s no hiding. 

 

“Hey,” Alice says softly.

 

”The driver’s dead,” Rio chokes the words out, followed by a heavy sigh masking another sob that threatens to escape. When she looks up at Alice, the tears fall and trail down her cheeks.

 

”The driver..?” Alice repeats, frowning, but the realization hits her a moment later. “Oh! The driver! Shit, Rio, I’m sorry - are you okay? Did they just call you?” 

 

“No, it was  years ago,” Rio struggles to verbalize. “ Two years ago.”

 

“And they’re just telling you now?” Alice, perplexed, asks. “That’s fucked up.”

 

Rio shakes her head, “ They didn’t tell me anything.” 

 

Alice stares at her a moment longer and then begins to slowly shake her head as she pieces the metaphoric puzzle Rio gives her into place. “No,” she states firmly. “No, no, no, no - that’s fucked up. She looked you up? How would she even have acc—“

 

”Can we not do this here?” Rio practically begs. “Their practice is about to end and I really don’t want to be here anymore, Al.” 

 

“Say less, then,” Alice sidesteps around Rio to grab the driver side’s door handle. “Let’s get out of here.”

 

Much to Rio’s relief, the drive back home to the apartment is a silent one, though not as short as she hoped it would be. Alice insists on stopping at the grocery store for what she further insists are necessary items. Rio stays behind, curled up in the passenger seat, trying to hide from the world that seems just a bit too big. Too scary.

 

She feels like she’s six years old all over again, waking up in the hospital to the life shattering news that she’s completely and utterly alone.

 

Alice returns with a singular bag of ‘necessities’: two pints of rocky road ice cream, a bag of gummy worms, and double stuffed Oreos. The gesture makes Rio well up again.

 

Once home, Alice hands her a plastic ziplock bag and their meat tenderizing hammer, officially putting Rio on Oreo duty (and unofficially letting her release any built up tension from the last hour). It’s when the ziplock bag rips open in the middle from the excess pounding that Alice reminds her they need chunks, not crumbs. In response to Rio’s glare, Alice thinks crumbs would work just fine, too.

 

In separate bowls, they throw in various scoops of ice cream and a handful of gummy worms, topped with the crumbs and dust of the smashed cookies before collapsing onto the couch with their concoction. 

 

For the longest time, neither of them speak. They sit, leaning against the couch and each other, and pick away at their ice-cream. Rio focuses on excavating the gummy worms - focuses on anything else than the events of this evening.

 

Alice is the first to speak.

 

”I’ll still jump her for you, you know.” 

 

It’s short and curt, but Rio lets out a singular laugh. 

 

“She’s a defense attorney,” Rio answers her question from the high school drop off. “I think that’s how she has access.”

 

”So, what? She does a background check on all her potential dates?” Alice asks through another spoonful of ice-cream. 

 

“God, I hope not,” Rio murmurs under her breath. “We did some dumb shit back in the day.”

 

”Don’t drag me into it - your record is your own,” Alice points out.

 

Rio decides to ignore the comment. Record is too strong of a word anyway. It’s only a couple of misdemeanors at best. “She says she only looked at the first page and the verdict. So, that would be the accident report, I think?”

 

”That’s a first page too many,” Alice grumbles, trying to rip apart a cold gummy worm where the seam of the two flavors combine.

 

“I don’t think she meant to find anything-“

 

“But she looked.”

 

“That’s not what I’m most upset about,” Rio admits, stirring the melting ice cream.

 

Silence.

 

“Two years,” Rio murmurs, shaking her head. “For two years, I didn’t know.”

 

“Did you want to know?” 

 

Rio sighs and continues to stir - a distraction. “I don’t know,” she ultimately confesses. “It doesn’t change anything. Nothing is different.”

 

“Everything is different.”

 

“Everything is different,” Rio repeats in a mumble with a slow nod. “You’d think it would’ve been a moment - a moment where the universe shifted or something? Anything to let me know what happened - a sign. Hey, the reason why you and your sisters never started that band? The reason why your parents never got to see you grow up? Yeah, karma finally came for that asshole. But I got nothing. I didn’t know. No one told me.”

 

“Agatha told you.”

 

“Agatha told me,” Rio echos the confirmation.

 

“But she only told you after looking up your entire childhood trauma.”

 

Rio throws her head back against the cushions so she’s staring up at the ceiling and releases a disgruntled, heavy sigh. “Not all of it, if she’s being honest. That can’t fit onto a page.”

 

“Your ice cream is turning into soup,” Alice informs.

 

“It’s better that way,” Rio mumbles, looking back down into her bowl of mostly melted ice cream and gummy worms. She pushes the pieces of candy around, from side to side. 

 

Rio adds softly, “Thank you, by the way, for coming.”

 

“Any time. You know that,” Alice replies, just as gentle.

 

She’s also thankful that Alice doesn’t push her to talk, that they sit there in silence just finishing up the rest of their ‘dinner’ (Rio tries not to hear Agatha’s voice in her head, commenting on her ability to eat exactly like a six year old - she, the woman holding onto her bowl of ice cream she’s calling dinner - fails miserably). She is equally thankful in the morning when Alice offers to take her substitute plans in so Rio doesn’t need to leave the apartment.

 

Mental health day, she decides, is necessary; she can’t possibly channel her bubbly teaching persona today. The mere thought is enough to drain what little energy she possesses. 

 

When Alice leaves, manila folder in hand, Rio retreats to the sanctity of the living room couch, with its throw blankets, and returns to the ghost hunting ‘docu-series’ from the previous week (or was it the week before that? She doesn’t remember - time is both an illusion and melding all together anyway). She’s still not a fan of ghosts - would never want to encounter one herself, but the program satisfies a morbid curiosity, even if the hosts tend to get under her skin in nearly every episode she watches.

 

She’s nearly done with her second episode of the morning when there is a knock at the apartment door, one that startles her into a jump. Furrowed eyebrows, Rio pauses the television and stares over her right shoulder towards the door. She’s not expecting anyone, nor anything for that matter (most of their packages get delivered downstairs anyway). Had the television been too loud? Did she accidentally disgruntle a neighbor?

 

The notion of ignoring it and hoping it goes away is thrown out the window when a second series of knocks raptures from the door, relentlessly forcing Rio onto her feet which she drags all the way over. Still clad in hunter green pajama pants and the same hoodie from yesterday, Rio opens the door just a crack - enough space to slam it back shut if need be.

 

When her swirling mind registers that it’s Agatha standing on the other side, holding a bouquet of white tulips in one hand and her other behind her back, Rio isn’t sure if she wants to slam it back shut or open it.

 

Instead, she settles with a soft and confused, “How..?” Because Rio is sure she never gave Agatha her apartment number.

 

Agatha shifts back and forth on her feet before sheepishly admitting, “I figured yours would be the one with the four potted plants outside of it. Or rather, I really hoped it was otherwise this would’ve been pretty awkward.” She reveals the hand behind her back, clutching onto a bag of mini three musketeers. 

 

“And how did you know I wasn’t at school?” 

 

“Oh, I tried making a delivery there first - the ancient looking man at your desk looked very delighted until I told him they were for someone else,” Agatha explains.

 

A slow nod. 

 

“What’s all this for?” Rio asks as she continues her line of questioning, still not opening the door beyond the three inch crack she’s peeking through.

 

“An apology,” Agatha answers, truthfully. “One that I am really hoping you’ll accept. I’m sorry, Rio. You should’ve been the one to share such intimate and personal information. And I’m sorry for bringing it up at practice - for wanting to bring it up yesterday morning at your work. Neither would’ve been or are appropriate.”

 

”I mean, kudos for picking the better of the two shitty options,” Rio comments. “At least you brought an emotional support rabbit.” 

 

“I’m sorry,” Agatha repeats again. “I just.. once I saw it - knew about it - I panicked. I felt caught and I needed to confess immediately . I didn’t think about your own well being.” 

 

Then Agatha’s voice becomes uncharacteristically small, “I can be good, Rio.” 

 

Eyeing the tulips, Rio tilts her head to the side as she listens to Agatha’s confession. The question escapes her rather bluntly, “Did you google ‘flowers symbolizing forgiveness’ and went with the first choice?”

 

A beat.

 

“Yes,” Agatha admits, wincing. “Is it that obvious?”

 

Wrinkling her nose, Rio shrugs her shoulders and finally opens the rest of the door. “I appreciate the effort. The lack of the emotional support rabbit dings you some points, though,” she teases, taking both the flowers and the bag of chocolate.

 

Agatha enters with a cautious step. “Scratch is currently serving out his near daily sentence known as the Harkness school and work day.” 

 

Rio closes the front door and notices Agatha’s shoulders tense. “Not the best analogy, I’m —“

 

”It’s fine,” Rio interrupts, offering up a small smile of reassurance. “Do you want some coffee? Can’t promise you espresso. Or oat milk, but I find it drinkable. We only have a coffee pot.” 

 

“What are you trying to offer me, Vidal?” 

 

Rio shrugs, “Probably something you’d consider a war crime against taste buds is my guess.” 

 

Agatha’s shoulders relax at the return of the bantering - the return of familiar territory and playing field. She follows Rio across the living room and into the kitchen where the earlier brewed coffee awaits them. Rio sets the bouquet and candy down on the counter before rounding it to fetch two mugs from the cupboard. 

 

She pours and then pushes one of the mugs, along with a small container of brown sugar, towards Agatha who remains standing on the opposite side of the counter from Rio. Agatha helps herself to a spoonful as Rio mixes in some milk into her own mug.

 

Their eyes meet when Agatha lifts the mug to her lips for a sip. Agatha tries to hold back any sign of a reaction, but Rio catches the small twitch of an eyebrow and she can almost hear the biting comment trying to claw its way out.

 

”Best cup you’ve ever had?” Rio goads. 

 

“Best I’ve had in this apartment,” Agatha remarks. It’s technically the truth. Rio can’t fault her for it, but leave it to Agatha, of course, to find herself a loophole.

 

A soft laugh escapes Rio as she reaches over for the sugar. She pulls it over to herself and scoops two spoonfuls into her own coffee. Watching the liquid carefully, she slowly begins to stir. 

 

When she stops and sets the spoon on top of a paper towel, she begins to speak, “It was around Christmas that year, we were driving back late from the city. I think there was ice on the road-“ 

 

“Rio, you don’t have to-“ 

 

“It’s fine; I’m fine,” Rio says, looking up to meet Agatha’s soft gaze. “It.. It was a long time ago.”

 

But there are times when grief raises its ugly head, engulfs her, and makes it feel as though it were still fresh - as if no time has passed and, at the same time, it was truly an eternity away.

 

The fact that she doesn’t remember any of it bothers Rio most of all. The head injury, the brain swelling and bleeding, she sustained stole those memories from her, along with the ones of her last family outing. It was the most consequential day of her life and she had to be told about it through reports, police officers, and medical professionals. To her, it was all a series of facts and cold details. There was nothing personable, yet.. the consequences were anything but.

 

The consequences were real and irrational and so unfair . 

 

“We had just spent the day in the city - it was my and my sisters’ first time,” Rio recalls fondly. The trip had been on the calendar in the kitchen for weeks. Each Vidal sister took turns crossing out the days leading up to it. 

 

Rio smiles fondly at the memories she does have. At least she has something . “We were so excited to see the tree and go ice skating. Our parents even surprised us with tickets to the Lion King on Broadway a couple days before. It had collectively been our favorite movie - it’s the only Disney movie I remember ever really watching.”

 

Listening intently, Agatha takes a sip from her mug. Rio pauses to follow suit.

 

“My parents decided to drive us back here after the show,” she continues. “Hotels were expensive and it’s not that long of a drive back. From what I’ve pieced together, we had just taken the exit ramp - the one where you come off the freeway and the ramp does a 360 loop?”

 

“Mmhmm,” Agatha acknowledges. “That’s a terrible ramp.”

 

“Yeah,” Rio sighs heavily, casting her gaze down to the faux marble countertop. “Even more so when you have a drunk driver driving the wrong way pushing 90. The report says everyone died on impact.” 

 

She pauses when she hears Agatha exhale sharply. Don’t look up , Rio tells herself, reaching the moment where people start to look at her with a deep pity that makes her want to crawl out of her skin. It always has.

 

Rio shrugs her shoulders, tracing little doodles on the counter. “Small mercies, I guess,” she murmurs.

 

Agatha sets her mug down with a gentle clank . “You don’t remember?”

 

It is not the usual reaction, so curiosity tilts Rio’s head up. Agatha is watching her. Intently. But it’s not with the gaze of pity she feared. Instead there’s.. a faint frown plays her lips in confusion. Is that.. admiration?

 

“I don’t remember the accident,” Rio says, shaking her head. Her voice drops, becoming small as she adds, “ Or the trip.”

 

She sees Agatha’s jaw clench. The older woman also bites down on her lower lip. “Agatha..?” she cautiously asks.

 

“I keep thinking how you were Nicky’s age,” Agatha remarks, voice low. Her gaze darkens. Her grasp around the mug tightens. “And it just.. it makes me so angry.”

 

“I was angry for the longest time - definitely did my fair share of lashing out, I’ll tell you that much,” Rio confides. “Now it comes in waves, like grief - mostly when I least expect it.”

 

”Like when some moron with a rabbit-“

 

”Yes, exactly that,” Rio chuckles lightly, taking one last swing from her mug. Now empty, she carries it over to the sink. “But, I also have to admit that I don’t know when I would’ve learned about.. him if you didn’t say anything.”

 

She walks around the counter and stands next to Agatha who turns to her. Agatha searches through Rio’s features, lingering especially on her eyes - her dark, tired eyes - for any sign warning her to stay away or welcoming her closer. Rio reaches out and takes Agatha’s hand, clearing the air of confusion.

 

Rio pulls gently on Agatha’s hand, encouraging her to take a step closer. When she does, Rio leans into her and snakes her arms around Agatha’s waist, resting her forehead against the other woman’s shoulder.

 

With slight hesitation, Agatha follows suits, placing her arm around Rio’s middle and her other hand stretching up to and running through hair. Rio leans into Agatha’s hand, inhaling deeply at the soothing comfort - a fleeting moment as once the tips of fingers brush over the edge of a scar running along the side of her head, she freezes.

 

“It was pretty gnarly,” Rio speaks against Agatha’s shoulder, not looking up. “They had to remove a part of my skull due to the brain swelling. I was in and out of the hospital for the rest of the academic year.”

 

“And that’s why you had to repeat the first grade,” Agatha concludes. “I’m sorry for teasing you about it.”

 

There’s a moment of silence before Agatha speaks up again.

 

“Why didn’t I notice this Saturday?” she asks, confused. They spent so much time exploring - discovering - one another. Agatha would not have forgotten this.

 

Rio tilts her head back, but not away from their touch, so she can look at Agatha. “Because I didn’t let you,” she answers simply, as if it is the most obvious thing in the world.

 

Agatha immediately frowns, narrowing her eyes at Rio.

 

Rio copies the very frown, though out of confusion. “What?”

 

“Is that why it took you so long the first time?” Agatha questions.”You were all up in your head while I was giving you-”

 

“Oh, Jesus Christ ,” Rio hisses, lowering her head back down against Agatha’s shoulders.

 

“No, no way - he’s not welcomed in this conversation,” Agatha retorts. “You were distracted , weren’t you? Fess up.”

 

“I was nervous ,” Rio groans against her. 

 

“ Nervous ?” Agatha purrs back. The playfulness in her tone makes Rio whine - the older woman is insufferable. 

 

Agatha takes a half step back, forcing Rio to pull her head up. She meets Agatha’s gaze with a slight pout. “Have you seen you?”

 

“Have you seen you ?” she remarks right back, running the pad of her thumb back and forth over a small portion of Rio’s scar. 

 

Rio sighs, content, as she leans back into the touch. She tilts her head down, meeting Agatha’s eyes. Leaning forward now, she brushes her lips softly against hers and Agatha presses harder against Rio’s. Both smile into the kiss, both–

 

A sharp vibrating emitting from Agatha’s pant pocket causes both of them to pull away. Rio glances down at the vibration and then back up at Agatha. She doesn’t say a word, but she raises an eyebrow.

 

“Don’t look at me like that, you menace,” Agatha huffs.

 

“ I didn’t say a word , Harkness,” Rio points out.

 

To Rio’s dismay, Agatha carefully untangles her hand from the younger woman’s hair and takes her phone out of her pocket. She silences the alarm and slides it back to its spot. “I have a meeting in the office in an hour,” she sighs, not bothering to cover her disappointment.

 

Rio squeezes Agatha tighter for a moment, steals one last kiss, and then breaks their embrace. “Don’t want to make you late,” she says.

 

She’s not convincing. 

 

Not even to herself.

 

“Liar,” Agatha calls her out.

 

Rio grins and shrugs her shoulders. “Just trying to behave. I gotta shake this menace title somehow.”

 

“That’s what your number is saved as on my phone,” Agatha deadpans. 

 

“ Menace ?” Rio huffs, puffing out her cheeks. “I’m saved as-”

 

“Am I still Señor Scratchy in yours?” she challenges.

 

Rio deflates and wrinkles her nose.

 

It’s an answer in itself.

 

Agatha chuckles; Rio smiles instantly, grateful for falling right back into their ridiculous banter. She follows Agatha, who abandons half of her coffee on the counter (Rio doesn’t blame her), to the apartment door. Rio unlocks and opens it for her, allowing for Agatha to step out into the hallway.

 

She turns around and Rio waits for the salutation, but instead she is met with a request: “Come have dinner with us this Friday.”

 

“ Us ?” Rio repeats.

 

Agatha nods, “Me, Nicky, and the love of your life.”

 

“I am so glad my status in Scratchy’s life is well known,” Rio deadpans. “But would that be okay..?” 

 

“Nicky was bummed he didn’t get to see you after practice - again, the kid adores you,” Agatha reiterates. “And if there’s a way for me to make him happy and, well, I get to see you - two birds, one stone and all that animal cruelty.”

 

“Can I bring anything?” Rio offers, leaning against the doorframe.

 

“It.. has been politely requested that you be put on dessert duty,” Agatha replies. “Something about having better taste.”

 

“Well, yeah, your favorite chocolate is an Almond Joy,” Rio teases. “If you have to announce the joy, it can’t really be that joyful.”

 

“I have the power to veto this request, you know.”

 

She sees right through Agatha.

 

Rio shakes her head, “But you won’t because you wouldn’t dare disappointing that little boy, would you? What time should I be there on Friday?”

 

–

 

6:58pm - almost 7 o’clock on the dot, Rio’s phone shows her. She tucks it away into her jeans pocket and returns her free hand to the white box containing the chocolate cake she picked up after work from the bakery on main street. She bounces up the handful of porch stairs and rings the doorbell.

 

The quiet neighborhood’s peaceful lull vanishes to the sound of running feet against wooden floors with an echo of ‘ Mama, she’s here! Mama! ’

 

The rampage stops just on the other side of the door. “Hi, Ms. Vidal!” Nicky shouts through the door. “I’d answer the door, but I need Mama’s permission first.! MAMA! ”

 

Agatha’s voice doesn’t radiate as loud from wherever she is further in the house, but a muffled “Make sure it’s her.”

 

“ Okay !” he yells back.

 

His little voice disappears. Rio stares at the door, perplexed. She takes a step back to inspect the windows on either side, half expected to see Nicky pushing his round face against the pane, but both have their curtains drawn.

 

She’s half way to reaching the doorbell again when he finally speaks up again. “Ms. Vidal, I need to make sure it’s you,” he says. Rio can hear the seriousness in his tone from through the door. She bites back the smile threatening to take over her lips.

 

“We have a class plant,” Nicky states loudly. “You don’t like the name we gave it.”

 

“Geez,” Rio murmurs under her breath, knowing the kid couldn’t hear her. “What’s with the guilt trip there?”

 

“What is the name of the plant?” he asks boldly.

 

Rio rolls her eyes. “Ferny,” she grumbles. When she realizes he probably didn’t hear, she speaks up louder, “Ferny!”

 

“Correct!” he calls back. “And what did you want to name it?”

 

“Fern Ando,” Rio states proudly.

 

A second of silence passes as if Nicky is taking a moment to truly consider her provided answers and trying to see any flaw.

 

The door unlocks and pulls open. Nicky walks around it, beaming up at her.

 

Rio’s smile takes over, but she forces it into a frown. “You know who’d appreciate that kind of humor, though? Second graders,” she taunts.

 

“I’m not in second grade yet , Ms. Vidal,” Nicky reminds. “First grade just started.”

 

“True, very true,” Rio nods in agreement. She steps through the door after Nicky fully opens it for her, allowing her to enter. “That means you’ll fully appreciate what a great name that is for our plant next year.”

 

As an afterthought, Rio adds, “Are you feeling better, bud?” In the late morning, she had to send him to the nurse’s. She gave Agatha a heads up: nauseated and headachy. She would keep an eye on him and keep in touch. He perked right up after lunch, as if nothing happened.

 

“Yup! We’re making spaghetti with tomato sauce and meatballs,” Nicky explains as he closes and locks the door again behind them. He starts to lead Rio around the stairs she is already very familiar with. 

 

Nicky leads her through the open concept living room and dining room. “I wasn’t allowed to help cut the vegetables for the sauce this time because Mama said I was jittering like a jitterbug,” Nicky explains matter of factly. “Do you know what a jitterbug is, Ms. Vidal?”

 

“A term from the ancient 1900’s,” Rio snorts.

 

“I heard that.”

 

Rio’s head snaps up towards the dining room in which Agatha seemingly appeared out of thin air (or, more logically, through the double door behind her - the kitchen, Rio guesses).

 

“Hey, Nicky, can you check to see if Scratch’s eaten his dinner? He’s been real picky this week with his new food,” Agatha requests. 

 

Nicky nods, running past her and through the double doors that swing back shut.

 

She walks up to Rio who holds up the cake box. Agatha places her hands over Rio’s and leans over their shared grasp. It’s quick; it takes her by surprise; and it leaves Rio reeling and wanting. Agatha steals a short, but hungry kiss. 

 

Rio blinks, stupefied. “I..” her voice trails off. “I thought we weren’t going to-”

 

“We’re not, but I wanted at least one.”

 

“Is there even new rabbit food?”

 

Agatha shakes her head, “He’s been eating this brand since we got him.”

 

Before Rio could remark, Nicky comes hurriedly through the doors again. “He ate everything, Mama!” 

 

“That’s great !” Agatha grins, taking the box from Rio. She gestures over her shoulder, indicating Rio to follow. “Do you want to show Ms. Vidal Scratchy's space?”

 

“You can call me ‘Rio’ when we’re not in school, Nicky. Your mom, too,” Rio says, following both Harkness into the kitchen. Two pots were simmering on the stove, almost ready by the looks of fragrant smell of things. 

 

“Oh, okay, Ms - umm,” Nicky hesitates for a moment. “Ms. Rio! Come on, he lives over here.” 

 

“Close enough,” Rio shrugs.

 

Agatha diverts from the group, heading left towards the fridge to store the cake. Nicky leads Rio to the right, through another door into the laundry room. The back half, however, is fenced off by a baby gate. Although she wouldn’t admit it, Rio is now the one jittering. 

 

Nicky walks her up to the edge of the gate and points to Scratchy sitting lazily next to his empty food dish. Nicky shows her and explains what each toy is for scattered in the enclosure (looks like Scratchy had a pretty playful day, she comments).

 

He also shows her that he has two harnesses and two leashes, both sets a different dark shade of purple - it was his and Mama’s favorite color. He explains that they don’t really walk him - you don’t really need to walk a rabbit for exercise, but he gets the zoomies some times when they take him outside (Rio is determined to bear witness). Scratchy loves being outside - he never doesn’t mind snow if he wears a jacket.

 

The mental image nearly makes her combust.

 

It’s not a problem being in the backyard because they have a wooden fence and Scratchy can’t get out. The front yard is too open. There have been more than two occasions where Scratchy has been found eating Principal Davis’ petunias - that sneaky rabbit!

 

Rio can still very much recall crystal clear Agatha offering to let Scratchy loose in Principal Davis’ flower beds for Rio’s sake. She just needed to say the word.

 

Sneaky rabbit, indeed.

 

They help themselves into the enclosure and sit with the rabbit. Nicky gives her toys to offer him. Within a couple minutes, Scratchy hops into Rio’s lap and contently accepts all of her pets. Agatha calls them to the table not too long after. Nicky instructs the both of them to wash their hands in the laundry room sink before joining Agatha at the table.

 

As soon as they sit, Nicky leads them through a very detailed retelling of dinner prep, about all the ways he helped Agatha in the kitchen this evening - the perfect shoe chef. Nicky has no idea what shoes have to do with any of this.

 

Rio is very impressed by his hard work (and Agatha’s too). She’s never had a better bowl of spaghetti in her life. Nicky tells her there’s better in Italy - that’s where spaghetti is from. He wants to go there, but doesn’t think it would be a good idea. Scratchy is afraid of the car. Can you imagine an airplane? 

 

Nicky can’t. He’s never been on one. Rio explains how it takes off, the weird sensation you get in your tummy when the wheels lift off from the ground, and how it lands - the landing can feel a little scary, but pilots know how to do their job safely. 

 

For a moment, Nicky wonders if he should be a pilot. Agatha reminds him he can be whatever he wants. For now, Nicky settles on pilot. Maybe Scratchy would be less scared if Nicky is the one in the cockpit.

 

At the end of dinner, they all help clear the table, retreating to the kitchen to each help with the dishes (it’s only fair, Nicky stresses). After Agatha closes the dishwasher door and begins the load, she turns her attention back to the other two hovering around the kitchen island.

 

Agatha’s eyes focus on Nicky first. “Movie and dessert?” she asks.

 

Nicky nods and then looks up at Rio, asking the same question, “Movie and dessert?”

 

Perhaps not as enticingly, Rio nods, too. “Movies and dessert,” she states.

 

“ Finding Nemo ?” Nicky suggests. “That’s my favorite!”

 

Rio frowns, “I’ve never seen it.”

 

Nicky gasps and he looks immediately at Agatha. “Mama!”

 

“I know!” Agatha replies, dramatically and matching Nicky’s energy. “How’s that even possible? It’s the greatest piece of Disney cinema. You don’t even need to watch the other 100 or whatever other ones. Who needs them when you can quote Finding Nemo word. For. Word.”

 

Rio bites back a laugh. “Seems like we’ve decided then.”

 

“I’ll go set up the tv!” Nicky exclaims. Before either one of them could protest, he vanishes from the kitchen.

 

With the boy gone, Rio now allows her to laugh lightly. She helps herself to the cake box in the fridge and sets it down on the island. Agatha fetches three dessert plates.

 

“Seems like I have a seal of approval, yeah?” Rio asks, slowly and carefully lifting the lid.

 

“We’re a packaged deal,” Agatha replies, not missing a beat. “You don’t get one without the other and he’s already smitten.”

 

“Seems like a sweet deal,” Rio grins, unfolding the walls of the box so the cardboard lays flat. “Is he the only one that’s smitten?” she asks. Although she means to be gentle - to be teasing, she knows she comes across boldly.

 

She looks up at Agatha for an answer. Rio spots flushed cheeks and avoiding eyes. When Agatha clears her throat and changes the topic, Rio doesn’t push. It’s fine if there’s no answer.

 

“Chocolate cake, huh?” Agatha gestures. “Just like you promised.”

 

“Can’t celebrate your second week of first grade without it,” Rio points out.

 

Agatha calls out for Nicky, but the little boy provides no response. “Probably looking for the remote,” she guesses. “Rio, can you see how big of a slice he wants?”

 

“You’re leaving that question to a six year old?” Rio asks, raising her eyebrows.

 

“It’s the end of the second week of first grade, isn’t it?” Agatha repeats. “Plus, it’s Friday night. Cut loose a little bit, babe.”

 

Babe .

 

“Uhh,” Rio stammers. “Yeah, I’ll, uhh. I’ll go check on him. Be right back.”

 

Rio pushes her way through the kitchen and into the empty, but now clean, dining room. “Nicky?” she calls out. 

 

Movement from the couch in the living room catches her eye. She makes her way towards it and comes around the armrest. The sight steals the air from her lungs. Rio’s eyes widen and she quickly yells for Agatha.

 

Nicky’s laying on his side against the cushions of the living room couch, eyes rolled into the back of his head and convulsing.

The kitchen doors swing open with a loud bang followed by Agatha’s hurried steps and her concerned question, “What’s wrong?”

 

“How long were we in the-” Rio begins, pulling the other throw pillows towards herself. 

 

“Nicky!” Agatha exclaims, rushing to kneel to Rio’s left, hovering over his head which she reaches out to hold.

 

Rio grabs her wrist out of caution. “We don’t want to restrain him,” she warns, releasing her hold on Agatha. “Help me get him on his side.”

 

Both women sit up on their knees. Agatha supports Nicky’s shoulder while Rio his lower back, allowing for them to roll the boy onto his side, facing the two of them. Rio places the throw pillows behind him and then turns to Agatha who is pale as a ghost watching helpless as Nicky continues to jerk. A trail of foamy spit begins to trickle down from the corner of his lips.

 

“About three minutes? Four?” Rio guesses. “That’s how long we were alone in the kitchen?”

 

“I-I don’t know, maybe?” Agatha stammers. “We need to call - my phone, I left it-”

 

“I have mine,” Rio says, pulling away from her hold on Nicky to fetch her phone from her pocket. After unlocking it, she immediately dials 911, puts the call on speaker, and holds the receiver up between her and Agatha. 

 

The dispatcher takes an infuriating amount of time to pick up - at least, that is what it seems to Rio. Any amount of time with Nicky unresponsive feels like too long. When the line is picked up, a sigh of relief escapes Rio.

 

“911, what’s your emergency?”

 

“It’s my son,” Agatha immediately blurts out.

 

“He’s been seizing now for at least five minutes,” Rio adds. “He’s unresponsive.”

 

They hear typing from the other end. “What’s your address?”

 

“2804 Sherwood Drive,” Agatha answers. 

 

“Alright, we’re sending a unit to your location,” the dispatcher informs. “How old is your son? Does he have a history of seizures?” 

 

“No. No, he doesn’t,” Agatha answers, shaking her head. Her voice cracks, she struggles to hold back a whine. “He’s only six.”

 

The scared, painful cry breaks Rio’s heart. This is not the Agatha she has come to know over the last month - a far cry from the strong, stubborn, and confident woman Rio has feelings for. Feelings she doesn’t dare voice. This version of Agatha.. Rio wants to make everything right for her; she wants to take away her fear.

 

Rio sets the phone down on the couch cushion, cautious of Nicky. She pulls herself back onto her feet and places a hand on Agatha’s shoulder. Agatha can barely tear her eyes away from her son to glance up at her.

 

“I’ll go outside - flag them down,” Rio offers.

 

As Rio makes a beeline for the front door, she hears the dispatcher relay, “Ma’am, I’ll stay on the line with you until they arrive. Is your son still seizing? Is he breathing?”

 

Rio unlocks the door, hearing a shaky Agatha confirm both accounts, but he isn’t convulsing as much. He still isn’t responding. 

 

The fresh air hits Rio’s lungs in an oddly refreshing way, providing the smallest amount of calm in the ocean of adrenaline and anxiety. She doesn’t feel like she’s completely drowning in panic - a panic she wouldn’t let Agatha see. It would help either of them any right now.

 

She almost prides herself in the calmness she is keeping - she’s usually pretty good at it. Rio has to be. She grew up alone, with only herself to watch out for, and she couldn’t afford to allow anyone to see her panic.

 

The soccer practice had been a close call.

 

And crumbling in front of Alice? Alice is the closest thing she has to family. She has seen her at her lowest of lows and celebrated her highest of highs. Alice’s seen her through the best of the worst. Rio doesn’t need any walls with Alice. 

 

She wants that with Agatha. 

 

She wants a lot with Agatha.

 

It scares her how much she wants.

 

And Rio usually doesn’t allow herself frivolous things like wants.

 

Now’s not the time to think about it, but it lingers. She wants to do what’s right by her and Rio really hopes that she is.

 

Rio takes the porch steps two at a time. She can hear the wailing of an ambulance’s sirens in the distance, quickly approaching. She jogs down the driveway, reaching the end where it meets the road as the ambulance turns the street corner. She waves her hands over her head, catching the driver’s attention.

 

The ambulance backs up into the driveway, stopping a few feet away from the house. Rio walks up along the edge of the concrete, still watching as one of the paramedics bounce up the porch steps and through the front door Rio left open. The other rounds to the back of the ambulance to help the third bring down the stretcher before following inside.

 

Hovering to the side of the porch steps, Rio waits. She doesn’t want to get in anyone's way and the living room is already awfully crowded. She wouldn’t be of any help.

 

The waiting feels like an eternity. 

 

Is time even moving?

 

She can vaguely hear what’s happening inside, but she can’t really make out the conversation between the paramedics and Agatha. She can only guess. The seizing has stopped, but given the duration and it being his first episode, they’re going to take him to Westview General.

 

Rio’s heart painfully tightens in her chest when they carry the stretcher past her - Nicky somehow looks smaller and more fragile. Agatha follows suit, hesitating in front of Rio when she catches the woman’s eye.

 

”Go - I’ll take care of everything here,” Rio reassures, giving her a confident nod. She reaches out and grabs Agatha’s hands to give it a small squeeze. “And I’ll meet you there at the hospital, okay?”

 

Agatha matches her nod. She pulls on Rio’s hand, making her take a half step closer. “Thank you,” she whispers, placing a small kiss on her cheek before taking the couple of strides that separate her from the ambulance.

 

Rio takes a couple of steps closer. The paramedic offers Agatha a hand and helps her climb into the back of the emergency vehicle. His attention then turns to Rio, then to Agatha, and back to Rio. 

 

“Are you coming too?” he asks. “You’re his other mother, right?” 

 

Rio begins, “I’m not-“

 

”She’s not his-“ Agatha begins at the same time, but stops mid-sentence, struggling. “She’s his.. She’s just a friend.”

 

”Okay then,” the paramedic responds, closing the door and leaving Rio with Agatha’s response hanging heavily in the air.

 

She’s just a friend.

 

The ambulance pulls out of the driveway and turns back, retracing its route here. Even when it is completely out of sight, Rio continues to stare at the empty road. The gravity of the evening starts to settle in her bones. She begins to shake. 

 

She almost doesn’t hear the high pitch voice calling out her name from up the street. It takes her a second longer to realize it’s her boss (well, one of them.)

 

”Ms. Vidal!”

 

Rio turns and instantly clocks Sharon Davis walking towards her. Ah, shit . How much did Principal Davis see? Rio wonders if she could release Scratchy to cause a distraction. 

 

But she probably shouldn’t risk losing the rabbit on top of everything else this evening.

 

“What happened? Are the Harknesses okay?” Sharon asks, her worry genuine. Rio pegs her to be a very dotting, friendly neighbor - one that Agatha probably found overbearing. “What are you doing out?”

 

”I, uh…” Rio’s voice trails off in search for a believable answer. She finds none. “I was in the neighborhood - saw the commotion. Ms. Harkness was distraught, so I wanted to help-“

 

”Oh, it’s not sweet, little Nicholas, is it?” Sharon interrupts.

 

”Nicky,” Rio corrects. 

 

“And look at that!” Sharon huffs. “The house is still open and everything!” 

 

“I’ll take care of it,” Rio says. “I told Ms. Harkness I would.” 

 

“Well, isn’t that very good of you, Rio!” Sharon beams. “Such a good samaritan - Westview needs more people and educators like you. I hope you can get back to your walk soon, dear.”

 

”Yeah, thank you,” Rio murmurs.

 

”No, no, no - thank you . Have a good evening, dear,” Sharon replies. “I’ll make sure to call Agatha and ask about the rabbit - Itchy, isn’t it?” 

 

Scratchy , Rio mentally corrects. She keeps it to herself. It’s not a far-fetched idea that Nicky’s first grade teacher would know the name of his pet rabbit, but there’s enough on Rio’s plate at the moment. “Good evening to you too, Principal Davis,” she says before walking up the porch steps.

 

She steps into the foyer and looks out into the living room: the armchair and ottoman are pushed aside. The coffee table is further back - closer to the television stand. Her phone is also there. The paramedics needed more space - needed to get the stretcher in. 

 

Rio takes it upon herself to move each piece of furniture (and phone) back into its original place, using the indents on the rug as her guide. She also puts the throw pillows back in their intended spots. 

 

It almost seems like nothing happened. 

 

Almost. There’s a lingering charge in the air that does nothing to calm Rio’s nerves.

 

She turns off the television and fiddles with light switches until they do turn off. Rio inspects the dining room they previously cleaned. With nothing else to do in this room, she continues into the kitchen where the forgotten chocolate cake resides on the kitchen island, knife pierced in a mid-slice.

 

A reminder of what this evening could’ve been.

 

Should’ve been.

 

Grasping the handle of the knife, Rio pulls out the sharp blade from the moist cake and rinses it off in the sink before returning it to the others in the knife block. She does her best to reassemble the box the cake came in from the bakery. It’s not perfectly straight, but it will get the job done. 

 

Cake safely back in the container, Rio slides it onto a fridge shelf and then makes her way into the laundry room to check on one last thing - arguably the most important thing.

 

Scratchy is hiding in the open pen, draped with a blanket in the left corner of his enclosure - nose twitching and eyes darting. Rio doesn’t know what stress looks like in rabbits, but his actions mirror those from the soccer field, when Agatha told her that he’s not a fan of people. 

 

“Did you hear all that ruckus, Scratch?” Rio asks gently. “Sounded pretty scary, huh?”

 

She knows the rabbit can’t answer.

 

She doesn’t care.

 

Rio reaches into the cabinet Nicky pointed out earlier. Scratchy immediately perks up. She grabs a bag of treats - dried banana slices. She takes them with her as she climbs over the gate. It doesn’t take Scratchy long to climb into her lap once she sits down and opens the bag. She places a couple of sliced bananas on her lap to Scratchy’s delight.

 

”Nicky’s gonna be okay, Scratchy,” Rio reassures, giving the small animal a reassuring nod as she pets along his back. 

 

She also needs to hear it for herself.

 

With a heavy sigh, Rio takes her phone out of her pocket. No notifications. She isn’t expecting any. She assumes the ambulance must be at the hospital by now. Hell, she hopes Nicky is already being seen.

 

She texts Alice first, if only to give herself a few more minutes of peace with Scratchy, to explain the turn of events. That she is going to meet up with Agatha and Nicky at the hospital.

 

This is not the night over I wanted you to have , Alice immediately texts back from her one person evening at their apartment.

 

On a serious note: I hope the kid is okay.

 

”Me too,” Rio finds herself murmuring at her screen. Alice is still typing.

 

Her message comes through a moment later: I’ll keep my sound on, in case you need anything for any reason.

 

Rio sends off a quick thanks and then finds her text conversation with Agatha. Hey, not expecting an answer, but I’m leaving the house in a few minutes. I’m bringing your car to the hospital.

 

She let’s Scratchy enjoy a couple more pieces of the dried sliced bananas as she watches the screen of her phone. There is no typing, no texts. Rio’s not surprised, but a small grim smile plays on her lips when she needs that Agatha liked the message - confirmation of life.

 

”Okay, bud,” Rio says, putting her phone back in her pocket. She scoops Scratchy into her arms and moves him onto the floor before she stands up herself. “Let’s go make sure your little human is okay.” 

 

She double checks the gate is locked and leaves the laundry room door open just a crack, just as Nicky did when they were called to the table for dinner. She turns off lights along the way on her walk back to the foyer.

 

Rio finds a key hook next to the door to the left of the entrance to the house. She grabs Agatha’s keys, steps into the garage, pushes the button to open the garage door, and slips into the driver’s side of the black Mercedes. 

 

The drive to Westview General is a short one. Any drive to anywhere in Westview in a short one in the small town. Rio follows the signs to the emergency bay and parks in the designated parking lot. She steps out of the car and locks it. She sends Agatha a quick message to let her know that Rio is here.

 

Looking up at the building - at the entrance to the emergency room specifically, Rio sighs heavily. Her heartbeat begins to race. She hates hospitals. She hates this hospital.

 

Not that she was ever mistreated, no. She received the best care possible. Every single nurse dotted over her - of course they did. How could you not be anything but sickeningly sweet to the child who lost her entire family tragically? A child that they knew they’d have to turn over to the state, to be put in the foster system.

 

Rio hates this hospital because this is where she woke up, confused and scared - and god, the pain . She remembers waking up sobbing from the pain and calling out for her mother.

 

Subconsciously, she ranks a hand through her hair, over the scars - permanent reminders that it wasn’t her mother sitting at her side to offer comfort.

 

It was Ms. Calderu.

 

Her first grade teacher.

 

Lilia.

 

Lilia, who came to her ICU room every single day when six year old Rio was in a coma, medical professionals unsure when she would wake up - if she would wake up.

 

Lilia, who made sure that the pain was under control and she was no longer crying before anyone could tell her anything.

 

Lilia, who chased the social worker out of the room because she refused to let such heavy news come from a complete stranger.

 

Lilia, who told her about the accident and about her family.

 

Lilia, who held Rio as she wailed for so long and so hard, she made herself sick.

 

Rio hates this hospital because this is where her life changed forever. The sentiment weighs heavily on her - it always does - and settles in the pit of her stomach, forming a tight ball of anxiety. Swallowing hard, Rio takes a deep breath and walks.

 

The doors slide open and she steps inside of the waiting room. Rio makes a beeline for the reception desk until she catches a wave from the corner of her eye. She spots Agatha to her left, peeking behind heavy doors leading into the emergency room. 

 

“How is he?” Rio asks, breathlessly, approaching her.

 

Agatha grabs her hand and leads her. “He’s stopped seizing and he’s responsive,” she answers. “He’s really tired.”

 

”And how are you?”

 

The only response Rio gets is a sigh; it’s a loaded question after all.

 

They pass a couple of curtained off rooms and turn right into the third they come across. Rio follows Agatha through the curtain into the small room where sleepy Nicky is propped up on a hospital bed, connected to an IV. 

 

“Hey, baby, look how I found,” Agatha says gently, coming up to his left side. She runs her hand through his hair. “Try to stay awake, okay?”

 

”Ms. Rio,” Nicky murmurs softly.

 

”What’s up, buddy?” Rio, standing next to Agatha, greets. “You gave us a scare there.”

 

”Are we still going to have chocolate cake?” he asks. 

 

Priorities.

 

Rio catches Agatha’s eye. When she nods in approval, Rio does the same. “Yeah! Of course! It’s how you celebrate the second week of first grade. Of course we’re still going to have chocolate cake.” 

 

“When we get home,” Agatha adds. 

 

Nicky nods slowly and narrows his eyes, as if pondering a very important question that he then asks, “Ms. Rio, what do we do to celebrate the third week?” 

 

Agatha turns to her, waiting for an answer. Rio notes how tired, drained Agatha suddenly looks, but when she also catches the older woman’s bemused smile (one that Rio wants to keep there during such stress), she plays right into the utmost serious conversation.

 

”Oh! The third week, hmmm,” Rio takes a second to think. “You know, what? I have to go back and refer to the handbook. I don’t have it all memorized yet.”

 

”You don’t ?” Nicky gasps.

 

”Kid, it’s my first year of first grade, too - cut me some slack.”

 

”It’s technically your third,” Agatha mumbles under her breath.

 

” You cut me some slack, too!” 

 

“What’s slack?” Nicky asks, wrinkling his nose in confusion. “And why are we cutting it for Ms. Rio?”

 

Before either woman could explain, there’s a loud knock against the propped open door and the curtain is then drawn open, revealing a middle aged man in dark blue scrubs. He pauses when he sees a new addition to the room. “Oh, you must be the-“

 

“Rio. I’m Rio,” she interrupts, not allowing the sentence to be finished.

 

“Alright then, well,” the nurse begins again, walking to the head of the bed on the right side. “The room upstairs is ready for transfer, so if you’ll follow us right this way, please.”

 

A younger gentleman steps into the room to help disconnect the various wires connected to different machines. Both Rio and Agatha take a step back, as close as they can to the wall to allow them to work.

 

When they start to roll the bed out of the room, Rio lingers to allow Agatha to follow first. Rio brings up the rear, following them down the hallway and into an elevator that takes them to the third floor and into another nearly identical hallway like the one downstairs.

 

Rio hates it.

 

Hates that everything looks the same.

 

Hates the beeping coming from the occupied rooms they pass.

 

Hates the silence of the ones that are empty.

 

Hates the squeaking of rubber soles on tiles, mixed with medical chatter.

 

Hates the smell of-

 

Suddenly there’s another pair of footsteps in line with her. Rio looks up and turns her head. How long has Agatha been watching her? How long did she frown at the ground as if it had committed a grave crime against her? She didn’t think she was being watched.

 

Agatha, now in step, brushes her fingers against Rio’s - an invitation that she instantly accepts. She takes a hold of Agatha’s hand. Agatha gives it a quick squeeze.

 

The hallway, it feels, goes on forever, but Nicky is eventually turned into a room and reconnected back to the different machines. The IV bag is placed back on its hook. Agatha lets go of Rio’s hand to stand by his side. Rio hovers at the foot of the bed.

 

Before taking leave, one of the nurses fills out the whiteboard with Nicky’s information: name, birthday, symptoms, and labels him a neuro patient. The other informs that depending on the results of the blood test, they may have to do more - whether it will be before or after the MRI is anyone’s guess at this point.

 

Rio catches Nicky flaring his nostrils at the board when the nurses leave. She catches the culprit immediately. Picking up the magnetic dry erase marker from the board, she uncaps it and writes “Nicky” under Nicholas Harkness.

 

Agatha sighs dramatically, sitting on the edge of the bed. “Whoever named you ‘Nicholas,’ what were they even thinking?”

 

“You're silly, Mama. It was you!” he points out with a giggle that makes Agatha smile wide.

 

“You’re so right!” Agatha confirms, giving him a wink. “‘Nicky’ suits you much better, sweetheart.”

 

Nicky nods firmly in agreement before he’s consumed by a worrying thought, “Mama, what if Scratchy is scared? He doesn’t know what’s going on.”

 

“Oh, I let him know,” Rio confirms, capping the pen and sticking it back onto the whiteboard. She takes a seat at the end of the hospital bed, opposite of Agatha’s side. “He was a little scared, so I gave him some of the treats you showed me earlier.”

 

“The sliced bananas?”

 

“The very ones,” Rio answers. “I told him that everything was going to be okay. Now, he’s just waiting for you to get home.”

 

Again, Nicky nods, slowly this time as he absorbs Rio’s response. To both Rio’s and Agatha’s surprise, the little boy frowns - pouts. Tears well up on the brim of his eyes. He lowers his head to try to prevent either of them from seeing.

 

“Hey,” Agatha says softly. Rio hears her voice crack. “Hey, sweetheart, what’s wrong?”

 

His voice is so small. He doesn’t look up. “ I’m scared,” he admits, quietly. “They tried to make that machine sound cool, but it sounds scary and I don’t want to do it, Mama.”

 

It is not about Scratchy being alone and scared - never was.

 

It’s all Nicky.

 

He’s scared.

 

He doesn’t understand what’s going on.

 

Agatha cups her hands around his cheeks, using the pads of thumbs to wipe away at the tears that now trail from his eyes. “You are being such a brave boy, Nicky,” she tries to soothe. Leaning forward, she very carefully wraps him in a hug. “I need you to be brave for a little bit longer.”

 

“I’m not brave,” Nicky whimpers against his mother’s chest. “I’m a scaredy cat. We didn’t have to do the big scary machine last time..”

 

Last time - he split his chin open playing soccer, Rio recalls Agatha telling her. Over the summer, before the start of the school year.

 

Rio reassures, “It’s okay to feel scared when you have to be brave. It’s not easy - or fun - sometimes when we have to be brave.” 

 

“Rio’s very right,” Agatha agrees, placing a kiss on the top of his head. “It’s okay to be scared,” she echos.

 

“You know,” Rio begins slowly. “When I was your age, I had to get an MRI scan, too. A ton of them, actually.”

 

Nicky peeks around Agatha. “Really?”

 

Agatha turns slightly to look at Rio, too.

 

Rio nods.

 

“Did your mom tell you to be brave, Ms. Rio?” Nicky asks.

 

The innocent question sucker punches her straight in the gut and steals the air from her lungs. Rio forces a smile - she knows both Nicky and Agatha are watching her - and blinks perhaps a bit too much, but her eyes are stinging and she refuses to allow them to betray her. 

 

“She always knew I was brave,” Rio answers, side stepping his question, but still doesn’t lie. She knows her mother would have, if she could. Her mother would tell her she’s been the bravest in the face of everything.

 

”Were you scared?” Nicky follows up.

 

”Mmhmm, but you know what helped?” Rio asks in return.

 

Nicky shakes his head.

 

Rio scoots herself closer to him (and Agatha). She leans towards him. “When it starts to feel a little too loud or a little too claustrophobic-“

 

”What does claustrophobic mean?” Nicky interrupts.

 

”That there’s not enough space, sweetheart,” Agatha explains.

 

”Exactly what your mom said,” Rio agrees. “If it feels like there’s not enough space, close your eyes and think about being in your happy place.”

 

”My happy place?”

 

Rio nods, “Think of a place where when you know you’re there, you’ll be happy. Do you have a place like that, waffle fry?”

 

”Curly fries are better,” Nicky argues.

 

”You’ll understand when you’re older that you are so wrong, hash brown,” Rio huffs. 

 

“Is your happy place a potato field?” Agatha cuts in.

 

Rio wrinkles her nose, “Idaho doesn’t really call to me.”

 

”Can our backyard be my happy place?” Nicky asks, looking up towards Agatha who nods at him.

 

”It can be wherever you want it to be,” Agatha reassures. 

 

“And with whoever you want,” Rio adds. 

 

Nicky nods slowly, thinking over this new bit of information before proclaiming, “Then Mama will be there. And Señor Scratchy!”

 

”A given,” Agatha grins.

 

”Billy and Tommy, too,” Nicky continues. “And Aunt Wanda and Uncle Vis. Oh, Ms. Rio, do you want to be in my happy place, too?” 

 

“You’d want me there..?” Rio asks, genuinely touched. 

 

Nicky nods eagerly.

 

”Then I’ll definitely be there, tater tot.”

 

“Where’s your happy place, Ms. Rio?” 

 

Rio doesn’t have to think about it for it is a place that she visits more often than not, but she still hesitates for a second before telling him, “There’s this beach in Puerto Rico - it’s an island in the Caribbean Sea. I’m there, in the bluest water you’re ever seen with my two older sisters. My mom and dad are watching us from their spots in the sand.” 

 

Nicky listens intently, but he doesn’t push with any follow up questions. Instead, he turns his attention to Agatha. “And you, Mama?” 

 

“Anywhere I’m with you,” Agatha answers, not missing a beat.

 

”I’m always gonna be with you, Mama.”

 

”So, it seems I’ll always be happy then.” 

 

With a longing ache in her chest, Rio scoots herself over to the edge of the bed and stands herself up. Before dismissing herself, she points towards the door and explains, “My legs fell asleep. I need to stretch them.”

 

A heavy sigh escapes her as she steps into the hallway, she takes a few long strides and comes to a stop when she is certain she’s no longer within hearing distance of the room. Hands on her hips, Rio tilts her head back, allowing herself to look up towards the ceiling. Her dark eyes are stinging again, dammit. She blinks furiously, but instead of keeping the tears at bay, they’re unleashed.

 

A silent sob parts from her lips, Rio immediately clasps her hand over her mouth, desperate to not be overheard. She needs to pull herself together; she needs to find a bathroom to hide—

 

A gentle hand rests against the small of her back. Rio doesn’t need to turn around to know who it is.

 

”I don’t want you to be selfless, Rio,” Agatha says softly. “And sometimes, being brave means admitting when something is too much.” 

 

Rio slowly turns around to face her, dark eyes meeting blue.

 

“You can tell me the truth,” Agatha continues, tone still just as gentle. “If being here is hard for you, then it’s okay-“

 

“Agatha-“

 

“I signed up for this, willingly. All of it,” Agatha points out. “From that first positive pregnancy test, I knew I was accepting everything that came with it. The late nights, the bumps, the bruises, the heartbreak, and hospital trips..”

 

The words spill forth from her lips before Rio can process what she’s saying - before she can stop herself. “And what if I want to sign up for you?” she asks. “For the package deal?”

 

Rio never reveals her wants and she never takes; she keeps things to herself - never one to ask, never one to tell.

 

The little space between them disappears when Agatha takes a step forward. She cups Rio’s face; she holds her tenderly, as if she were fragile.

 

She feels fragile. She’s trembling and she knows Agatha can feel it, too. Being here, surrounded by these walls, sounds, and smells - it’s putting cracks in her delicate facade. Rio can’t hide it and with Agatha’s sharp gaze searching her features so carefully, she’s not sure that she can even if she tries.

 

The kiss is slow. It’s soft. It’s a reassurance Rio doesn’t realize she’s seeking. Taking a hold of Agatha’s wrists, Rio leans into her - into the kiss until Agatha pulls away, leaving Rio breathless.

 

“I can’t thank you enough for your help,” Agatha whispers, resting her forehead against Rio’s. “But, I know you’ve been on edge since you got here and I can’t blame you.”

 

“I’m fine-”

 

“You come in here and give that sweet little boy of mine such good advice to take care of himself - I wish you’d take some of it and take care of yourself, too.”

 

With a heavy sigh, Rio lowers her head onto Agatha’s shoulder, forcing Agatha to remove her hands from cradling her cheeks. Agatha snakes them around Rio’s waist and holds her close. Rio allows for herself to be held. She likes being held by Agatha - likes the steady comfort it brings.

 

“Sounds like he has a brilliant first grade teacher,” Rio murmurs.

 

“I don’t know, actually - verdict’s still out,” Agatha says with a shrug. “I’ve heard she had to repeat the first grade. His Ms. Rio, though, she’s the definition of brilliance.”

 

Rio snorts, “Wait till you find out about her academic record.”

 

“She is a bit of a nerd,” Agatha teases, pulling away from the embrace. “Go home and get some rest.”

 

As much as Rio wants to fight back, there’s a finality to Agatha’s voice deterring such an effort. “Will you be okay..?” she asks.

 

Agatha takes a moment before answering, “I’ll have to be.”

Rio doesn’t leave the hospital without saying goodbye to Nicky, without giving him one more round of extra assurance and a hug for give measure, too. Maneuvering around the wires he’s connected to causes an ache in her chest that remains for the rest of the night.

 

He asks, in such a small voice, one trying to mask the fears that quivers ever so slightly in it, if that maybe he can call her if he’s feeling extra scared?

 

Agatha tries, in a tone so soft, to deny such a request - Rio hears it before it can fully leave her lips, but she interrupts. She ensures him - both of them, actually -  that he can call whenever. They can call.

 

She’ll answer. 

 

She promises.

 

The path out of the hospital is old and familiar. When she steps outside into the evening air, she begins to type out a text to Alice - one that is never sent. Rio decides to walk. She needs to. Agatha would protest, if she knew, and Alice as well, but the pent up anxious energy begs - claws - to be released.

 

So, instead, she walks, knowing it will take her longer to get back to the apartment, it also knowing it gives her the opportunity to process through old, enormous tidal waves of emotions to work through. 

 

She hasn’t been on this side of town in ages - and for good reason, too. The hospital is in the same area as her childhood neighborhood. Even in the quiet of the night, Rio hears the echoes of the ghosts of the past and the shadows of a robbed life and future.

 

The cracks of the sidewalk begin to look eerily familiar. Rio doesn’t bother to look up when passing the house her parents used to rent - the place she called home for the first six years of her life. 

 

The guilt sits in the pit of her stomach, a reminder that she also hasn’t been to visit her family since moving back to Westview. She wonders if her mother would be disappointed in her - if her father would come to her defense, given that their little girl was starting a career and is super busy with all that entails.

 

Rio pauses when she reaches Main Street, in front of the gazebo. The Vidal sisters really should have won that talent show that year. They really work hard during those two weeks of practice.

 

Then again, Rio knows that she is biased. 

 

She catches herself wondering about the type of women her sisters would’ve become. It’s an odd feeling: being the baby out of the three of them, but the oldest any of them would ever be.

 

This is the most she’s thought about them in a while; Rio feels a bit ashamed of herself.

 

Survivor’s guilt.

“Bye, Ms. Rio!” Nicky waves and shouts from the backseat, as if she would have any problem hearing. “See you tomorrow!”

 

”See you tomorrow,” Agatha echoes. 

 

“Bye, Nicky,” Rio replies, matching his wave and his energy - it’s contagious. He’s done a complete 180 from their late morning lunch picnic in their classroom. He’s almost - almost - the energetic little boy she’s come to know. It’s faint, but it’s there: the tiredness and worry from a weight that shouldn’t be placed on his small shoulders - a weight she knows his mother is bearing the most of.

 

Rio silently promises to help in any way that she can, on any front that she can.

 

She turns to the older woman with a gentle smile, tone growing softer. “Bye, Agatha.”

 

”Bye, Rio .” 

 

She says her name with such warmth and clarity - a hidden apology for Monday’s antics and behavior, not that further apologies were needed or necessary. Regardless, the warmth sits comfortably in Rio’s chest. With one final nod, Rio turns and walks back towards the school. She hears the car shift into reverse and pull away from the spot.

 

Returning to her now empty classroom, Rio busies herself with the intentional mess she made not even an hour ago, finding slices of bread perhaps a tad too far from the front of the room.

 

There are a few clear across on the other side of the classroom - just how much force did she rip that plastic bag? 

 

“I did want to give them a show,” she grumbles, kneeling down with a grunt to fish a couple of slices that landed a bin of books. 

 

“At what cost, Vidal?” Rio murmurs, walking over to the half shelving units. She leans over them, taking a peek and counting her blessings; she spots only dust bunnies.

 

From her desk, her phone vibrates. She crosses back to the other side of the room to fetch it and check.

 

Señor Scratchy.

 

She still refuses to change the name.

 

This is the first time since last Friday that Nicky’s been himself. Thank you for helping him forget, even if for a little while. He’s excited for Sunday.

 

Rio smiles down at the message and quickly types out, No problem. I’d do anything to help him. To help you.   But Rio immediately hesitates and deletes the last couple of sentences. She nearly starts over and then sends: No problem - I’d do anything to help.

 

After a second, she adds, He was upset up until then. I’m guessing the news from the appointment is hitting him hard, too?

 

It is. It’s been hard to cheer him up. So, again, thank you, Rio.

 

With a smile, she tucks the phone into her pants pocket and then returns to her task at hand, tackling a different corner of the room to glance over. She considers writing the custodial staff a card, already apologizing for the bugs she’s going to potentially attract.

 

It doesn’t take long for her phone to vibrate again: still Agatha.

 

He is politely asking/requesting if Señor Scratchy can join us (but please don’t let us impose).

 

Rio can clearly envision Nicky, holding his dear rabbit close to his chest, pleading with Agatha to include their beloved pet. Rio would’ve joined him in a heartbeat because how could they just leave Scratchy behind during their lunch date?

 

I’ll ask Lilia and I’ll let you know. Do you think the picture I have of him would be convincing evidence of good behavior?

 

No . Agatha responds immediately, quickly followed by: Please don’t show my former first grade teacher a picture of our family rabbit looking like he just committed a heinous murder, Rio. I will not stand for that kind of Scratchy slander.

 

Rio snorts. It’s now your contact image when you call.

 

Honestly, that very photo almost became Rio’s home screen wallpaper altogether. It must’ve been the best day of that rabbit’s little life: the sheer amount of raspberries he ate, given to him by all three of them to encourage the growing mushy mess so Rio could get the best Monty Python-sequel photo.

 

Agatha’s final text that afternoon is incredibly short - a singular word: Menace.
