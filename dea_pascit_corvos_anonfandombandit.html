Summary

All witches possess wings, which are a reflection of their soul and magic.

On the night of Samhain, 1675, Agatha Harkness is born with black wings; the wings of a crow, but mistaken to be the wings of a raven. Wings of Death. She is ostracized by the coven flock and raised to believe Death has marked her evil, sinful soul, and will one day come to claim her.

What she does not know is that Evanora is partially correct. Death will, one day, claim her - because after millennia of waiting, Death's Soulmate has finally been born.

Chapter 1: incipit

Since the very beginnings of humanity, when the threads of magic throughout the universe began to weave themselves into souls, creating the first witches, they have possessed wings. Or at least, that is the lore of the witches. The story behind the wings is that those first witches were of Spirit and Nature, and could travel between worlds, flying between Earth, Heaven, Purgatory, and Hell by using the wings bestowed upon them by the Divine Goddess.

Witches were, in fact, the true angels that humans came to believe in. But humans were prone to hunt creatures that intrigued them, and so witches began to glamour their wings using spells passed down from generation to generation, so that only other witchkin could see them, to avoid detection by humans.

Their wings were a reflection of their soul and magic. Often, a witch’s wings may resemble those of a bird that may possess a link to their personal and moral values, or their specific type of magic. A wise Divination witch may possess the wings of an owl; a witch connected to the element of fire may be blessed with the plumage of a firehawk.

A witch’s wings were also meant to represent a connection to family members and potential Soulmates. Blood witches commonly shared wings of the same bird family, such as the Bishop family all possessing falcon wings, and Soulmates could end up with very similar coloured plumage, or the wings of birds that could pair.

Covens rever their sisters’ wings, anointing their fluffy down feathers with oil at birth to welcome them to the flock; they preen their wings in elaborate coven fledging ceremonies, when they develop flight feathers and manifest their first sparks of magic. Their feathers, imbued with power, make for incredible talismans, potion ingredients, and ritual components, which they shed and share amongst their coven members, and give as gifts to other covens as well.

A witchling born with fledged wings, already possessing some flight feathers, is a rare and immensely special individual; it is believed to be a sign from the Goddess that they possess great power and will one day ascend to become a witch queen, with their own crown and coven. Covens love, cherish, and nurture every winged witchling born into their flock, but fledged witchlings are considered truly miraculous.

The infant is no longer regarded as a miracle - she is an omen of strife and evil.

Tell the rest of the coven she was stillborn. She will only bring misfortune and ruin to our coven.”

“Then give her a strong, virtuous name, in hope that the Goddess may take pity on the babe and allow only light magic into her heart,” the midwife suggests.

Dawn casts deep shadows that stretch along the ground, like long, gnarled fingers attempting to intrude and steal the infant in her slumber to claim her for the Green Witch.

She knows who the woman is.

The Green witch nods, sheathing her knife and then entwining her fingers together. “I do, yes.”

“Yes.”

“Wings of Death,” Agatha finishes. And the Green witch inclines her head in acceptance. Agatha is certain now. She knows her identity. “You are She. Lady Death. Queen of Darkness,” she whispers.

Death, in physical form, hums. “You are perceptive.”

Evanora in particular, made sure to fill her head with tales of how Death would eventually come to claim Agatha’s wicked, evil soul, because Agatha was born with black corvid wings, indicating that her soul belongs to the cosmic entity.

“My mother said you claimed me from birth. That I am destined to be your slave, your future concubine. Is that true?” she queries harshly.

She wants to hear a yes or no answer directly from the entity’s mouth. She wants to know if her mother and the coven’s abysmal treatment of her was at all justified. Well, that’s not quite true. She knows that none of the neglect and abuse she suffered through was justified, but she wants to know if anything her mother said was correct.

Death looks baffled for a second, and then entertained, snorting out the most adorable laugh. “No more true than any other young witch becoming their Soulmate’s servant whore,” she replies in a chuckle.

Her other cheek also feels hot, but she chooses to believe that’s just a phantom sensation, rather than her blushing. Her eyes flutter shut, her breathing shallowing out as her whole body begins to tingle, and she basks in the first non-violent physical contact she has experienced since… well… ever.

She likes it. She didn’t quite realise how touch-starved she’s been until this moment.

Death smiles at her with those bright, soulful doe eyes, and Agatha has to wonder how anybody could ever call this cosmic entity a fearsome, evil monster. “If you are destined to be mine, then I am equally destined to belong to you, little crow,” she murmurs.

Destined. “We are Soulmates?” Agatha breathes. How is that even possible? How could she, a mere mortal Spirit witch, be the Soulmate of the great and all-powerful Lady Death? It sounds fantastical. Her wings rise high, flaring; the hands disappear from her feathers, and Death ducks beneath her wing to stand before her once again, her own wings spreading and matching Agatha’s in their poised stance.

Crow and raven. The undersides of their faces facing one another, exposed, in the greatest display of vulnerability and trust two witches can engage in. Death stares at Agatha so intensely that she wonders if her gaze is capable of piercing down to her very soul. And then - without warning, a measured step. Death slinks to the left, one foot placed behind the other, never breaking eye contact.

They couldn’t care less about the bodies littering the ground below them; barely cold, and they have already been forgotten. And Agatha feels fuzzy, like she has slipped into a trance.

Some part of her mind recognises precisely what this is: a courtship dance.

The Spirit witch, having already anticipated this manoeuvre, also changes course at the same time. “How do you know about that?” she questions, dazed.

“I have dreamt of you, in the way only a Being as such I can dream,” Death replies. “There is a River, one that allows the passage between this world and the next, and one I help wayward souls cross upon their deaths. To Agatha, that sounds abominable. Not the part where people get to relive the memories of their existence, but that Death sees absolute zilch when she stares down into a River that is meant to show a person their greatest, most significant life events. Perhaps because she is not exactly a person.

And Agatha also halts, jolting when Death cups her cheek devotedly. “I see you. A baby, a child, a girl, a woman. Curious, defiant, courageous, disobedient, beautiful. An unruly little creature. I look down into those dark waters and see blue eyes and black feathers. Black feathers that perfectly match my own.” Her fingers extend, grazing over Agatha’s plumage.

“So you have been watching me? All my life?” Agatha asks, bemused.

Death responds with a tiny shrug, that causes her wings to shudder. “Not always. I often don’t have the time. Approximately one hundred deaths per second, on this planet alone.” She sighs, and it’s a tired sound. Even if she cannot feel fatigue as an entity, Agatha imagines that it must become exhausting, and even boring, after a while, to need to collect souls every second of every day, for eternity. “I have visited you, occasionally. Waiting for this precise moment. Fate has finally drawn us together.”

Agatha raises an eyebrow, no longer feeling as if she is stuck in a haze, as she tucks her wings into her back comfortably. She is beginning to feel at ease around the cosmic entity, now she knows that she is technically safe because Death is her Soulmate, and not here to collect her. Maybe cannot even collect her - if she is the Soulmate of Death, does that mean she can’t die? Does that mean she is immortal? Of nights locked in a cold, damp cellar, her back bleeding, covered in marks caused by a whip. Of days out by herself in the forest, crying as she tends to her scruffy, dishevelled plumage, because nobody else in her life dares to touch them.

“It sounds to me as if Lady Death is a voyeur,” Agatha jokes, leaning on humor as her heart thrums in her chest like a hummingbird’s wings.

Death laughs. “I suppose that wouldn’t be inaccurate.”

Sidling closer, Agatha asks, “Were you watching today to see if they would kill me? Is that the ‘moment’ you are anticipating?” She knows that she endured many near-death experiences throughout her childhood. Are those the instances in which Death visited her?

“I was watching to see when you would fight back,” the Green witch corrects. “You were magnificent. To see you stretch your wings and claim your power, breaking free of the cage these wretched women locked you into so they could beat you and crush your spirit - it was glorious.” The praise triggers Agatha to smirk and raise her chin proudly. Her wings start to fluff up again, but this time due to gratification rather than out of the desire to intimidate. “Your soul, it shines brighter than the most dazzling star my sister Infinity has ever created. Your power is remarkable. You are so beautiful. And now you are free, you can soar!” Death twirls around, offering Agatha her hand with a bright grin. “Fly with me?”

Agatha startles. Fly with her? As in - a courtship flight? A nuptial flight? She bursts out laughing just out of sheer astonishment. Death is essentially getting on one knee for her. “We have just met!” she protests.

“And yet you have known me your entire life, as our souls are two halves of a whole,” Death declares dramatically. Agatha just continues to laugh harder. She drops her arm, her brow furrowing. “You are laughing.”

“Yes, I am.”

“Why?” Death sounds genuinely puzzled.

Agatha wipes away her tears of amusement. “I hope I do not offend you when I say this, my Lady, but you are a fool if you think I would partake in a nuptial flight with you without at least prior courtship,” she states. “I am not a girl of easy virtue, and if you so wish as to take possession of mine, you must earn my hand first.”

The truth of it is that Agatha has spent so long thinking that Death would eventually whisk her away and treat her like a slave bride and force her into worshipping her, that she revels in the idea of the almighty Lady Death engaging in courtship with her. Doting on her, bringing her flowers and gifts.

Agatha has never known love, or been cared for, by anybody in her life. She doesn’t even think she knows what love is. She would very happily welcome her into her bed, yes, but that would be in lust. If Death truly is her Soulmate, then they are destined to love each other. And she wants to learn what that means.

“You wish… for me to court you?” Death questions. But then, without waiting for Agatha to respond, she stands up straighter, raven wings fluttering as she asserts, “Very well.”

Part of her feared that Death might scorn her for requesting it. She is, after all, an immortal cosmic entity, while Agatha is just a human witch. By all accounts, Agatha should be the one earning her hand, and desperately trying to court her. Instead, Death is committing to be the one to pursue her. She will be the suitor, and Agatha the lady she is aiming to attain the hand of. And the idea of that thrills her.

“May I request to know the name of my Soulmate, if you are truly serious about pursuing a relationship?” Agatha asks curiously. She is starting to tire of just calling her Death in her head.

The witch cocks her head. “My name? I am known simply as Death. I am an abstract metaphysical entity, the personification of death itself. I am not a person. I have no other name.”

That won’t do. “Well, then, you need one. May I have the honour of bestowing one upon you, my Lady?”

“As my Soulmate, I think that is only fair.”

“Hmm. Let me think.” Agatha glances around, searching for inspiration. Lady Death needs a good, honest, bold name, one that reflects her power and magic. “You are a Green witch, the original Green witch. You represent all death, yes, but also life. Growth and decay,” she mutters aloud, contemplating. And then she snaps her fingers, an idea popping into her head. “You said that your job is to row souls across a River, that shows them all of their memories so they can relive them before entering the afterlife?” Death nods. “Then that is what we shall name you after. The River of Life.”

“Rio Vidal,” Death announces, her wings twitching. “This body, I modelled it after a woman from Puerto Rico, an island to the South of this land. They speak Spanish there. Rio Vidal is Spanish for the River of Life.”

Agatha beams at her. She performs a little curtsey, much to the entity’s amusement. “Then I look forward to seeing you once again, Rio Vidal. Lady Death. My Soulmate.”

Death - Rio - bows deeply, and lowly, her wings spreading and folding over in their own bow. “And I, you, Agatha Harkness.”

When Death departs that night, leaving the Spirit witch standing alone, surrounded by the dead bodies of her mother and coven, she does not take flight. She dissolves into the shadows, vanishing. And yet for the first time, Agatha finds that she is no longer afraid of the darkness. Not when her Soulmate is the Queen of it.

The wind catches her wings and unfurls them to their full wingspan, and with a couple of steps, she is in the air, soaring high above the treetops. Free, for the very first time in her life.

And Agatha knows that the next time she lands, her Soulmate will be there waiting for her.

Agatha stares at it for a moment, swallowing thickly.

The fire crackles between them, spitting out ash at their feet, and the cold breeze presses at the oversides of her wings. The rabbit is going to burn on its spit if she doesn’t tend to it, but she’s distracted by the deeply inconvenient fact that she is having an emotional response to the flowers, one that she has no clue how to deal with.

“You brought me flowers,” she deadpans.

“Yes,” Rio agrees. “It is a courting gift.”

“You are the personification of Death.”

Rio looks down at herself, as if she needs to check. “I am, yes.”

“You’re an immortal, all-powerful deity, and you picked… asters.”

“And witch hazel,” Rio grins. “Witch hazel represents resilience and protection. The asters are for patience. The honeysuckle means devoted affection.” A small pause, unhurried and unashamed. “I thought the symbolism was appropriate.”

Agatha carefully picks up the flowers and studies them, because it’s easier to look at them than at Rio's face. Rio's face is doing things she is not equipped to process, smiling at her with fondness and hope, waiting for Agatha's reaction to the bunch of flowers. As if she wants Agatha to be pleased with them and cares about what she thinks.

Nobody has ever watched Agatha with care. Her mother watched her with suspicion, the rest of the coven with revulsion and fear. Nobody has ever simply looked at her the way Rio looks at her, with… kindness.

It is, frankly, overwhelming.

The scent of burning meat helps ground her.

Tossing the bundle of flowers aside, she snatches her dinner off the fire with a hiss, checking it over. The meat is singed a little bit, but it is salvageable. Barely. Definitely still edible, which is all she cares about. The fat that drips off the carcass makes the embers sizzle so that black smoke emerges, and Agatha hurriedly flaps her right wing so that it dissipates, not wanting it to curl up into the air beyond the trees and give away her location.

She swears under her breath as she uses another stick to pry the meat off the spit onto the tin plate that she pinched from a trader back in Salem before she was chased out of the town. When she peers up, Rio is still smiling, looking at her as if she wants to laugh. Her wings are shaking a little from the effort of holding it in.

“Shut up,” Agatha grumbles, her feathers twitching.

“I said nothing,” Rio replies innocently, twisting a dried leaf between her hands. “I was just thinking.”

Agatha narrows her eyes at her, left wing jerking. “Thinking what?”

“You are so very delightful when you are irritated.”

Agatha opens her mouth, and then closes it again, because she really doesn’t know how to respond to that or even what Rio might mean, which is aggravating. “You are a very strange creature,” she mutters instead.

“Am I?” Rio cocks her head. “I suppose you would know.”

The witch’s jaw drops at that. “You -” she splutters. But then falls silent, taken aback maybe at how she doesn’t feel at all insulted by that because somehow, her soul knows that Rio meant it as a joke. Death is joking with her. She falls back on being defensive. “You didn’t need to bring me flowers, my Lady. I have no way to take care of them… they will die in a few days.”

The Green Witch hums. “You don’t need to use an honorific with me. Do you like them?” she questions. “The flowers?”

Agatha exhales sharply. “Yes,” she forces out, cheeks pinkening as she admits it. “And I appreciate the thought, but as I said, they’ll wither and die anyway, especially given the cold.”

Rio blinks. She clearly didn’t consider that. “I can place a spell on them to preserve them?” she offers quietly. Agatha doesn’t reply straight away, too busy using her bone knife to slice up the rabbit into quarters so it’ll be easier to eat. “Perhaps I shall bring you more practical courting gifts in the future.”

More gifts? Her left wing jolts in a surprised flap, and Agatha’s eyes dart over to the cosmic entity, wide. She didn’t think that Lady Death was actually serious about courting her, and yet here she is bringing Agatha flowers and saying she will bring other things for her as well.

“That… would be appreciated,” she answers cautiously. She tacks on a quick, “Thank you,” at the end as well, which does not come naturally to her at all. Being thankful to somebody for being kind to her is not something she has experienced before. Most of the time she’s been thankful to people when they have stopped causing her pain, knowing they would hurt her again.

They eat - well, Agatha eats, and Rio sits, because apparently cosmic entities don’t need to eat - and the fire continues to smoulder as the forest breathes quietly around them. While Agatha is occupied with stuffing morsels of roasted rabbit meat into her mouth, Death slinks closer, shuffing until they are seated almost side by side.

Rio flares the wing closest to her so shield her back from the wind, and Agatha mentally notes that her feathers smell of something akin to lightning. Her plumage is such a deep shade of black that they actually appear to absorb the firelight.

As Agatha digs into her dinner, Rio asks her about the grimoire set aside next to where her coat is laid out to dry. She was reading it earlier while the rabbit was cooking. Agatha tells her, because there is no reason not to, that it was her mother's, and that she is annotating it, correcting the errors, because it was a grimoire about elemental magic, and although Evanora was a powerful witch, she tried to remove every single line about Spirit magic given that she didn’t want Agatha studying it at all.

Agatha finds herself talking longer than she intends to, just because of how Rio is listening attentively without an ounce of judgement. It’s the most she has spoken to another person in over a month.

When Agatha has finished eating and the fire starts to burn low, her Soulmate stands, and for a moment Agatha wants to reach out for her and ask her to stay. She can tell from the solemn look on Rio’s face though that she has duties to attend to. Lady Death has souls to claim.

“I will come back,” Rio assures her, with a tone of decisiveness that makes it sound like a promise.

“I don’t think I believe you.”

“Then I will have to convince you.” Grasping Agatha’s hand, she raises it to her mouth and presses a kiss to the back of it, her lips lingering for a moment. “I will see you again soon, mi alma.”

“Maybe don’t take so long to come visit, next time,” Agatha replies with faux haughtiness, lifting her chin. “Or I will once again find myself questioning your intent to court me.”

Rio chuckles. And then she vanishes, with one great sweeping flat of her wings that causes the fire’s ashes to scatter and the flames to leap, dissolving into the darkness in an instant. Agatha immediately feels colder, her chest tight and wings half-flared. The Spirit Witch sits with the dying fire and the flowers in her lap for a long time, her tired gaze darting over to the spot where Rio disappeared from every so often.

Later that night, once the moon is high overhead and she has built the fire back up again, Agatha presses the asters between the pages of the grimoire that she and Rio discussed, which she tells herself is for their botanical properties, so she can use the fried petals in a spell later on. Aster flowers have legitimate magical applications. It’s entirely practical and definitely not because since Rio left, she has felt as if there is a gaping hollow threatening to consume her heart that is synonymous with longing.

She falls asleep with the scents of winter honeysuckle and lightning lulling her to slumber.

 

 

Rio comes back.

Agatha half-expected - more than half, if she is being truthful - that the grand gesture at Salem and the flowers would be the end of it. After all, it all was rather ridiculous… A divine entity being destined Soulmates with a mortal witch, making a romantic declaration to court her, and then departing to go collect souls.

She thought that for Rio, the whole endeavour was just an interesting but ultimately brief distraction from her duties of serving the dead.

She was not prepared for this. For Rio to actually return, and return again.

Her visit is fleeting. She appears while Agatha is out one morning checking her perimeter wards, apologising that she cannot stay long as some war has broken out across the ocean. Agatha is left reeling when Rio presses a kiss to her cheek and then leaves behind a small bundle wrapped in cloth next to her sleeping space before flying off again.

Curled up on her little bed of blankets, Agatha glares at it for a full minute before she touches it, yanking it towards her. Her first feeling, embarrassingly, is not caution, but a quick, irrational pulse of something bright and foolish that she identifies as anticipation. Rio said that she would keep bringing Agatha courtship gifts, and she’s curious to know what the cosmic deity has chosen.

She’s shocked and impressed. First to emerge from the bundle is a length of good rope, approximately fifteen feet of it, strong and waterproof. It’s the kind of rope that she knows would cost two weeks of a working man's wages.

There’s a small glass bottle of oil, which upon sniffing, Agatha wrinkles her nose at; it has a pungent, earthy smell, almost like decaying plant matter. It’s probably meant for wing preening, rather than human consumption. The strong scent would do a good job at deterring feather mites.

There are also buttons. A handful of bone buttons of various sizes and two pewter ones, along with two fat spools of sturdy thread, one dark grey and the other in brown. Two needles were tucked into the spools, as well as a little brass thimble shaped like a rabbit.

She turns the pewter buttons over in her fingers, and picks up the spool of dark grey thread, a lump forming in her throat. Her winter wool coat has two buttons missing from the front. She has been wearing it fastened with the remaining buttons, which makes it functional but still allows the chill in. Rio must have been watching her… and noticed that she was cold.

Tears well in her eyes before she can stop them. Agatha has spent her entire life neglected and treated like a burden. A curse. The absence of care has been so constant and total that she has stopped perceiving it as an absence. It was just… her life. One did not mourn the absence of something they have never known they were supposed to have.

Except that now there is this. Because Rio sees her. Not as a problem to be managed, or a threat to be neutralised, or a child servant to be abused, but as a person whose life is worth paying attention to and nurturing.

Holding her breath and wiping away her unshed tears, Agatha picks up the needle and grey thread, and sets about fixing her coat, sewing the pewter buttons onto the front where they were missing, neat stitches that her hands know how to make from years of practice.

The rope she uses that afternoon, cutting it to create a snare. The rest she uses to help build herself a shelter, tying a thick fallen branch about four feet off the ground to the trunks of two thick-trunked pines. There are plenty of other fallen branches around the area, which she piles up against the tied crossbeam. She weaves twigs into a framework and layers them with dead bracken and fried leaves, gathering enough dry moss to stuff into the gaps and keep the wind out.

It’s not the best shelter, but with the fire set up across from it, the heat radiates into the small space and ensures that she is kept warm. It won’t completely shield her from the rain, but perhaps next time the Green Witch visits her, she can ask Rio to persuade the wood and dried plants to become impermeable to water.

She could very easily leave the mountains and find some abandoned shack or house in a rural village to live in, but with the witch hunters after her, and other witches after her, she doesn’t want to risk it. Best she give it until spring before she ventures back to civilisation.

Agatha lies down that night beneath the shelter’s branches and uses the preening oil on her wings, whimpering silently as she runs her fingers through her knotted feathers.

Her wings have always been in terrible shape because of how her coven refused to allow her to participate in their flock grooming sessions. Living alone in the forest has made them even worse. There are twigs and pine needles stuck in her tangled plumage, and although she has grown used to them now, it makes flying a painful affair.

She knows Rio would help if she asks; Rio’s hands have been gentle and kind so far, offering tender, soft touches. But the thought of it makes Agatha feel dizzy and short of breath. To let somebody else touch her wings would be to admit how badly they’d been cared for, every crooked quill and damaged plume telling a story of being rejected by her own flock.

All she has ever known is that somebody touching her wings means pain. So she struggles alone with the oil and her trembling hands, wishing that she were brave enough to trust Rio with the most fragile parts of herself.

Agatha works for hours to try and groom herself, eventually growing so exhausted that she passes out before she can finish.

On the next visit, only a couple of days later, Rio brings her food.

It’s a crisp, cold November morning; there is frost on the ground that has not thawed yet, due to the clouds covering the sun. Agatha fortunately can tell from having spent many winters in New England that it will be a couple more weeks before the first snowfall hits. She has no idea what she will do when that happens - she knows it would be ridiculous of her to try and survive it whilst living homeless in the woods, exposed to the elements.

For now, though, Agatha is sitting on a fallen log by a stream, snacking on rose hips, and practising a transmutation spell that will return watercress into mint. She wants to make mint tea later to stave off the cold.

Earlier, she used water from the brook to clean her feathers; she dipped a rag into it to get it wet so she could run it over her frayed flight feathers, which, after nights of sleeping so close to her campfire, have become caked in a layer of fine grey ash.

She looks up with an embarrassing level of excitement when her soul starts to sing, and she hears the flapping of giant wings buffeting the air. Hearing the wings of other witches would usually fill her with fear and anger, but when she knows they belong to her Soulmate, Agatha feels a thrill run down her spine so that her own wings flutter.

“Good morning, little crow,” Rio greets her.

There’s a flush to her cheeks that is likely caused by the cold, but she seems unbothered by it otherwise, her feet still bare even as the frost crunches beneath her soles. Her raven wings have a light dusting of snow on them, making them look like a star-speckled night sky. When the cloud cover breaks to reveal the sun shining above, the snow specks glimmer.

“Good morning, my Lady,” Agatha replies, lips ticking up into a smirk.

“I’ve told you before, there’s no need to use honorifics,” Rio says dismissively, smiling. “You are my Soulmate, after all.” She appraises the Spirit witch, beaming when she sees her repaired coat. “I see you made use of your gifts.”

“The buttons were very useful,” she murmurs. “As were the other gifts.”

Rio’s wings fluff up. “So I picked well?”

“Are you so eager to receive praise? Maybe instead of appearing as a witch, you should change to the form of a puppy, whining to please its mistress,” Agatha teases her, but rolls her eyes and reassures her, “You did very well.”

“Let us hope that I do just as well this time, then.” 

Rio passes over her courting gift. Agatha is perfectly capable of foraging and hunting for herself, and Rio probably understands this from having observed her, which is why she brings her food that she can’t get her hands on so long as she lives out in the wilderness.

It’s a cloth-wrapped parcel again, but this time containing a small wheel of hard cheese, a pot of salt, a cloth bag filled with dried fruits, including some Agatha has never seen before, a slab of honeycomb wrapped in waxed cloth, and finally, a small paper bag of cream filberts. They look like small mothballs, dusted in powdered sugar.

Agatha holds the bag in her hand and stares at it, astounded.

She can already feel tears welling in her eyes.

She hasn’t tasted cream filberts since she was twelve years old. She nicked one from a merchant's cart on a market day in Salem, one single hazelnut encrusted in a creamy sugarly coating to form a tiny biscuit, sweet and nutty and rich.  

In that moment, it was the most luxurious and delicious thing she had ever eaten. Not because it was at all rare, necessarily - although they were expensive - but because nobody caught her or punished her for stealing it, and for those five seconds that the filbert lasted, she felt happy. She thought about it for months afterward, in the same way that a sad person likes to reminisce about a small pleasure when they feel particularly miserable, which she had been throughout her entire childhood.

“I love cream filberts,” she whispers to herself.

“I know,” Rio preens.

“How?”

Rio settles herself on the other end of the log and folds her hands in her lap, her wings shaking off the last remnants of snow from them. “I told you. I see you in the River.” She helpfully says nothing about the way that the Spirit witch is wiping at her eyes to hide her tears.

Agatha peers over at her warily, but with an undercurrent of wonder. Rio did mention that before, that she has observed Agatha since her birth in the River that allows the passage between the living world and the Realm of the Dead. It’s only because Agatha is her Soulmate that she is able to peer into the waters and glimpse moments of her life, however mundane or boring.

“You watched me steal a cream filbert off a trader as a child… and you remembered it,” she elucidates.

“Yes. Of course I did. You were happy, in that moment, and that was a rare occurrence. I liked seeing you happy,” Rio responds honestly, unabashed. “You had that expression you wear when you’ve got away with something.”

Agatha raises her eyebrow. “What expression is that?”

“Cunning, bright-eyed, triumphant.” Rio says it with warmth, not as an insult. Her wings shift slightly, the dark feathers catching the sunlight that ripples through the canopy above. “You were an adorable little thief.”

Agatha feels the tips of her ears go warm, which she finds annoying, as she’s sure that her cheeks are likely turning noticeably red as well. “I’m not sure that’s a compliment.”

“I mean it as one,” Rio responds with complete sincerity as she gazes at her affectionately. “Crows are exceptionally intelligent birds. It’s all too appropriate that you possess the wings of one.”

The protective emotional shield Agatha has erected around her heart develops another hairline crack, which she is going to have to address at some point, but not now.

Rio does not fly off and remains with Agatha throughout the day, observing her practice her magic with intrigue, and even offering her tips on her hand movements so they are more fluid and allow her power to flow with ease from her core.

Agatha wasn’t allowed to practice her magic around her coven when she was a witchling, her mother suppressing her abilities with a binding spell and refusing to teach her. As such, she is extremely eager to study more. Agatha had no idea that her power also flows from her wings, through her feathers, and realises that she will likely have to continue regularly preening her wings if this is true.

Whilst Agatha bundles the watercress and mint, Rio perches like a heron on the side of the stream. Her wings are poised above her to cast a shadow over the water, which she stands in with her bare feet up to her ankles. She lunges down and plucks a brook trout out with her hands, no fishing line required. Rio laughs in delight, showing Agatha her prize with a toothy grin that has the Spirit witch shaking her head fondly.

The fish is a good ten inches long, which is an ideal eating size. Agatha kills it - Rio, strangely, refuses to - and washes the guts out in the river so that the water also chills it, before wrapping it in some spare waxed cloth from the bundle that Rio gifted her.  

They share the honeycomb that evening, after Agatha has eaten the fish, roasted over the fire and seasoned with Rio’s salt and watercress. Agatha consumes most of the comb, breaking pieces off with her fingers and letting the delicious sweetness dissolve on her tongue.

She’s gleeful, having not tasted something this sweet in a while, and her wings twitch in tiny happy flaps. Death watches her with an expression of tranquil, adoring contentment that is so open that Agatha keeps having to look away and then glance back, face aflame.

“Don't look at me like that,” Agatha mumbles, self-conscious.

“Like what?”

“You know like what.” Like Agatha is some wonderful, amazing creature to be admired. To be loved. Most of the time, she feels like anything but.

Rio considers this for a moment, her feathers rustling behind her. She understands what Agatha means, the Spirit witch can tell. “I could try not to, I suppose,” she muses aloud, “But I think I would likely fail. You are my Soulmate and I care very much about you. I have been waiting a long time for the chance to look at you, and I find that now I have the opportunity, I am disinclined to waste it.”

Agatha does not have a response to this. She eats another piece of honeycomb and stares at her honey-covered fingers, and allows herself, privately and without acknowledging it in any way, to enjoy Lady Death’s company, and enjoy being cared about.

 

 

The cold sharpens and becomes more stark in the last week of November. It’s not dangerous yet, but it speaks of snow that will arrive soon. Agatha reads the weather, and can tell by the way the temperature drops three hours before dusk now, and in the particular thickness of the frost that forms on the bark of the forest trees. Her breath fogs now, even when she is close to the fire.

Agatha’s not afraid. She’s lived through cold before. She’s survived cold that would kill an ordinary human, and done it with less than she has now - when her mother threw her out of the house during a blizzard for accidentally knocking an oil lamp when she was nine years old, and she huddled, sheltered only by her own wings, on the porch until dawn, when Evanora was forced to let her inside before the neighbours saw her.

But Agatha knows that if it’s this cold already in December, that January and February are going to be even colder. And she won’t survive in the mountain woods without proper shelter. Already, she has been struggling to fall asleep, and waking up with the cold sitting on her chest like a boulder because her fire has burned down to ash in the early hours of the morning. She doesn’t want to know what will happen if that boulder starts to crush her lungs.

Agatha goes on a flight to warm her aching muscles and exercise her wings, flying a long eastward arc above the forest, when she spots the roof of a cabin.

The dark timber sticks out like a sore thumb against the hillside, tucked into a fold where two ridgelines meet and there is a break in the woods. She banks and circles twice before she lets herself descend, scanning the ground carefully.

There are no tracks, no horses, and no smoke coming from the chimney. No wards that she can feel, no residue of any recent magic. Agatha would be able to sense that as a siphon, after all. She hasn’t needed to feed on any other witches’ magic since she drained and killed her coven, although she suspects that come spring, she will have to go hunting again.

The cabin door hangs ajar, which is how she knows it has been empty for a long time. Agatha lands thirty feet from it and approaches on foot, wings half-spread so she can take off immediately if she needs to, cautious and alert. She pushes the door open, sticking her head inside.

The cabin is a single room, perhaps fifteen square feet, built of white pine. There’s a stone fireplace with a hook to hang a cooking pot on the north wall, blackened but intact, with a stack of dry cut wood still beside it. A sleeping shelf is set against the far wall, straw long since turned to dust but the frame solid.

Near the fireplace, there’s a table and two chairs, and a larder with abandoned crockery and a rusted iron pot. Whoever lived here has been gone a very long time, given the dust that flies up from the floorboards as Agatha slinks inside.

It’s a building. An empty one, with four solid walls and a roof to keep the wind, snow and rain out. It’s what she needs for the winter.

She moves her things from her little shelter in the forest in two trips, made easier by the fact that she can fly her belongings up the mountain. Agatha starts a fire in the hearth with the firefood, and then spends some time laying down new wards around the cabin’s perimeter.

There’s more chopped wood stacked behind the cabin in an alcove, so she brings armfuls of it inside. All of her blankets and clothes and rags are thrown onto the sleeping shelf, and the food is stored in the small larder. Agatha uses a wind spell to blow all of the dust out of the open door, flapping her wings to disturb it where it’s settled heavily.

When Death arrives for another visit a few days later, she finds Agatha seated at the cabin’s small table with both hands around a cup of actual hot pine needle tea, her grimoire open and one of her annotations half-finished. Her wings are relaxed and drooping, speaking to her comfort and peaceful mental state.

Rio stops in the doorway as she takes in the cabin. Something shifts in her expression, which she swiftly hides so she can plaster on something more indifferent, but Agatha has been studying Rio’s face for long enough now that she catches the edge of it. Lady Death is relieved.

“You found a new place to live,” Rio says, sounding surprised. She steps inside, her raven wings adjusting automatically as she passes through the doorframe. She closes the door behind her, her wings flicking to rid her feathers of any droplets of water that have clung to them from outside.

“It’s temporary,” Agatha replies quickly. “It’s not home. I’ll stay here for the winter, and that’s all.”

“Of course. How long do you think it’s been empty?”

“Not sure. There was firewood stacked, but there weren’t any supplies otherwise. I doubt any human has been here in a very long time. No traces of witches or any kind of magic. I won’t be disturbed here. It’ll be a safe place to stay until spring.” Agatha motions to the chair opposite her. “Sit down. There’s tea. There should be a spare cup on the shelf. I know you don’t eat, but please don’t tell me you don’t drink tea as well.”

Rio finds the spare cup and fills it from the pot where the pine needles have been steeping, and then sinks down on the other chair, her wings arching. The cabin is a little bit too cramped with two pairs of huge wings inside of it, but there’s not much they can do about that. Agatha would offer her some molasses to sweeten the bitter drink, but she knows that Rio will probably just nurse it anyway, since she doesn’t actually need to drink it.

They sit across from each other in the firelight for a while in comfortable silence.

“This is better,” Rio observes. “I know you were staying in the forest out of necessity of staying hidden, but this will be much warmer and more secure for when the snow arrives. I’m surprised, really, you did not find this place earlier, given its proximity to where you were previously staying.”

Agatha wraps both of her hands tighter around her cup. The flames pop and crackle in the foreplace, casting warmth throughout the cabin, which her black feathers absorb. “I wasn’t looking for somewhere else, earlier. This week, I was cold and couldn’t sleep, so I decided to start searching for winter shelter,” she murmurs, sipping her tea.

Rio appears troubled. “You didn’t tell me you were too cold to sleep.”

She narrows her eyes, suddenly feeling as if her Soulmate is criticising her. “I don’t need to tell you,” Agatha snaps. “There’s no obligation for me to! You may be my Soulmate, and you may be courting me, but you hold no power over my body!”

Despite her barbed words, Rio barely reacts, merely staring at her placidly. “I know that, Agatha.” Her name sounds beautiful in Lady Death’s voice. “And you’re right, you don’t need to tell me things, but you could have, if you’d wanted to. I’m not upset that you didn’t, I merely want you to know that you can tell me these things. So I can help,” Rio informs her calmly.

Agatha feels guilty for barking at her then, and it’s an emotion she despises. She wants to avert her eyes so that she doesn’t have to look at her Soulmate, but finds herself scrutinising her instead. The firelight is doing what light does to Rio’s wings, pulling greens and purples from the black, making the iridescent sheen gleam every time she breathes.

“I’m aware,” Agatha finally replies in a mumble. “And I’ll… consider it.”

She thinks, later, after Rio has gone and she is lying on the sleeping shelf with the fire smouldering and the cabin warm around her, that she maybe isn’t so opposed to Rio’s help, as long as she continues to freely offer it.

 

 

Lady Death continues to court her Little Crow, and the gifts continue too, with the new way of living in the cabin changing what kind of gifts she chooses. The first is a new knife, properly balanced, because Agatha has been using the bone-handled one for everything, and the tip is wearing.

The second, a set of pairs of tin bowls, plates, cups, and some more cooking utensils, as the cabin doesn’t have any.Then, shortly after, Rio brings her some tallow candles, which extend her grimoire reading hours past what the fire alone can give in the winter darkness.

Very useful is the reel of linen which the cosmic entity hefts into the cabin; Agatha uses the cloth to make some new shirts and pants, since she is tired of wearing dresses around the cabin constantly.

And Rio starts to bring her flowers again.

Agatha opens the cabin door on the first day that it snows in early December to find her Soulmate on the threshold with a bunch of holly in her hands, with dark glossy leaves and blood-coloured berries.

“Holly,” she states, opening the door wider to allow Rio in. Her wings are so massive that she struggles sometimes to duck beneath the doorway, which Agatha is always very amused by. “An odd choice. I know that birds can eat the berries, but they’re poisonous to humans and witches, you know.”

“Foresight,” Rio explains the symbolism. “And domestic happiness.” She holds the bunch out with a shyness that Agatha would not have expected to come from the Queen of Darkness. “I thought the second one was apt, given your new home. It’s also associated with protection and good luck, through to the Winter Solstice. I don’t expect you to eat the berries, I thought you might want to hang it up inside - I’ve seen many witches do so.”

Agatha takes the holly with a sigh, deciding that it’s not worth arguing about calling the cabin ‘home’ with Rio. “I’m not a typical kind of witch. Sit down,” she orders. Nn the two weeks since moving into the cabin, it's beome something she always instructs the Green Witch to do, with the acknowledgement that the chair exists and is specifically Rio’s to sit in.

She does hang the holly over the fireplace just to prevent Rio from puouting, and then presses one of the leaves between the grimoire’s middle pages. Whilst making tea for them both, Agatha pulls out a book on Green magic, which she has been neglecting to study thus far, given that it’s not her element. Rio perks up upon the sight of the book, though, clearly sensing the energy radiating off it, and is all too happy to spend the rest of the day chatting with her about it, until the time comes when she must leave for her duties once again.

“I’ve noticed you don’t use much magic for everyday things,” Rio says later, observing Agatha stripping dried rosemary leaves from their twigs, for a tincture she will make to ward off winter sickness. “Is it because you learnt as a child how to live without magic, given your mother bound your powers?”

Agatha lifts her wings in a shrug. “That, and I have a finite amount of magic currently,” she murmurs. She has read up on a lot of Spirit magic and her own nature as a siphon, since gaining custody of her mother’s grimoires and spell books. “I’m not like other witches… my core does not generate magic the same way, given my Spirit inclination. I have to draw on the power of other witches.”

“Like you drained your coven that night we first met in Salem,” Rio remembers. “You absorbed their magic when they struck you with it, pulled it into you and out of them until they were so empty that it killed them.”

Agatha flinches at the reminder, and turns defensive. “I didn’t mean to do it,” she snarls. “I was starving for magic, they attacked me and I couldn’t help it!” She looks away, clenching her jaw. “That doesn’t mean I’m not happy they are dead, though.”

“If it’s any consolation,” Rio murmurs after a moment of frigid, tense silence, “I am glad they are dead too. They treated you terribly. They deserved to die painful deaths.”

The Spirit witch chuckles darkly. “And you would know, I suppose, given that you are Death.” She plucks idly at a loose covert feather. “But anyway, that is why I do not use my magic very much. I don’t need to use it all the time, and although I have enough, at the moment, given I drained my coven of theirs, I need to conserve it. I’m not hungry for it like I was before all the time when my mother had me bound. But I think I will have to wait until spring to find other witches I can source magic from. Or at least, until the witch hunters have grown bored of pursuing me and the other covens along the East coast have largely forgotten about the Spirit witch with black wings who killed her entire flock.”

Rio tilts her head, questioning intriguedly, “Will you kill other witches for their magic?”

Agatha stares back at her. She honestly hasn’t really thought about that before. Hasn’t made the connection between needing to feed on magic, and needing to kill to do so. “Probably,” she mutters.

She largely feels indifferent about it, and is more shocked by that than the idea that she may have to kill other witches to feed her magical core. Perhaps it’s because she knows that with her carrion crow wings, she will never be accepted, welcomed, or even tolerated by the rest of witch society, marked as she is as Death’s Soulmate.

They will look upon her as some wretched, cursed, broken creature who would be better off dead and in the arms of Death, just like Evanora and the Salemites did. No witch would ever give Agatha some of their magic willingly, so Agatha will have to take it.

It’s no different from a killer whale needing to kill a shark to feed on it. Cannibalism is natural, something observed in many species across the world. Agatha is just… an apex predator. She needs to consume the other magic of witches to survive, and survive she will.

“Is that going to cause issues between us?” Agatha asks Rio, narrowing her eyes suspiciously, given that the cosmic deity hasn’t said anything. “That I am going to have to kill people and deliver souls to you to ferry and therefore give you work to do? Is that some kind of… ethical or moral problem, for you to reap the souls of your Soulmate’s victims?”

“No,” Rio replies with a smile, her raven wings ruffling. “It won’t be an issue. All it means is that I will get to see you while I am working. If anything, it is a good thing.” She pauses, and adds, “You killing other witches does not upset me, little crow. Corvids are predators as well as scavengers. And I am Death, it is my job to ferry the souls, not judge those who have delivered them into my arms.”

“Well… good,” is Agatha’s response, because she’s a little lost for words otherwise. “There likely won’t be any witch killing until spring though, so you won’t be able to visit me on the job.”

Rio hums thoughtfully. “I will just have to keep visiting you whenever I can take a break from my duties until spring, then.”

Agatha tries not to think about how the idea of Rio spending all her time off-duty with her makes her feel valued. It’s not a feeling she is very well accustomed to… but with Rio, she thinks she could grow used to it.

It’s both frightening how much Agatha has grown used to her Soulmate’s presence, and an utter relief to know that she has an all-powerful cosmic deity guarding her in her sleep, so that she no longer has to remain as vigilant and restless as before.

The one thing she remains cautious and cagey about is her wing grooming.

Eighteen years of her mother and coven refusing to touch her feathers - out of superstition, disgust, and fear of contamination by the Spirit witch with wings of Death, who they decided was cursed from birth - has left her entirely self-reliant. Agatha knows that her carrion crow wings are in awful shape.

She tries her best to preen herself, though. She knows every feather by feel, and oils her primaries with the strange-smelling oil Rio gifted her. Pinfeathers are the worst, painful and irritating, but Agatha always takes care in easing the keratin sheath away with careful fingers, even if she knows that the feather is bound to get damaged and worn.

She has never let anyone else touch them. Not willingly. In Salem, when she was a child, her mother's preferred method of control was inflicting wing pain, because the agony was sharp and immediate, and whipping wings didn’t leave any visible marks that humans can see, given that they can’t see witch wings. And when she was dragged to her trial and execution, her wings were bound with leather belts so tightly that they were bruised to the bone.

She knows, rationally, that Rio would never cause her pain like that. Rio completely respects her boundaries and has only ever been soft to her. The problem is not knowledge. The problem is her body, which has its own memory, and is very hard to override.

One late afternoon, Rio is sitting behind Agatha, watching her as Agatha extends her wings to give herself a quick grooming before bed. She went flying earlier, as it was one of the rare December days when it didn’t rain or snow. She developed some twisted, tangled secondaries as a result.

Rio’s own jet black wings are tucked behind her politely, but her right one keeps twitching every time Agatha touches her own wing, as if she is experiencing a phantom sensation.

She offers, very quietly, “May I help?”

Agatha stiffens instantly. She whips her head around and glowers at her, baring her teeth instinctively. “No,” she replies shortly.

Rio nods and lowers her eyes submissively. “Alright.”

She continues to preen herself, grunting and struggling to reach behind her back. There is a pinfeather emerging high on the outside of her left wing, in a location she can’t reach with her fingers; it’s a secondary covert located behind her shoulder joint, too far down for her fingers to stretch.

She’s been aware of it for four days. It’s been creating a prickling, itching, building pressure, and she knows she needs to sort it out soon, or the feather inside will start to die. In a coven flock, a sister witch would have spotted it on sight and dealt with it in thirty seconds. But Agatha has never had a flock member willing to help her before, and so is determined to fix it herself.

Her arm wrenches at a painful angle behind her, fingers spread, making contact with her median coverts, nowhere near the pinfeather. She growls in frustration. Tries again. Doesn’t get any closer. 

“Let me?” Rio offers tentatively.

Agatha lowers her arm and turns to stare at her, tensed like a cornered animal. The fire is burning low, the cold creeping into the cabin. She curls up on the sleeping shelf, her wings folding against her back to hide them from view.

“I don’t want your help,” she mutters, but her weary tone neutralises the venom she tries to inject into her voice.

“You may not,” Rio nods. “But I think you do need it.”

She could very easily tell Rio no again, and the cosmic entity would respect her wishes and back off. But that covert is really starting to hurt now, and she doesn’t want the feather to get infected or die just because she is too stubborn to accept offered help.

“Fine,” she croaks, her voice rough.

“I'll be careful,” Rio promises. “I won’t hurt you. I swear.” 

“I know,” Agatha exhales.

She shifts herself so she is sitting cross-legged on her bed, her wings hanging behind her, and then spreads her left wing slowly until the full span is exposed. Her whole body is rigid, barely suppressing her tremors, but she’s committed now.

Rio moves behind her; Agatha hears the soft sound of her footsteps on the wooden floorboards as she draws closer, and the faint shift of her raven feathers.

Rio’s first touch is electrifying. Fingers part the feathers behind her left shoulder with a gentleness and care that is clearly deliberate, her palm pressed against her coverts in a manner that is meant to reassure her.

Agatha is shaking; she counts her breaths, trying to prevent herself from panicking and hyperventilating by inhaling and exhaling for five seconds each, which is a technique she developed as a child when being beaten. She holds herself perfectly still as she feels Rio’s hands move.

She braces for pain that never comes.

Rio’s nimble fingers find the embedded pinfeather immediately, with a precision that tells her that she’s not guessing, but knows exactly where it’s located. Probably from having observed her struggling to grasp it herself. Rio presses the heel of her hand lightly against the surrounding feathers to stabilise them, and begins to ease the trapped shaft free.

The relief is so acute that Agatha cries out. The sound escapes her mouth involuntarily, a single muffled little whimper, the release of four days of accumulated irritation and discomfort dissolving. She hears her Soulmate chuckle, feels her brush away her hair from the nape of her neck so she can press a rewarding, soothing kiss there.

Lady Death doesn’t pull away once the pinfeather is free, her fingers moving along the edge of the wing, checking her plumage, finding a crooked secondary and straightening it, and then smoothing over a ruffled section of scapular feathers. She works as if she has done this before, grooming another person, which Agatha would bet that she hasn’t. But she has watched Agatha groom herself before, and has obviously been paying attention and learning visually.

Agatha’s body decides after a minute or two that she’s safe, and all of the tension leaks away, leaving warm, cosy pleasure behind. She likes to imagine that this is what a purring kitten feels like when it’s being stroked. Rio's hands continue raking and combing through her feathers, and it’s truly not like anything Agatha has experienced before.

She’s crying. Uncontrollably, because even though she wants to stop, finds it utterly mortifying, she can’t. The tears are just springing from her eyes and rolling down her cheeks, gathering on her chin before descending as salty droplets to dampen her bedding.

She didn’t know before how much she wanted this until now. Didn’t think she should want it, or deserve it.

The Spirit witch completely loses track of time, falling into a daze. After a long while, Rio asked in a hushed voice, “May I do the other?”

“Yes,” she keens, not even caring how the word emerges as a desperate moan.

Rio works through her right wing with the same unhurried, comprehensive attention she brings to everything, and Agatha feels each feather being straightened out and tangle being eased free like a knot in her heart being untied, each one releasing tension she had no idea was weighing her down.

When Rio finishes, she helps Agatha, who is so relaxed that she is dozing and barely conscious, to lie down on her side. Her wings feel weavy, two giant feathered weights dragging on her shoulder blades, but her Soulmate gently folds them up into her spine and maneuvers them onto the sleeping shelf as well, before tucking a threadbare blanket over her, and then draping her green velvet cloak over her as well, for good measure.

“Thank you,” she slurs out.

“It was my pleasure. And my privilege,” Rio responds quietly, squeezing her shoulder.

Agatha turns to look at her then, sleepily peering up at Death over her shoulder. Rio’s expression is so unguarded and so soft that it makes Agatha’s stomach somersault. The fondness shining in Lady Death’s eyes in the dim ember-glow is steady and unapologetic, genuine and real in a way that has Agatha’s breath stuttering.

She looks away and closes her eyes. “I suppose you'll want to do that a lot more now,” she murmurs.

“Only if you'll permit it,” Rio whispers in response.

Agatha swallows the lump forming in her throat. She shrugs and mumbles, “We'll see.”

She already knows deep in her soul that she will allow Rio to groom her wings again.

And when Rio departs that night, Agatha finds herself wishing that Death didn’t have to leave her.
