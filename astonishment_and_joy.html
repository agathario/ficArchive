Chapter 1: with little pride and less hope

It was the winter of 1856, bitterly cold and unforgiving, when the newcomers arrived at the old homestead in Schoharie County.

There was ice in the bedroom for the first time all winter. Agatha noticed it on the sill as she stood at the window and tamed her hair into one long braid down her back. She preferred to leave it loose and wild, but there was no use for that these days — it would only get in the way. Her hands shook slightly with the cold as she scrubbed the potatoes, the water freezing on them as soon as they were washed. When she went out to check on the chickens, only one small egg lay in the hay. She set it carefully in the bowl, and feared Dyer’s response.

In the early afternoon she went to collect kindling on the hill, so concentrated on her task that she almost didn’t notice the horse and cart pull up at the dilapidated house in the valley below. The movement in her peripheral vision made her start — as a rule people didn’t pass through here. The landscape around her and Dyer’s homestead was barren and sprawling, scattered with small clusters of trees, but rarely signs of human life.

She bundled the sticks under her arm and used one hand to shield her face against the white sun, hidden behind a blanket of clouds. Below, a man dismounted from the cart, his movements brisk and careless. Behind him a woman, gathering her skirts to clamber down. The man did not offer her a hand, already beginning to sling bags and boxes from the back of the cart to the ground. He gestured to the boxes, and the woman nodded and picked one up, resting it on her hip.

She was too far away for Agatha to make out her features well, but her hair was striking — dark and loose, cascading down her back. Her skirts were unusual too, a shade of green Agatha had never seen in fabric before, verdant as fresh spring. The woman paused, breathing in the air deeply as though she rarely had opportunity to do so, surveying the surrounding hills. Agatha’s breath caught as her gaze drew nearer, and it seemed her eyes had alighted upon her. The woman gave a hesitant wave. Agatha paused, then slowly waved back. Though she could barely see her face, she thought the woman gave a curious smile.

Then the woman’s husband — he must surely be her husband — called, and she started and turned away as if burnt.

 

 

Back at home, Agatha was distracted. She sorted the firewood into piles to dry, finished her tasks about the barn, and began to prepare the food for dinner. Dyer was out in the fields with the sheep, and would be until evening. Usually this was a moment when she felt as close as she could feel to some kind of peace, her tasks complete, the rest of the day a blank slate until Dyer returned. Most days she took this moment to journal, seeking comfort in the possibility of the blank paper and ink. But as she sat down to do so her mind strayed to the woman with the dark hair and green skirt, who looked as though she had stepped out of another world. She would surely see her again, but when?

Her curiosity surprised her — she hadn’t felt so alive with a feeling in a long while. But then again, nothing new had happened in a long while either. Every day was the same: wake at dawn, make oatmeal for herself and Dyer, exchange a few words of monosyllabic conversation and then embark upon the practicalities of life. She had been visiting Nicholas’ grave, on the hill above the house, more rarely these days. She didn’t like to see it in winter — it was too much of a cruel reminder of the winter which had taken him from her. She had preferred to tend to it in the spring and summer, leaving sprigs of flowers on the headstone, sitting there for hours and telling him stories about flying horses and castles in the sky. Dyer thought it strange that she did this, and told her so, but for her talking to Nicholas was as normal as breathing.

She turned to a fresh page of her journal and dated it at the top. January 1st, 1856. “With little pride and less hope, we begin the new year,” she wrote. After a moment’s pause she dipped her pen into the ink bottle again and wrote below, as if as an afterthought, “Newcomers at the Zebrun farm.”

By the time Dyer returned that night, Agatha had imagined a hundred conversations with the woman; a hundred different ways that they might cross paths. She hadn’t had a friend in years, not since she was a child, and hadn’t much desired one either. But now she felt a keen yearning — for the company of somebody other than Dyer; for something to change.

She had to remind herself that for all she knew the woman and her husband could be awful, clueless city dwellers who thought they could handle the country and would soon be proven wrong. They’d figure out they couldn’t hack it and leave within weeks. Perhaps the woman would be mean, catty, like some of the women Agatha encountered at the market. Or perhaps she would be kind. Perhaps she could be a friend.

“We have new neighbours. They’re renting the Zebrun farm,” Dyer told her, sinking into his chair with a heavy sigh.

“Oh?” Agatha reached over to put a baked potato on his plate, her interest barely disguised.

“Arrived today. It’s a man, Finney, and his wife.”

“And what is the name of his wife?”

Dyer started eating, wearily, barely concentrating. “Something strange, a name I’d never heard before. Rio, I believe.”

“Will they be here long?”

“Yes, Finney said they’re wanting to get settled. Start a family. They’re young, but not so young.”

Dyer looked at Agatha pointedly as he said this, and she felt something heavy settle in her gut. She couldn’t bear it if this couple became yet another point of comparison for him; yet another reason for him to remind her that time was ticking, and there was only so long left before they would have to abandon the idea of another child. Agatha had let go of the idea long ago, not that she’d told Dyer that in so many words; had known at the very moment of Nicky’s death that there could never be anyone else for her. She had already been a mother to the most wonderful child she could have asked for. And yet Dyer persisted.

That night, after she blew out the candle and slipped into bed, facing away from him, he threw an arm around her waist and began to touch her. She closed her eyes, breathed out a small sigh, and allowed herself to dissociate a little. She was fortunate — Dyer was gentle, and his touch did not repel her, in fact in some way it made her feel safe. But she felt none of the heat he seemed to feel; none of the desire. She never had.

She knew what he was imagining as he slipped inside her — that this would be the moment that they created another child; this would be the second turning point of their life, their relationship, and everything would fall into place once again. Agatha felt herself float away from her body as he planted his hands on either side of her face, pulling her hair slightly, and knew that within a month the blood would come and there would be no child.

“Good night,” he murmured as he pulled out, leaving an uncomfortable sticky feeling behind, and an unpleasant ache between her legs — something not fully formed, and still unfulfilled. He kissed her forehead then collapsed onto his pillow, sated.

“Good night,” she replied, turning away once again.

Behind her closed eyelids danced visions of dark hair stirring in the wind, fresh spring green and a hesitant wave. For the first time in months, she yearned for the day to come.

February 23rd was Agatha’s birthday.

The weather was appropriately ominous — dark clouds forming overhead, birds scattering to each gust of wind. Dyer was already outside when she woke, tying things down with rope in preparation for a potential storm. She knew that she should go out and help him, but she felt frozen, her limbs heavy against the sheets. Today was her second birthday without Nicky; the second birthday when she had not been woken by small arms around her neck and soft kisses. She felt empty from the lack of it.

Rio had come by a few times since the dinner, but Dyer had been more present than usual, coming in and out of the kitchen constantly without warning, so conversation between the two of them had been minimal. Agatha woke every morning aching for Rio’s company; for her warm smile and her trusting gaze, her eyes steadily willing Agatha to drop her guard.

She had wondered whether Rio would be embarrassed by how candid she had been, first in Agatha’s kitchen over the chicken feathers and then in the seclusion of her own kitchen at dinner, but she showed no sign of it. She must be more accustomed to friendship than I am, Agatha had realised with an envious pang. These compliments and gentle touches must be commonplace to her, rather than a shock to the system as they were for Agatha. The thought of that — that to Rio this might be just another neighbourly friendship — made Agatha feel sick. Because it seemed to be becoming Agatha’s whole world.

When Rio and Finney had first arrived at the farm she had been so deep in misery that she would have latched on to anything, probably, she realised. Did Rio feel like she had latched onto her? Did she see her as needy, an inconvenience? Agatha felt herself spiralling a little, as she always seemed to when she hadn’t seen Rio for a few days, growing unmoored and uncertain. She forced herself to get up and dress.

“You’re awake,” Dyer said as he entered the kitchen, not bothering to take off his boots and treading in tracks of mud and melted snow. “It looks like there’s going to be a snowstorm. I’d best head to the Zebrun farm right away, to help Finney with their livestock. I’ll send Rio to help you with your tasks here.”

“Alright,” Agatha said, her pulse quickening at the idea of Rio being here while their husbands laboured elsewhere. Dyer looked almost excited, too. He seemed to enjoy storms, battening down the hatches against the world outside so that it couldn’t touch them. It suited his reclusive disposition. Sometimes the two of them would go days without leaving the confines of the house, if the storm was really bad. It always made Agatha crazy, claustrophobic, and at the first sign of it letting up she’d throw open all of the windows, much to Dyer’s irritation.

“I’ll see you later,” Dyer said, donning a second jacket.

“See you later.”

It was only once he’d left and closed the door behind him that Agatha realised he had not wished her a happy birthday.

 

 

An hour later Rio arrived  — preceded by Azalea, as always, who bounded across the field towards the house with great excitement.

Agatha couldn’t help herself. She rushed out onto the porch and once Rio had climbed the steps she threw her arms around the other woman, holding her tightly and breathing in her distinctive lavender rosemary scent. Rio held her tightly back, and they swayed a little.

“You really missed me this time,” Rio teased, her voice muffled in Agatha’s neck. Her breath tickled and Agatha gave a little involuntary shiver. “Happy birthday, Agatha.”

Agatha drew back. “You remembered?”

Rio looked affronted. “Of course I remembered.” She brandished the basket she was holding, covered in a cloth, which Agatha hadn’t even noticed in her elation. Azalea jumped up onto her hind legs, trying to catch it, and the two of them laughed. “I brought you gifts,” Rio said. “Let’s go inside?”

As Agatha closed the door behind them she felt the chill of the oncoming snowstorm and registered the fact that all signs of animal life had disappeared from their surroundings. But she felt none of the dread she usually did, because when she turned Rio was beaming at her, unloading her basket onto the table. Her hair was windblown and tangled but she didn’t seem to have noticed, or simply didn’t care.

“I brought you eggs, because I know you haven’t had any recently.” Rio’s childlike enthusiasm was infectious. “And honey.”

“Aren’t you supposed to let me discover for myself what my gifts are?” Agatha teased.

“Oh. Yes. Sorry.” Rio seemed genuinely apologetic, like a kicked puppy, and Agatha didn’t like how the excitement had died in her eyes.

“No, no, Rio, I didn’t mean it. Thank you so much. It’s so kind of you.”

“There’s something else too,” Rio said, handing her something rectangular and heavy, covered in cloth.

Agatha unwrapped it slowly and her eyes widened. “You brought me an atlas?”

Rio looked shy. “You said you’d wanted one for a while.”

This was an understatement. One afternoon over the laundry Agatha had spent about an hour waxing lyrical about her desire for an atlas, and her lack of her own money to spend on one. “My self-education seems the only way to keep my unhappiness from overwhelming me,” she had said to Rio, and Rio had nodded in understanding.

“Rio, it’s perfect. I don’t know what to say.” Agatha stroked the pages reverentially, following the cartographer’s lines and tracing the illustrations. She really was speechless, both about Rio’s remembering, and the trouble she had surely gone to to save up for and source the gift.

“You don’t have to say anything,” Rio replied. “But I want you to tell me everything that you learn from it. All of the secrets of this far-reaching country.”

“Of course.”

“Can we sit by the fire? My feet are freezing.”

“Let me warm them?” She noticed that Rio had taken off her boots at the door out of respect for Agatha’s clean floor, not that it made a huge amount of difference now that Dyer had tracked mud everywhere.

Once Rio was settled into the chair Agatha knelt by her feet and took one of them in her hand, massaging it between her fingers. Rio gave a small, blissed-out sigh, watching her. As Agatha moved her fingers higher, to the arch of her foot, she seemed to melt.
