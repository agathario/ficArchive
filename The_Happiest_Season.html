Chapter 1

Snow tapped gently against the living room windows, a soft, uneven rhythm that blended with the low hum of the heater and the distant city noise below. It was the kind of winter evening that made the whole world feel muffled, held in place. Inside, the apartment glowed with warm lamplight; golden, low, cozy in a way only Wanda ever managed to make it.

Wanda was curled on the couch, half-buried in a knitted blanket and wearing one of Agatha’s old charcoal sweaters, the sleeves swallowing her hands. Her hair was still slightly damp from her shower, curling at the ends as it dried. She nursed a too-big mug of hot chocolate piled with an objectively irresponsible amount of marshmallows, courtesy of Agatha, who would deny to her grave that she’d made it on purpose.

Across from her, Agatha sat with a book open in her hands, her glasses perched low on her nose. She wasn’t actually reading; her eyes had lingered on the same paragraph for so long it had practically faded from the page. Instead, she watched Wanda between the lines, watched her little soft smiles at the holiday movie flickering on the TV, watched the way she pressed her knee gently against Agatha’s leg as if to make sure she was still there.

Wanda’s bare feet were tucked securely under Agatha’s thigh, warming her through her black leggings. Agatha kept one hand loosely draped across the couch cushion, fingers close to Wanda’s hair. Every few minutes, almost without thinking, she reached out and twirled a curl around her finger; slow, absentminded, affectionate in a way she’d never let anyone else see.

Their home was a patchwork of their tastes, Agatha’s stern dark-wood furniture softened by Wanda’s throw pillows in warm reds and creams; Wanda’s fairy lights draped over Agatha’s bookcase like it needed a little magic. Wanda had snuck a tiny potted pine onto the windowsill last week, and Agatha had grumbled about it until Wanda pressed a kiss to her cheek, after which the tree stayed without further comment.

On the TV, some Christmas rom-com played at low volume; Wanda’s pick, of course. One of the ones where everyone’s problem could be solved if they communicated for five minutes. Agatha had scoffed at it earlier, but now she let it run, flipping a page she definitely hadn’t read.

Wanda shifted, settling more fully against her side, cheek brushing Agatha’s ribs. “You’re not reading,” she murmured, not looking away from the screen.

Agatha snorted. “I am. I’m multitasking.”

“You’ve turned the same page four times.”

“Maybe it’s a very complex page.”

Wanda smiled into her mug, dimples peeking out, and Agatha felt something warm and unfamiliar expand in her chest. She leaned down and pressed a light kiss into Wanda’s hair, quick, almost shy for someone with a reputation for being sharp-edged and impossible.

It was the kind of quiet evening she never thought she’d have in her lifetime, soft, safe, full of gentle things. A room warmed by more than just the heater. A place where she didn’t have to brace herself.

She watched Wanda lick a stray marshmallow from her thumb, her brow furrowed as she focused on the movie. Agatha was hopelessly gone for this woman, she knew it, she felt it, and she didn’t bother hiding it in moments like this.

She reached over, brushing a curl behind Wanda’s ear. “You okay, sweetheart?”

Wanda nodded. “Mmhm. Just comfy.”

Agatha let her fingers drift through her hair again, letting herself sink into the moment, the movie, the warmth, the weight of Wanda’s feet under her leg, the scent of cocoa and pine.

It was calm. It was peaceful. It was perfect.

And in a few minutes, Wanda would shatter it without meaning to.

Agatha felt Wanda shift beside her again. It was small, just a twitch of her thigh, the tightening of her fingers around the mug, but the motion had a pulse of tension behind it that didn’t match the cozy scene around them. Wanda had been doing it all night. Fidgeting. Checking her phone every time the screen lit up. Zoning out in the middle of scenes she usually gasped and giggled at.

Agatha let her eyes drift from the page she wasn’t reading to Wanda’s face. She’d gone soft in the cheeks from the heat of the blanket and the cocoa, but her brows were drawn tight, her lips pressed together like she was mentally rehearsing a speech she absolutely did not want to give.

“Alright,” Agatha finally murmured, snapping her book shut with one hand. “Spit it out, sweetheart. You’re vibrating like a damn tuning fork.”

Wanda’s head jerked toward her, eyes wide, guilty. “What? Nothing! I’m not- I’m fine.”

“That was fast.”

“What was fast?”

“Your lying.” Agatha raised a brow. “Even for you, that was record-breaking.”

Wanda’s shoulders went up to her ears. “I’m not lying.”

“You’re twitching like a squirrel in traffic.”

Wanda made an offended little squeak, burying half her face in the collar of Agatha’s sweater. “I am not twitching like a squirrel.”

Agatha snorted. “You absolutely are.”

She let the teasing hang lightly in the air, hoping Wanda would laugh, roll her eyes, and finally confess whatever was eating her. But Wanda didn’t laugh. She didn’t even smile. Instead she took another sip of hot chocolate, set the mug very carefully on the coffee table, and drew her knees closer to her chest.

Agatha’s stomach pricked with something cold.

Before she could push further, Wanda grabbed the remote and turned the movie down another notch, then another, until it was barely a whisper of sound.

Agatha knew that move. Wanda only turned the volume that low when she was about to say something she wished she could drown out with noise.

She watched Wanda breathe- slow in, slow out- preparing herself. Agatha waited, patient but wary.

Then, just when Wanda seemed ready to speak, she chickened out again and reached for her phone.

That was it.

Agatha reached out and plucked the phone cleanly from her hand.

“Hey!” Wanda protested, but it was weak.

“No more stalling,” Agatha said. “Tell me what’s going on.”

Wanda hesitated, then inhaled sharply. “Okay. So. Funny story-”

Agatha tensed. Oh no. Not a funny story.

“-my dad wants to see you!”

Agatha blinked. “…Why?”

“Well, not you you.” Wanda waved her hands. “Just… you as my friend.”

Agatha’s book slid off her lap and thumped onto the floor.

Wanda winced. “Surprise?”

Agatha stared at her. “Try that again.”

Wanda’s words came out in a single breathless spasm. “So-I-may-have-invited-you-home-for-Christmas.”

Agatha’s entire body went still.

“And,” Wanda continued, already panicking, “I may have-kind of-accidentally-told them you’re my best friend.”

Agatha blinked once. Twice.

Wanda rushed on, her voice rising like she was sprinting downhill with no brakes. “It wasn’t planned! I was on the phone with my mom and she asked if I was bringing anyone this year and I panicked and said yes and then she asked who and I panicked again and then suddenly you were my best friend and then she got excited and then she told my dad and then it was too late to correct it and then-”

“Wanda.”

“-and then Pietro texted me seventeen emojis in a row including a snowman and a winking face and I don’t know what that means but it felt ominous and-”

“Wanda.”

“-and then my dad asked what you like to eat and whether you’re allergic to pine because he bought a new tree and then Lilia asked if you were single because she wants to marry me off before the new year even though she’s like eighty-seven and-”

“Wanda.” Agatha’s voice finally cut through, low and firm.

Wanda clamped her mouth shut. She looked like she was going to cry or combust.

Agatha took a slow, heavy breath. “Start. Over.”

Wanda inhaled like she was about to dive underwater. “I invited you home with me for Christmas.”

Agatha’s pulse throbbed in her throat.

“But…” Wanda squeezed her eyes shut, wincing at the word. “I told them you’re my best friend.”

Agatha swallowed. Hard.

“They don’t know about us,” Wanda whispered. “Not yet. I didn’t tell them.”

Agatha’s chest tightened.

“I was going to!” Wanda added quickly. “I swear I was. I just-my mom asked so many questions and my dad was shouting from the other room about the guest linens and Pietro was FaceTiming me and showing me his kids fighting with wrapping paper tubes and it just happened and I panicked, Aggie, I panicked-”

Agatha felt her vision blur for a second. Not with tears, her eyes didn’t sting, but with something sharp and cold that tunneled her focus.

Her hands went still on the couch.

Her breath stuttered.

Her shoulders tensed, curling inward like she was bracing for impact.

The edges of her sight flickered with ghosts, glimpses, nothing more:

Christmas lights reflecting in a window.

A woman’s shadow, tall and angry, pacing.

A voice like claws: Can’t you do anything right?

The scent of pine and alcohol.

The feeling, raw and familiar, of being a disappointment, a secret, a burden.

Agatha blinked. Once. Twice. Three times. Forced herself back into the room.

Wanda was still talking, still rambling, her voice cracking under the weight of anxiety she didn’t know how to carry.

“They’re already stressed,” Wanda said, twisting her hands in her lap. “I didn’t want to add anything more.”

Agatha felt herself shrinking. Quietly, invisibly.

Wanda kept going, oblivious. “My family just… expects a certain version of me. The one who had perfect grades and joined choir and never dyed her hair and-God-I know how it sounds, I do. But it’s only a few days. Just a few. And once the holiday is over, I’ll tell them everything. I promise.”

Agatha’s voice, when it came, was eerily calm.

“So you want me,” she said slowly, “to play your little closeted roommate act? For Christmas?”

Wanda flinched. “Aggie, it’s not like that.”

“It sounds exactly like that.”

“It’s complicated.”

“It’s cowardice.”

Wanda’s eyes filled instantly, bright with wounded surprise. “That’s not fair.”

“What part of this feels fair to you?” Agatha shot back, but her voice never rose. It stayed level, controlled, dangerously so.

Wanda’s breath hitched. “I’m trying. You know I am.”

Agatha didn’t answer, and the silence between them grew taut enough to snap.

Wanda pressed her palms to her eyes, voice cracking. “I don’t want them to look at me differently.”

Agatha exhaled a shaky breath. She wasn’t angry, not exactly. She was hurt. Deeply. In a way she wished didn’t feel so young, so old, so familiar.

What she heard wasn’t I don’t want them to look at me differently.

What she heard was I don’t want them to know about you.

She leaned back slightly, instinctive self-protection tightening her posture. Her arm withdrew from around Wanda’s shoulders. The warmth faded instantly.

Inside her mind, the old truths whispered:

I hate Christmas.
I hate pretending.
I hate being anyone’s secret.
But she loves her. God, she loves her.

And loving Wanda meant swallowing things she’d rather spit out.

Wanda felt the shift immediately. She looked up, red-eyed, panicked. “Aggie-please. Don’t pull away.”

“I’m not pulling away,” Agatha lied.

Wanda reached for her hands, small, warm fingers curling desperately around hers. “I want you there. I need you there. Please, Aggie. I can’t do Christmas without you.”

Agatha stared down at their joined hands.

Wanda’s thumbs were trembling.

Her eyes were wide, terrified, not of Agatha, but of losing her.

And in that fear, Agatha saw the truth: Wanda wasn’t being malicious. She wasn’t trying to hurt her. She was scared. Cornered by her own family’s expectations, by her own history, by the impossible weight of becoming someone different than the girl they remembered.

Agatha understood that kind of fear. Too well.

She sighed, long and low, the kind that carried years of exhaustion. Wanda watched her with breath held like a child waiting for a verdict.

Finally, Agatha rasped, “...Fine.”

Wanda gasped in relief and surged forward, throwing her arms around her. “Thank you. Thank you, thank you-”

“But,” Agatha cut in, voice still rough, “you owe me. And you owe yourself the truth eventually.”

Wanda nodded frantically. “I know. I know I do. And I will. Someday, I promise.”

Someday.
Not now.
Not soon.

Agatha let her wrap around her like a blanket, let Wanda press desperate kisses to her cheek, let her bury her face in Agatha’s neck.

But over Wanda’s shoulder, Agatha’s eyes drifted to the window, the snow falling in slow spirals, the reflection of the movie lights flickering on the glass. It felt too much like a memory she didn’t want to revisit.

She held Wanda tighter anyway.

Because she loved her.

Because she was needed.

Because she didn’t know how to say no to Wanda when she was trembling.

Wanda pulled back just enough to whisper, soft and earnest, “This is going to be good. You’ll see. They’re going to love you.”

Agatha forced a smile. “What could possibly go wrong?”

Wanda laughed weakly, relieved.

Agatha kept smiling until Wanda rested back against her chest and the room quieted again.

But the smile didn’t reach her eyes.

And deep inside, a familiar ache settled in, a quiet warning she chose, for now, to ignore.

The apartment felt different now.

Not immediately, nothing so dramatic as the lights dimming or the temperature dropping. But something in the air had thinned, like the room had been holding its breath and finally realized it couldn’t anymore.

Wanda felt it first.

She stayed close to Agatha on the couch, their knees still angled toward each other, but the space between them had changed. Where warmth had hummed earlier, soft, lived-in, familiar, there was now a jitter of static. A quiet that wasn’t peaceful. A quiet that was waiting.

Agatha didn’t speak. She’d said yes. She’d meant it. And yet the yes sat heavy on her tongue, bitter around the edges, like a swallowed curse that hadn’t dissolved.

The movie continued to play, its bright holiday cheer suddenly sounding hollow, tinny, too sweet in a way that made Agatha’s teeth ache.

Wanda tugged her blanket tighter around herself. “We… we don’t have to keep watching this.”

Agatha didn’t look away from the TV. “I don’t mind.”

That was true. She didn’t mind. She just didn’t care, either.

Wanda watched her for a long moment, searching, hopeful, then quietly frightened as her searching found nothing.

“Are you mad at me?” Wanda’s voice was small, fragile. The kind of voice that usually made Agatha’s chest soften automatically.

This time, the softness didn’t come right away.

“I said I’d go,” Agatha replied. It wasn’t an answer. They both knew it.

Wanda tucked her feet under herself. She didn’t lean into Agatha this time. She wasn’t sure she was allowed to.

“I didn’t mean to hurt you,” Wanda whispered.

“I know.” And she did. Wanda wasn’t cruel. Wanda wasn’t calculating. Wanda was anxious and conflict-averse and catastrophically earnest. But intention wasn’t the issue tonight, not entirely.

What mattered was the echo inside Agatha. And echoes didn’t care about intentions.

The snow hit the windows harder now, the wind picking up, whistling faintly against the glass. A draft slipped under the sill, brushing against Agatha’s bare ankle.

She rubbed her thumb absently against the spine of her abandoned book.

Wanda swallowed. “You’re retreating.”

Agatha blinked at the TV. “Am I?”

Wanda hesitated. “Yes.”

The word landed between them like something dropped.

Agatha could feel herself doing it, the invisible steps backward. The inward folding. The automatic shutting of internal doors one by one. Her mind trying to protect her heart the way it always had: quietly, ruthlessly, efficiently.

But Wanda wasn’t supposed to be someone she needed to protect herself from.

That was the problem.

“Talk to me,” Wanda whispered. “Please.”

Agatha finally turned. Slowly. As if her neck were stiff or the movement required conscious effort.

Wanda’s eyes were already shining, too honest to hide anything well. “Tell me what’s happening in your head.”

Agatha wanted to. God, she wanted to. The words pressed at her teeth, heavy and frightened: I hate being hidden. I hate feeling like your shame. I hate how familiar this feeling is. I hate that I love you enough to say yes anyway.

But all that came out was:
“It’s been a long night.”

Wanda didn’t look away. “I told you the truth. Even if it came out messy. Even if it was awful. Can you tell me yours?”

Agatha’s jaw clenched.

Her truth wasn’t simple. Her truth was a long, ugly thread that led back years, decades, to rooms where she’d sat small and silent, watching adults pretend nothing was wrong, pretending right along with them because pretending kept the peace.

Christmas had always been the worst. A performance wrapped in tinsel.

She didn’t want to admit any of that. Didn’t want to give those memories power in the present. But Wanda’s gaze stayed steady, gentle, insistent in that quiet way that said she wasn’t trying to force anything, just trying not to lose the person in front of her.

Agatha exhaled through her nose. “I don’t like secrets. That’s all.”

Wanda nodded, even though they both knew that wasn’t all.

“I don’t want you to be a secret,” Wanda said softly.

“No?” Agatha arched a brow just enough to make the point without cruelty.

Wanda winced. “I’m scared.”

That, finally broke something in Agatha. The smallest crack, but a crack nonetheless.

She softened. Visibly. A fraction. “I know.”

Wanda shifted closer, only an inch, but she stopped herself from reaching out again. Agatha could see the restraint trembling in her hands. “I’m not scared of them knowing about you,” she said quietly. “I’m scared of what they’ll do with that information about me. I’m scared they’ll look at me like I’m… wrong.”

Agatha breathed in deeply, her chest tight. She had no fight left, not the sharp kind, anyway.

“You’re not wrong,” she murmured. It was instinctive. It was true.

Wanda’s lips trembled. “But they might think I am.”

“And you’d rather protect them from discomfort than protect me from feeling erased.” It wasn’t an accusation. It was a quiet truth.

Wanda closed her eyes, a tear slipping free. “I’m trying,” she whispered.

“I know.” And Agatha meant that, too.

The wind moaned against the windowpane. A pile of snow slipped from the fire escape above, thumping downward in a dull spray that startled Wanda enough that she flinched. Agatha’s hand moved automatically, just a brush on Wanda’s knee, steadying.

Wanda leaned into the touch like it was gravity.

“Are we okay?” Wanda asked, barely audible.

Agatha didn’t answer right away. She weighed the question. Weighing them. Weighing herself.

Finally, she said, “We will be.”

Wanda’s breath left her in a trembling sigh.

Agatha looked around the apartment then, the dark wood furniture, the soft blankets Wanda insisted on, the cheerful lights that blinked unevenly because Wanda was terrible at checking bulb wires. An hour ago, it had all felt warm. Safe. The little universe they’d built together.

Now it felt like a place holding two heartbeats that were desperately trying to sync back up.

“We’re not done talking about this,” Agatha added gently.

“I know.”

“But not tonight.”

Wanda nodded, exhausted by her own anxiety. “Okay.”

Agatha lifted her arm, slowly, giving Wanda the chance to refuse, which Wanda didn’t. Wanda scooted in, tucking herself small against Agatha’s side.

Agatha held her. Carefully. Thoughtfully. A little stiff, but real.

The quiet returned, but it wasn’t the soft, contented hush from earlier. It was tentative. Bruised. Mending itself in small breaths.

On the TV, the movie’s characters kissed under falling snow, all shining music and uncomplicated joy.

Wanda let out a weak laugh. “That used to feel romantic.”

Agatha made a low noise, half hum, half sigh. “We can still get there.”

Wanda pressed her face into Agatha’s shoulder. “Promise?”

Agatha hesitated for a heartbeat.

Then: “Promise.”

Outside, the snow kept falling.
Inside, the warmth would take longer to return.

But they stayed right where they were.
Together.
Even in the uneasy quiet.

And that was enough. For tonight.

Snow thickened as they left the highway and crossed into Westview, the town unfolding in a string of postcard-perfect details Wanda had always spoken of with a fond, embarrassed smile. It was late December, the kind of cold that sharpened the world to crystal edges. Streetlamps lined the road, each wrapped in garlands so fluffy they looked like they came prepackaged from a craft store. Wreaths hung on every post, every door they passed, some tasteful and some… less so. Hard lefts in red glitter, bows that would blind a person in full sunlight, lights blinking in erratic morse code.

And then, as if Westview wanted to make absolutely sure no visitor missed the point:

 

WELCOME TO WESTVIEW!
 

Painted in red and green and outlined with actual twinkle lights.
At three in the afternoon.

Agatha stared at the sign for a long beat before muttering, “Christ, it’s like a Hallmark movie threw up.”

Wanda let out a strained little laugh, the sound people make when they agree but desperately need you to pretend otherwise. She kept both hands tight on the wheel, eyes forward, posture already shifting. Straighter spine. Tighter jaw. An eagerness that was more muscle memory than genuine excitement.

Agatha noticed it instantly.

Wanda only acted like this around one group of people.

“You okay?” Agatha asked, voice softer than her earlier snark.

“Mm?” Wanda blinked, too quickly. “Oh-yeah. Of course. Just… you know. Home.”

Home.
The word sat strangely between them.

As they drove deeper into town, storefronts appeared: the bakery Wanda had once described as “the place that almost killed Pietro the year he tried to eat an entire challah by himself,” the florist with its lopsided menorah display, the community center putting up a hand-painted banner for the CHRISTMAS TREE LIGHTING & WINTER PARADE!!!- three exclamation marks absolutely mandatory in Westview, apparently.

Agatha watched it all with narrowed eyes, as if bracing for impact. “Can’t believe they didn’t throw in fake snow machines. Really committing to the bit.”

Wanda’s knuckles whitened around the steering wheel. “Actually… they do that. For the parade.”

Agatha groaned. Wanda smiled, but it didn’t reach her eyes.

The farther they drove, the more Wanda transformed, small ways at first, but unmistakable. Agatha had spent enough time cataloging the micro-expressions of the woman she loved to recognize when something was off. Wanda was breathing a little too fast. She kept checking the mirror. She smoothed her hair five times in five minutes.

Family mode.
Onset: rapid.

By the time they turned onto the Maximoff street, a quiet residential road lined with single-family homes and aggressively decorated lawns, Wanda had gone nearly silent.

Agatha’s stomach tightened.

The Maximoff house came into view, large enough to hold entire holidays worth of extended relatives and decorated within an inch of its life. Lights framed every window. A huge wreath hung on the door. The walkway was lined with candy-cane stakes.

The second the car rolled to a stop in the driveway, Wanda inhaled sharply. Agatha reached over and brushed her fingers lightly against Wanda’s knee in reassurance.

Before Wanda could exhale, the front door burst open.

“WANDAAAAA!” Pietro’s voice cracked through the cold like a firecracker.

“Oh God,” Wanda whispered.

Agatha barely had time to push open her door before a blur of silver jacket and too much cologne sprinted across the yard. Wanda yelped as Pietro grabbed her in a crushing bear hug, lifting her off the ground.

“Look at you! Look at you!” Pietro spun her once. “The prodigal twin returns!”

“I saw you two months ago!” Wanda wheezed.

“Long enough.” Pietro only squeezed harder.

Then Iryna Maximoff swept out the door, a whirlwind in a long wool coat and a knitted scarf. “Wanda, darling! Your face! You look tired-are you eating? Is the city feeding you? Pietro, put her down!”

Behind her came Olek, slower but booming, calling out, “Our girl is home!” Two cousins trailed after, carrying a stack of mismatched serving dishes for no apparent reason other than chaos.

Agatha stepped out of the car with the luggage, standing there like an unused prop in the wrong scene. Wanda disappeared beneath a wave of arms, voices, overlapping questions.

“How was the drive?”
“Did you bring winter boots?”
“Did you hear about the new neighbors?”
“Your hair is so long!”
“We made your favorite stew.”

No one noticed Agatha.

Not her leather gloves gripping the suitcase handles.
Not the slightly raised eyebrow she wore as armor.
Not the way she waited, seconds, then nearly a minute, for Wanda to glance her way.

Finally Wanda surfaced from the swarm, breathless, hair disheveled. She looked around, searching, guilty, and spotted Agatha beside the car at last.

“Oh! Um-” Wanda cleared her throat. “Everyone, this is… Agatha.”

The pause stung.
The smile Wanda plastered on after stung worse.

“My friend. From the city.”

Friend.
The word dropped like a stone.

“Ah!” Iryna said, giving Agatha a once-over that was polite but uninterested. “The roommate!” Olek nodded, already turning back to ask Wanda about her work schedule.
One cousin waved awkwardly before stepping between them to hand Wanda another container of food.

Agatha stood still, snow melting slowly into her coat, the bags heavy in her hands.

Unseen again.
Of course.

She didn’t show it. She simply lifted one suitcase in a sarcastic half-wave.

“Hi.”
No one heard her.

Snow was still drifting around Agatha in lazy flakes when Wanda finally peeled herself out of the family knot and hurried to her side. Her cheeks were flushed, part cold, part nerves, and she flashed a smile that was too wide, too bright, too rehearsed. The kind she used when she wanted people to think everything was fine.

“Okay guys, I didn’t properly introduce Agatha,” Wanda tried again, still breathless. “My friend, who’s spending the holidays with us.”

There it was again.
Friend.
Dropped like a casual detail, light as air, heavy as a stone.

Iryna turned politely, offering a thin-lipped smile that didn’t quite reach her eyes. “Oh yes, yes, the roommate. Lovely. Welcome.” She gave Agatha a quick pat on the arm, the kind you give a stranger’s dog, and immediately turned back to Wanda to fuss with her scarf. “You’re freezing! Did you not wear layers? Honestly, Wanda, you know better than this.”

Before Agatha could respond, Pietro cut in. “Wait-this is the Aggie? The one who got Wanda to try yoga and then yelled at the instructor?”

Agatha narrowed her eyes. “He deserved it.”

Pietro barked out a laugh before turning to Wanda, already launching into a story about someone from their childhood who’d apparently gotten divorced again.

A cousin poked his head in, blinking sleepily. “Hey, Wanda. Good to see you. Oh, hey-”
He looked at Agatha, hesitated, then added, “Friend.” And walked back inside.

Someone else immediately asked Wanda about her job. Someone else asked if she brought the cookies she promised. Someone else shouted across the yard asking if the car needed to be moved so Uncle Rudy could park later.

Agatha stood there with her suitcases, watching the choreography play out, feeling the sting of invisibility pulse against her ribs.

She wasn’t unfamiliar with being overlooked. She just hadn’t expected it here, not today, not after the car ride, not after last night’s promise-wrapped wounds.

But Wanda slid so easily into the current that there wasn’t even space for Agatha to step in.

Wanda didn’t correct them.
Didn’t hesitate.
Didn’t even glance at Agatha when someone said roommate.

Agatha clocked it with clinical precision.
Every erasure.
Every half-smile Wanda offered to cover the lie.
Every quick apology smile, Wanda shot her when no one was looking, thin, brittle, gone too quickly.

Agatha swallowed it all down like something sharp.

“Let’s… go inside,” Wanda said finally, touching Agatha’s arm as if that made it better. “You must be freezing.”

Freezing wasn’t the word.
Numb worked better.

The Maximoff house was a sensory assault the moment they stepped in.

“Homey” was the polite descriptor. “Chaotic museum of mismatched furniture” was the truer one.

Every wall was covered: family photos, hand-carved crosses, fading watercolors taped to the fridge, embroidered quotes in languages Agatha didn’t recognize. The living room looked like it had been decorated in layers over thirty years, with nothing ever thrown out.

It smelled like cinnamon and other spices, like stepping into a Christmas store, instead of a family home. Warmth radiated from the kitchen. Voices bounced off each other from every direction, someone arguing about ornaments, someone laughing, someone humming loudly and off-key.

Agatha felt the walls move in around her.
Not in a claustrophobic way, she didn’t fear the space.
She feared… the belonging.

This visit is going to hurt.

Without a word, Rio reached for her hand.

Agatha took it.

They moved together toward the front door, weaving past half-empty glasses and abandoned conversations. The house felt different now, smaller, tighter, like it was already rejecting her.

Just before they reached the door, a voice stopped them.

“I see you chose a booth.”

Agatha froze.

Lilia stood near the entryway, arms crossed loosely, expression unreadable but sharp with knowing. Her gaze flicked between Agatha and Rio’s joined hands.

Rio glanced between them, brow furrowing. “A… booth?”

Agatha met Lilia’s eyes.

Something unspoken passed between them, recognition, maybe. Or approval. Or simply the acknowledgment that some lessons, once learned, couldn’t be unlearned.

“Yeah,” Agatha said evenly. “I did.”

Lilia’s lips twitched. “Good choice.”

That was all she said before stepping aside.

Once they were outside, the cold hit Agatha like a shock. She sucked in a breath, the night air crisp and clean, and only then did she realize how loud her thoughts had been until now.

She started talking immediately.

“Okay, so- I didn’t exactly plan this part,” she said, words tumbling out. “There’s a late train back to the city, I think, or I could grab a hotel, or- hell, I could probably sleep at the station if I had to-”

Rio stopped walking.

Agatha took one more step before realizing she was being pulled back.

Then Rio kissed her.

Not frantic. Not desperate.

Certain.

Agatha froze for half a heartbeat before melting into it, her hands curling into Rio’s coat like it was the only solid thing left in the world. When Rio pulled back, she rested their foreheads together.

“Agatha,” Rio said softly.

Agatha blinked up at her. “Yeah?”

“Come home with me.”

The words were simple. Steady. No pressure wrapped inside them, just an open door.

Agatha searched her face. “Are you… are you sure?”

Rio smiled, something warm and stubborn and impossibly gentle. “I’ve spent the last week trying to convince you that Christmas doesn’t have to suck,” she said. “I’m not giving up now.”

Agatha huffed out a breath that might’ve been a laugh or a sob. “You’re really bad at backing down.”

Rio leaned in, brushing her nose against Agatha’s. “Only when it matters.”

Agatha nodded slowly.

“Okay,” she said. “Okay.”
