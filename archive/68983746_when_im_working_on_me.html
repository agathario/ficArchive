Summary

When Rio was a teenager, life was good. She reached the point of making plans for herself, plans that included her best friend and the girl she'd fallen in love with, Agatha.

One night, after being found kissing, Agatha's mother stormed the house, shoving a shotgun and the very real potential for death in Rio's face. So, Rio did what Rio was best at: she ran.

Two footfalls turned to four pawfalls, leaving her with a new understanding of herself, her abilities, and who she needed to be to escape who she once was. She started over, rebuilt and found a life for herself in some small little corner of the earth.

When a careless mistake redirects the course of her life yet again, the only place Rio knows to run to is the same one she ran away from ten years earlier. Knowing she cannot show her face, she hides in the form of a dog, something that allows her to glimpse into a past that no longer needs her.

Chapter 1: One.

What it was to be seventeen and in love.

It was refreshing, it was vitalizing, it was… beautiful.

Rio Vidal had known from the very first time sophomore year when she saw those shockingly blue eyes and that sharp and pointed smile that she was going to fall in love. It was immature, it was rooted in wants more than feelings, but she had not been wrong.

Agatha Harkness had been special from the very first moment Rio set sight on her. She was toned and built, lithe but strong in appearance. Those blues were beautiful, but they were set perfectly above pointed cheekbones, ones that bore pink flesh often licked by the briskness of the air on a cool morning. Her nose was defined and pointed in a striking way, but that smile?

That smile could freeze the blood in Rio’s veins in an instant, making her feel like she’d forgotten how to exist in human form.

Maybe she’d been young, they both had, but Rio had known from that very first moment that Agatha Harkness was someone she wanted around.

Their friendship had grown and blossomed over the years, entwining them like wild vines that wrapped and tightened over the course of time. They were adorned in the leaves of their compassion and understanding for one another, bright and green like a fresh spring morning in the hues of a rising sun.

By their last year of high school, they both came to the conclusion that while friendship was great, it was not the only thing stirring between them.

It was just that they lived in the deep south (Belle Rose, Louisiana, to be precise) and in the early 2000’s, there was a mass majority of their community that would have frowned upon such a choice.

Rio’s mother, Pilar, was not one of those people. Her father, had she ever known him, might have been, but the man had been long gone since shortly after Rio’s conception. There was a reason for that, one she never thought she would know.

Agatha’s mother, Evanora, was one of those people.

Where Rio’s mother was loving and nurturing and caring, Evanora was everything but. She was cruel and unkind to Agatha and had been through the duration of their friendship. Often when Rio stayed, Evanora was not necessarily happy about it, but wanted to let off the air that she wanted her daughter to have some kind of a life.

She often made snide and underhanded comments, things that didn’t need to be said, or grumbles about townsfolk that didn’t live how she thought they should.

If she knew that night, at seventeen, that Rio and Agatha laid in bed in tangles of legs and arms and hair and kisses, she would surely have erupted in her anger.

And then, she did know. And she did erupt.

Rio would always remember it, the way Evanora had opened the door, the way she had spotted the two of them and deepened ten shades of red. The anger. The fury. The thrown glass and the shouting. When she had stomped away, Rio pulled on her jacket and slipped into her shoes, but when Evanora returned, shotgun in hand, Rio knew there was nothing else to do other than run.

“I never want to see that disgusting face of yours again, Vidal!” she had spat with venom as she chased Rio out the front door, waving around that gun like it was enough of a warning.

It was.

Seventeen and terrified, Rio knew she could never see Agatha again.

And so she ran.

She ran harder and faster, the wild winds whipping around her as something began to crackle and prickle in the air.

It was in the air.

And then, it was on Rio’s skin. It sank further, into the meat of her muscles and the calcium of her bones. She was running as fast as she thought she could possibly go until…

There was a tearing in her body, a searing pain as something broke apart and then mended together again, but different. Her mind was spinning, racing, but choked by the pain. Throughout it, she did not stop running.

If anything, Rio realized, she was running faster.

And.

She was running on all fours.

Fours that were now padded at the bottom and covered in a dark fur she could not discern in the light of the moon. She was bolting, not just running, to a point where she lost track of where she was. It was a mad dash to nowhere except further away from the Harkness Household and the terrifying woman that would have undoubtedly shot her with buckshot without a second thought.

Rio thought of little more than the terror, the fear, the way she knew Evanora would have killed her simply because she claimed to love her daughter.

Agatha.

Rio hadn’t even gotten the chance to say goodbye, to tell her how she truly felt. Yes, Agatha knew she cared, but Rio had never been able to actually tell her to what extent. She had never used that four letter word, but running through trees and mud and residual puddles from the week’s rain, she knows she’s going to regret it. Maybe for all eternity. Maybe even after she passes on from the mortal coil.

But there were bigger things to be concerned with; like the fact that she was able to run on all fours in a form that felt impossible. She was Rio, tanned and dark-haired with sparkling amber eyes that shone in the sunlight and even in the moonlight, but she wasn’t. She didn’t even know what she looked like right then.

All she knew was that the longer she ran, the more winded she felt. Her mouth had opened and tongue lulled out to the side, jumping and whipping around with each bounding leap as though its home was not within that snouted trap of hers.

By the time she tired out, the sun was beginning to whisper its first breaths over the quiet and still morning. The petals of the flowers did not shake until Rio breezed by them, pawprints left in the mud and dirt. The blades of grass bent to her weight and the trees seemed to make way for her, as if they knew she was coming.

Finally slowing enough to gauge her surroundings, Rio found herself in a town that was not all that dissimilar to the one she had been raised in, the one she had run from.

This town did not have her mother, did not have Agatha, and by relation, did not have Evanora.

It was new.

It was old, but still fresh.

Rio could begin again.

And so, she did.

After her first night of breaking into an abandoned cabin on the edge of the town to steal enough clothing to keep her warm for the following nights, Rio devised a plan.

She lied.

About her age, about her name, about where she was from.

She told people she was a wanderer at the ripe age of nineteen, looking for purpose. A sprightly older woman had shown her kindness, offered her a place to sleep in while she found a job. It was a quaint little room in a dusty but resolute old home, the chipping paint and torn wallpaper offering more comfort than it should have. The room itself was a pale pink – a hideous color – with beige curtains that let too much light in when the sun rose. It smelled like mothballs and old cedar, but Rio hadn’t cared. It was a warm place to sleep.

In a year, she had managed the impossible. By eighteen, she had a job as a bartender at the town haunt and by nineteen, she was managing both that as well as volunteer work at the local fire department. By twenty one, she had gone through every bit of schooling she could (including getting her GED, having never finished at Belle Rose High) and had secured the proper licensing to become an EMT. She still picked up shifts at the bar, having grown to be favored by the locals, but spent a great deal of her time bumping around the city in the back of an ambulance, ready to save what lives she could.

Because she couldn’t save her own.

In the early hours of the morning with twilight still milky and the moon a friendly guiding light, Rio always ran.

She didn’t dress herself in workout clothes or secure sneakers to her feet. No, that was too pedestrian for who she was and what she had become.

Rio would shift; sometimes it was a limber and lean doberman. Sometimes, she took on golden fur and a rounder snout. Her favorite form, though, was the Belgian Malinois with its tanned fur and black crest upon her chest, one that snuck up to mask over her eyes. She wasn’t always in charge of what coloring might have appeared, but she’d been working on it.

When Rio ran, it gave her time to think.

She thought about how she hadn’t spoken to her mother in four years, and Agatha in just as long.

But that did not mean they didn’t cross her mind every day.

She also thought about her father.

Maybe she would never know him, or who he was, but she knew what he was, now, or what he had to have been. Her mother had never braced her for this, never explained that this could be a possibility, that it could be something that happened to her. Rio had come to realize it was because her mother had no clue.

Her mother was no witch; she was merely a mortal who had created a child with a man who had powers beyond her understanding.

They had been beyond Rio’s, too, until that fateful night.

She often wondered if anyone spent time looking for her. Had her mother put up posters and made phone calls? Had Agatha gone to the homes of their friends, hoping Rio was simply crashing on couches? Had Evanora tried to track and hunt her like some kind of prey who needed to be eradicated?

None of it would have mattered; Rio’s new homestead was over a hundred miles from Belle Rose and she wanted to keep it that way.

Rio didn’t want to be found.

Rio wanted to hide, to stay as far away as she could feasibly manage from Belle Rose.

Because she was a coward. She ran from the things that scared her – and even those that did not – because she could not face a life where she had to see Agatha and know she could not have her. She could not walk back into that town with the chance that Evanora was still alive and pulling all of Agatha’s strings, knowing that she could never be what she wanted to be.

Rio wanted to be Agatha’s.

She had been since the moment their eyes first met, but she knew the time had come and passed for that to be possible.

Now, Agatha was probably living her life, away at college somewhere while Rio started IVs and poured drinks. She was probably dating women who realized how gorgeous she was but could not keep up with her wit and intelligence. She was probably at clubs in the nighttime hours, drinking and laughing and partying. After those hours, she was probably being worshipped in the bedroom.

By anyone who wasn’t Rio.

Because Rio had run and left Agatha nothing else to do aside from try to move on. She hadn’t called, hadn’t written, hadn’t even tried to make contact. She was scared, maybe of the idea that Agatha would not want her anymore a little more than the idea of Evanora herself.

The days rolled onward, the years threading through a timeline that Rio knew never could have been predetermined, but fell into routine nonetheless.

Making a life for herself had seemed like an insurmountable task at the age of seventeen, but almost a decade later, Rio could say that she was content. Maybe not quite thoroughly happy. But content.

Her day-to-day life was much the same; daylight hours spent in the back of her ambulance with a rather grumbly and morose coworker who seemed to dislike his life a little more than he disliked his job. His name was Brian, but Rio never used it. Affectionately and in a way that offered little negative response from him, she had dubbed him Eeyore. When she used it, it felt like it was the only time he actually laughed.

And then came another night that would change Rio’s life forever.

It was a mistake.

She had grabbed the wrong vial, hadn’t even considered the weight or how it felt in her hand. She had simply picked it up and jammed the needle in, feeling as though she were about to save a woman’s life.

When she immediately began to choke and sputter, Rio realized something was wrong.

The medication she had given was the medication explicitly named on the woman’s dog tag, a medication she had a vicious allergy to.

By the time the bus stopped and Brian put it in park, the woman was dead and the back doors were already kicked open and Rio was doing what Rio did best:

Rio ran.

She ran long and hard to the point of exhaustion, the point of curling up in the hollow of a decaying tree or two to rest and rebuild her stamina. She drank from clouded ponds and puddles, ate what berries she could and kept moving.

But where did she go?

She could go back to Belle Rose…

It was a terrible idea.

But where else was there?

She had no home, those who had once loved her probably no longer even thought of her. She didn’t have family in the traditional sense of the word. Hell, she could not have even known if her mother was still alive. And yet…

Something like the tug of a thread pulled at her heart, guiding her. She took the long way around, but after days on the move, the moment Rio reached the edge of Belle Rose, she knew it was inevitable.

The first place she found herself was the home her mother had once owned.

It was empty, and a threatening red and white sign that had been aged by the sun and shouted its intent was planted in the yard:

FOR SALE.

It had been there some time, causing Rio’s slightly smaller heart to fragment inside of her chest. She could never have known when her mother left, not without showing her face again and asking questions. Despite being ‘home,’ Rio had no intention of that.

There had been entire weekends over the last decade where she had roamed the forests and countrysides as a canine, using her sense of smell to guide her where she wanted to go. Often it landed her on back porches and at family BBQs where attendees took pity on her and threw her scraps. Also, the taste of kibble – at least in dog form – wasn’t actually all that bad.

Maybe she had been preparing herself for this, for the chance to run away and simply… Let Rio Vidal disappear into nothing more than a memory.

Even with ten years between appearances in her hometown, Rio knew that people would still recognize her and she didn’t want them to. She didn’t want to live amongst them, didn’t want to converse with them or make friends. As she wandered the town, too much of it appeared the same to let her believe its occupants may have changed.

Her next stop?

The Harkness home.

Or… What was left of it.

The grand two story with its white pillars and eggshell paint was just… gone.

Rubble.

Dust.

Dirt.

But for Rio?

Memories.

Gone. Lost. Forgotten.

If the home was gone, so too was Agatha, most likely.

For as much as her heart started to splinter at the idea of a mother long gone, it fully shattered at realizing Agatha was probably living a lavish life in some big city with a massive rock on her finger and a heartstoppingly gorgeous wife who had put it there.

All there was left to do was roam.

Rio wound her way on scuffed paws through the town streets, though alleys and break-offs, hopping fences that were just a smidgen too short. She nosed around dumpsters behind restaurants and grocery stores, hopeful for anything she might be able to find.

Her last meal had been before her shift the day she’d accidentally killed that woman.

She didn’t even know her name.

She was meant to heal, to give a second chance, and all she had done was take.

She took a life. And now, she had to live with it.

Rio would do it as the dog she felt herself to be.

After beguiling a couple of workers at the local chicken shop who were throwing out the day’s leftovers, Rio devoured her three chicken strips and took off for the park, one she knew had a gazebo. It had several benches, perfect for sleeping under.

By morning, no one had come to shoo her off or call animal control; the sleep she managed was nowhere near peaceful and was tainted with nightmares of all that she’d run from in her life. She ran from those who loved her, cared about her. She ran from potential because she was scared. She ran from hatred and anger. And now, she was running from her own failures. She’d had a good life in that little ramshackle town, but it was over.

Rio Vidal was over, gone, a distant thought to any and all who had ever once known her.

Instead, a nameless canine roamed the streets of Belle Rose for two days until a Tuesday afternoon, ripe with potential, opened up in front of her.

She had been haunting one of the more favored alleys when something caught her nose – a breeze of lilac and lavender (ever so faint) mixed with the smell of kibble. With her ears perking up and shifting slightly, Rio let herself follow the scent. It got stronger the closer she got, but then muddied with other odors in the form of cats, dogs, and rodents alike.

Coming around the corner, Rio realized why.

She was behind a veterinarian’s office. The vet herself (clad in a white coat with dusty brown hair tied loosely into a bun at the nape of her neck) was knelt by a dumpster, cleaning something off of the ground. She smelled so good, so familiar almost that Rio wanted to come closer. She wanted to pad forward, to investigate, but something held her in place. She parked her hind end on the concrete, just watching the woman’s back as she tended to her chore for only a moment longer. When she stood and threw away whatever she was collecting into the dumpster, Rio had only needed her profile to know.

She saw those plump cheeks, the defined slope of her nose, a flash of a blue iris.

It came out, even if it was not how it was intended.

‘Agatha!’ she cried.

“BOOF!” was all that sounded instead.

There was a soft scuff of rubber on cement as Agatha jumped, startled by the sudden appearance of a normally aggressive breed of dog practically shouting at her. While it had been unintentional, the reaction was pretty warranted; Rio’s presence was unexpected, as was the sound that had come out of her. It even surprised her, actually.

“Well, hey there, gorgeous,” Agatha cooed at her in that sweet, sweet accent, careful not to move too quickly. “What’cha doin’ out here all alone?” Her tones were smooth like velvet and rich like steeped honey.

In answer, a puff of air blew through Rio’s nose and her head shook as though something had irritated her ears. Really, she was facing an inward battle to maintain the form she was wearing. Hearing Agatha’s warm voice and that deep southern drawl had stirred something within her, something that wanted to make herself known, but as Agatha slowly started to shuffle forward, Rio was hit with the truth.

Agatha didn’t know her, maybe had even forgotten her. It wasn’t as though she could have shown up in town looking like herself, talking like herself, and expected Agatha to hold a conversation with her. That would have been pushing it. Shifting from an animal into her human form right before Agatha’s eyes was… Well, it was a really fucking bad idea.

Rio made another decision.

This time, she didn’t run.

What she did do was lower herself to the ground, legs like soldiers as they outstretched side by side and provided the perfect cradle for her jaw to rest on, her ears dropping only barely. Her big brown eyes were wide and probably too-trusting for a beast of her breed, but she did not wish to scare Agatha away, not when she was getting closer every second.

With a few more careful shuffles, Agatha reached the tips of her untrimmed claws and dropped herself into a squat, though she did not reach outward in an attempt to make contact.

The smell of lilac and lavender, while once faint, hit Rio’s sensitive receptors at full force with the space between them suddenly non-existent. Rio’s little heart began to hammer a little harder, her tail picking up in pace as it thumped at the uneven pavement behind her.

“Well, aren’t you a beaut?” Agatha asked her tenderly, visually running a diagnostic.

On the inside of that head of hers, the head that still belonged to Rio and not the form she wore, she wanted to cry out, to say she wasn’t beautiful, she wasn’t a sight to behold; she was just an ugly coward indulging in a reunion she had no right to. Maybe deceiving Agatha was not the best course of action, but running again did not feel right either.

Instead of shouting this time, a low whine squeaked between sealed teeth and her form dragged through pebbles and small rocks as she scooted forward in encouragement, though Agatha did not meet it immediately. She was looking over Rio’s body, the way her ribs showed just enough to be deemed unhealthy, the way she was covered in dirt with patches of fur that had matted together in the mud. She even craned to get a look toward Rio’s hind legs, making sure there were no visible injuries.

“You’re bein’ awfully friendly for a natural protector.”

It was said like a compliment, but Rio’s response came immediately in her mind.

The only thing I should have protected was you.

Finally, as if Agatha had somehow heard such a thought, a slow and tentative hand raised. Three timorous fingers found the top of Rio’s head, just between her ears, and gave a light scratch.

“Oh, you are pretty, aren’t ya?” Agatha whispered.

Rio’s head lifted, mostly to garner more of the contact, but it caused the most beautiful sound to echo off Agatha’s lips.

She laughed.

It struck Rio in the direct center of her little dog heart, gripping and squeezing and twisting around it in a vice of something familiar but lost. It had been ten impossibly long years since Rio had heard such a delightful noise, a joyous reverberation of things that made Agatha happy. Rio (actual Rio) used to be able to make her giggle and chuckle and howl with such laughter with ease.

It left her wondering…

How many times had Agatha laughed since she’d been gone? Not just a compensatory snicker or a forced chortle, but a real, hearty, true laugh? If she had, why? What had gotten her there? What had given her enough happiness to bestow such a symphony on the world?

Rio would never know because Rio would never ask.

She wasn’t going to run right then, but she knew it to be an inevitability. Rio couldn’t stay, no matter how much this reunion was restoring parts of her that she had thought broken for some time. She could find reprieve in the meeting, but she knew it would not last. The way Agatha was stroking her fur, the way she was looking at her as though she were some kind of rare being that had stumbled too far from home, it was melting Rio in all of the right ways, but it had to be temporary.

It reminded her of the days in bed (at Agatha’s house or her own where her mother was substantially less nosy) when Agatha would run her fingers through Rio’s hair as they laid with their fronts pressed, whispering dreams big and little alike to one another as if they were closely guarded secrets that were only poured into the vacuum they had created between them.

Love wasn’t supposed to be a challenge, but it always had been for the two of them. Now, as Rio watched Agatha with tender and thankful eyes, she knew it was an insurmountable one; Agatha had her life and Rio had no place in it. She was merely a stray, lost to the world, nameless and homeless and destined to roam.

But then Agatha spoke again at the very same time her hand dropped and slightly pointed nails scratched adoringly at Rio’s chin.

“You hungry, sweet girl?” The question hung in the air like the smoke of burning incense between them, thick and warming to an unnamable degree. It was a beguiling, a spell without magic, and Rio wanted to abide.

Agatha didn’t know. Agatha would never know.

She still cared, though.

Rio didn’t know much in those moments about the woman lightly dusting her in affection, but what she did know was that Agatha was taking the time out of her day to tend to a disorderly stray who had turned up outside of her clinic. It was a clinic she had opened for a reason, with intent, even if Rio was not understanding of such reasoning.

Instead of a vocalized bark or huff, Rio pushed to all fours, her tail beginning to swing enthusiastically behind her. Her head lifted, eyes trained on Agatha, alert, thankful, accepting.

She knew she shouldn’t indulge in the whimsy of a little longer in Agatha’s company, yet Rio couldn’t force herself to leave. Not then. Not when Agatha was above her, regal in her work attire that held station in the tiny little town. What she did helped people, in what way she could, and Rio did not doubt the reverence for her that had come as a result.

The one time she probably should have run, she stepped forward instead, her head dropping in request.

“Gimme a minute, okay?” Agatha drawled. She gave another light caress of Rio’s head before she stood and paced into the clinic, the sharp sound of the door slamming shut behind her crisp in Rio’s sensitive ears.

What was she doing? This was so remarkably stupid; she had fucked up enough in life and she had especially fucked up enough in Agatha’s life that she knew leaving was the best option. She knew she should take off and run until her body crumbled to ash and dust, but…

Like magnetization, now she had been drawn to Agatha, she did not want to leave.

She would leave, because she had to.

But if Agatha wanted to give her a bowl of kibble and some fresh water? It was a kindness bestowed upon her that she was utterly undeserving of, but thankful for all the same.

The sounds of little yips and yaps from inside the clinic caused Rio to shift on uncertain paws; she didn’t belong and yet, she had never felt more at home. When the back door sprung to life again and Agatha bustled through it, Rio knew why.

For three years, Agatha had been all that made sense in the world. She’d been the rebuttal to every senseless argument, the defiant one in the face of those who dared challenge them. And then, scared and with her tail literally between her legs, Rio had simply… left her behind.

Agatha Harkness deserved better than that, always would.

But for now… Maybe it wouldn’t be the worst thing in the world to just keep an eye on her from afar.

“Here you go, precious,” Agatha murmured, kneeling again to place a small metal dish of rather high-end kibble. With a quiet clank, another empty one settled on stone, ringing just loud enough to burrow into Rio’s brain, an ear twitching in response. Quickly, it was filled with bottled water which was then capped then stowed, Agatha rising to tower above her again.

“Eat up. Go home, if you have one. I bet your owners are missing a pretty girl like you.”

It was a light command, maybe a knowing one, but Agatha was wrong.

Rio had no owner, no one to belong to.

And no one was missing her.

Before she turned to head back inside, Agatha hesitated above her, interested but critically sharp blue eyes looking her over one more time in concern. She found nothing different; the same gaunt form, the lack of collar, the gentleness in those thankful eyes. And then, Rio was alone in the alley and thankful for the meal.

It took her little time to clean the bowl and even less time to decide that she was not going anywhere. In fact, when she found a piece of pressed cardboard a few feet from the dumpster she had first seen Agatha by, it looked to be the perfect place for a nap.

She was right.

She slept well, better than she had since the accident. It was only for a few hours, but the sweltering afternoon heat began to simmer into an evening mist, still hot and heavy as it hung over the dwindling hours. The only reason Rio even woke up was because the back door to the clinic slammed and she was suddenly aware of heavy footsteps over the asphalt around her.

“You’re not very good at listenin’ are ya?” Agatha stood, hands on her hips, but lacking her white coat this time. She had on a light mauve button up, sleeves rolled and top three buttons undone. More of her hair had fallen out of the loose bun she’d tied it into and she looked frazzled after a long day’s work.

Rio’s soft eyes raised to her, her head lifting as she rolled to her belly and climbed to her feet, padding toward where Agatha was standing in her indecision. Her rear end plopped down and her mouth fell open, tongue lolling out in contentment as she stared up at the woman expectantly.

A breath of a huff passed through Agatha’s nose, her weight shifting to one leg and her lips pressed. She looked like she was thinking, like she was trying to make a choice that she did not know she should be making.

The choice was made the moment she spoke.

“Come on, then,” she coaxed, clicking her tongue softly to pull Rio’s attention. It was an unspoken invitation of comfort, if only for a night. Rio stood again, following her obediently and wishing she could offer her gratitude through any possible means.

But she was just a dog, an animal. She had no voice. She had no reason to speak or to be present.

She trotted along, nails clacking quietly on linoleum as Agatha led her to a small room (filled with only two sinks and a large steel table… and one rather large and notably vacant pet bed) and clicked the gate shut behind them. She was quiet, save for a few notes hummed off-key to a song Rio didn’t even recognize, but she worked with precision as she located more food in a cabinet.

With another pairing of kibble and fresh water set beside the cabinet, Agatha leveled again, her eyes dropping down to the new neighborhood nuisance.

“Y’can sleep here tonight, but I’m gonna check ya for a microchip tomorrow. Someone’s probably missing you and we should try to get ya back home.” Agatha paused, arms folding sternly over her chest.

The moment Rio slinked right up to her side, plopping down dutifully next to her, she all but melted. With a resigned sigh and the tiniest twitch of a smile, she offered one last warning. “For the love of Christ, please, please don’t destroy anything. I know you won’t listen, but if you could keep the damages down to a minimum? Fantastic.”

The way that accent floated over her words, it made Rio’s heart flutter, fond with memory. It had not been so strong when they had been younger, but it had been over a decade for it to deepen in its drawl. Rio decided, already, that she loved it even more than she ever had all those years ago.

She, of course, had one of her own, but it was nowhere near as thick and sweet as the one Agatha had taken to. It warmed her, in some undeniably distant way.

“I’ll leave one of the lights on. Y’know, in case you’re one of them that gets scared in the dark.”

The teasing, the gentle soothing, the light one-sided conversation… It was clearly how Agatha got through her days. She spent her time in the company of animals, beings without true voice, and yet, she still cared. She didn’t know if they actually understood her, if they could make sense of the soothing tones she tried to use, but… Rio could hear that she still cared.

Without another word, two of the three lights shut off in the room and Agatha made her exit, the sounds of locking clicking filling the air before she exited through the front doors and left Rio alone, but not alone.

She was surrounded by smells and sounds and sudden emotions that she could not regulate, and yet, she only focused on one:

Lilac and lavender as the remaining mists of it lingered in the room around her.

It offered peace and allowed her to accept a new reality. It was the only reality that mattered, one where she had found Agatha, Agatha was safe, and she could milk her time just a little longer in hopes that some pieces of her might start to heal in ways she never could have expected otherwise.

With hope pulsing in that small little heart in her hollowed ribcage, Rio found peace enough to drift off, allowing her bones to rest for the first time in days. 

 

Chapter 2: Two.

The next day, when Agatha hoisted Rio up onto the table to do as she promised and check for a microchip, something in Rio broke.

She knew it made no sense, knew it was stupid, but that didn’t stop it from happening.

To be held by Agatha, in whatever capacity, was something Rio never thought she could have again in her life. She felt destined to loneliness and isolation. It was why she was living the way she was, why she was making the active choice to hide from anyone and everyone who might know her or recognize her.

When it came down to it, Rio Vidal was little more than a spineless coward who could do nothing but run away from the consequences of her actions. She got scared and she ran. She made a mistake and she ran.

Calling what she did a mistake was a disservice to the woman’s life she took.

Rio was not a careless person. She had worked hard to get her license, to serve the town she lived in by saving what lives she could. She was committed to her cause, driven by her need to make some kind of a difference after leaving her old life behind.

In that one moment, she was careless. Now, it was going to chase her for the rest of her life. The likelihood of outrunning it was higher on four paws than it was too feet; if Rio needed to leave at any point, being in form was the only way to do it. What was more was the fact that she could never even dare to dream of showing her face in Belle Rose after so long gone. Someone would see her, would tell her mother if she was even still alive.

Rio couldn’t risk that. Her mama had moved on with her life, found better things to fill her time. No matter the potential she might have had at the time, Rio only ever could have been a disappointment.

So Rio would stay a dog so long as she was in Belle Rose.

A dog that, by her third day with the freedom to roam the clinic, was finally given a name.

“You’ve been walking around here like you own the place,” Agatha told her, staring her in the eyes as her tail thumped at the ground happily. In her hand was an apple slice, her third in a row that she had not offered to Rio. As she took a bite and chewed thoughtfully, it seemed like she was weighing something heavily in her mind.

“You need a name.”

Rio’s heart faltered in her chest, her ears dropping in a docile manner while her tail thumped a little harder at the floor. Agatha wanted to name her. Having absolutely no clue who she was, Agatha wanted her to stick around and wanted it to mean something.

With the tiniest shuffle of her paws, Rio inched forward but was unable to keep her attention from shifting to the apple, practically drooling at the thought of tasting one for the first time in weeks.

“You like the good stuff, do ya?” Agatha drawled, leaning forward as she surveyed the apple between her fingers. “I really need to restock on peanut butter. You’d eat that up, wouldn’t ya?” She lowered her hand, nodding her permission toward the piece of fruit in a way that excited Rio exponentially.

With her ears perking back up, Rio gently craned her neck forward, her hackles raising just enough to politely and gently remove the piece of apple from Agatha’s fingertips. The moment it was fully within the confines of her bite, she simply devoured it, chomping happily with a series of very, very loud crunches.

“Did ya know some flowers sprouted in the alley where you were nappin’ the other day?” It was a rhetorical question, obviously, but Rio looked up at Agatha in interest anyway, her head tilting to the side to inform her she was listening. “I think you’re a Demeter in disguise. Just makin’ things grow wherever you go.”

As Rio watched her, a bright light began to shine in Agatha’s eyes and the smile she wore was almost even brighter. She looked truly amused and completely thrilled with her own thought process.

“That settles it, then, doesn’t it? If you’re gonna keep hangin’ around, you have to have a name. So… Demeter it is. Or… Do you like Deme better?”

At the gentle nickname, Rio hunkered down with her paws sliding on the tile floor as she huffed her approval. Pushing herself back up, her mouth fell open, leaving her tongue lolled out to the side in a perfect pup-like smile.

Agatha had given her a name. She gave her a name because she wanted her to stay.

When she reached out to scratch beneath Rio’s chin, Rio chose to do something she probably shouldn’t have.

She licked at Agatha’s hand.

For a few days, Rio hadn’t been affectionate in any dog like manner. She listened to commands, she kept herself busy by pacing around (the first day it had just been the room Agatha kept her in, but by the second, Agatha let her roam around the rest of the clinic even if she still slept in the clean room at night) and she sat or laid at Agatha’s side when she was at her desk. She wasn’t a nuisance and if anything, she wanted to prove she could be worth keeping close by. It seemed like Agatha agreed with that.

In response to the overt show of affection, the most wonderful thing happened.

Agatha laughed.

It was such a beautiful sound, such a joyous noise that Rio felt like she could cry. She’d heard Agatha chuckle many times since she’d shown up but the last time she heard Agatha laugh had been a decade ago. Hearing it again for the first time in so long felt like an awakening, like Agatha had completely reached into her chest and graced her heart with a feather light touch.

It felt like being seventeen again, like laying in bed with their noses bumping and Rio telling the stupidest jokes known to mankind.

“What do you call a secret group of llamas?” she asked, tracing her fingertip down the length of Agatha’s nose.

“What?” Agatha asked, already rolling her eyes.

“The illamanati.”

“Rio, you’re so fucking stupid.”

And then she laughed.

But they weren’t seventeen anymore. They were verging on thirty and Agatha had an entire life that was doing just fine without Rio in it.

Rio was entirely discounting the part where it might have been a little better now that Demeter was part of the picture.

Because she was just a dog. She was just a dog that didn’t have a home, didn’t have anyone that would call her theirs. Now, at least, she had a name. She wasn’t just… the dog. She was someone. Something. Meant a little bit to someone who knew not enough. But that was okay, it was how it needed to be for both of them to make it through.

Rio knew the truth: it wouldn’t last. She would enjoy it while she could, but Rio knew that her taking off again was inevitable. It was the life she had resigned herself to and she was okay with it. She had to be. There really was no other option.

She’d made a complete and utter mess of her life and nothing was going to change that. She was too pitiful to take accountability and spend years in prison for her mistake, so the rest of her life could know no more than fur and paws and kibble and water bowls. She was a dog in all senses of the word, but maintaining the physical aspect was something she knew she would do for the rest of her life. Maybe there would be times to sneak back into who she used to be (to grab a cheeseburger or maybe go for a swim) but overall, Rio needed to hide.

As it turned out, being able to hide while still being seen felt better than she thought it might.

When Agatha turned back to her desk, Rio was ready to depart, but something drew her attention. Agatha’s hand dropped low, fully extended, and pinched in her fingers was another apple slice.

“Take it and go lay down, hm?” she hummed in encouragement, waving it in front of Rio’s nose. Happily and with another doggish smile, Rio did as she was told and plucked the slice from Agatha’s grasp, padding over to the big bed Agatha had moved out into the main room for her in order to eat it.

Some could have said the life of a dog was an easy one and honestly, Rio could agree with them. She lounged around the clinic all day, paced and sniffed around, and then repeated the process. She rather enjoyed watching from her bed in the corner as people filtered in and out, dropping off and picking up their animals. A couple of faces Rio recognized, but most of them she did not.

It was truly amazing how severely things could change over time. It was amazing that the town she had barely remembered as a teenager had so many new shops, so many closed down, and too many new faces for Rio to even count. Life had moved on without her, and so had Agatha’s. She seemed happy when she was in the clinic, when she was baby-talking the puppies or the kittens. She was friendly with everyone, though Rio could practically smell it when she got agitated with a particularly difficult patron in a way that caused her heart to beat a little harder and her skin to put off a little more heat.

Rio noticed every time, mostly because Agatha was all she was actually able to focus on. Sleeping was nice, wandering around was okay, but when Rio was tuned into Agatha, nothing else really mattered.

A little before quitting time, the bell over the door jingled and the strong scent of something flowery breezed in, causing Rio’s ears to perk a little as she lifted her head from her spot laid in her designated bed. A tall, lithe woman wearing a pink blouse and skinny jeans practically floated in with a wide smile on her lips.

“Hello, Jennifer,” Agatha offered with a smile on her own. It was a little… less genuine than Jennifer’s, but that was because it was half-forced.

“Bad day, Harkness?” she questioned, tilting her head as she placed both palms flatly on the counter, leaning over it enough to peer down at whatever Agatha was working on. The woman wasn’t threatening in any way and seemed more like an annoyance than anything else, but the nosiness compelled Rio to move.

She pushed to her paws and slinked her way past where Jennifer stood, winding around the corner to plop her hind end right next to Agatha’s chair. Settled, Rio’s black eyes lifted upward, fixing on Jennifer in the most menacing way she could manage.

“Did you get yourself a dog?” she questioned, glancing from Rio to Agatha.

Agatha waved a hand and leaned back in her chair. “She’s a stray. Well… Was. She doesn’t seem to wanna leave. So I guess… she belongs to the clinic now.” Agatha picked up a pen and scribbled something down on a small pad of paper before tearing it off. “I suppose you’re here for this, so here ya go.”

“It’s almost like you’re psychic or something.”

“You called at nine this morning.”

“Fair point.”

Jen’s eyes dropped to Rio again, her lips pressing together while she cooked up her question. It was like she was trying to size her up, to make some kind of judgement about her. Fuck if that wasn’t a feeling Rio was used to, both in and out of dog form.

“You think it’s a good idea to keep a dog like that around with no lead?” Even though it seemed like her inquiry came from genuine concern, Rio decided right then that liking Jennifer is off the table.

She has been nothing but a good dog. Would never bite a soul. Barely barked.

But she sure could huff, and that’s exactly what she did before Agatha could answer. It was her way of voicing her disapproval, of making a statement that she was right there and Jennifer didn’t need to talk about her that way.

In surprise, Jen’s eyebrows shot up as her gaze snapped to Rio in both mild alarm and immediate curiosity.

“Agatha, can your dog understand English?” The way she asked it, it was like she knew it was an insane question to ask, but all Agatha did was laugh.

“She is pretty good at following orders,” Agatha commented, popping off the lid of the container that had been sitting on her desk. “I just think she’s food motivated.”

Hearing the word food, Rio’s canine instincts overwhelmed her, her head lifting as her nose twitched with her sniffs. Agatha opened up the apples again and damn it if Rio couldn’t help but want another. Her tail began to wag, thudding quietly into the ground as she scooted the slightest bit closer to the chair in anticipation of her treat.

The moment Agatha held it low enough, Rio gingerly pried it from her fingers and lowered herself to the ground to munch on it. She was almost too focused on how damn good it tasted that she barely caught the latter end of Jen’s next question.

“Does she have a name? Or did you name her?”

The grin that instantaneously lit up Agatha’s face as she leaned forward was so impossibly wide that even Rio saw it when she glanced upward.

“Her name is Demeter. But I think she likes Deme a little more,” she gushed, reaching down to rub between Rio’s ears.

“Of course you named your fucking dog Demeter,” Jen huffed, rolling her eyes. She held up the script and offered a tight smile and a nod of her head. “Got what I came for. Thank you. I will see you in six weeks for a refill.”

“You be nice to that cat,” Agatha called after her as she turned for the door.

“That cat’s an asshole!” Jen hollered just before the door closed behind her, leaving the two of them alone again in the quiet of the clinic.

It stayed that way for a little while, just the ever-so-often mew or whimper from the back as the animals sometimes grew restless. Agatha would occasionally dip into the kennel to spend a little bit of time with them, leaving Rio to lay by the door, just… observing.

Agatha was a lot of things; she was beautiful, she was smart, she was kind, she was caring. In those moments, the ones where she bounced a small puppy in her arms or cradled a kitten while she scratched at its belly, Rio saw her for what she had always been: a good fucking person.

Even as teenagers, Rio had known it. Agatha could be a bitch, but she chose to be one for the right reasons. She stood up for the underdog, stepped in when someone was outnumbered. She spoke up when no one else would. Agatha had always been stubborn and loud, but she was precise about how she utilized those skills. It was an entirely new awakening to see her all that time later and realize very little had changed about her. She still had a good heart, still cared for the right reasons. She was smarter than they ever could have been as kids and she used that knowledge to help.

Rio just used hers to hurt. And take human life.

Forgiveness would never come for her. She knew as much to be true. Because of that, she would live this half-life for as long as she needed to, burying her shame as if it were a bone. When she’d run the first time, she wanted to be better than what she felt herself to be when she saw the gun in Agatha’s mother’s hand. That time, she knew that there was no getting better. She’d committed the ultimate crime, had done something she knew she could not take back.

So she had to live with it, for as long as she could. For as big of a coward as Rio was, she wasn’t big enough to take her own life. It was something she’d considered many times, but she didn’t have the guts to follow through.

Wasn’t what she was doing kind of the same thing?

Anyone who knew her could look for her, seek her out, hunt her down and it wouldn’t have ever mattered. No one was ever going to find her like this.

For all intents and purposes, Rio Vidal was dead to the world, never to be heard from again.

And she liked it that way.

The clock ticked on into the evening, the sun sinking lower in the sky. It left orange streams of light pouring in from the front window, dusting the floor of the small lobby area. Just as Agatha got to her feet and announced aloud it was time to lock up (more for Rio’s benefit than her own), there was a jingle above the front door.

Rio’s head lifted from her paws, ears twitching in alert as she surveyed the new arrival.

That was a new face.

It was a face Rio had not seen since she’d arrived in town and judging by the way Agatha’s heart began to race and her fingers gripped at the edge of the desk, it was a very unwanted face.

She was pretty, Rio supposed, with her long blonde hair and her done-up face. The smile she wore, however, was ugly as could be. It was sharp, a snarl almost, and Rio didn’t like it. At the same time Agatha came around her desk, Rio got to her feet and made a line straight to her side.

“Natalie,” Agatha voiced tightly. “What the fuck are you doing here?”

Rio didn’t know who this Natalie was, but the way Agatha said her name made Rio hate her instantly.

“I’ve been trying to reach out, Agatha,” the woman rushed, surging forward.

Agatha stepped back.

Rio stepped in front of her, hackles raised and teeth bared. She didn’t growl, but she made her presence known. She also made it known that if Natalie was going to try to make a physical play for Agatha, she was going to have to get through Rio first.

“Yeah, I fuckin’ know. It’s why I changed my number and thought I’d done enough for ya to catch the hint that I don’t fuckin’ want ya around. You cheated on me! Come the fuck on!” The shrillness of Agatha’s voice caused Rio’s heart to hammer in her chest. The words made it beat so hard it nearly rocketed out of her chest.

This woman cheated on Agatha?

This woman had Agatha in her corner, had her attention, maybe even had her love, and she decided to fuck someone else?

Rio hadn’t bitten anyone in her life but right then, she wanted to. She wanted to sink her teeth into that woman’s leg and cause her to run off screaming for her life. She wanted Natalie to know pain, the same way she’d given it to Agatha. Maybe Agatha wasn’t perfect, maybe she made mistakes, but it didn’t constitute someone she cared for betraying her trust.

It could have been said that Rio didn’t know the newer version of Agatha, but what she had witnessed in her time at the clinic was more than enough for her to know Agatha didn’t deserve something like that and this woman certainly did not deserve even a minute more of Agatha’s time.

“And I’ve been trying to apologize this whole time! But you ran, you hid from me!”

One thing Rio realized instantly was that she hated the sound of Natalie’s voice. She hated it even more when she realized it was once used to tell Agatha sweet nothings, to moan her name, to moan someone else’s name.

The further Rio delved into the thought, the angrier she became. Agatha’s rebuttals weren’t exactly helping her temper that anger, either.

“Don’t ya think that was for a fuckin’ reason?!” Agatha shouted, her face growing deeper in color. “So ya… What? Scoured Google until ya found this place? And thought it was at all a good idea to fuckin’ come here? Are ya insane? This is actually considered stalkin’ in the eyes of the law.”

Nothing Agatha said landed; the woman just stared at her with a dopey look on her face and something akin to shame shimmering in her eyes. She wasn’t going to take ‘no’ for an answer. Rio could already see it.

“Agatha, please –” she attempted, moving to step around Rio to get to Agatha.

That was not going to happen.

Snapping to attention, Rio darted in front of her. That time, when she hunkered down, her ears were pinned back and her hackles raised again. Instead of only showing her teeth like she had in the beginning, Rio did something else.

She growled.

It was a deep, rumbling sound that clearly worked, forcing Natalie to take a step back as the shock immediately appeared all over her face.

“Why don’t you have that mutt on a leash?” she snapped, her eyes dropping to Rio as she took another tentative step back.

“Because she belongs here. You do not.” Agatha’s voice was cold and hard, stating a fact as opposed to an opinion. “Now g’on. And don’t come back.”

Natalie seemed to struggle with such a blatant rejection, her face falling as her lips turned down sharply at the corners in a frown that said her feelings were hurt. Agatha didn’t care and Rio most certainly did not care. This woman clearly came to cause problems and neither of them were going to stand by and let her.

“But Agatha…” She tried it again, her voice sweet but her movements anything but. She attempted to cross the space, but Rio was having none of it. She darted out, this time showing less restraint as she invaded the woman’s personal space.

And then Rio did something she hadn’t done in Agatha’s presence since she’d found her in the alleyway.

Rio barked.

It wasn’t just a bark, it was low and deep and threatening. It was a clear instruction: ‘If you do not leave, I will make you.’

Natalie’s wide eyes snapped to Rio, her heart beginning to thud the same way she’s made Agatha’s thud. She was nervous; good. It meant Rio’s intimidation tactics were working.

Rio never stopped moving, shuffling forward an inch at a time to force Natalie’s hand. She only stopped when the woman’s back pressed to the front door.

“Agatha –”

“I think we’ve been clear. Goodbye, Natalie.”

Rio could see it in the woman’s face, how she didn’t want to leave, how she wanted to stay and argue. That wasn’t ever going to be an option; she had hurt Agatha and Rio was not going to let her stick around to do it more.

So she barked again.

That time, the woman was alarmed enough to turn and leave. When the bell jingled to signify the door was closed, Rio didn’t move. She simply stared through the glass until the woman’s form disappeared around the corner and she was satisfied the threat was gone. Lifting herself from a sitting position to turn around, Rio was a little startled to realize that Agatha was just… gone.

Rio hadn’t heard her move, hadn’t heard a door slam or even one open. However, when she began to scour the clinic, she heard a sound come from the other side of the back door. It was the sound of metal being struck. Quietly, she padded toward the door and plopped down, peering outside.

What met her was the sight of Agatha kicking the dumpster.

“Stupid fucking bitch,” she spat, giving the metal another kick. “Who the fuck does she think she is?” BANG. “Shoulda let Deme eat her alive.”

There was no audience to listen to her, no one there to care about how upset or frustrated she was.

…Or so she thought.

Rio sat at the screen door just watching her, aching for her, wishing she could somehow find a way to help.

But she was a dog and she had no thumbs to open the door.

Even if she wasn’t, seeing her face was probably not on a list of Agatha’s wants anyway. After all, Rio was just another ex. The only difference was that Rio hadn’t been looking for her, she’d just found her by accident anyway.

As she watched Agatha pace and grumble and sort through her anger, Rio realized something.

She couldn’t leave.

For as much of a mistake as it might have been to come back to Belle Rose, Rio knew that running away again, at least any time soon, wasn’t an option. She would never get to love Agatha again the way she ached to, but if she could stand by her side and take on the world, even in the walls of that clinic, she would.

It was a silly notion given that she was a dog, but Rio was firm in her belief that for the time being, she’d found somewhere to rest. It just happened to be a bonus that she could keep an eye on Agatha at the same time.

When Agatha returned inside, Rio skittered away from the door and placed herself dutifully beside her desk. It was her way of saying she wanted to offer a goodbye before Agatha left for the night, her big and soft eyes sweeping up to take her in as she breezed through to gather her things.

Instead of heading straight for the door, Agatha pulled her purse over her shoulder and dropped herself into a squat in front of Rio, looking at her directly. Her blue eyes were so gentle, so full of admiration and respect.

“You didn’t have to do that, ya know,” Agatha scolded softly. In complete contradiction to her words, both of her hands raised to cradle Rio’s face as her thumbs brushed tenderly over her jowls. “You’re a very brave girl. Whoever lost ya must really be missing ya, but… I’m glad you’re here. And if you ever wanna leave, ya know ya can.” One of her hands moved to brush lovingly between her ears. “How about you roam out here tonight? You’ve been real good about not chewin’ things up, so I’m okay with it if ya promise to behave.”

A delighted huff jostled her lips as her head lifted to find more of the contact Agatha was offering. It was her way of both agreeing and managing to show her gratitude.

After shutting most of the lights off and giving Rio the last apple slice, Agatha pulled the door closed behind her and locked it with a soft click. Rio watched from the door as she climbed into her car and drove off, surely to find a bottle of liquor and drink away her anger. It seemed like a very Agatha thing to do.

Rio sat like that for a little while, just watching as nightfall swallowed up the village and the town fell asleep.

Only then did she take her rest, satisfied that she had done her duty to protect someone she cared for.

 

Chapter 3: Three.

Being a clinic dog wasn’t so bad. If anything, it was actually kind of nice. It was a place to lay down at night, only the noise of other animals to be heard. It could be annoying sometimes when the puppies would whine or the kittens would carry on until the early hours of the morning, but it was tolerable.

For the most part, Rio was just happy to have somewhere dry to stay out of the heat.

She was also happy that during business hours, she got to keep Agatha company. Some part of her still felt guilty, but seeing Agatha smile and hearing her hum and watching her work with great care were all simply too much to give up. Rio had missed her for years on end, no matter her own cowardice, and so being able to be so close to her was something she couldn’t take for granted.

Truth be told, Rio knew if she were to magically turn back up in town, there was a good chance Agatha would want nothing to do with her. The idea of it happening was a hard pill to swallow. If she could simply take what she had in the present, it would be enough to get her through having to inevitably leave again.

Agatha wasn’t going to keep her around forever, right?

Rio spent her days mostly tucked in her corner of the clinic, though she would often happily greet patrons who seemed to want her attention, even if they were too scared to approach. She knew her coat made her look intimidating, made people feel as though they had to be wary of her, but the behavior she consistently showcased was nothing short of well-mannered and mild. Only once had she jumped on someone and she felt it justified.

Because it was Jen.

Agatha seemed to like it when Rio gave her a hard time any time she turned up and Rio had just as much fun with it, truth be told. It was a way to get everyone laughing, even if Jen often tried to hide it behind her disgust.

“I’m a cat person for a reason!”

It didn’t matter. Rio liked pestering her all the same.

After about two weeks of wandering around the sterile walls and clean floors, something changed.

Rio noticed it earlier in the morning; it was the way Agatha kept glancing at her or the way she would stare at her for prolonged periods of time. Rio pretended not to notice, lounging instead on her cushy little bed as she half-napped and half-stayed alert to watch for any incoming trouble.

For lunch, she got apples with peanut butter and for a snack later in the day, Agatha snuck her a couple of cucumbers beneath her desk as she talked with someone picking up their dog. She was paying little mind to the other canine, happy for the crunchy snack she was being awarded. Surely, as a vet, Agatha knew certain things were okay in moderation, but Rio was happy for the non-stale kibble. It was a considerate treat and a reminder of the things she could have if she just stopped being such a coward.

Yeah, that was not going to happen any time soon.

For the rest of the afternoon, she mostly lazed around in her bed, getting up every so often to go for a leisurely stroll through the clinic, even back to visit the couple of other animals housed there. It was peaceful, a way for her to keep busy while Agatha bustled about, taking care of one animal or another.

The pained cries as a result of shots or stitches were always hard and higher-pitched, but it was simply proof that Agatha was good at what she was doing. Yes, the animals were in a place they didn’t understand under the care of someone they did not know, but they were safe and well taken care of.

If anyone could attest to that, it was Rio.

Agatha had taken her in, taken care of her, done everything she could to get her back to a decent weight. She kept her clean, brushed her at least once every other day.

Agatha gave a damn.

It was nice.

Because for most of Rio’s life?

It was all too easy for people to not give a damn. Of course, that was her perception of the matter and little more. People did give a damn (like her mother, Agatha, and probably some of the friends she left behind when she ran for Belle Rose) but allowing herself to see it would have been devastating for Rio.

It would have told her how every inch she ran over the last several years was pointless. She simply could have just gone home, to her mother, to her bed, to her life.

But more than Evanora charging at her with the shotgun, Rio learned who she really was. She learned about a part of her that was hidden, that bubbled just beneath the surface for so much of her youth.

Not knowing her father never bothered Rio much, but realizing he was where her powers likely came from, she wished she could have reached out. He might have been able to offer advice, to help her navigate the world as her new self.

She was left to do it alone.

Agatha’s clinic under the guise of a stray dog was where it landed her.

As she laid in the corner, she watched Agatha through one open eye, at her desk as she scribbled something onto a piece of paper and set it down with some cash.

It was probably for that weekend boy.

Okay, the weekend young man.

Billy, Rio thought to be his name.

Agatha got to her feet and paced over to a storage closet, causing Rio’s head to lift and then tilt as she watched with gentle curiosity. Agatha never usually went to that closet at closing time; it was where she kept things like harnesses and leads and extra food stock.

When she stepped out again, there were two things in her hands.

A harness and a leash.

Was she taking Rio out?! Was she finally going to get a chance to explore the world a little more by Agatha’s side?!

“Hey hey, come here sweet girl,” Agatha called to her as she strode over to sit in her chair again.

Without hesitating, Rio pushed to her feet and trotted across the space between them, curious but a little excited all the same. That excitement only doubled the moment Agatha spoke again.

“Wanna go for a ride?”

She sounded so sweet, so damn kind, and like she really meant it.

Was she taking Rio home? Even if that wasn’t the case, she was being offered an opportunity for a brief escape.

So impossibly excited, Rio could not contain herself. Her tail began to wag immediately and she actually barked. It wasn’t an aggressive sound, but one that showed her joy and even almost turned into a whine at the end in regards to how badly she wanted it.
