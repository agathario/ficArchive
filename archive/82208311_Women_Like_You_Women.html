Chapter 1: Six Feet Under / Beacon Hill

Agatha blinked. "What?"

Speechlessness. It was almost impressive, the sensation of it, like cotton dissolving on her tongue. She watched with gritted teeth as Jen typed away at her computer like she hadn't just told a joke without the punchline.

"I said-"

"Oh I heard what you said. And now you're going to say something different before I fire you."

Agatha crossed her arms tightly across her chest and leaned back in her chair. Her glare was a needle to the eye, but the woman on the other side of the desk didn't even twitch.

Jennifer Kale was the only literary agent on the East Coast Agatha hadn't scared away with either her actions, her words, or her reputation alone. She stood her ground with the calmness of someone whose fear had at some point faded into background noise.

Agatha, on the other hand, sat at the pinnacle of Jen's client list ever since the agent's more creative early business decisions had come back to bite her in the ass. They'd bonded over their ambition, rude comments, and a begrudging respect for each other's resourcefulness.

Jen sighed, soft and unsurprised. Her manicured nails tapped on the glass surface of the desk. Her face was perfectly corporate; expressionless. The frills on the collar of her blouse didn't make her any less lacerating.

"Agatha-"

But Agatha wasn't interested in explanations. Not now, not ever. She held up her hand, stood, and started pacing.

The spotless beige carpet of Jen's office dulled the stab of her heels. The sound of her steps blurred with the pulse in her wrists and the drum of rain against the window panes. On sunny days, the pastel colored crystals and vases on the sills cast light reflections through the room. But that afternoon, the sky pulled a brooding bitch-face of clouds.

She could already smell the thunder.

"Agatha." Jen said again. "You're a cold-hearted, conniving, catty witch. I like that about you. The public does not. And the public's opinion of you has a direct impact on both of our bank accounts."

"Fuck you. I'm a writer, not a clown. I don't owe them a performance."

Writing was Agatha's home turf. There, she made the rules of the game and still got to bend and break them as she pleased. In her stories, she held the strings of the plot in one hand and her readers' sanity in the other.

Stories were loyal, people unpredictable. That was hardly a hot take coming from someone like her.

She may have snapped at fans on the street once or twice, maybe twenty times. It wasn't her fault those people thought they knew her just because they were literate enough to pick up her work. She was just trying to live her life, to protect her privacy.

So yes, fuck Jennifer for reminding her of her social deficiencies. Kale could discuss them with Agatha's mother if they bothered her so much. Strangle the problem by the root.

"We agreed that wasn't good for our professional relationship." Reminded the other woman, unaware of the morbid turn Agatha's thoughts had taken.

There was that, too. No casual sex with the agent. Agatha's life really was a funeral. She stopped her steps and let herself fall onto a plush little sofa by the office door. Her legs dangled over the armrest; her hair was fanning around her head.

Ophelia drowning in a river of bullshit.

"So what if I'm not likeable?" She asked the ceiling. "I'm an exceptional writer. You should remind the public of that instead of trying to marry me off to TikTok's favorite tradwife for PR!"

"I want you to date Instagram's favorite baker for PR." Jen corrected her with the patience of an elementary school teacher. "Think about it, Aggs! She needs to officially come out and get rid of the tradwife rumors. You need relatability points with the public to promote your new book. It makes total sense. Two birds, one stone and all that."

Agatha was too busy drowning to answer.

Her private life was hers. She had done a damn good job of keeping him away from the press, and the press away from him, for the past eight years. And now Jennifer-know-it-all-Kale wanted her to play house with a stranger on her arm like this was all fun and games.

"Don't you at least want to know more about her before deciding?" Her agent coaxed. Agatha could hear her typing again. Like this was just another meeting.

"I know you want to tell me. And I can't be assed to stop you."

That was invitation enough.

"Well, she goes by the username live-love-loaf, and as of now she has 6 million followers across platforms. I've seen her stats. Her brand's a masterpiece. She targets an audience of everyone from teens to their grandmothers. All genders, all tax brackets. And all that just with bread recipes and good vibes."

"So she's a sourdough starter lesbian. How original." Agatha had dated one of those, years ago. And look how that turned out.

"She is the sourdough starter lesbian. The Internet's darling. The Bob Ross of baked goods. Also, she's really hot. In that alt cottagecore kind of way I know you go for."

Agatha had no idea what 'cottagecore' meant. Nothing good, probably. Which did sound like her type. She scoffed, turning her head on the sofa until Jen's skirt-clad legs and delicate ankle boots came into view:

"Don't be so full of yourself. You don't know me."

"I literally know your social security number. I'm your only emergency contact. You showed me the weird mole on your back last week. That was really gross by the way, you should get that checked out ASAP."

"What's her name? The real one, not the tacky home decor handle she uses online."

"Rio Vidal," Jen practically sang.

It was pretty. Lyrical. And there was a metaphor there that made the skin under Agatha's nails itch.

"And you're sure she's even single and up for this?" By the sound of it this Rio didn't actually need Agatha. Didn't people these days come out by posting a flag in their bio and moving on with their lives? That alone should diffuse any tradwife allegations in Agatha's mind.

"Definitely single, and her manager sounded very enthusiastic when I called."

"That could be your delusions making you tone deaf. Talk to your shrink recently?" She waved a hand in front of her face for emphasis.

"Look who's talking. At least I'm going to therapy. Anyway, Alice — that's the manager — said Rio is constantly being cyber-harrassed by creeps because her content makes her look like their personal dream wife. Though from what I hear she's anything but."

Well, that sounded messed up. Agatha would have hunted those guys down with a phonebook instead of getting into a fake relationship. She would've called their mothers and told them exactly what their sons were up to in their free time. But she could appreciate a bit of calculated psychological warfare.

Still, she couldn't help biting a little:

"How do you know I don't want a perfect dream wife?"

"I've seen your dating history, Agatha." Jen appeared too sharp for a woman dressed entirely in baby pink. She leaned over the table on her elbows. "We both know you like the bad boys."

"I hate you." Agatha rolled her eyes and dug her fingernails into the cushions.

"So you'll do it?" Jen asked when she didn't say anything else. Like Agatha had a choice if she wanted Professional Working Goddesses to be as successful as its predecessors. Which she did. Desperately. It was her best work yet, and she deserved the recognition.

"I'll meet with her." Agatha scrunched her nose. "But I make no promises."

Agatha came home late that evening.

Putting away the groceries and changing her professional clothes into something softer stole some time, but it never took Agatha long to end up right where she belonged:

Six feet under Beacon Hill and squinting at her computer screen.

The moment her butt touched the smooth leather of the chair, the squall in her chest was bottled up and put away. Time was faster here, barely noticable; like it couldn't quite wrap its greedy fingers around this quiet patch of a place.

She would need to turn the lamps on soon. The light from the two small basement windows had changed from white to yellow to orange in the past hour. But for now she was too settled to move.

Originally built as servant quarters, the cellarage under her typical Boston Brownstone had low ceilings (old), cherry wood floors (new), and shivery air (perpetual). Among Agatha's newer additions were a soft rug to dig her toes in and paintings of all the places she'd never visited.

But the true centerpiece of the room was the antique desk. She had picked it up from the street curb when she was nineteen, on her own and directionless — but just crafty enough to bring the piece of furniture back to life.

Every sentence she wrote that was worth a damn was born here.

The few outsiders she'd ever allowed inside her office were surprised to see how few books it held, especially in contrast to the rest of the house that was more library than living space. But when working, Agatha liked to surround herself with only the best. Woolf, and Tartt, and Poe. Alongside her own novels, of course; they were carefully displayed in the corner of the desk.

Today, there were no outsiders here. Two artists, a mother and a son, sat in comfortable silence.

Agatha watched Nicky over the edge of her desktop screen. He sat curled in an armchair, scribbling away in a notebook with the Batman logo on the cover. Personally, Agatha preferred Poison Ivy. But she knew better than to try and argue the pros and cons of comic book characters with her eight year old.

Despite all her success, he was her masterpiece. The one good thing Agatha had ever made.

No other work had caused her more joy and more pain than him. The pain had come first, in the moment the midwife had laid him into her arms with the warning that he would not survive.

Of course he proved them wrong. Nicky was hers. He could survive anything. And these days only his below average immune system was a reminder of the weeks she'd spent at the hospital, holding his too-small hand in her clammy one.

After that, the joy only tasted sweeter. Agatha hadn't even known, before him, that she could be happy like that.

"Use a tissue, not your sleeve." She scolded lightly at her son's sniffles. She didn't need to be a clairvoyant to know there was another cold in his future.

"But the tissues are so far away, mama. And I don't want my words to run away." Nicky argued, biting the end of his pencil.

Agatha rolled her eyes, dug a pack of tissues from a drawer, and put them on the arm of his chair.

"Thank you."

'My words are going to run away from me' was a common saying in their house. Something Agatha had made up to explain why she couldn't look at his Lego towers every five minutes when he was younger. Hers as he was, he had long since learned to use it against her.

"Are you working on a new song?" she asked.

"Yes. But it's a secret." He pressed his notebook against his chest. "Ms. Lilia said she'll help me with the melody next week. I'll play it for you when it's finished."

It was one of the great ironies of her life. When Agatha had been his age, she had to be dragged to her piano tutor kicking and screaming. And here was Nicky, insisting it was the best thing in the whole world.

"I can't wait to hear it."

Hours later, after Agatha had thrown together a semi-healthy and mostly edible dinner, Nicky was safely tucked into bed. He had insisted she read three chapters of The Hobbit, and Agatha had haggled him down to two.

By the time she returned to her office, the day had run all out of light. Agatha hit the switch on her way through the door.

She loved having Nicky here. Sometimes she liked to just listen to him breathe. But she also knew she only got work done when she was alone. The best she did at night when all the responsibilities of the day fell away and she could concentrate on nothing but being alone with her words.

Only she wasn't alone. The idea of Rio Vidal haunted the room like heavy perfume. Like the ghosts of the servants who slept, and dreamed, and died down here in ye olden days.

The whole thing was batshit insane, of course. Fake dating someone for PR. Agatha snorted. It was the premise of a bad romance novel. Something painfully enemies-to-lovers in which the heroes inevitably fell in love and overcame all their childhood trauma.

She poured herself a glass of wine from the cabinet. Agatha wasn't a hero. No one who'd ever loved her hadn't regretted it.

Not that she expected there to be any falling in love in the first place. If this Rio had agreed to date a stranger for her own gain, then she was probably the sort who locked her heart in the freezer every morning before going out the door.

Agatha could respect that.

Rio Vidal, she mouthed into the silence. It wasn't a name she'd heard before. Agatha didn't spend more time on social media than necessary. She didn't even have a private account. Only the one she and Jen used to promote Agatha's books.

She was curious. That was a solid cornerstone of her personality. Still, Agatha refused to look Rio up. She may have stared at the search bar of her internet browser for a while, fingers hovering over keys, but she didn't type in the name.

She was a forty year old woman and would not stalk someone she was supposed to date on social media like a teenager with a crush.

Agatha was a writer; she would write. Or stare at a blank page the whole night.

It was a gamble worthy of a table in Vegas. Either she would end up putting down six thousand words before dawn. Or she'd stress snack the whole night and look up 'How to write a novel' on YouTube. This information she would take to the grave (and all the way down to hell).

After half an hour and a Snickers bar, the fuzzy idea of Rio was replaced not by progress but with Jen's very clear and extremely annoying voice cartwheeling around in her brain.

'Look, Aggs: I hate you like a sister. And I know you'd rather drink bleach than feel vulnerable in any way. Since you refuse to see a therapist, that's for you to deal with. But. do. not. mess. this. up. Give Rio a chance.'

She had scoffed at that, claiming it sounded like 'Lie back and think of England'.

'You don't have to sleep with her! But knowing what she looks like and how you are that won't be the problem. Just don't hurt her feelings. Because if she ends up hating you, she can make the public tear your reputation apart for good. You will be unmarketable.'

Agatha didn't give a monkey's what the public thought about her. Her work spoke for itself. At least to those with enough brain cells to rub together. She had left her mark in the world, and no one could take that away from her.

'In your academic circles you're the most popular person in the room. The smartest, the most cultured, the wittiest. Just how you like it. But out here, in the real world where us regular people live? Where most of your readers live? Here Rio Vidal has all the power. And I really, really need you to get that into your thick skull.'

Agatha ripped open a bag of cheese puffs: "We'll see who has all the power."

"I'll do it." Rio chewed on a fry and wiped her greasy fingers on a questionable napkin.

She and Alice sat at the bar of The Green Witch, a hole-in-the-wall restaurant with sticky floors, smoke-choked air, and patrons so far from Rio's target demographic they could only be found in here and on Facebook. It sold beer from the tap and fast food so divine, it was worth the risk of heart failure. Behind the hum of conversation, a stereo played.

Billy Joel sang about a woman who could ruin your faith with her casual lies.

The two best friends used to play terrible gigs in haunts like this during their younger years, when they had big dreams of sticking it up to the man. Well, look at them now: perfectly successful in the least controversial way possible.

Until recently, that was.

"I didn't even tell you who you'd date." Alice grinned and ran a hand through her hair. Judging by the faded stains on her forehead and fingertips, she had recently re-dyed her fringe.

The fire hydrant red made her stick out among the dull greens and browns of the bar. But Rio knew that was just how she liked it.

Alice Gulliver may have failed to follow her mother's footsteps into the music industry, but that didn't stop her from committing to the star aesthetic. With her leather jacket and shaggy hair, she wasn't what most people imagined when they thought of a talent manager.

(And to be fair, the only talent she managed was Rio.)

But Alice was a bloodhound when it came to sniffing out trends before they were even trending. And she sauntered into the offices and boardrooms of important business people like she was right at home, never accepting a bad deal for her client.

"I trust you." Rio smudged the condensation water on the outside of her glass with her pointer finger. "And I would literally lick this floor to get the creeps out of my DMs."

She dug her phone from her bag, opened a random social media app, and read an equally random message from some guy called @ralphb71:

*I respectfully eye you up and down before I smile slyly to one side.*

good morning beautiful. I couldn't help but notice your social media profile hahaha It's nice to see a girl of your generation who still knows her way around the kitchen. I'm sure you hear that a lot, but I wanted you to hear it from me. I'd like to meet with you and get to know you better. You seem to be a very nice, pretty girl. and total dream wife, if you know what I mean.

*winks at you as you blush*

I will give you beautiful and intellectual babies.

"Ew." Alice made a vomiting sound in the back of her throat. "Fucking asshole."

"Exactly." Rio stuffed the phone back where it came from and snatched another fry from their shared plate.

She loved her job. She really did. But sometimes she hated her phone with a passion that made her want to go off the grid and live in the forest all by herself.

Not that she would have survived the loneliness. Rio enjoyed people, and that included chatting to fans. She felt a surge of pride every time someone recreated one of her recipes and added her in a post or a story. That was the good stuff. The part of her work that allowed her to connect to people.

live-love-loaf was all about comfort food and sharing a bit of positivity in a world that seemed to be drowning in venom and drama.

What she would trade for a tent in the wilderness were the screen time, stat anxiety, the algorithm, and the pushy men who tried to connect with her in less than platonic ways.

They were necessary evils of the job, but the whole thing had spiraled massively out of proportion a couple of weeks ago. Rio felt a tug of dread under her breastbone just thinking about it:

A newbie video essayist had posted a four hours long video on YouTube in which they accused Rio of being a tradwife. The thing had gone viral. And within minutes, Rio's reputation online had shifted from a place of serenity to digital slaughter.

She frowned: "Dating anyone is better than dealing with that. Especially if social media shout outs and a piece of my vanilla reputation are all she wants for it. So? Who is the lucky girl?"

"Agatha Harkness."

And with those two words, the worry lines were wiped from Rio's face. A borderline maniacal glow replaced the glower. Because she was the lucky girl. Luckier than she could've ever imagined.

Her soul left her body. She felt it levitate to the tobacco-clogged ceiling before snapping back into her chest like a sticky hand. The mother of all fireworks went off in her stomach.

Agatha Harkness.

She would date the Agatha Harkness. The author of Ashmouth and Born Evil. Both of which had changed Rio's brain chemistry and reading habits on a fundamental basis.

Sophisticated. Gorgeous. Brilliant. That Agatha Harkness.

"Sign me up," Rio popped the 'p' and didn't even try to suppress the grin that stretched her cheeks. The sole of her boot tapped a jaunty rhythm against the footrest under her chair. She felt like dancing. Singing. Throwing up butterflies.

"You know her?" Alice asked after ordering another beer. "I had no idea who she was before her agent rang me up."

"That's because you only read piano sheets," Rio quipped before sighing and melting against the table: "That picture of her in the back of Ashmouth? The one where she wears that gigantic pair of glasses and smirks like she knows exactly how hot she is? That was my queer awakening. The woman's a goddess. And not Aphrodite or anything. She's Hekate or Hades, something with claws. Ohmygod I'm gonna date Agatha Harkness." She couldn't help but squeal a bit and lunged forward to hug her friend.

Alice was shaking with laughter by that point, shoulders jumping as she hugged her back:

"Hold your horses, Persephone. We have a meeting with your goddess and her agent next Thursday. To talk about if this will happen at all. And maybe don't tell Harkness you drooled all over her glossy face when you were sixteen. That's a turn off for most people."

Thursday morning approached slowly and then all at once.

The bedroom windows stood wide open. Curious bees floated in and out through the gauzy curtains. They buzzed in the planters on the windowsills in search of something colorful. The air smelled of a heady, blooming sweetness and yesterday's rain.

Rio Vidal — one foot sticking out of the covers and with a pillow imprint on her cheek — woke like a kid on Christmas morning. She'd slept like one, too. Which meant hardly at all; a night filled to the brim with anticipation.

Her heels barely touched the ground as she danced into the bathroom and started her morning routine. The bees watched. And if bees could wonder, they might ask themselves why the woman in the fluffy towel was humming along to their songs.

Rio's bedroom was part comfort zone, part greenhouse, but mostly a graveyard of abandoned hobbies. There were planters on every surface, a sewing machine one a desk. Shelves filled with books on tropical spiders, blacksmithing, mortuary science. And that didn't even encompass the wide array of novels growing from on the floor in crooked towers.

Except for Agatha's two novels. These had moved to her bedside table the other night.

Ashmouth lay on top. The hardcover edition, with a purple dust jacket so dark it almost looked black. In the middle it had a silver imprint, an oval broach depicting the triple goddess. Maiden. Mother. Crone.

If Rio could be considered a fangirl in any sense of the word, this was the object of her fanaticism. She vividly remembered being a teenager soaked in confusion and cold sweat. Reading about the group of Salem trials era witches who were trying to survive in a time and place that was designed to annihilate them. It was the first book she ever held in which two women got to kiss—and got away with it, too.

The pages of Ashmouth were searingly sapphic, dark, and hungry. A victory, despite the sacrifices made along the way. And Rio had filled in the margins with notes, post-its, and tears.

Born Evil, a paperback with a curved spine, was fragile in comparison. Here, there were no notes. No highlighted or underlined passages. It inspired less excitement and more bone-deep sadness in Rio. The kind that cut deeper than tears, where crying would do more harm than good. Because the lines were walked too carefully to risk blurring them.

The story was an embodiment; a metaphor of a dragonfly's wing ripped off by the joint and stuck between the pages of a child's diary. It captured the life of a young girl from a prominent family in 1910s Rochester. Throughout the years, she is tormented by her mother so deeply that the girl ends up murdering her. It happens slowly, unsparingly, and through a narrator too reliable for comfort.

Agatha Harkness never gave a single interview about that book. Rio would have found it if she had. And so she and all the other readers had to sit alone in the feeling of it all.

And while Rio would always love Ashmouth more than any other book — she could live in it's story forever — she wholeheartedly understood why it was Born Evil that won the Pulitzer.

It was the kind of book you could only stomach to read once but which stayed with you forever, like a scar. The kind that sucked all joy out of your body by the fingertips that touched the creamy pages. Grave of the Fireflies in novel form. This Other Eden, if it unfolded entirely behind the blown-out pupils of a broken girl.

Rio dragged her gaze away from her nightstand and let the towel drop to the ground.

Light streamed through the bedroom's high windows in bright sheets and cast perfect rectangles onto her hardwood floors. It chased away the literary crisis of Agatha's work.

Rio stood in one of them and stretched like a cat.

The sun prickled on her skin. It kissed a faint scar on her hip. It trailed strong hands attached to delicate wrists attached to forearms curved from kneading dough.

She padded to the closet and slid open the door. The right side was mostly dresses, skirts, and blouses. Work clothes. Things Rio felt pretty in but were too precious or uncomfortable to wear all day. On any other day when she didn't plan to stand in front of the camera, she would have gravitated to the left side of the closet. The one that held soft jeans and t-shirts with silly prints.

Today was bound to be both precious and uncomfortable. Giddy, with a clump of nerves to clog up her throat. So it seemed only right to ignore the jeans and tees and go for something nicer.

She wanted to look like herself but different. A sophisticated, alluring version of Rio Vidal. Someone that could make a woman like Agatha fall in love at first glance. Rio let her fingertips trail the hangers until she stopped at the last one. She'd bought the dress online a couple of months ago but hadn't worn it yet.

"That one works." She told the bees.

She put on simple underwear and a pair of thin black tights before slipping into the jade green dress and cinching the ties at the sides. Rio paired it with her platform docs and shrugged on a black jean jacket on top.

It was a mix of feminine and masculine she loved on herself and she completed it with her signature smokey eye. The dress was technically too cold for the temperatures outside, but it looked too good on her for Rio to care. She wanted to be hot today. Being warm came second to that.

"You can do this. Sweep her off her feet before she even knows what hit her." She told herself with one last nod at the mirror, grabbed her purse, and not quite jogged down the stairs and out of the door.

Roses were too intense for a first meeting.

Rio worried her lip and cast a glance over her shoulder. "Do you think she likes tulips?"

The flower shop smelled green and watery. Outside, the morning was crisp; wind tore at the trees and rippled the puddles on the sidewalks — the leftovers of the past winter months. But in here, between rows and rows of plastic buckets filled with blooms, spring had taken over in an avalanche of color.

Rio had dragged Alice through the door to pick out a gift for Agatha. She didn't want to meet her soon to be fake girlfriend empty handed, and having something to cling to on the way there sounded like a good idea. Nerves roamed like bugs under Rio's skin as she abandoned the velvety roses and moved around the shop. A vague sense of nausea took her by the hand.

Alice trailed after her looking like, well, a former scene kid in a flower shop. She had settled on her regular 'I'm going to an epic concert after this'-look and only shaken her head when she saw that Rio had gotten all dolled up.

"We have no way of knowing what Harkness likes. She's a recluse." Alice eyed a bunch of petunias like they might bite her.

The friends stepped out of the way of a harried mother dragging a bawling child towards the door of the shop. Its face was tantrum-red and its lung-capacity alarming. God, Rio hated kids.

"She's a genius. And she values her privacy...so something tasteful but not flashy, I think." Rio muttered, tearing her eyes away from the scene, and searching the sea of plants for the loveliest ones.

She touched leaves and petals to check for any brown spots or dryness. The action was part quality control, part a distraction from the jitters.

She wanted to do this right.

Sure, it was meant to be pretend. But she got the chance to date the hottest woman in New England, and Rio wouldn't squander it. She would be suave and chill. But also genuinely interested enough to maybe, just maybe, turn the charade into something real.

"Are you sure you're not overthinking this?" Alice watched as Rio abandoned the spring display of tulips and daffodils for one with a more muted color pallet.

"It's Agatha Harkness! Her books are a fucking maze. She strings the reader along by the nose for five hundred pages and then stabs them in the face with the theme in the last paragraph. There is no way I'm not overthinking this. Now, please google 'flower language for azaleas'."

Her best friend grumbled but unlocked her phone.

Purple azaleas symbolized grace, nobility, and regality. They were perfect. Dark in color, but light in her hands. Not forward, just appreciative. Recognizing. Rio bought a bouquet and tucked it into her arms as they made their way to the subway to commute to Jennifer Kale's office.

Just give me a chance, she thought as they reached their platform. This could be a really good thing.

"Why are you all dressed up?" Nicky asked.

Agatha stopped the car at a red light and met his eyes in the rear view mirror. They were a lot more awake than her own.

"Something wrong with the way I look?" she stifled a yawn. The minty taste of toothpaste lingered in her mouth. The painkillers she'd taken while putting on her shoes hadn't kicked in yet.

It was too early for an interrogation. Too early for most things, actually. The sky wore more gray than blue, still. The streets were rumbling rivers of rush hour metal.

Agatha was dressed for war.
