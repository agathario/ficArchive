Chapter 1: don't get around much anymore

Jennifer Kale ran party setup like the goddamn navy. Chairs straight with exactly fourteen inches of space between them—according to Jen, a foot between chairs at round tables put them far too close together, but a full eighteen inches was cold and impersonal. The centerpieces weren’t even a millimeter out of place, the flowers in them arranged under penalty of death. It was enough to make Agatha swear then and there that if she ever got a new job and threw a party for her successor, she’d hire a professional and keep it entirely out of her hands. But this was Jen and the midwife’s license she had given blood, sweat, and tears for. The least she could do was keep her mouth shut and decorate for the passing the torch party.

“She’s going to notice,” Lilia whispered, and Agatha smirked down at the balloon in her hands that she’d been repeatedly inflating and deflating since she and Lilia had shown up, under the guise of helping.

“Please, I’m not stupid enough to get caught,” she whispered back, tying off the balloon and batting it at Lilia like a cat.

“Agatha,” Jen groaned. “Please tell me blowing up that one sad balloon isn’t the only thing you’ve done since you got here.”

“Blowing up that one sad balloon isn’t the only thing I’ve done since I got here.”

“Now tell the truth.”

“I did!” Agatha argued. “I also threw it at Lilia. That’s two things.” 

Jen rolled her eyes. “How do you put up with her?”

“Oh, I manage,” Lilia said softly, planting a kiss on the side of Agatha’s head and moving to wrap Jen in one of her bone-crushing hugs. “I’m sorry I couldn’t stay, dear. Tell Hela congratulations for me. Kale Kare is in good hands. And those babies you’ll be bringing into the world…luckiest babies out there. You’re the path ahead, Jennifer.” 

Jen waved goodbye as Lilia walked out, rounding on Agatha the second the door closed behind her. 

“Hela’s bringing a friend,” she said. “Since I’ll be running around talking to people all night, I thought it could be nice if you had someone to talk to. Especially if the alternative is you standing in a corner looking out at everyone like you’re trying to decide who to curse first.”

“You got me a babysitter?” 

“Not a babysitter. Hela’s friend. Who could be your friend if you behave.”

“Sitting at the bar by myself all night isn’t an option?”

Jen ignored this. “I just thought since Wanda…look, if you’re going to be a bitch, forget it. Hela said she’s really sweet, maybe it’s better you don’t run her off.”

At the mention of Wanda, Agatha’s gaze dropped to the floor. Their split had desperately needed to happen, but she couldn’t pretend not having a guaranteed tolerable person to get her through the party wasn’t an unfortunate complication, especially with Lilia gone. 

“Sorry,” Jen said softly. “Didn’t mean to bring her up like that. You just haven’t been yourself lately and I think tonight could be fun. You know, if you let it be fun.”

Her gaze shifted to a spot over Agatha’s shoulder and Agatha turned her head to see the first guests walk through the door. 

“Go,” she assured Jen. “I’m good. I’ll…behave or whatever.”

Left to her own devices, Agatha did exactly what she told Jen she wouldn’t do and found a corner of the room to silently assess her surroundings as more and more people trickled in. Almost like school, the guests found themselves in cliques. Jen’s hospital friends clumped together so closely that they looked like they would have to be surgically separated. The Kale Kare group was slightly more scattered, but Agatha wrapped her arms around herself and watched them in disgust (or was it jealousy?) as they greeted each other with the warmest of embraces. 

If she and Wanda were still together, they would be…well, for the full three years, Agatha never knew until they arrived somewhere what they’d be doing, something that couldn't be determined until she knew which version of Wanda she’d be getting. Most frequently, it was the Wanda who would down four or five glasses of red wine, pull a tense and uncomfortable Agatha toward her as hard as she could for a sloppy broad-daylight makeout session, and demand to be taken home without so much as a goodbye to the friends they’d been out with. Less often, it was the quiet, calculating Wanda. The one who would summon a hex of the cruelest words she could string together and whisper them in Agatha’s ear like sweet nothings. The “You know no one will ever believe you, right?” was unspoken. No, it was better like this, Agatha reminded herself as she walked over to the bar. A little liquid courage and she’d be good as new.

“What can I get you?” the bartender asked.

“Some brutal honesty.” Agatha smirked at her puzzled expression, sliding into the only unoccupied chair of the three in front of the makeshift bar. “On a scale from one to making me want to walk in front of a moving car, how bad is the champagne?”

The woman next to her laughed, badly attempting to disguise it as a cough.

“Something funny?”

“Nothing.” The woman turned her head and it was all Agatha could do to keep her jaw from dropping like some kind of cartoon character. Between the doe eyes and the gap in her front teeth, this woman could ruin Agatha’s life, and Agatha might just happily let her. “Just so no one plays in traffic tonight, give her one of these.”

Agatha eyed the woman’s glass suspiciously, biting back a comment on how pink its contents were. The pink was on brand for Jen, after all, and if she was being saved from bad champagne, the least she could do was to be nice.

“Vodka, prosecco, strawberry purée, and a splash of lemonade. Do you trust me?” 

“Not at all.”

“No nonsense. I like that. I bet you make a lot of people uncomfortable.”

“I’ve been told being off-putting is part of my charm,” Agatha said, accepting her drink from the bartender, cautiously taking a sip, and cursing internally at how much she liked it. Before she knew it, a third of the drink was gone.

Recognition washed over the woman’s face.

“You must be Agatha. I’m—”

“My babysitter?”

“Rio. Hela’s roommate. And for the record, I don’t think you’re off-putting. I think you say things no one else will. The fearless type.”

“You got all that from me not wanting to drink shitty champagne?”

“No, I got it from you acting like an asshole, which I think I’ve correctly deduced is a defense mechanism.”

Agatha raised her eyebrows, ready to unleash an argument and shocking herself when she realized she really didn’t have one. Point, Rio.

“Anyone ever tell you that you’re too forward?” Agatha snarked back, though lacking any kind of real bite. “I like to wait until we’ve at least had dinner before I let strangers dissect my personality.”

“So finish your drink and let’s go.”

Agatha froze with her straw hovering at her lips. “Go where?”

“To get dinner.”

She took another slow sip, wondering if the vodka was hitting her bloodstream that fast or if she was actually considering leaving with Rio.

“Won’t Jen lose her shit?”

“Jen would be more upset if someone died at her party.” Rio cracked a smile at the confusion on Agatha’s face. “The appetizers that have been out have had shrimp in them and there hasn’t been a glove in sight. I’m allergic. Going into anaphylactic shock isn’t on my list of things to do tonight. I know a place and it’s just at the end of the block. We’ll be back before Jen and Hela figure out we’re gone, I promise.”

Agatha considered the proposition. “My mother always told me not to go places with strangers,” she said in an attempt at a mocking voice, but the grin on Rio’s face told her she sounded more amused by the situation than anything else. 

“So ask me some questions about my life, I’ll answer them, and there. Not a stranger anymore.” Rio waited patiently while Agatha drained the last of her drink, only wandering away for a second to pluck a flower out of one of the perfect centerpieces. “For you…m’lady.”

Agatha accepted the flower and took one final uneasy glance around the room. Sure enough, Jen was crowded by a swarm of her soon-to-be-coworkers from the hospital, hands waving wildly as she told a story that had several of them throwing their heads back with laughter. 

“If you’re a serial killer, you have to tell me now. But fine. Let’s go.”

Rio adorably bounced a little on the balls of her feet and linked her arm through Agatha’s to make weaving through the crowd a bit easier.

“The license plate on my car does say ‘Lady Death’,” Rio said as they stepped outside. “But I’m a tattoo artist. Not a serial killer.”

“And that will be my first question. Lady Death?”

“My studio is called Queen of Swords, after one of the tarot cards said to represent independence. But I’ve always been drawn to the Death card. Transformation and the end of cycles that don’t serve you. The freedom of it. I came in on my first day in a t-shirt with the card on it, coworker said ‘good morning, Lady Death,’ and it kind of stuck.”

“That’s some serious commitment to a nickname.”

Rio shrugged. “I never do anything halfway. If you only get one shot at being alive, why waste time doing things you don’t want to give all of yourself to?”

“What if you don’t want to give all of yourself and do it anyway? What if the consequences of half-assing it are worse?”

“Then you deserve better.”

Agatha fell silent, focusing hard on the click of her heels on the sidewalk. Her childhood was marred by ulterior motives, a father nowhere in sight and a mother whose hand was the only thing sharper than the words she would wield as weapons. Falling in love with Wanda was more of the same, and she had yet to determine if it was a curse that these were the people who’d taken up the most space in her life or if this was her fate—punishment for mistakes and unworthiness on a level she couldn’t begin to fathom. Rio was an enigma, blunt but not cruel, kind but not weak. 

“You okay?”

Agatha shook herself out of her daze as Rio stopped at the door of a diner, pulling it open and motioning for her to go inside. 

“I’m fine,” she said once they were settled in a booth. “Favorite movie.”

“Star Wars.”

“Nerd. But which one? And there is a correct answer.”

“Empire, the only answer.” Rio stared thoughtfully for a moment. “I bet I could get your hair into any of Leia’s styles, though.”

“Can I start you two off with something to drink?” 

Agatha hadn’t even seen the waitress approach, but dropped her eyes to the menu to buy herself a few seconds of composure. Her icy exterior was by far her favorite accessory, and this stranger melting it was unexpected and taking every ounce of her brain power to process.

“I’ll take a black coffee,” Rio said cheerfully. “We’re going back to a party after this.” 

“For you?”

“One word about this and I’ll destroy you,” Agatha hissed playfully at Rio before turning back to the waitress. “Hot chocolate, extra whip, but only if it’s Reddi Whip or heavy whipping cream. If it’s one of those store brands that comes out tasting like butter, I’d like a Diet Coke. Please,” she added in a hurry at the look on Rio’s face.

“Alright, out with it. I said please, didn’t I?” Agatha sighed and folded her arms across her chest.

“Nothing,” Rio said, clearly fighting back a fit of giggles. “I just…I think I understand why you’re single.”

“And how do you know I’m single?”

“Well, first of all, if you were dating someone, you wouldn’t be here with me. I’m a little too good at reading people and I think you’re a lot of things, but you’re not a cheater.”

“An empath.” Agatha leaned back into the booth, running a hand through her hair and wincing when one of her rings attached itself to a strand.

“You could say that. There are worse things.”

Before she could think of a response, the waitress reappeared with their drinks, and Agatha kicked herself internally for the way her eyes lit up seeing her sugar monstrosity being placed in front of her instead of the Diet Coke she didn’t actually want.

“This place has the best burgers. Could I get one of those, medium, everything on it?”

The waitress turned to Agatha, a flicker of fear or at least intimidation appearing on her face for a second.

“Could I do the chicken caesar…but as a wrap, not a salad, and fried chicken, not grilled?”

“I think I could convince her to share some fries with me without changing anything, too,” Rio said, a maddening smirk on her face.

“You got it,” the waitress said, rushing off in a clear hurry to get away from their table.

“Something funny?”

“Just you, Sally.”

Agatha tilted her head in confusion.

“You’ve never seen When Harry Met Sally? Oh, Agatha…I was just starting to like you.”

“I don’t do romantic comedies. Or romance. Or comedy.”

“We’ll work on that,” Rio promised. “Now go back to questions, I was having fun with those.”

“Okay. Um…something about life that you wish would happen but it never does.”

“Eulogies for birthdays.”

Agatha’s drink had been halfway to her lips but she paused and set the glass back down.

“What…for birthdays?”

“You heard me.” Rio leaned back. “Why is it we only tell the people we love how much we love them once they’re gone? Why can’t we do that when they can still hear us? Why let them leave without ever knowing?”

“Sounds like there’s a story there.”

Rio shrugged. “Not really. Humans just have this habit of never saying the things they want to say. We miss out on so much love while we’re alive. It’s been ten years and I’d still give anything to be able to give my mom birthday eulogies.”

Agatha nodded slowly, making a mental note to write more than just “Love you!” in Lilia’s next birthday card. 

“I’ve been baring my soul to you, by the way,” Rio said. “I feel like I should get to ask some questions, too.”

Agatha motioned for Rio to continue.

“If you had to choose…piercings or tattoos?”

Agatha said nothing, just took another sip of her hot chocolate and tucked a strand of hair behind her ear, putting her helix, forward helix, conch, and four lobe piercings on full display.

“Got it. I’ll get you to come around on tattoos though. I’d even do one for you if you want.”

“Right…if I ever want someone to color on me with a needle, you’ll be my first call.”

“You know it’s really hard to take you seriously with whipped cream all over your face, right?”

Agatha flushed scarlet, wiping furiously at her mouth and whipping out her phone to use the camera to assess the damage. 

“Made you look,” Rio said, having the audacity to wink.

“You’re evil.” Agatha tried to put as much venom as she could into those two words, but despite her best efforts, was giggling loudly enough to cancel out any genuine upset.

“You’re calling me evil?” Rio gasped, feigning offense, one hand to her chest as though she was clutching invisible pearls.

Agatha shrugged. “I’ll get you back before the night is over.”

“Okay, I told you about birthday eulogies,” Rio continued, pivoting back to the questions like nothing had happened. “You, fearless one. You have to be afraid of something. So what is it? Spiders? Snakes? Being nice to people? Ordering something at a restaurant in under thirty words?”

“Smartass. Airplanes.”

Rio nodded thoughtfully and for just a moment, Agatha almost hated her for how wholeheartedly she seemed to care. How someone she’d known for an hour was treating her more gently than Wanda had in three years. But the moment passed and she felt her last remaining wall come crumbling down.

“I’m not scared of heights,” she continued. “I’d go skydiving right now if you asked me. It’s more…I’m a bit of a loner. As evidenced by our friends scheming to make sure I had someone to talk to tonight. If I went on a plane and the worst happened, I just can’t help wondering…would anyone find out? Would there be anyone to call? What if that’s how I go, as a Jane Doe on a flight to somewhere pointless, like Florida?”

Rio pondered this for a second. 

“You know, statistically, you’re more likely to die in a car accident than a plane crash.”

“That’s reassuring,” Agatha muttered, leaning back against the booth again.

“You have Lilia.”

She sat up at this. “You know Lilia?”

“Everyone knows Lilia. But yeah, I did the queen of cups tattoo on her shoulder.”

Agatha eyed Rio skeptically.

“Lilia doesn’t have any tattoos.”

“I promise you, she does. And when she opened her phone to show me a picture of what she wanted, a picture of her with you was her background. It took me this whole time so far to figure out why you looked so familiar, but that’s it. You’re Lilia’s girl. And I’d put money on it, if you went down in a plane she’d be the first person on site looking for you. I don’t know how you know each other, but she loves you to death.”

Agatha swallowed hard, hoping the lump in her throat and the tears in her eyes would go unnoticed.

“I’ve known Lilia forever, not too long after I came out. I met Jen in college and she took her in, too. She’s kind of the queen of the lesbians. I don’t think there’s a dyke in a fifty mile radius that she hasn’t taken under her wing.”

“If she knows Jen that well, where is she tonight?” 

Agatha’s shoulders tensed, relaxing when she reminded herself that unlike Wanda’s accusatory nature, Rio was genuinely curious.

“She visits a nursing home every Saturday. There are a couple women there who lost their wives and don’t have any other family. I’m not sure they still fully recognize her but the staff say they’re calmer when she’s around. Laugh more. That might have something to do with the fact that she brings the dirtiest books in her possession and reads to them. After the last time the staff told her to stop, she snuck one in between two bibles.”

At the last part of the story, Rio was laughing so hard there were tears clinging to her eyelashes.

“I’m sorry. That’s so kind of her and so Lilia, but the bibles? Incredible.”

By the end of dinner, Agatha was forced to concede that there was just something about Rio. Something about the way her head tipped back when she laughed. The way her tongue poked into her cheek when she was deciding what to say next. The way she swore there were better condiments than ketchup and proved her point by reaching across the table to gently stick a fry in Agatha’s mouth so she could try the house-made honey mustard. The way no one around her was a stranger, as she told no fewer than six other customers that she loved their outfits, or that they had a beautiful smile, or that their hair color was stunning, on their way out of the diner. The way she snatched the check before Agatha had a chance to move her arm, telling her that she wasn’t a babysitter but she was a gentleman. Being around her was lighter and freer and Agatha had to admit she was disappointed walking back to Jen’s party, knowing their time would be over.

“Where have you two been?” Jen shrieked as the door swung open, and Agatha was horrified as it dawned on her that the music had stopped, the guests were gone, and the party was being torn down. But the look on Jen’s face wasn’t the slightest bit angry, more…smug satisfaction? She looked downright pleased with herself.

“I didn’t want Rio to die,” she answered. “Pretty noble of me, actually. Selfless.”

Next to her, Rio laughed softly. “It’s true. She might just have saved a life today, shrimp be damned.”

“Mhm. Have a good time?”

Agatha rolled her eyes. “Yes, mom. We had fun.”

“You’ve fulfilled your best friend duties. I won’t even make you take the balloons down. I’m glad you came, Ag. It’s good to see you doing better.”

Jen slid one arm around Agatha’s shoulders, gave her a little squeeze, and went back to gathering centerpieces.

“I’m sticking around a bit, Hela’s my ride,” Rio said. “You’ll be good getting home?”

“I’m good,” Agatha promised. “I promised Lilia I’d stay over, she’s outside. But I don’t get to see the Lady Death-mobile?” 

“Next time.”

“Oh…so there will be a next time, huh?”

Rio shrugged. “If I’m lucky. Now…I should warn you, I don’t know what Jen just did, but I hug with both arms.”

Agatha stiffened slightly as Rio’s arms wrapped around her, one hand rubbing softly between her shoulder blades. She couldn’t remember the last time someone besides Lilia had hugged her like this. Wanda, maybe, if she was feeling kind, but it was rare. 

“It’s just a hug,” Rio whispered. “It’s not going to bite.”

Agatha breathed out a laugh and let her body relax, hesitantly putting her own arms around Rio.

Rio gave her one final squeeze and pulled back, one hand still on Agatha’s shoulder, the other slipping something into her pocket.

“I hope I see you again. But if not…have a nice life, Agatha.”

“You, too, Rio.”

In the passenger seat as Lilia chattered up a storm about the chapters of the book she had read that night, Agatha reached into her pocket, hand closing around a card. She pulled it out, cracking a smile when she found it to be a business card from Rio’s studio, personal phone number scrawled on the back.

“See you soon, I hope,” it read, “love, Lady Death.”

It took Agatha an hour to realize what Rio had done, handing over contact information wrapped up and tied with a pretty bow, in a way that meant she was forced to make the next move. It took one text a few days later for her to realize it was completely intentional.

Well played, Lady Death.

To her horror, before she could start typing out the next text, that same phone number that she hadn’t even put into her contacts yet was calling her.

“Consider yourself special,” she said dryly. “I don’t pick up the phone for just anyone.”

“I’ll accept my award later,” Rio shot back, but Agatha could hear her fighting back a laugh. “What are you up to?”

“Oh, riveting stuff. Don’t know how I even made time to pick up the phone.”

“Agatha.”

“Okay, fine, my X Files DVDs are on and I’m boxing up my ex’s crap, are you happy?”

“I only had one client today and they cancelled. Want to go on an adventure?”

Agatha’s heart sank. Truthfully, yes, she really did. She just didn’t have any hope that she would be great company. She had been putting off going through everything Wanda had left in her apartment since the split and the mess of items extended to all corners of every room. A light blue jacket with a couple red hairs still clinging to the shoulders hung in the closet. A tube of lipstick on the bathroom counter. A bottle of hazelnut coffee creamer that was somehow still good in the fridge. Letters from when Wanda’s visits were over were still in a decorative box on the dresser. But most embarrassing by a landslide was the ring still on Agatha’s finger, a glittering reminder of everything that had gone wrong.

“I’ll go on an adventure with you on one condition,” she said finally. “Admit that you didn’t get my number because you wanted to be the one to hear from me.”

“Damn right,” Rio said, and Agatha caught herself actually shaking her head at the unapologetic honesty. “You didn’t need an opportunity to be bold, but I wanted to give one to you anyway. You’re welcome.”

“Hmm. Don’t seem to remember thanking you for making me blind text you and you replying with a call.”

“I’m a caller,” Rio said. “Sue me if, when I care about people, I want to hear their voices when I talk to them.”

“No way have you known me long enough to care about me,” Agatha scoffed. As soon as the words left her mouth, though, she wished she could take them back. The image in her head of Rio across from her in the diner, listening to her with nothing but pure fascination, was enough evidence for her to know that what she had just said was not only untrue but a surefire way to hurt Rio’s feelings. Like you’re good for anything else, she thought bitterly. 

“I’ll prove it to you. I always do. Now, do you want to ride in the Lady Death-mobile or not?”

“Am I allowed to text you my address, or do you require that to be over the phone, too?”

“Text it and go get ready. Put something comfortable on, but something you’re okay walking around outside in.”

For a split second, Agatha wondered what on earth she was getting herself into, but typed her address and retreated back into her bedroom to trade her pajamas for yoga pants, a t-shirt she only vaguely remembered buying from a concert a few years back, and her favorite black Vans. She contemplated brushing her hair for a moment and decided against it, running a hand through a few particularly difficult tangles and twisting it into a bun. 

She didn’t have to wait long for Rio to call again, but took extra care to be waiting outside when she pulled up. No, Rio wouldn’t be allowed to see the apartment until the graveyard of her relationship with Wanda was cleared out.

“What do you think?” Rio said, grinning as she stepped out of the car. “Meet the Lady Death-mobile.”

It turned out to be a sage green Jeep Wrangler, the back covered in an assortment of stickers that Agatha had to stop and take in.

Horny for the environment.

Please let me merge before I start crying.

All nervous, no system, with a picture of an absolutely heartbroken-looking raccoon.

Hot girls hit curbs.

I brake for cool rocks.

My tummy hurts and I’m mad at the government.

“My friend Alice gave me the mad at the government one,” Rio said fondly. “My pride and joy.”

Agatha’s eyes traveled down to the license plate which, sure enough, read LDY D34TH.

“Last chance to change your shoes, by the way.”

“My…what’s wrong with my shoes?” Agatha put her hand to her chest in mock offense.

“Can you walk in them?”

“Confused what you think I’ve been doing in them so far.”

“Funny,” Rio said, stepping around Agatha and opening the passenger door for her. “M’lady.”

Agatha slid into her seat, correctly assuming Rio would be the type to scold her for not putting her seatbelt on fast enough. 

“We’re going to be walking a bit,” Rio continued as she buckled her own seatbelt and put the car in drive. “Anyway. What season?”

“Season?”

“You said X Files DVDs.”

“Oh.” Agatha leaned back in her seat and relaxed a little. “Six. I’d pretend I was productive but if you hadn’t called, I would have abandoned everything I was doing and been on the couch watching by now.”

“Don’t you think it’s funny how it’s aged? The ‘you’re my one in five billion’ line used to feel like this insane confession of love and now five billion is almost two thirds of the population.”

“Do you always do that?” 

“Do what?”

“That,” Agatha said, gesturing vaguely at Rio herself. “The deep introspective thoughts about everything.”

“Oh, that.” Rio smirked. “Pretty much. My mom used to say I’ve been here before. Like this wasn’t my first life. I’m an old soul, I guess, but I prefer it to the alternative. I don’t think I could do the early twenties chaos, early thirties lower back pain is all I can take right now.”

Agatha laughed a little, wondering what Rio would say about her contact in Jen’s phone being “Chaos Creator.”

“I guess I can’t fault you for that,” Agatha admitted. “I never told you what I do. I’m an associate professor, English lit. I teach early twenties chaos and get tired just looking at them sometimes.”

“You’re a professor? You’re so young.” Rio appeared to be in genuine awe.

“I skipped third and fourth grade. Finished undergrad a year early and did a masters program right after. I’m at a few local colleges now. I took a break from collecting pieces of paper right after I met Wanda so I could do some actual teaching, but I’m working on my doctorate so I can get tenure somewhere bigger.”

“I take it Wanda’s the ex whose crap you were throwing out?”

“You catch on fast. And I didn’t say throwing out, I said boxing up.”

“Same difference,” Rio said, merging onto the highway. “Were you planning on mailing any of it to her?”

“I can’t. She moved to some small town in Jersey and we broke up before I could get an address. Agatha nodded.

Promise.” Rio smiled again and Agatha noticed she had the same reaction to the gap between her front teeth sober that she’d had after the vodka drink at Jen’s party. “We just became friends. I can’t have you dying on me.”

“Let’s get this over with,” Agatha grumbled, and to her annoyance, Rio positively beamed as she took the first steps onto the trail, just as slowly and patiently as she promised. 

“So, are you a talker or do you like to just enjoy nature?”

“I’m not really sure,” Agatha said softly, ignoring the shock that came with realizing how honest she was when Rio was around. She was almost getting used to it. Almost. “Wanda was more of a ‘within the same four walls’ type. She liked me where she could keep an eye on me. I think some of it was a fear of loss, but…I don’t know. A three year relationship shouldn’t feel like an imprisonment, right?”

“No human should ever make another human feel that way. For any reason,” she said finally.

They walked in silence for a bit, and with the absence of conversation, Agatha’s mind wandered back to her last couple weeks with Wanda. How had they fallen apart?

I don’t know who I am anymore, she’d finally said. I fight so fucking hard for this every single day but I can’t remember why. Doesn’t that mean we’re too far gone?

She’d left that night and had felt like she was wandering aimlessly between two realities ever since, the one she’d spent three years in with Wanda and the one that housed the person she knew she had to become after. Both of those versions of herself were unrecognizable, but something about being with Rio was comforting. Familiar, like a glimpse of the life she’d had before when she was fierce, formidable. Before anything could touch her.

“Hiking?”

Agatha nodded, focusing on her breathing as they hit a slight incline.

“Not really. I like it when I need to clear my head or the weather’s nice. I don’t like to go alone, though, and I’ve never been able to get Hela to go with me. Maybe for the best. Your Vans are nothing, she’d be wearing Birkenstocks on a good day, maybe even flip flops.”

“Weather,” Rio answered. “And…well…I wanted to see you again. All Hela said was that Jen had a friend who wouldn’t know anyone else at the party. I didn’t know you’d be…” 

Their pace slowed even more as Rio’s voice trailed off. 

“Unhinged?” Agatha supplied unhelpfully.

“Striking. In every sense of the word.”

It was hard to tell in the July heat and under the cover of so many trees, but Agatha could have sworn Rio’s face flushed upon her admission.

“You’re not so bad yourself,” Agatha said. “I answered your phone call, got in your car, and willingly followed you into the woods. That’s almost the trifecta of red flags when you’ve only known someone a day.”

“Harry and Sally drove from Chicago to New York together after knowing each other for five seconds.”

“Who?”

“I will be making a list of movies to force you to watch with me. But you should know that I take my movie nights very seriously. No phones allowed.”

“I can live with that,” Agatha said. “Have you seen my phone even once since you picked me up? If you’re going to challenge me, make it harder than that.” 

Before she could stop herself, she bumped her shoulder against Rio’s.

“Oh, you’re up for a challenge? Careful what you wish for.”

Rio playfully bumped Agatha’s shoulder back and she let herself laugh, a real laugh, but it only lasted a second before she realized what was about to happen—she had been watching Rio instead of the trail and didn’t see the tree root in her path, only aware of its presence when she was mid-flight, ankle making a sickening popping sound on her way down.

“I’m okay,” she breathed, but Rio wasn’t convinced.

“We’ve only made it about half a mile,” she said, checking the Apple Watch on her wrist. “We’ll turn around if you can’t walk on it, okay?”

Agatha shook her head.

“I’ll be fine. It’s adventure day, I can’t ruin the fun before it ever gets started.”

So by that logic, anything we do today is an adventure, even if that means leaving here.” Rio stood up, offering Agatha both of her arms. “I got you. Stand up first and then we’ll see about putting weight on it.”

“Moment of truth,” Rio said. Agatha gingerly touched her other foot to the ground, yelping and crashing back into Rio’s waiting arms when the pain was too much. “Ooookay. So we aren’t doing that. I have an idea, but it’s kind of crazy.”

Agatha shifted her weight a little, wincing and pressing her face into Rio’s shoulder. “I’ll take crazy right about now.”

“Do you trust me?”

Agatha’s mind raced back to Jen’s party where she’d answered the same question with an immediate no, not giving it a second thought.

“Against my better judgment, yeah, I do.”

“Okay,” Rio said, “but the catch is you have to let go of me for a second. Just a second so I can turn around.”

Agatha stood awkwardly on one foot feeling a little too much like a flamingo, but with a little extra gratitude for the core work she’d been doing at pilates that Jen said was “overkill.”

“Hop on,” Rio said, and Agatha bit back about ten different retorts as she took in the sight of her slightly lunged position.

“You’re not serious.”

“I’m dead serious. It’s only half a mile and you’re tiny, I swear I won’t drop you.”

“I don’t want to hurt you,” Agatha mumbled, eyes cast downward.

“You won’t,” Rio assured her. “You can’t.”

Agatha’s ankle throbbed painfully, vastly outweighing any internal conflict about the piggyback ride she was being offered.

“How do you want me to do this?”

“Hold on,” Rio said, dropping all the way to the ground. “Three days and I’m already on my knees for you? Damn.”

“Rio,” Agatha whined.

“Legs around my waist, arm around my shoulders, okay?”

Agatha did as she was told, breath hitching slightly when Rio’s arms locked under her legs.

“I got you. I promise.”

Agatha burst out laughing at the sheer absurdity of the situation, cringing hard when she snorted before she could stop the crescendo of giggles. Then again, if she and Rio were going to stay friends, perhaps getting the first snort out of the way early was better.

“Where would I possibly go?” Agatha called after her.

“Don’t hate me for this,” Rio cautioned, returning to the bench and setting a cooler down on the ground. “I need to take your shoe off and make sure there isn’t a break.”

“You’re not touching anything until you tell me where you got your medical degree.”

“My mom was a nurse. Come on, it’s this or the emergency room.”

Agatha’s face paled and she leaned forward to slide the shoe off her foot, pleasantly surprised to find that it wasn’t as painful to the touch as it had been in the woods.

“Not super swollen and the bruising is mild. I think I can take you home to rest it, at least for the night, but take these.” Rio reached into the front pocket of the cooler and pulled out a bottle of ibuprofen. “And if it feels worse in the morning, promise me you’ll go to urgent care or something.” 

“I don’t do doctors.”

“Agatha.”

“Or hospitals.”

“Agatha.”

“Or x-rays.”

Rio sighed. “Would you do it if I call it our next adventure?”

“Hmm.” Agatha tilted her head up, pretending to put extra thought into the proposition. “I think I’d need ice cream after.”

“I bet I could make that happen.” Rio grinned and reached into the main pocket of the cooler, pulling out the ice pack she had used to keep the water cold. “Let’s get you in the car so you can put this on it.”

With one hand wrapped around Rio’s wrist for support, Agatha moved from the bench to the passenger seat. She was a little shocked to discover she could touch her foot ever so slightly to the ground and take steps on it, but was grateful to slide into the seat and fasten the seatbelt while Rio gently placed the ice pack on her ankle.

“Even when I was with Wanda, I’ve never had an adventure quite this exciting,” Agatha said.

Rio put the car in drive. “Can I ask you something?”

“You just carried me half a mile through the woods. You can ask me anything you want.”

Rio laughed softly. “What was so special about Wanda?”

“We were in love,” Agatha said a little too quickly. 

“Okay, but what did she do for you?” Rio challenged. “How did she make you happy?”

Agatha replied with nothing more than the glare that slid back onto her face like a well-worn glove. Comfortable and familiar. 

If she was one hundred percent honest with herself, she and Wanda had only been happy in the first blissful honeymoon weeks of their relationship. Before they really knew each other. Before she allowed herself to seek out affection to take for herself and allowed Wanda to take everything else she had. Before she stopped seeing her friends. Before she had bruises in the shape of Wanda’s fingerprints dotting her arm like a grotesque constellation because for once in her life, she was too tired to fight back and dared to walk away from an argument. 

“Agatha,” Rio said gently. “When were you happy?”

“Never, okay? Not even once. Is that what you want to hear? Want to watch me unearth the worst memories of the past three years?”

“Nope,” Rio said, obnoxiously popping the ‘p.’ 

“Then what is this? Why did I climb to the stratosphere in the woods with you today?”

“Because you’re my friend. And you’re a badass. And be honest…abysmal choice of shoes and probably sprained ankle aside…are you having fun right now?”

Agatha softened. “Yeah. First time in a long time.”

They didn’t speak the rest of the drive back to Agatha’s apartment, but it wasn’t the tense kind of silence that demanded to be filled. It felt safe and warm, like being wrapped in a blanket just taken out of the dryer, and Agatha didn’t want to face the cold she’d be hit with when Rio went home.

“Will you…I mean, only if you want to.”

Rio closed and locked the door, meeting Agatha at the couch and pulling the coffee table closer to prop her ankle up. 

“I can use this as an opportunity to make you watch When Harry Met Sally. It’s streaming a couple places and I have logins for all of them so you can’t protest.”

Agatha tossed the remote over to her.

“You do that, I’m placing an Instacart order if I’m going to be stuck here until I can walk. Want anything?”

Rio paused, one hand rubbing at her neck.

“Green? Had leaves? There were plants everywhere, was there something special about those ones?”

“Just a hunch,” Rio said. “Can I see your arms?”

Agatha extended her left arm and gasped. Her normally porcelain-pale skin was red and blotchy, covered in a rash that she hadn’t even noticed, but once she laid eyes on it, it was all she could do to keep herself from scratching the skin off.

“You get the calamine lotion, I’ll order a pizza?” Rio gestured to her neck. “When I was carrying you to the car I think I might have gotten caught in the crossfire.”

Agatha added a few more things to her cart and walked as carefully as she could without causing more damage to the kitchen, knowing there would be hell to pay if she woke up the next morning and found out she hadn’t restocked her cold brew.

In the thirty seconds Agatha had been gone, she had placed a throw pillow and extra blanket on the coffee table and moved the stack of books Agatha had left on the other side of the couch over to where she’d be able to reach. Upon closer inspection, everything around her in the living room had become centralized—phone charger plugged in, lamp slid closer so she could reach the switch. The gesture was so simple, but Agatha felt a stinging in her eyes that she tried to blink away before she’d have to go back.

“Agatha, you coming? I’m paused on the opening credits,” Rio called.

“Yeah, I…yeah. One second.”

Agatha looked down at her hand, at Wanda’s promise ring, then back up at Rio waiting excitedly on the couch. Without a second thought, she pulled the ring off of her finger and dropped it right down the kitchen sink.

“Press play, I’ll be right there.”

Agatha’s throat tightened again to the point where she wasn’t sure she could speak without at least one tear escaping, but gave Rio’s hand another gentle squeeze. 

“She’s going to have a field day. I’ve never brought anyone home for this before.”

“Not even Wanda?”

For the best, though. Lilia has always had some choice words for her that I’m not sure she would have been able to keep to herself.”

Rio smiled. “Look at what the wine is called. I like my chances of getting her seal of approval.”

Agatha let go of Rio’s hand to turn the bottle around.

“Evolution.”

“It wasn’t intentional, not really. I tried it somewhere a couple years ago and really liked it, that’s mostly why I picked it. But the name made it kind of perfect.”

“It’s a red, so it’ll still score you some points. Lilia has strong feelings about white wine. She has pretty bad luck picking dry ones, so all the ones she’s had anytime recently were too sweet. She said it was grape juice that was lying about being wine.”

Agatha pouted as much as she could with the smile that was betraying her. “I thought you were my best friend.”

Rio pulled into a spot on the street in front of Lilia’s house, smirk already on her face. “Get a tattoo, and we’ll see.”

“Yours, always. Come here, bella.”

“Hi, mama,” Agatha whispered as she allowed herself to be held, near-suffocatingly squeezed, then rocked back and forth until she nearly lost her footing. “Careful, careful, hug me too hard and I might sprain my other ankle.”

“We might have had an incident the other day,” Agatha mumbled.

“Tell her what you did,” Rio added unhelpfully.

“Fell?”

“No, sorry. I mean, tell her what we were doing.”

Lilia paused halfway through opening the front door. “Good lord, child. What were you doing?”

“Nothing crazy,” Agatha defended. “She said it like that because when I tell you, you’re going to think I’m lying.”

Lilia raised an eyebrow. “Out with it.”

“Hiking.”

“You got her to go hiking? I can’t even get her to hang out in the backyard with me.”

“That’s not hanging out and you know it,” Agatha argued. “That is nearly always some kind of ritual. If you did something normal out there, like read, or have a fire that doesn’t involve herbs and burning written intentions on the summer solstice, then maybe. One time, couldn’t we just make s’mores?”

“Lilia, are these you?” she asked finally, pointing at a series of black and white photographs on the wall outside the kitchen.

“Got this one a camera when she graduated from high school,” she answered proudly, gesturing to Agatha. “I was the most readily available and most willing subject. I tried to return the favor a few times but got this instead.”

Lilia picked up a framed photo from the wine bar. The hair gave away Agatha’s identity, the only thing truly visible besides the arm she was extending to cover the lens and her face in the slightly blurred background. She was clearly putting up a fight about being photographed but was, she was both surprised and relieved to see, laughing. 

“Jen took this one.”

They had been in the backyard, all three of them, kicked back on the lounge chairs on the patio with homemade sangria. Jen had been basking in the glow of the unseasonably warm October day, stopping only to pour more sangria, spray a little sunscreen on her shoulders, and tease Agatha for the number of textbooks around her when she had just pulled an all-nighter working on a paper the night before. At the time, she hadn’t known Jen took the camera. She just finished her drink feeling floaty, sleepy, and touch starved, and curled up next to Lilia.

Her other hand was mid-run through a tangle of brown hair that, she was kind enough not to point out, clearly had not been brushed that morning. Agatha’s legs were scrunched to keep from sliding off the chair, and her head was resting on Lilia’s stomach, both arms around her waist, one hand holding the hem of her shirt in a death grip. It was another in the never-ending cruel ironies of Agatha’s life, how she was so easily able to let the ice inside her melt in the presence of people she loved, but only while asleep.

Agatha stayed behind at the wine bar for another moment, staring at herself in the picture. It was almost disconcerting to see herself looking that relaxed, jaw not clenched, nails not digging into her palms, shoulders not tight enough to trigger a headache that would eventually work its way up her neck and into her temples. It was, she realized as she looked at the only two people in the world to have ever made her feel that way—the only two people in the world she’d fallen asleep in front of without meaning to, even if once was on the phone—safety.

“We drink while we cook on gotcha day,” she explained to Rio, passing her a glass.

“Three of pentacles,” Lilia added.

“Yeah. That.”

Rio smiled. “Like a coven.”

Lilia pointed a finger at Agatha. “Keep this one.”

“I wasn’t expecting you two to gang up on me,” Agatha muttered.

“Incident?” Rio asked.

“The last time I was allowed to do this part of making the sauce, I didn’t know I was supposed to squeeze them with my hands as far into the bowl as I can get them. We tried to get it off with a mop but some stains just don’t come out.”

“I kind of want to try this.” 

Rio reached into the bowl and Agatha didn’t even have a second to blink before the tomato juice hit her in the face. Rio clapped both hands over her mouth, barely bothering to stifle her giggles, and waited for the reaction.

“Oh, it’s on, Vidal.”

Agatha picked up a tomato and held it out like a weapon, pressing in just the right spot to send a red spray that started from Rio’s forehead and went down her nose and across her right cheek, that bore an uncanny resemblance to David Bowie’s lightning bolt.

“Children,” Lilia scolded without turning around.

Agatha took one step closer, trying not to react when Rio’s hand cupped her chin.

“She loves you, too, you know,” she whispered.

“It’s not like that, and no, she doesn’t,” Agatha hissed back. “She’s my friend, didn’t you want me to make more of those? And it’s been five days. Let’s not scare off the only one.”

“I’m just telling you what I see.”

“Bella, I am not going to explain the difference between psychic and clairvoyant again. But you can’t fight fate, remember that.”

“Chop,” she instructed, sliding over a cutting board with peeled garlic on it and handing Agatha a knife. “And so help me, if you were about to ask me who would want you.”

“Minced, bella, not slices. I’m not even entertaining that with an answer. I can’t tell you your power, you have to find it on your own. So go. Seek. Find.”

“Did I get it all?” Rio wandered back, face now slightly pinker where she had scrubbed at it, but tomato-free. Agatha nodded and turned her eyes back to the garlic. She could handle minced, but had never mastered the way Lilia could do it without looking. Not without almost losing a finger or two. Lilia shrugged. “Show her anyway.”

“Don’t laugh.”

Rio sat down on the bed next to Agatha. “I take it there’s a reason there’s no bunny here.”

She said it was to teach me a lesson.”

“Winning. Being the best. I at least know he was okay, they let me see him sometimes. For all I know, he’s still living a long, happy bunny life. But I missed him a lot when I first came here. I think Lilia thought these guys would help.”

Agatha absentmindedly stroked the ear of one of the plush bunnies, the very first one, and the one she had loved the most. 

“Did they?”

“As much as anything could.” Agatha shrugged. “The years and years of therapy probably helped more, but they were a really close second.”

“You should get a bunny,” Rio said, with the same genuinely thoughtful tone she seemed to use when she was being especially sweet. “Not a replacement, don’t name him Scratch or anything. Not Mr. Scratch either, I think that’s an unsub on Criminal Minds. Maybe…I’ve got it. Señor Scratchy.”

Agatha laughed at this, a real laugh. “Fine, fine. I live in a one-bedroom apartment that was a studio before some walls were renovated, but sure. I have bunny space.”

“When you want something badly enough, you make room for it. Even if you’re used to how you’ve been living. When it’s meant for you, it fits without crowding you.”

Agatha sat with this for a moment, brain cycling through a million different meanings, finally resorting to her old fallback, deflection.

“Did you get that from a fortune cookie?”

“Bite me,” Rio said, giving Agatha a playful shove. “I heard Lilia, by the way. Her whisper is most people’s talking voice. I do love you. You’re the most genuine, real friend I’ve ever had and it’s only been a few days. Can you imagine us in a few years? We’ll be unstoppable.”

“Oh, so you’ll still be my friend in a few years?”

“I better be.”

Agatha gave Rio an uncharacteristically shy smile. “I think I’d be okay with that. Now come on, we need to get back downstairs before Lilia thinks we’re doing what other people take people up to their bedrooms to do. I’m actually terrified that she wants us to, it’s honestly concerning that she hasn’t come looking for us yet.”

“Menace,” Rio said fondly. “Like mother, like daughter.”

Sure enough, Lilia gave them less of a “look” and more of a questioning stare when they got back downstairs, but had the good sense not to say anything about it, even when they sat down at the table.

“She hates when I do this every year,” she said to Rio. “Even though it really is just a few words.”

“Mama, stop,” Agatha groaned, this time letting Rio hear her say the name. 

“Fourteen years, bella. My time with you is almost old enough to drive a car. And guess what?”

Agatha put her face in her hands, too aware of Rio’s eyes on her.

“You know I’m not going to continue until you say it.”

“What, mama?”

“I love you even more than I did yesterday. But not as much as I will tomorrow.”

Agatha’s eyes flooded before she could fight it back. Lilia said it every year, but before that, she said it when she said goodnight in that bedroom with all the bunnies. It had been a while since it came with a light tug on the covers and a kiss on the forehead, but it still hit the same.

“Fourteen years, bella,” Lilia said again. “Look around. You’ve found your family.”

“I know you’re a birthday hater, but is Lilia?”

Agatha snorted. “God, no. She’s an August Leo. But she refuses to call it a birthday party because it makes her feel old. And gifts are banned. All she wants every year is people to put arms around and homemade sangria. Jen and I are getting an Uber at 4:30 if you want to go.”

“I…yeah. I want to. Should I bring anything?”

“Jen’s on call tonight so she’s only staying for an hour and not drinking. We could get another Uber, but it’s more likely that there will be one more birthday demand.”

“Which is…?”

“For us to spend the night and make crepes in the morning. It’s kind of a tradition. You’d just have to, you know. Bring your own clothes this time. Unless you’re hell-bent on wearing mine again, but keep in mind it won’t just be me who will see you.”

“Oh god.” Rio laughed. “We saw a movie and Lilia thought we were dating. If I go to bed in your pajamas and change into another one of your shirts in the morning, she’ll hear wedding bells.”

The phone slipped in Agatha’s hand and this time she really did drop it, wincing as she heard the crunch on the pavement signaling the end of her screen protector’s life.

“Do I need to call an ambulance?” Rio asked when she put the phone back to her ear.

“Dropped my phone. And it scared away the ducks—fuck, that hurt.”

A jagged piece of screen protector had chipped off, leaving a clean slice down the far side of her cheek.

“You good?”

“Face might be bleeding a little, but I’m fine. I needed a new screen protector anyway.”

“Don’t worry,” Rio said. “With your hair like that, no one will be able to tell.”

“You’re probably right, it’ll be…how do you know what my hair looks like?”

The call disconnected.

“Turn around, goofball.”

Rio was about ten feet away, waving far too excitedly for being that close. She was wearing a black tank top and her arms were once again covered in purple ink, this time ghosts and leaves.

“Very spooky,” Agatha commented.

Rio settled on the bench next to her and groaned. “I hate ghosts.”

“Lady Death hating ghosts?”

“Lilia’s daughter, I know you know that actual death and tarot death aren’t the same thing. It’s part of a fall promotion…even though it’s still the dead of summer…and I swear, this is what draws the most people in. I can do all those flowers, stencil loved ones’ handwriting, I recreate a lot of toddler drawings for parents, but without fail every year the blob with eyes is the winner.”

“My first one is going to be a ghost just to piss you off.”

Rio put her hand to her chest, feigning offense. “You wouldn’t.” 

“Try me.” Agatha folded her arms across her chest and smirked. “How did you know I was here? Your spidey senses can’t be that good.”

“They’re not. My studio is on the other side of those trees.” Rio pointed and as a slight breeze hit, Agatha could almost see the painted tarot cards on the windows through the branches. “I clocked out and decided to go for a walk and saw you way before you would have seen me. Fucking with you was impossible to resist. You have the best seat in the house, though. Hela said those ducks come back every year.”

“Mulder and Scully,” Agatha mumbled.

“You named them?” Rio shrieked, ignoring the frantic shushing that inevitably followed. “I’m never letting you live this down, I hope you know that. Unless…oh. You named them for a sad reason, didn’t you?”

“When Wanda was being…Wanda…they were fun to watch. It was nice to be around something that couldn’t hurt anything. It’s fine, though. Ancient history.”

Her face betrayed her and she knew it, but she kept the overly nonchalant demeanor. It normally worked as a mask, but around Rio, it was a masquerade mask at best—still on her face, but hiding too little of herself for Rio to be blind to who was underneath it. 

Rio took her hand and squeezed gently. “Do you name the babies or just dad and mama?”

“I’ve been watching them for three years now. They’ve had as many as twelve babies before, usually closer to ten. I don’t even think I know that many names.”

“Aren’t you a professor? At multiple colleges?”

“Details.”

“Important details.”

“I’m not letting my ducks have names that could be ruined by my students. Teaching is all fun and games until you run into them at CVS in the middle of the night with a box of tampons in one hand and pint of ice cream in the other. If there’s a chance I won’t be able to look someone with that name in the eye later, I’m not using it on a duck.”

Rio nudged Agatha’s side with her elbow. “So, uh…what name got ruined? That’s way too specific to be a hypothetical.”

Agatha sighed. “I named two of them Phoebe and Joey last year, another set of parents that didn’t come back this year. I…saw Joey at CVS. At least it’s ducks, it would be so much worse with a human kid.”

“You want human kids?”

Agatha stiffened and Rio’s hold on her hand got tighter in response. 

“Undecided. Sometimes I think I could do it. On more rare occasions, I even think I could be good at it. But then I come to my senses, every time. I don’t think Evanora Harkness DNA needs to be shared with an innocent baby. Her bloodline needs to die out with me. Oh god, I know that look on your face. You’re about to make me think.”  

“You know me so well.” Rio released Agatha’s hand to put an arm around her shoulder and pull her in a little closer. “It would be more your DNA than hers. What if you don’t kill her bloodline? What if you save it?”

The static feeling in Agatha’s brain was almost deafening, and certainly not something she thought she could speak over. She just hugged her arms to herself a little tighter, tilting her head back to let the breeze catch her hair.

“I can’t imagine a worse time to have to tell you this,” Rio started. “But speaking of bloodline. Your face.”

“Screen protector got me?”

“You definitely don’t need stitches but maybe a band-aid, I can’t tell. Hold still for a second.”

Rio fished around in her bag, pulling out a water bottle and travel pack of tissues.

“Do you keep your entire house in there, or is it just a storage unit?”

“Hush.” Rio tilted the bottle, letting it soak into a tissue. “Seriously, stop wiggling so I don’t miss, the blush you used looks really good in this lighting and I don’t want to mess it up.”

Agatha bit back the urge to admit that she wasn’t wearing makeup beyond a quick swipe of mascara on both eyes. Any blush she was wearing, well. That had other sources.

“Almost got it. I don’t think you need a band-aid, I think it just bled a lot.”

“I feel like I’ve told you before that I’m a walking disaster. You took me hiking, I’m definitely injury-prone.”

“I don’t know what I’m going to do with you,” Rio said affectionately, putting the water bottle back in her bag and pointing at Agatha’s phone. “Replace that, though. I’m not going to stop calling you anytime soon and if you keep holding that up to your face, it won’t be Hinge that does you in, it’ll be death by a thousand cuts.”

“Lilia wouldn’t like that on her birthday.”

“Lilia wouldn’t like that at all.” For once in her life, Agatha didn’t have an argument. “Break’s almost over, by the way. I wish I could stay here longer, but I have more ghosts to draw and a couple appointments this afternoon before I can take off.”

Their earlier conversation flooded back and upon looking closer, Agatha could see that Rio’s eyes were still a bit puffy and her eyeliner was smudged in the corners like it had been furiously wiped at. Before she could lose her nerve, Agatha pulled her into a hug bordering on strangulation, silently promising herself that this time, for maybe the first time in her life, she wouldn’t be the one to let go of a hug first.

“You’re coming, right?” she murmured into Rio’s hair. “Don’t be like me. Don’t withhold it from yourself because you think you’re intruding. Please.”

“I’m coming,” Rio said finally. “Room in that Uber for one more? I promise…I’ll only bring clothes that belong to me.”

They’d reached the point of hugging where Agatha tended to get a little jumpy, but Rio hadn’t made any moves to let go, so she kept her arms locked firmly in place. There was a warmth on her shoulder, and she strongly suspected Rio was crying again, but didn’t ask. If the roles were reversed, she’d more than likely be alone, but the memory of the drive-in and the grounding feeling of Rio’s hand running through her hair while she lost her shit over cartoon fish stuck with her. This was grief rearing its ugly head, and grieving people needed people, whether they knew how to ask for that in the moment or not. All those nights Lilia had held her that first year, rubbed her back until she drifted back to sleep after the nightmares had so rudely interrupted the only thing resembling rest she’d gotten in over a decade—as an adult, she was able to concede that those nights had saved her. Rio definitely didn’t need saving, but the way her hands clung to Agatha’s shirt, nails digging into her shoulder blades…someone else carrying the weight for a few fleeting moments didn’t hurt. 

“Don’t worry,” Rio said, pulling back and wiping at her eyes again. “If anything, Nat will think I’m high. Actually…yeah, you can worry about that. On my honor, I have only ever been stoned off the clock.”

“You okay though?”

“I’m good,” Rio promised. “Nothing an unbirthday party and a little sangria won’t fix.”

“Through the Looking Glass.”

“A classic. Your students are too old for that, right?”

“These ones are. When I was student teaching, I got paired with a class of fifth graders. Did a whole unit on it with the littles. They loved Alice.”

Rio extended her left arm, tilting it so her forearm was more clearly visible. In the middle of the sea of flowers were the words ‘we’re all mad here’ in cursive just flawed enough that it was impossible it had come from a stencil.

“Mamí’s handwriting.”

Agatha ran her fingers over the words. “It’s perfect. I know it doesn’t fix it, but we’re pretty mad here, too.”

“I know,” Rio said, pulling Agatha in for one more hug. “I love this rabbit hole you’ve pulled me into.”

“I think you’re the one who did the pulling. I was so ready to spend Jen’s party at the bar looking angry and unapproachable.”

“I got you to go to a diner looking angry and unapproachable.”

“No,” Agatha said softly. “Definitely unapproachable, but this has been…I haven’t had good days like this in a long, long, long, long, long time.”

“I love you to death, you know that, right?” Rio asked, walking backward a couple steps toward her studio.

“Real death or tarot death?”

“Both. Always both.”
