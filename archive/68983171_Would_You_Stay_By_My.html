Summary

At seventeen, Agatha thought she knew where her life was headed. She was destined for bigger things and was convinced that she would see them out with her girlfriend, Rio, by her side.

When her mother chased Rio off one night with a shotgun after catching them together, Rio was never heard from again.

Agatha moved on, tried to do something with her life, but has found herself right back in the place she swore she'd never be again. Running her own Vet clinic, her past has been left behind her, but when an unassuming stray turns up outside her door, Agatha's life changes forever.

Chapter 1: One.

There was this thing that sometimes happened in life, something Agatha Harkness learned very early on.

It was a thing that made little sense, a thing that felt as though it existed beyond space and time.

It was a thing of beauty, a thing of wonder.

It was a moment, a singular pocket of ticking seconds where she looked at someone, they looked back, and she felt as though the whole world had suddenly splayed out before her, open and intent with every new beginning imaginable.

That was what it felt like the first time (late August of her sophomore year) Agatha laid eyes on Rio Vidal.

She was new in town, having come from a town perhaps a bit smaller than Belle Rose, but her mother had said they needed a change. That change came from the fact that everyone around had hated her mother and tried to run her out of town. It had worked, obviously, and they had moved. Agatha had been ready for another year of trying to acclimate, trying to fit in, trying to find a way, but she hadn’t needed to.

The moment she laid eyes on the girl with her books hugged to her chest, her dark hair in soft ringlets as it laid over her shoulders, with those impossibly big and brown eyes, Agatha knew her way then and there. She knew that the kind smile, the sweet head tilt, the warm voice of the girl she swore could speak with the wind itself, Agatha knew where she needed to be in life.

It was right by Rio Vidal’s side.

They were the closest of friends for those first couple of years, sharing in typical teenage banter of celebrity crushes and the frets of what to wear to school the next day. They watched films, went on walks through the woods outside the town. They ran the streets like absolute heathens until the hours of the night when their mothers beckoned them home.

They giggled in grocery store aisles and made too much noise after the sun went down, but Agatha didn’t care. All she cared about was that in a world where her mother tried to see to it that her life was bland and miserable, Rio still existed as a defiant middle finger to such a way of thinking.

It was the beginning of senior year when Agatha finally realized just how deeply her feelings had become for the other girl and while it was shocking to embrace the idea that they could be more, it was all Agatha had really wanted.

While she had known, there had still been so much left to be determined on Rio’s end, at least until the night of Agatha’s seventeenth birthday.

They’d gotten malts at the soda shoppe run by the town’s Gentle Giant, Mr. Davis, and he had wished Agatha the best year ahead with a small stash of sweets. They had dug into it on the sidewalk outside, with Rio pulling out a Bit-O-Honey, one of Agatha’s favorites.

“You don’t even like those, give it to me,” she ordered, an expectant hand flipping out.

“Agatha, really? These are what the elderly use to toughen up their gums,” Rio told her matter-of-factly, both of her eyebrows arch to show her skepticism.

“I don’t give you shit for your weird obsession with Pop Rocks. Let me have this, would you?” Agatha asked back through a giggle. She made a play for the piece of candy, reaching across Rio to do so, but Rio knew what she was doing the second she pulled her hand away, leaving Agatha nearly pressed to her chest.

Their eyes met, steady but unsure, and Agatha could smell the sugar curling off of Rio’s breath, sweet like butter mints.

Time stilled, the wind stopped blowing. The street light above them flickered and then burned brighter, as if it were reacting to the electricity surging between them. Then and only then did Rio gift her the most important thing on the most important day.

She kissed Agatha.

It was a moment outside of time, a moment that existed in some dream-like plane where only good things could happen. It had been slow and soft, but the heat it had brought forth had been completely unmistakable.

It was their first kiss, but it was not their last. They shared them in bathroom stalls at school, in back alleys behind the grocery store or hardware shop. They shared them under the bleachers and behind the equipment shed. Even bolder, they started to share them in their bedrooms when no one else would ever be aware.

Or so Agatha thought.

Evanora usually left them to whatever it was they wanted to do, only casually keeping an eye on them, but they had miscalculated just how much they could get away with. One moment, it was soft lips and fingers tangled in velvety strands of hair and the next, it was shouting and screaming and every slur imaginable that Evanora could use that tiny pea brain to come up with. And then, she was gone.

Rio was up, moving, redressing and getting ready to run when Evanora made her return.

Shotgun in hand.

Was she out of her fucking mind?!

Agatha screamed herself hoarse that night, before Rio had taken off and after, asking her mother what in the fresh hell she was thinking. She had turned those slurs on Agatha, struck her in the face hard enough to bring her to her knees and demanded she never be questioned or disrespected in such a fashion ever again.

The weeks that followed were all but destitute. Agatha had turned up at school on Monday ready to spew any and all apologies she could possibly find to the girl she knew she’d come to love, but… Rio never showed up.

Not Monday, not Tuesday, or Wednesday, Thursday, or Friday.

Rio never came back.

Agatha had gone to Pilar’s home, asking where Rio was, but Pilar had been frantic and sobbed relentlessly, having thought she was simply hiding away with Agatha for whatever reason.

Rio was just… gone.

Never to be heard from again.

A seventeen year old tender-hearted and compassionate human being was just… nowhere.

To Agatha, she was still everywhere all at once.

She sometimes swore she saw her; sometimes, it was around the corner of the equipment shed or floating like a shadow beneath the bleachers. Sometimes, Agatha thought she spotted her coming out of the soda shoppe only to realize it was another girl from school. It wasn’t Rio, it never was.

Her relationship with her mother stayed contentious for the year she had to stay to finish high school, but the moment she was able to run, it was exactly what Agatha chose to do. She grasped at her acceptance letter to LSU, packed every single item that meant anything to her (including the silver framed photo Pilar had taken of herself and Rio on her seventeenth birthday only hours before everything had changed) and she took off.

She moved into a dorm room, she buried herself in her studies (which first started with psychology and then diverted to veterinary medicine her second year) and knew she would make something of herself, if only for the excuse to never return to Belle Rose so long as her mother was still alive. She moved at the beginning of each school year to reduce the risk of being tracked down and by the time she graduated veterinary school eight years after she’d left her hometown, she knew she was finally free.

New Orleans had been the place to plant roots, or so she thought. It had been a good first year; she joined another clinic, worked under a seasoned vet who taught her plenty of things that a university curriculum could not and helped her hone her skills. The second year, however, was a little harder.

Things went wrong left and right; Agatha’s apartment building caught fire when children were left unattended in the next unit over and somehow (and STUPIDLY) had access to fireworks they then detonated inside. Shortly after that, her parked car was struck in the parking lot of the grocery store while she had been purchasing things to make a damn gumbo, totaling it out.

By the time her temporary placement had been struck by lightning and caught fire as well, Agatha was beginning to feel as though the universe was trying to tell her something.

Three days later, she realized she was right.

In the process of finishing up paperwork at the end of her shift, Agatha had not expected her phone to ring. She had also not expected the mortician from Belle Rose to be the one calling her. When she heard what the man had to say, though, everything changed.

Evanora was dead. Heart attack a month prior. But no one had been to check on her, so she’d simply sat in that walled up castle and rotted away, just as she deserved. There was satisfaction to be found, Agatha thought, in knowing that no one had cared enough to notice her passing. No one cared that she hadn’t left the house or that she hadn’t heckled the townsfolk. No one seemed to give a shit that the stain on the town was suddenly less visible; how could they? Evanora had been vicious and cruel to all that had crossed her path.

Agatha knew that more than anyone.

Evanora’s cruelty was the reason Agatha laid down at night and stared at the photo on her bedside table, tracing out every last feature of the girl she had once loved until her mind was tired enough to fall asleep.

There were too many nights Agatha spent wondering about Rio, about where she was, what she was doing. Pilar had kept in contact since Agatha’s departure, keeping her updated on a search that never bore any fruit and her own life as she grew tired of the town that held shadows of a daughter lost to time. Agatha had been honest with her at one point, telling her that it was Evanora’s fault and it was where the blame should be placed, but Pilar was too kind for that. She wasn’t kind enough to care for Evanora in the way she did the other folks in town, but she hadn’t completely choked her to death and held her accountable for Rio’s disappearance the way she probably should have.

With the news of her death, Agatha had reached out again, but Rio’s mother had informed her that she’d since left and was existing in her own peace in a home that sat along the edge of Mobile Bay. It was an escape for her, one that offered her sights and visions free from the spectre of Rio and what her life could have been if she would have simply stayed.

Agatha knew it all too well; it had been a decade since the last time she had seen Rio Vidal and yet, when she stepped foot back into Belle Rose, Rio was all she saw.

She saw their picnic spot in the part, the gazebo they spent too much time in. She saw the closed down buildings they spent so much time in when they were coffee shops and little delis. The town had become a shell of what it once was, but something about it breathed familiar, like a whisper Agatha had longed to hear. She knew she should have only been there to sign off on her mother’s creation, but when she found herself outside of that dilapidated and run-down home she’d spent her youth in, something bigger began to bloom.

What if she came back?

It was a stupid question; her life was going… Okay, who was she kidding? With the way things had been happening since the start of the year, her life had been fucking miserable. One inconvenience followed the six before it and she knew it was pushing her closer and closer to a breaking point. At least if she was back where she belonged, the inconveniences might be softened by the dream-like idea that maybe someday, Rio would return home.

It was a faulty line of thinking and an irrational one at that, but there was the tiniest murmur in the back of Agatha’s mind that said if Rio would turn up anywhere at any given time despite not having done so in almost ten years, it would be Belle Rose.

She had slept in a hotel outside of New Orleans for two nights before making up her mind. She did care about her job, but she had the means and the drive to do something more, something different, something better. So, she bought one of the empty buildings in Belle Rose, gathered every grant and loan she could, and started the process of opening her own clinic. It had taken several months, but it had finally come to be in the most grand way Agatha could have ever imagined.

Now, if she could just get her ex to stop calling.

Agatha had dated a woman, Natalie, when she’d been living in the city, but it had ended long before the news of Evanora’s fortunate passing and long before Agatha had decided to move, but she never stopped calling. She wanted to try again, to fix what she had fucked up, but Agatha wanted no part in it. How could she when the last memory she had of the other woman was her pinned to some bar back alley being devoured whole by the leggy bartender?

Natalie had made claims to come see her, to do right by her, but Agatha ignored her every time. She didn’t share her location, didn’t even drop hints as to how she could be found. Once she had the Harkness home demolished and moved into a two-story farmhouse near the outskirts of town, Agatha just wanted peace.

She became reacquainted with the buildings, the streets, the people she had known growing up, but there was still something missing.

Rio. It was Rio that was still missing.

There were still times Agatha combed through news reports of unidentified bodies, perhaps both hoping and dreading to maybe find her there. Of course she didn’t want Rio to be dead, but at least being dead, she still would have been found. It would have been an answer, a definitive one with an underline that wouldn’t have left Agatha living out the rest of her life with questions.

They had been young and naive and it had been ten damn years ago. So why couldn’t Agatha let her go? She had dated throughout college and even after (ie; Natalie), but nothing ever felt right, not the way it did when she had been young and in love.

That was what it had been, between her and Rio. It was love. Maybe they’d never said it, never been brave enough, but it didn’t change the actual feeling itself. It didn’t negate it or divert it or morph it into something it wasn’t. When Agatha had been in the presence and arms of Rio, she’d always felt it. Rio had loved her much in the same way she had loved Rio.

But they had been kids. Life happened. Her mother happened.

So all Agatha could do, even being back where it all began and ended, was wonder where in the world Rio could possibly be.

Maybe she was living on the coast of California, sipping at strong drinks and watching the sun sink over the ocean as another day came to a close. Maybe she moved up north; Rio always said she wanted to experience the cold. Perhaps she’d found herself a nice little cottage on a snowy mountainside with endless coffee and strong whiskey. Those all seemed more plausible than ever thinking Rio would stay nearby, not with that terrified look on her face when she had run off.

But Agatha would always ask herself…

Why hadn’t Rio ever come back? Why had she stayed away? Did her fears of Evanora really outweigh what it was that they had been letting burn between them?

Then again, that was a lot to ask of a teenager. But her mother…

Pilar carried Rio’s disappearance with her for years, Agatha had known. She did not blame Agatha, but in a way, Agatha blamed herself. She knew it was not her fault, exactly, but she had taken on the guilt of knowing she had not been enough of an anchor to bring her home. She had even maintained hope in her absence that Pilar might reach out, might share the joyous news that Rio had finally come home, but she never did.

Wherever Rio was in the world, Agatha simply hoped she was happy.

And as such, she tried to stop thinking about her so often. She tried, yes. It rarely worked.

After three months and the clinic still going strong, Agatha was looking to grow her staff. It was solely her manning all duties and while she was more than capable of sailing the ship alone, she couldn’t help but feel a bit lonely. Perhaps some form of company might cheer her up.

The universe heard such a thought, but the answer it had to provide was not one she ever could have expected.

It was a Tuesday afternoon and Agatha had been tending to the end steps of a parvo shot for the Shar-Pei pup that belonged to Alice Gulliver, one she had recently purchased from a less than reputable breeder. The puppy was not in terrible shape, but still had a little ways to go before she could be laid back in the arms of the human who clearly already loved her dearly. With a lack of spatial awareness, Agatha turned to throw the needle into the biohazard bin when the bottom of her hand caught the lid of a glass jaw filled with swabs and sent it toppling over the edge of the counter.

The sound of the glass shattering was impossibly loud, but with an eight week old puppy in her hands, it was not the first thing she could tend to. Quickly, she stowed the dog in the small kennel and tended to the mess, sweeping it up with care into a dustpan and taking enough care to not injure herself in the process. With all shards and now-ruined swabs collected, she made her way toward the back exit and the awaiting dumpster to do away with the product of her carelessness.

The moment she hit the air, despite it being unbearably hot, something close to a shiver shot up her spine, strengthening by the time she reached the edge of the dumpster. It was why, as she lifted the dustpan, the end clipped the lip of metal and dumped onto the concrete at its base. With a defeated sigh and that strange chill still wrapped around her spine, Agatha knelt to tend to her mess. This time, more care was needed to make sure no fragments were left behind for some unaware wild or stray animal to step on. Once satisfied, she rose again and successfully dumped the contents into the dumpster. Before she could retreat back into the comforts of her air conditioned clinic, something scared the absolute fuck out of her.

It was loud, it was deep, and it was entirely unexpected.

“BOOF!”

She spun on her heel, wide blue eyes landing on the perpetrator only a few feet away. Upon taking a full survey of what laid before her, those eyes widened even more.

There, poised and perky, was a perfect depiction of a Belgian Malinois. The dog’s fur was beautifully colored, a deep rust around her body and haunches, but the chest, eyes and snout were all masked in black, giving the beast a regal but somewhat frightening appearance.

“Well, hey there, gorgeous,” she soothed, though no sudden movements were made. She was not certain where the animal had come from (as it was not one she had noticed in town before, not one that seemed to belong in such a place) but given her profession, the impulse to check the dog over was immediate and strong. “What’cha doin’ out here all alone?”

On slow and slightly uncertain feet, Agatha shuffled forward, careful to refrain from making any sudden movements that might startle the beautiful creature before her. The dog breathed a huff and then began to lower to the ground, giving Agatha enough space to glance to try and gauge the sex. Judging by what she could see, the dog appeared female, but she knew she could not be entirely certain without a closer inspection.

It appeared as though it was exactly what she would get as she dropped herself into a squat. Still tight in stature, the dog placed herself to the ground and let her dark snout rest in the comfort of the lull between the front two legs, big brown eyes shifting up as Agatha came closer.

“Well, aren’t you a beaut?” she murmured softly, critical blues scanning over the dog’s form, checking for any visual bumps or cuts that might have lent to the currently docile nature she appeared to be exhibiting.

Truthfully, for her breed, this felt… unnatural. Malinois were high-energy, demanding of attention and care. Yet, there this beautiful specimen sat looking so lost for the world.

She was still running her scans in her mind, taking note of no physical wounds but of the state of the poor girl nonetheless. She looked hungry (ribs clear on display) and dirty (dusty fur that was matted in spots) and like she hadn’t had somewhere nice to sleep in a very, very long time.

“You’re bein’ awfully friendly for a natural protector.”

Still tentative and still overly cautious, a non-threatening hand reached toward the crown of the canine’s head. Agatha had been expecting the worst in the form of a snap or a clamping of jaws, but it never came. Instead, the dog stayed rather still and embraced the touch of a human for the first time in who knew how long.

She was a beautiful dog, truly, and Agatha had a very difficult time believing that someone was not looking for her. That was even harder to buy with the way she was reacting to a simple touch; it wasn’t a fearful reaction, but it seemed as though it was welcome in the way that it was familiar.

“Oh, you are pretty, aren’t ya?” came her quiet whisper, a sound filled with wonder and curiosity.

The very second the words left her lips, the dog responded in kind, lifting her head into the touch, trying to get more of it. It was so playful, so undemanding, and strangely, Agatha reacted in turn.

She simply laughed.

It was soft, but there was an unmistakable joy there at the reception she was getting from the animal. She did not have to trust Agatha, or to allow her any access to her at all but she sat completely still other than enjoying the gentle petting that was being offered.

It had been a long while since Agatha had laughed, since she’d truly found something to laugh for, not at. Yet, there was this silly little dog who would have had every reason in the world to bite her hand clean off, acting like this attention was the first she had been paid in some time. How could that be? How was no one scouring the streets for her, calling her name? How did she not have a collar, or a place to call home? She seemed like a good dog, though Agatha was making surface level assumptions based on what she was seeing then and there.

A dirty, well-traveled, tired pup who just wanted a break and maybe a decent meal.

Slowly and carefully, Agatha’s hand moved from the top of the beast’s head down to her chin, offering a new brand of light scratches along it as well as her jawline.

“You hungry, sweet girl?” she asked, though she did not pull her hand away. She knew that no answer would come (she was not a Dolittle, after all), but she still watched, gauging whether or not the dog might have understood or had any interest.

She might not have been expecting an answer, but she got one anyway.

The Malinois pushed to all four of her feet and her tail began to wag excitedly behind her, like the promise of food had offered her a new found burst of energy. Her head tilted back and those warm but soft dark eyes lifted, almost pleading in their gaze. It was clear that the ball of matted fur below her had most certainly seen some things, but it was also clear that all she really needed was someone to pay her some attention and give her a little boost to continue on her way. Maybe she was just passing through, or maybe this was where she wanted to stay. Nevertheless, Agatha knew she couldn’t turn a blind eye to a creature in need.

The moment the dog padded forward and dropped her head, she knew she could never have left her out there with nothing.

“Gimme a minute, okay?” Her hand lifted from the dog’s chin to her head, giving one last little round of pets before she pushed to her feet and paced back inside the clinic to retrieve some kibble and a fresh bottle of purified water. Feeding the nearby strays wasn’t an uncommon task, but Agatha’s mind stayed rooted in the visual of that beautiful canine sitting so soldierly behind her when she’d been tending to the broken glass. She seemed like the kind of pet that belonged to someone active, someone with authority, someone who liked to scare off anyone who didn’t need to be around. At the same time, the demeanor the dog possessed was… off.

It wasn’t necessarily a bad thing, but either she had been abused or she had been abandoned for far longer than Agatha wanted to be comfortable with. If that was the case, however, there were more questions.

Why was this the first of her that Agatha had seen? No one in town had made much mention of seeing her, or that Agatha should be on the look-out for a threatening-looking dog scouring the town streets. It wasn’t like the town was that big and she could have missed her. So where did she come from and why did she not seem so keen to keep moving?

With the questions still swirling around pointlessly in her mind, Agatha returned outside with a bowl of kibble and a bottle of water, intent on at least filling the poor girl’s belly enough to give her some energy. Maybe the lack of it was what was keeping her stagnant and idle.

“Here you go, precious,” she offered sweetly, laying out the meal atop the asphalt before pouring some of the water into a spare bowl she’d brought with her. “Eat up. Go home, if you have one. I bet your owners are missing a pretty girl like you.”

Again, having conversations with animals fell a little on the needless side, but the human voice was capable of many things and if one of them could be calming a nervous and skittish animal, she would utilize it for that.

She wanted to reach out again, to lightly jostle the fur between those black ears, but she kept to herself and hovered for only a moment, simply assessing. After an additional beat of silence, she retreated inside to take on the rest of her day.

After a couple more shots and an afternoon feed, Agatha scrolled through Facebook, chuckling softly at some of the shenanigans posted by townies in the local Uncensored group and had to roll her eyes. For as much as the town had changed in some ways, there were still plenty of others in which it had not. It would always be the kind of echo chamber where opinions and thoughts were mostly shared, but never by Agatha. She would always be free, be an independent thinker. It was part of her charm.

As the day began to close in on itself, there was little left to do and she knew that she was destined for another night alone on her couch with a pint of Chunky Monkey and a glass of red wine that came out of a box she bought at the closest gas station.

Before she departed, though, she thought to peek behind the clinic to see if her guest from earlier in the day had run off.

Finding her asleep on a cardboard box not too far from the dumpster, Agatha realized she had not wanted to leave. It was probably the first kindness she had been shown in some time, not to mention the first sizable meal she’d had likely in weeks.

The dog stirred at the sound of her boots atop the concrete, her head lifting in alarm, but Agatha did not shoo her off or tell her to leave. She placed both hands on her hips, a helpless gaze landing on the ball of fur at her feet.

“You’re not very good at listenin’ are ya?” she asked quietly. Her lips folded over sharp teeth, rolling and rubbing as she tried to take an emotional inventory of the things she was feeling at seeing the helpless pup who appeared to have been defeated by nothing other than life itself.

As if the dog could somehow hear her thoughts, she rolled to her stomach and stood, marching right over to plant herself resolutely in a sitting position right at Agatha’s feet. As if that were not enough of an answer, her mouth fell open into a somewhat charming dog-like smile, her tongue falling out of her mouth for added effect.

She was cute. Even more, she seemed like a truly good dog, one that someone somewhere surely had to be missing.

A soft huff of a sigh rushed through her nose and she knew there was nothing else left to do than simply take the animal inside and give her somewhere cool and dry to sleep for the night.

“Come on, then,” she offered, tongue clicking in a coaxing manner in an attempt to be a little more convincing. She whisked her fingers together, a soft sound of skin rubbing that was not quite a snap, and headed for the back door.

Thankfully, the Malinois followed.

Agatha didn’t know what she would actually be able to do past letting her sleep in the clinic, but if she heard nothing in regard to anyone seeking the dog out, she supposed she could do a general exam and at least look for a microchip the next morning. It wasn’t a guarantee, but if there was somewhere this beautiful dog needed to be where she could be loved and cared for, Agatha wanted to help her get there.

She was surprised at the level of obedience and calm exhibited as the dog trotted along behind her, following her into a room that was typically used for cleaning and recovery. In it was a rather new and decently sized bed, one she hoped the dog could find comfort in for the night.

It took only a few moments to gather a fresh bowl of food and one for water, setting them next to a nearby cabinet. When she righted herself, she turned her head to find the dog still sitting quietly, just watching her, pensive almost. Agatha knew enough to know that whatever had happened to her? To that beautiful, too-sweet girl that clearly only wanted safety and comfort? It wasn’t a pretty tale, but it was also one she would likely never know.

“Y’can sleep here tonight, but I’m gonna check ya for a microchip tomorrow. Someone’s probably missing you and we should try to get ya back home.” She’d said it before, but she meant it even more as she said it again. Looking down at those doughy and soft brown eyes, she tried to steel herself, arms folding over her chest as she prepared to offer a warning. Before she could speak, though, the dog lifted herself up and trotted forward, placing herself directly next to her. The way she looked up, it almost felt like she was offering unspoken adoration, but it melted Agatha all the same.

“For the love of Christ, please, please don’t destroy anything. I know you won’t listen, but if you could keep the damages down to a minimum? Fantastic.”

All the dog did was lick at her chops, as if saying she was waiting for a chance to bite at the drywall.

That could be tomorrow’s problem.

Agatha fought the urge to reach down to pet her again, instead offering a consolation for having to leave. “I’ll leave one of the lights on. Y’know, in case you’re one of them that gets scared in the dark.”

With a lopsided smile at the doggy-like grin she received, Agatha turned and headed toward the doorway, flicking off two of the lights before she latched the gate and made her exit.

Even after returning home, showering, and making herself dinner, Agatha simply couldn’t stop thinking about the new arrival or the strange air of potential she brought with her.

Maybe she belonged to someone, maybe she didn’t.

Whatever the case, Agatha was already happy that she would get to see her again in the morning.

 

Chapter 2: Two.

Arriving the next morning to the clinic, Agatha was prepared for the worst. She knew that keeping the stray (even overnight) was a terrible idea, but it would have absolutely weighed on her conscience to let her continue to roam the streets. After all, given her breed she seemed rather docile. Maybe there was still a chance she wouldn’t have completely obliterated the cabinetry.

When Agatha stepped into the doorway, her hand falling to the top of the gate, a relieved sigh left her at the sight of the dog curled up on the bed and not a thing amiss.

Agatha would admit, she was surprised.

The dog hadn’t seemed aggressive or skittish the day before, but something in her just seemed… anxious. It was in her eyes that morning as she padded over to the gate, her tail wagging behind her happily in greeting. She looked up at Agatha like she was thankful, like she felt relief that she had returned. It was stupid; Agatha couldn’t speak to animals or hear their thoughts, but when she looked into those big brown eyes, she saw more.

She saw miles traveled, toughened paws. She saw matted fur and dirty ears.

After she checked for a microchip to find nothing, Agatha knew only what she was capable of doing. She couldn’t offer the dog a permanent home, but she could clean her up and give her somewhere climate controlled to rest for a little while.

It somehow still surprised her when she washed the dog down just how calm she was. She didn’t fight, didn’t snarl or growl or try to get away. She stood with her eyes barely closed and the tiniest smile across her snout, almost like she was enjoying it.
