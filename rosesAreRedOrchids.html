Chapter one

0830, September 30th 2026

A clattering sound from the backroom disturbed Agatha from her work. She’d been finishing off a flash sheet of different snakes heads and skulls with intricate vines wrapped around them, something Teen noted would be ‘perfect for spooky season’ - whatever the fuck that was, when a noise that sounded very much like a large box of small jewellery falling to the ground rang out. 

“Shit”, came a groan from the back, “Jen is going to kill me.”

Alice, the source of the noise and Agatha’s former apprentice, was, in her entire fifty-one years on this earth, the most unlucky person Agatha had ever met. Indeed, instances like this happened at least once a week. This time she noticed, hovering in the door frame of the storage room, the younger woman had knocked over a tray of newly autoclaved jewellery that Jen was no doubt planning to use today. Shit indeed. 

Jen was Agatha’s piercer. A position she’d been forced to hire as her clientele had slowly but surely evolved over the years, from misfits and miscreants to the kind of sweet young thing who wanted a septum piercing and Hello Kitty tattoo. 

May the record state that while Agatha had given in to the demand for cartilage rings and double nose piercings, she’d burn the entire place to the ground before branding someone with that stupid cartoon cat. 

Anyway, back to the matter at hand. Alice was down on her knees searching for the tiny pieces of jewellery that had rolled every which way when Agatha coughed, making her presence known. The woman looked up at her through too long bangs, a pink blush blooming across her cheeks. 

“Agatha”, she paused sheepishly, “I, uh, thought your first appointment wasn’t until eleven. How, uh, how are you?” Alice grimaced and stood up, dusting down her black cargo pants and sweeping the jewellery she collected from her hand into the tray. 

Agatha just stared her down, raised an eyebrow and spun on her heel. “Alice”, she called over her shoulder, “you’d better pop out and get Jen a coffee to apologise for that sorry state of affairs or she’ll have your guts for garters. And get me my usual while you’re at it.”

That’s how Alice found herself stuck in the work rush at the local coffee shop, waiting for two flat whites, a tea and Agatha’s ‘usual’ - an appalling order which involved four shots of espresso, vanilla and hazelnut syrup. It was a mystery the woman hadn’t suffered some kind of cardiac incident, or at the very least was able to keep her hands still enough to ink. As she waited, Alice made idle chat with Lilia, the coffee shop’s owner and resident Westview gossip. 

“Alice darling how are you? All going well at the shop, Agatha’s not overworking you is she.” Alice liked Lilia, she had a kind disposition and had welcomed her to Westview when she moved there after her mother’s death all those years ago. It was Lilia who had gotten her a job as an apprentice with Agatha, after she spent months plagued by bad luck, jumping from job to job all of which ended in disaster. Agatha and Lilia’s friendship was a strange one, not quite maternal, and yet it was clear from their closeness that Lilia had helped Agatha through more than one tough time. Agatha had even recently employed Lilia’s grandson, who went by Teen, as a favour to the coffee shop owner after the boy had been sent to live with her after being kicked out of his boarding school for reasons neither woman had cared to disclose. 

She was brought out of her thoughts by a hand waving in front of her face. 

“Earth to Alice. I said was everything alright dear, you seem awfully zoned out for nine in the morning.”

“Sorry Lilia, I was somewhere else. It’s already been a bit of a day, it won’t surprise you to hear that this is an apology coffee run.” She was rewarded with a chuckle from the coffee shop owner, who grasped her hand gently and shook her head. 

“Oh Alice my dear, whatever are we to do with you. Well, I at least might be able to take your mind off things with some news.” She arched an eyebrow, urging the woman to continue. “Well, it appears that someone is finally moving into the empty shop, across the way from you. In fact, rumour has it the moving vans will be here no later than this afternoon - exciting is it not?”

Exciting was one word for it, Alice pondered as she walked back with the drinks. She was sure though that it was not the word her boss was going to use when she relayed the information. Agatha was territorial by nature, and the idea that someone unknown to the small town of Westview taking over the space opposite her shop would likely put her in a foul mood for the rest of the week. She was even debating keeping the news to herself, loath to start her day on an even worse foot than she already had, when she spotted a car pulled up on the opposite side of the road to Agatha’s that certainly hadn’t been there when she left. 

A woman was rummaging around in the boot of the car, and emerged triumphantly, just as Alice reached the door of the tattoo parlour, key dangling from her hand. Alice stopped a moment to observe. 

She was beautiful, in a scary sort of way. Long dark hair, lots of kohl eyeliner and wearing tight black pants and a dark green blouse. She looked to be a little older than Alice, early forties maybe, and in the hand that didn’t hold the key she clutched a small plant pot with a purple orchid sprouting from it. Alice was still observing her when the woman unlocked the shop door and turned to push it open, meeting her gaze. The orchid lady smirked and gave Alice a wink, somewhere between sexy and deeply unnerving. Unsure where to go from here, and now painfully aware that this was something she could no longer keep from her boss, Alice retreated into her own shop and laid the coffees down on the reception desk. 

“Teen”, she looked at the newest member of their team, who was currently manning said reception desk, ”how would you like to break some really fun, great news to aunty Agatha.”  

The Teen baulked at the moniker Alice had given his boss. Sure he liked Agatha well enough, she was cool, if a touch overdramatic, and had done him a real solid offering the reception gig after what went down last summer. But ‘Aunt’ was a bit much and more to the point, if the woman in question heard herself being referred to as such she would surely flip her shit. 

“Why do I feel like whatever the news is will be neither fun, nor great, Alice?” 

“Right, okay fine. Not great and not fun. It looks like someone has moved into the shop next door.”

“What!” Agatha made Alice jump for the second time that day. The younger woman gave Teen a look and spun to face her boss. 

“Yeah, so, good news - we have neighbours again.” She put on a fake cheery voice in the hope it might appease - only to be met with Agatha mimicking the line back to her in an affected, childlike whine. 

Agatha was not a people person. Sure, she had her employees and Lilia who she tolerated, but aside from that she was about as unneighbourly as they come. This was clear enough from the relationship she’d had with the previous shop owners, and the ones before that, and before that. It wasn’t that she felt intimidated by a new business opening, her own business was booming and she was confident that wouldn’t change. No, it was the change in status quo that they brought, people in Westview that had no business being there, the types for instance who would come into her shop and ask for Hello Kitty tattoos. Sure things change, and in her defence Agatha knows she’s unable to stop them from doing so, but she sure as hell can help these yuppies from speeding the process up with their craft breweries, pilates studios and vegan beauty stores. 

“Anyway, this new neighbour of ours”, Agatha continued, tone trying to remain unaffected, “do we know what kind of shop they’re setting up?” Before she could get an answer, the bell above the door chimed and all three heads swivelled towards the sound. 

“M’lady.” A woman with dark hair, too tight pants and more kohl eyeliner than the Teen sauntered through the door and over to Agatha. She held out a pot with a purple orchid towards her and bent into a strange half curtsey, half bow. “I’ve just moved into the neighbourhood, pleasure to make your acquaintance.”

0830, September 30th 2026

Rio eased her foot off the gas, slowing to read the large sign that declared: Welcome to Westview, and below that, “Home: It’s Where You Make It”. She sure hoped that was the case as she thought about all her worldly possessions that would arrive in a few hours time, accompanying her to the place she had decided would become home. 

Sometime after 39th birthday, Rio had decided she needed a change. She couldn’t pinpoint one specific moment that had cemented the decision, a mediocre therapist might theorise that it was the thought of turning 40, or perhaps the fear that she had nothing but a string of vengeful ex-girlfriends and a failed business to show for it - Rio hated therapists. No. She was quite certain that it was just regular old antsy-ness, a need to get out of the monotony of her day-to-day existence and uproot herself (if you’ll pardon the pun) to somewhere more exciting. 

Exciting was perhaps not the word you’d use to describe Westview. Not that she’d ever been. Rio had heard of the small town in New Jersey from an old mentor of hers, who had retired there following the death of her husband a few years ago. Sharon Davies had been a big part of Rio’s formative years, teaching her everything there was to know about the flora and fauna that had interested her as a teenager. She’d often spend the hours after school under Mrs. Davies tutelage, and when she left for college Sharon was the only person from her hometown that Rio bothered to keep in contact with. 

Anyways. Westview. 

Driving through the town now, Rio could see the appeal it had to those pushing seventy. And actually, as she cruised down Main Street and turned on to the Witches’ Road it was clear that even the younger generation had made their mark on the town. She passed a smoothie bar with an Instagram-able shop window, followed by a mini-market selling ‘Home Grown Produce’ which included a punnet of strawberries at the very reasonable price of eleven dollars. She grimaced at that but continued to make her way down the road, her SatNav reminding her she was only 400 yards from her destination. 

Pulling into the space outside the empty store front, Rio turned off the car and sat for a moment to further take in her surroundings. To the left, her new venture, and above it, her humble abode. To the right. Rio took her seatbelt off and twisted to get a better view. To the right a large glass window, behind which stood one of the most attractive women Rio had seen in a long time. 

The woman in question looked to be in her early fifties, with long hair that had an untamed quality to it. She was wearing smart purple chinos and a white blouse that was open almost to her sternum, with the sleeves rolled up and a black lace camisole underneath. Tattoos adorned both forearms and from the the looks of it much of her upper body, stopping around her collar bones, where Rio could make out what she assumed to be two snakes slithering across her décolletage. Her hands were free of jewellery and she seemed to be gesticulating wildly to a teenage boy who stood behind the counter, and looked somewhere between amused and afraid. It was impossible to understand what exactly they were discussing, but by the looks of things the woman was acting out someone knocking something over. Before she could figure out anymore of the conversation, it seemed the woman was needed elsewhere as she spun on her heels with a dramatic flick of her hair and disappeared into the shop until Rio could no longer see her. 

Okay. Rio thought. Now this is promising.

Getting out of her car, the woman pondered her new neighbour - she seemed feisty, perhaps overdramatic and definitely hot. A mean combination and certainly one that would supply Rio with all manner of entertainment as she got to know her new home town. She opened the boot of her car, the back of which was filled with some of her more delicate plants, those she didn’t trust to be transported by the big lugs that worked for the company moving her entire life across the country. All still in one piece she was pleased to see. Now, if only she could find the keys to the shop. Aha. Rio spotted them in the corner of the boot, glinting in the morning sunlight behind a beautiful purple orchid. She went to grab them and as she did so, made a split second decision to pick up the plant too. What luck that it seemed to be the perfect match for her new neighbour’s aesthetic. So perfect in fact, it would be rude not to share it, a welcoming gift of sorts.

Making her decision she slammed the boot and put the keys in the lock, turning her should to push her way into the empty shop. As she did so, she caught the eye of a woman standing directly opposite, trying to look like she hadn’t been staring. Rio gave the younger woman a grin and a wink before shouldering her way into her new life. 

 

0930, 30th September 2026 

Rio crossed the road, purple orchid in hand, and pushed open the door to the tattoo parlour. The two women and teenage boy all turned to stare at her as she sauntered over to - hot neighbour didn’t seem like it would do the woman in the purple pants justice - “M’lady”, Rio decided at the last minute. She then made a point of entering a half bow before continuing, “I’ve just moved into the neighbourhood, pleasure to make your acquaintance.”  

It took all Agatha’s control not to wrench the flower out of the woman’s hand and throw it to the floor. But the fact she didn’t, she reflected briefly, was growth. Instead, she pursed her lips and gave a low hum as the woman returned to her full height and handed her the flower, seemingly undeterred by Agatha’s lack of reaction. The older woman had no choice really but to take the thing, which was admittedly quite lovely, with its mauve petals and deep green leaves. She coughed, awkwardly, before remembering herself. 

“If you expect me to say the pleasure is all mine, then you’re sorely mistaken.” She huffed, putting the flower to the side without a word of thanks. “Last thing Westview needs is another overpriced haven for these influencer types to hang out in, it’s a disgrace.”

“Agatha, don’t be rude.”

Teen better thank his lucky stars that Lilia was his grandmother, taking a tone like that with her. She scowled at him anyways for good measure. 

“Agatha,” the orchid-barer practically growled her name out, “a beautiful name, and a beautiful place you have here - very homely.” As if to make her point the woman plonked herself down on the couch by reception and threw both arms over the back, stretching out like a cat. “So, tell me about yourself, what is it you do here?” She picked up the booklet of designs on the table and began to leaf through. 

“Well,” Agatha snatched the book from her hand, “assuming you’re neither blind nor illiterate, I’m sure you can make those deductions for yourself. Now, if you don’t mind I have work to do, as I’m sure you do.” She ignored both Alice and Teen who were visibly cringing as she held the door open for the new neighbour, who had yet to move from her seat but looked to be enjoying herself despite the hostile atmosphere. Eventually, she rose from the sofa before going over to Agatha’s two employees and shaking their hands. 

“It’s been a pleasure,” she looked down at Teen’s name badge, “Teen. And, you too”, she paused waiting for Alice to offer up her name - which she did so, with a light blush. 

“And Agatha,” she invaded the older woman’s space as she stepped through the door, “I just know we’re going to be great friends. Te veo.” She fixed her with a smirk before exiting the shop, leaving the group in a somewhat stunned silence until Jen popped her head round the door. 

“Who was that.” She sounded amused. “Do I hate her, or do I want her phone number?” 

“Right,” Alice turned to her in agreement. Agatha on the other hand was still hovering in the doorway, scowl on her face as she watched her new neighbour ferrying small plants from her car into the new shop, all whilst wearing those impossibly tight trousers and sporting a grin that bordered on manic. 

 

2120, 30th September 2026

Agatha flopped on Lilia’s couch, exhausted from a day of inking and angsting over the new neighbour - not that she would admit the latter. She did her best to put the woman, whose name she had yet to learn, to the back of her mind. Something easier said than done it turned out.

“Agatha darling,” Lilia started, handing her a glass of wine as she went to take her own seat on the couch, “a little birdie tells me you received a visit from our new resident today. Do tell, what is she like? I’ve heard all sorts from the town already, some are even saying she’s Sharon’s goddaughter.”

“Who’s Sharon?”, was all Agatha said in reply. 

“You know Sharon, darling. Sharon Davies. Mrs. Hart?” 

“Oh that old crone. So our darling new neighbour is a little nepo-bitch then?” Sharon Davies was well know amongst townsfolk, moving to Westview not long after the death of her husband, and even less time after leaving her role as Mrs. Hart on a popular daytime soap that Agatha had claimed never to have watched. 

“Agatha, the woman owns a florist. I’d hardly count that as nepotism, and besides, why must you be so hostile to every poor soul who moves onto the Road? You’re never going to find a girlfriend with that attitude you know.” Agatha choked on her wine. 

“Jesus, alright Lilia, no need for that. And I thought we were talking about the nepo-bitch, not girlfriends, so back on the subject please dear.” 

“Right, of course, of course. Well anyways, what has she done to rub you up the wrong way already. From what I’ve heard Rio’s a nice girl, a little  odd perhaps, but a hard worker and”, she seemed to consider her words, “sweet.” 

Agatha’s huffed out a breath and took a deep swig of her wine. “Well, I’m not sure sweet is quite right”, she smirked. 

“Oh is that right? How would you describe her then, hmmm.” 

Agatha hadn’t yet had enough wine to be lured into this trap and so she merely shrugged her shoulders. “Annoying, overconfident, a brat.” Actually, the heat creeping up her cheeks was perhaps an indication that she had had too much wine, even if this was only her first glass. 

Lilia just sat back, watching her with that look she sometimes got, like she could see into your future. 

“It sounds like she made an impression at least.” Pause. “More wine?”

Agatha just nodded, rearranging her face back into its signature scowl.

9th October 2026 

It’d been just over a week since Rio had arrived in Westview, and finally she had managed to set up shop and move everything into the small apartment above it. Her days had mostly been filled with painting walls, unpacking plants and collecting orders, though she had managed to take the occasional break to wander around, grab a coffee and slowly get to know the residents of the quaint New Jersey town. A few days after arriving she’d even payed Sharon a visit, having not seen the woman in person for almost a year. This proved to be a fruitful adventure for a number of reasons. Firstly, she’d been given a full rundown of the best places in Westview to go for drinks, dinner, spin class - Rio doubted she’d be investigating the latter - and secondly, because she’d been able to get the lowdown on her new neighbour. 

Agatha Harkness, it turned out, was somewhat of a big name in town. She’d owned her tattoo parlour on the Witches’ Road for coming on thirty years, having inherited the space at the ripe old age of 22, after her mother’s untimely death. Since then she’d seen the road transform, and by Sharon and the rest of towns’ accounts, was not best pleased about it. Many had incurred her wrath over the years, and in one notable incident she had even dyed her neighbour’s dog bright purple. Unprovoked. All in all the word on the street seemed to be, don’t fuck with Agatha Harkness if you value you sanity. 

Rio thought she might be in love. 

Unfortunately, since the day she arrived Rio had not had the chance to see nor speak to the woman. She had however bumped into the one with orange streaks through her hair, who seemed to be inordinately clumsy. Rio knew this because on three separate occasions she had seen her spill, drop or break something through the window of Agatha’s shop. Not that she’d been staring, or trying to get a glimpse of its owner - she was merely being neighbourly. Anyway, the third thing that Alison, Alicia - whatever her name was - had dropped was a tray of coffees. Rio saw her be sent straight back out again by the Teen and a tall, dark, pretty woman dressed head to toe in a shade of pink that was borderline offensive. So, ever the opportunist, Rio had decided that was a good a time as ever to do her own coffee run.

That’s how she’d found Lilia’s. It was a smaller coffee shop, tucked away off the main high street and weirdly, not one that had been recommended to her by Sharon - despite the coffee being really quite fantastic. A story there for another day perhaps. 

Lilia’s not only did an excellent coffee, its owner also proved to have an excellent grasp of the comings and goings of Westview in its entirety. Rio had been immediately drawn to Lilia, an older woman who would’ve made an excellent witch in another life (or perhaps in this one, who could say). She had greeted Rio like an old friend, and seemed to know way more about her than was socially acceptable for someone you’ve never met. Something Rio found to be both amusing and impressive.  She’d gotten chatting to the woman and through her had been introduced - or re-introduced she supposed - to Ali, Allicent, Agatha’s employee. That was who she was speaking to now. Well speaking to may be the wrong phrase as the woman was looking at her expectantly as if waiting for an answer of some kind. 

“Sorry, would you mind awfully repeating that? I was quite away with the fairies.”

“Oh, uh, sure. I was just saying that you should come down to Lilia’s this evening. She does a live music night on a Friday and lots of us on the Witches’ Road come down for it. What do you think?” Alice, that was her name Rio remembered, looked hopeful and gave Rio a nervous smile. 

“Well darling, who am I to say no to a little live music on a Friday night. Does this place have a liquor licence?” Alice’s smile turned from nervous to mischievous in an instant.

“Ah. Now that’s where Agatha comes in. You remember Agatha right? Long hair, lots of enormously scary tattoos, short but makes up for it in personality.” 

Rio snorted. “Yeah, yeah I remember Agatha - she’s a hard one to forget I think. But care to enlighten me on what I can only assume are her illegal booze smuggling activities?” She raised an eyebrow. 

“Let’s not throw around words like illegal.” Lilia appeared from behind Rio, making her jump. “I’d like to think of it more as prohibition appropriation, let’s say. And we’d love to have you Rio, 9pm sharp.” 

It was clear the woman wouldn’t take no for an answer, not that Rio was planning to go back on her promise to attend - especially now that a certain small woman with enormously scary tattoos was involved.

After assuring them she would be in attendance, the brunette bid both women goodbye and headed back to her flower shop where she was making the finishing touches for Monday’s opening.

“So,” Lilia turned to Alice after watching their newest resident leave and do a funny little hop, skip and jump down the pavement. “What do we think, she’s peculiar right? I think so. I like it.” She did not give Alice a chance to answer. “I wish Agatha would be more receptive to her. I know for a fact that she’s been avoiding bumping into her after their first meeting, thought I can’t imagine why. She normally loves to terrorise the new shop owners straight off the bat.” 

Alice took this moment to jump in. “I don’t know, it seems like she might be planning something. I saw her and Teen huddled over the desk the other day, and I may be wrong but I could’ve sworn they had blueprints for Rio’s shop. I mean it may be nothing, but I’ve known that woman a long time - as have you - and wouldn’t be surprised if she’s got something up her sleeve.” 

Lilia’s look was one of exasperation. 

“The mind boggles with that one. And as for thinking it was ever a good idea to introduce her to Teen, well I don’t know. I just hope that whatever it is they have planned, Rio won’t take it too badly. She seems like she could rub off well on Agatha, and from the look on her face when you told her she’d be involved in the live music night, I’d be inclined to thinks she feels the same way.” Lilia smirked and raised an eyebrow. 

“Oh god, I did not need that mental image woman.” 

The older woman just chuckled and slid Alice’s coffees towards her.

“Apologies darling. You best get off before I traumatise you further. I’ll see you this evening?” 

“Oh you betcha, and I’ll let you know if I get any closer to uncovering exactly what it is that Teen and Agatha are plotting. See you later Lilia.” With that the woman picked up her drinks and made a beeline for the door, careful not to knock into anyone as she went.

9th October 2026 - Evening 

Agatha sat in front of her dresser, wrapped in a towel as she ran a brush through a mass of wet, tangled hair. She had promised to show at Lilia’s live music night that evening, bringing with her the usual supply of beers and cheap wine that the two would sell to guests out of the boot of Agatha’s car - which she parked in the alley down the side of the coffee shop. It was a gig they’d perfected over the last two or three years, and one that the Westview Police Department tended to turn a blind eye towards on account of the fact it made their own Friday nights much quieter.

Having tamed her hair into something vaguely acceptable, she turned to her makeup. A sweep of dark purple shadow across the eyelids with a touch of eyeliner and a lick of mascara, the usual affair, finished off with a pink lipgloss. Working as a tattoo artist meant she often left her rings at home, but tonight she decided to slide them on - a couple of chunky gold stacks as well as a thumb ring with a purple gemstone and a few bangles round her wrists. Agatha wasn’t vain per se, but she was also aware that she wasn’t the worst looking person around. Indeed, despite not being a mother herself, Teen had told her on several occasions that she was a milf. Something she wasn’t keen to unpack but was nonetheless pleased about. 

Moving across to her wardrobe she selected her outfit for the night. Black dress pants with a matching waistcoat - no need for a bra underneath - and a heavy wool coat to combat the recent chill in the air. She ended up doing two laps of the apartment before finding the shoes she was looking for, a pair of cream loafers with gold buckles, and finally threw her hair into something that resembled a bun, minus a few flyaways. Grabbing her keys and bidding farewell to Señor Scratchy she left the flat and made her way to Lilia’s. 

The band were already setting up when Agatha entered the coffee shop that evening. She gave them a wave before making her way into the back room where she knew she’d find Lilia and, most likely, Teen. 

“What’s a girl gotta do to get a drink around here.” She did her best impression of a Western drawl, one that may need work based on Lilia and Teen’s laughing faces. 

“Agatha dear, good to see you too.” She swatted the older woman away as she tried, as always, to hug her. Teen gave her a quick wave and a thumbs up that she wasn’t entirely sure the meaning of. 

They wandered out the back to where she’d parked the car, and set up in the usual manner - this more or less involved Agatha sitting on the edge of the boot with crates of beer and wine at her feet and plastic cups, should her patrons wish to feel a little more classy. That would normally be her for the night. She could hear the live music through the open side door, and would make occasional chatter to the other cafe-dwellers who came out for a drink, it was in her mind the perfect night. No need to pretend to be enjoying the company of the insufferable ‘bros’ that owned the local shops around her, nor make inane chat to the yoga girls and women who’s personalities revolved entirely around skin care and vegetables - yuck. If she was lucky, she might even get high off her own supply. 

By half past nine the night was in full swing and Agatha had a pleasant buzz on as she doled out cups of wine and bottles of beer just on the wrong side of warm. In fact, she was ready to consider the evening a roaring success until she spotted the woman hovering at the side door, rocking slightly on her heels and grinning. 

Agatha let out a groan. She was going to kill Lilia.   

Since her first meeting with Rio, Agatha had been doing her best to avoid the woman. Her hackles had been raised after the orchid incident and along with Teen she’d decided that her normal combative approach wasn’t going to work. Instead the two had spent the better part of the last week plotting how best to run their new neighbour out of town. Initially, they’d decided the best approach would be to slowly turn the town against her, something that was easier said than done considering Agatha’s own stand-offish personality and lack of repartee with the folks of Westview.  She’d even sent Teen out to do some recon and unfortunately, it wasn’t good news. Despite the doors to Rio’s flower shop not having opened, it seemed the woman was already making a name for herself in town. The tattoo artist was already in comfy clothes, so she needn’t wake her, instead pulling the covers up and over her girlfriend before kissing her lightly on the tip of her nose. 

She was still fast asleep when Rio returned from the bathroom, and she took one final look at her before turning off the light.

The florist smiled to herself in the darkness. 

“G’night Agatha. Merry Christmas.”

 

25 December 2026

To say Lilia’s house was chaotic would be an understatement. 

Teen greeted the two women at the door, his cheeks were pink from the warm kitchen and he was wielding a spatula like a magic wand, but he looked happy. 

“You guys made it! Come in, you can help Alice and Jen make drinks - I have a feeling whatever withes’ brew they’re creating won’t go down so well.” Agatha crossed the threshold first, giving Teen a smile and quick squeeze on the shoulder. Rio brought up the rear, arms laden with gifts. 

The three made their way into Lilia’s kitchen. Alice and Jen sat at the dining table, indeed, concocting a suspicious looking cocktail of some kind. Lilia meanwhile was peeling potatoes at an alarming speed, whilst Sharon flitted around the kitchen looking busy - though doing what, it wasn’t exactly clear. 

Rio and Agatha greeted the women, removing their coats and depositing the presents before helping with the preparations. By three o’clock, the house had become less manic. Food was in the oven and everyone had loosened up after a few sips of the drinks Alice and Jen had made. 

They now sat in Lilia’s living room, Sharon and Lilia shared the smaller of the two sofas - though, sat closer than was strictly necessary for the space they did have. Jen, Alice and Teen sat on the other sofa, while Agatha had taken the armchair, Rio on her lap. This had prompted an inappropriate Santa joke from both Alice and Jen, and given Rio more than a few ideas for later. 

“I’d just like to say a few words, before dinner.” Lilia piped up, looking around at the group and raising her glass in a mock toast. “Firstly, Rio, it has been an absolute pleasure to meet you and get to know you over the past few months. You’re an amazing woman, and a wonderful influence on our darling Agatha.”

“Here here.” Jen cut in, which drew a laugh from the whole group.

“And Jen, Alice. I am forever grateful to have found friends like you, and I look forward to everything there is to come.” The two women both smiled at Lilia, touched by her words. “And teen, my lovely grandson. I know the past year hasn’t been easy, and though I regret what you had to go through for it to happen, I’m so glad of the time I’ve been able to spend with you this year.”

“You too grandma, you too.” 

“And Sharon, I’m glad to have you back in my life. Truly. And I hope we can continue to,” She paused, considering her words, “repair our friendship, our relationship. I really do.” Sharon just took her hand, giving it a gentle squeeze. 

“Agatha.” 

“Lilia.”

“You are one of the most stubborn people I have ever met, and it’s been a pleasure to see you grow into the woman you are today. I know you would never admit it, but I appreciate the love and the care that you have for this group. So, thank you.”

“Ugh, Lilia. Are you dying? Cause that’s a real buzzkill if you are.”

“No Agatha, I’m not dying, though I appreciate your concern.” Agatha smirked at this, giving Lilia a thumbs up that conveyed her appreciation for the woman in a way only Agatha could. 

“Anyway, now I’ve got that off my chest.” The cafe owner chuckled, “what do you say we go and eat?”

The rest of the day passed in a blur. The group ate and drank and opened presents, they played games - until Agatha and Jen had to be separated for getting too competitive - and watched Christmas specials in the evening. It was only when Lilia started to doze off on the couch, around 10pm, that the rest of the group began to go their separate ways.

Alice was staying a Jen’s, who only lived a short walk away, and Sharon had booked an Uber home not long before them. That left Rio and Agatha, who had stayed to help Teen do a final clear up and who now waited in the doorway, bidding their farewells. 

The two made their way outside, where large snowflakes had started to fall, coating the road in a crisp white blanket that reflected the moonlight. The couple walked arm in arm down Lilia’s street and towards the Witches’ Road in a comfortable silence until they arrived at the door to Agatha’s shop. 

“Come in, I’ve got a present for you.” The tattoo artist said, pulling Rio towards the door.

“Oh baby, you shouldn’t have.” The eagerness in her voice betrayed her words as she followed the older woman into the shop and up the stairs to her flat. They made their way into the living room, Agatha directing Rio to the sofa as she went to located the gift. Rio was absentmindedly stroking Señor Scratchy’s ears when her girlfriend returned with a small package, wrapped in purple tissue paper. Rio plucked the package from Agatha’s outstretched palm and inspected it, trying to gauge what it might be from its size and weight. 

“Go on then, open it.” There was a hint of nervousness in her voice as Rio tore into the package. 

The younger woman let out a surprised gasp, though barely loud enough for Agatha to hear, on inspecting the present. It was a small brooch made from what looked to be cowrie shell, but what really stood out was the engraving. It was an intricate orchid in a rich purple, one that made her think back to that first day she’d met Agatha. 

 

She’d thrust a pot with a purple orchid out towards the woman and bent into a strange half curtsey, half bow. “I’ve just moved into the neighbourhood, pleasure to make your acquaintance.”

 

And now, on this brooch, the same orchid immortalised. 

The younger woman felt a tear threaten to fall. 

“I love it, Agatha. And.” She paused for a moment, looking deep into Agatha’s eyes though knowing what she was looking for was already in front of her in the form of the brooch.

“And?”

“And, I love you.” This time it was Rio’s voice that faltered slightly, nervous that the woman, despite everything, may not feel the same way.

She had no reason to be, as Agatha gently pulled her into a kiss. It was softer than usual, as if trying to convey everything she was feeling without taking a breath. She pulled away, wiping the stray tear from Rio’s cheek. 

“I love you too, Rio Vidal.”
