Summary

When music producer Agatha Harkness gets stuck in an airport due to a hurricane warning, her only option is to hole up in a local hotel to wait out the bad weather. Fortunately, they have one last room available.

A room with only one bed that she'll have to share with her assistant, Rio Vidal.

With six years of history and unacknowledged feelings between them, the storm outside may not be the only one brewing.

And Agatha might just find herself in the very eye of it.

Agatha Harkness doesn’t mind airports most of the time. Under normal circumstances, they can be quite enjoyable: the business lounge offers a degree of separation from the sweaty masses of economy passengers, and with a nice glass of red wine, she’s usually more than happy to pass the hours before her flight on her work laptop, or, if she’s feeling particularly bored, people-watching through the glass walls of the lounge.

Except what she’s currently experiencing are not normal circumstances, and she’s about two seconds away from murdering someone.

The guy in the metal chair next to her own lets out a snore. His head sways dangerously close to resting on Agatha’s shoulder. Across from her, a baby starts to wail again after approximately ninety seconds of blessed silence.

The electronic display board on her left remains unchanged. Every single flight is marked as CANCELED in bright red letters, mocking her from across the distance.

All she can smell in the air is misery. That, and somehow too much perfume and not enough deodorant at once.

Agatha breathes out through her nose slowly, clenching her fist. “You are so fired.”

Her assistant, currently sat on Agatha’s other side with her own laptop perched atop her knees, hums in response without looking up. “Contrary to what you think, I don’t control the weather, Agatha.”

“What do I even pay you for, then?”

Rio doesn’t stop typing. “For working on getting us out of here, for starters.”

“Yes,” Agatha sneers. “How kind of you to fix your own mess.”

No response comes. Agatha huffs and crosses her arms over her chest. Her blazer is restricting her range of motion and her heels are killing her feet, but she’ll be damned if she allows herself to take her shoes or any layer of her clothing in this cesspool of a place.

So maybe, if she was feeling less miserable, she could admit their current situation is not entirely Rio’s fault. 

No, Rio’s only crime was that she went on vacation when Agatha needed this flight booked, and the useless intern who sat at Agatha’s assistant’s desk in her absence bought tickets for the wrong date, and by the time they found the mistake, their only option was a layover in a tiny airport in the middle of fucking Florida, where they arrived six hours ago just as a hurricane warning was issued, promptly grounding them in what Agatha is ready to declare the worst place she’s ever been. Not a single lounge in sight.

But still. Rio spent a week partying it up in Aruba - not that Agatha ever looks at her assistant’s Instagram, because that would be wildly inappropriate, so she definitely did not see her photos on a picturesque beach in a teeny tiny bikini - while what’s-his-name ruined Agatha’s life. So really, as far as Agatha is concerned, Rio ruined her life by proxy.

And now some simpleton is drooling on her shoulder.

She shifts away in disgust, casting a glare at the younger woman at her side. Rio looks entirely unbothered by what’s going on around her, including Agatha’s fury, and that poise annoys Agatha more than she would care to admit. When she’s angry, she prefers to have someone around who will cower and at least give her some satisfaction.

Not Rio, though. Never Rio. That’s why Agatha hired her, wasn’t it? Because Rio could take anything she threw her way in stride and still always be on time with her lunch order. That was something no assistant before her had ever achieved.

Actually, no assistant had ever lasted six months, let alone six years.

She closes her eyes and pinches the bridge of her nose, fighting a headache gathering like storm clouds behind her eyes. The baby’s wailing hasn’t ceased, and Agatha thinks she might just join it.

Something heavy lands in her lap. She opens her eyes and looks down at what turns out to be a bottle of water and a packet of ibuprofen.

Jerking her head to stare at Rio, she finds the other woman still entirely preoccupied with her computer screen. She gives no indication of having moved an inch.

Agatha narrows her eyes and takes two pills followed by half a bottle of water. She pointedly doesn’t say thank you. Rio might have just saved her from a headache, but she still hasn’t gotten Agatha out of this hellhole.

It’s not like Rio needs a thank you anyway. She never does.

She wouldn’t have survived six years on the job if she did.

Agatha knows what people call her behind her back, bitch being the mildest of insults thrown around when everyone thinks she can’t hear them. She doesn’t mind; she relishes it, really. She didn’t become a senior music producer at the biggest record label in the country by playing nice.

If only the fuckers would let her take the jet one of these days. That she still has to endure all of this bullshit is beneath her.

She’ll show them, though. She’ll climb her way to the top eventually, and she’ll look damn good doing it. 

Provided she ever gets out of Florida, that is.

But having a certain reputation in the industry does make finding a competent assistant harder. That’s why Rio is unfazed by any of the threats Agatha has posed over the last few hours. She knows just as well as Agatha herself that firing her would be far more trouble than it’s worth.

Who else would carry ibuprofen, because they remember Agatha is allergic to acetaminophen, or a bottle of her favorite water that Agatha has no idea how Rio got through security, in their bag at all times just in case?

She takes another sip of water. The tension in her forehead begins to ease, and a ridiculous - preposterous, really - idea to apologize sprouts in her mind. She rips it out, roots and all, before screwing the cap back onto the bottle. Agatha Harkness doesn’t apologize. That would mean admitting she’s in the wrong, which she’s not. The metal chair and the resigned masses all around her are proof that her anger is justified.

Perhaps that is why her eyes keep drifting to Rio. What else is she gonna look at in a place like this? Amongst this sullen crowd, her assistant stands out from a mile away, a being alien to such a dreary environment. Even hunched over her laptop, her shirt wrinkled from the ordeal of today, something in her aura draws Agatha like a moth to a flame.

If she’s being honest with herself, which she doesn’t make a habit of, there are very few rooms in which Rio wouldn’t stand out. How could she not, with her dark eyeliner and black nails, combat boots all year round, a leather jacket draped over her shoulders ever so casually? Agatha learned early on in their professional relationship that Rio treats the idea of business attire with an equal mix of amusement and blatant disregard, and, well… let’s just say Agatha didn’t push back particularly hard.

Just like in this sorry excuse for an airport, she needs something nice to look at in the office too. Sue her.

She doesn’t even realize she’s staring at the way Rio’s hair is falling over her shoulder, shielding half of her face from view, before the other woman abruptly closes her laptop, making her flinch.

“Okay,” Rio says, sliding the computer into her bag. “Let’s get the hell out of here.”

Agatha is so relieved to hear those seven magical words at long last that she doesn’t even bother to ask where they’re going. Anywhere beats this.

Rio gets them out in record time, navigating the airport with the casual swagger of someone who will walk wherever she pleases, not unlike a cat. Agatha simply follows, gritting her teeth through each sting of pain that her tired feet send up her calves in protest against the tortuous heels she’s wearing. She pulls her carry-on behind her, splitting her focus between the sway of Rio’s hips and the sweet fantasy of a decent bed and something to eat.

As soon as they walk outside, humidity sticks to her like a warm, wet towel draped over her entire body. She can practically feel her hair getting frizzy from it. She pushes it away from her sticky forehead as Rio hails down a taxi.

“Remind me to never travel down this far south again,” she says as they shut the doors and the car, smelling equal parts of cigarettes and pine air freshener, begins to glide down the road, past the long stretches of parking lots clustered around the airport. “This climate doesn’t agree with me.”

In her seat next to hers, Rio smirks, tapping her black nails on the car window. “And here I was thinking you were gonna retire to a beach house in Tampa Bay.”

Agatha scoffs. For some reason, probably due to exhaustion, the joke amuses her far more than it usually would. “I would die of boredom five minutes in.”

Rio hums. “I know,” she says simply, like it’s the most obvious thing in the world.

Silence falls following her statement. Rio stares out the window, at the thick gray clouds gathering in the sky. Agatha takes another sip of her water.

God, she’s tired. She would take a roadside motel right this moment without a complaint.

Well, maybe one or two complaints, but nothing major.

But when they finally pull up in front of a hotel and Agatha climbs out of the taxi, cursing under her breath as her feet protest again, she’s surprised to find something that looks… nice.

At least by the standards of whatever backwater town they’re in.

It looks like one of the chain hotels that mostly survive off businessmen who average three flights a week. White facade, revolving doors, AC inside - that last part she notes with relief, gladly escaping the soupy air outside. The foyer is all fake wood and neutral colors, with soft music seeping through invisible speakers. Agatha breathes a sigh of relief. Maybe there will be something to salvage out of the wreckage of this cursed day after all.

Rio doesn’t ask for her ID, because she already has it. As she checks them in, it strikes Agatha for the first time that maybe she shouldn’t let someone else carry all her documents for her.

But then again, she would have to remember them herself, and that’s what she pays Rio for. So she doesn’t have to worry about mundane shit like a piece of plastic with an unflattering photo on it.

Okay, so the photo is not unflattering, exactly, because no photo with Agatha as the subject could be that, but it is probably the closest thing to a mugshot she’s ever taken. If she doesn’t have to look at it or remember its existence, that’s only for the better.

The foyer is busy, she observes as she waits. The line to the front desk is long, but somehow Rio makes it to the front before Agatha can blink, probably through an impressive combination of intimidation and devil-may-care attitude that puts people off too much to protest. Agatha has seen that particular skill in action a few times, and even she can admit it never ceases to impress. On all sides, there are groups of people making inane small talk, most of which seems to be related to the weather. Agatha is more than happy to tune it out.

Then, at last, Rio approaches her with two key cards held triumphantly aloft, and Agatha groans as she lifts herself to her feet once more. Maybe her doctor has a point about five-inch heels every single day being an overkill.

But then again, if she didn’t look like she could stab a bitch every moment she’s at work, she’d never have gotten any respect around the place.

They get on the elevator. Rio pushes a button to the fourth floor.

“No ocean view, I’m afraid,” she quips as they ride up. “So you can add that to the list of fireable offenses.”

Agatha raises an eyebrow. “Glad to see today hasn’t dampened your sense of humor.”

Rio gives a wry chuckle. “Call it gallows humor, Agatha.”

Agatha shifts. Rio always smells like cedar, which is more evident in the small space. “What, are you expecting us to die?”

“It’s a hurricane. You never know what might happen.”

Even as she says it, she looks completely unbothered by the idea.

The elevator opens into a carpeted hallway. Agatha follows Rio past rows of identical doors until they reach one marked with a number Agatha doesn’t bother to remember. She won’t spend more than one night in this place even if she has to rent a car and drive back to California.

Rio barely has time to slide the key card through the lock and push down on the door handle before Agatha is striding into the room, taking off her shoes with disgust. She’ll take bare feet on the questionably clean carpet over spending one more moment in the very expensive torture devices she willingly bought and took on this trip.

Only once she’s finally a little less uncomfortable does she actually look around the room.

It looks… acceptable. Neutral and boring, with a TV on one wall and a bed pushed up against the other. The window looks out onto the main street of the town, giving her a view of a string of mom and pop shops clustered under an increasingly dark sky. A nondescript painting hangs over the bed.

The bed. As in, Agatha realizes, the only one in the room.

She crosses her arms over her chest and turns towards Rio, raising an eyebrow. She doesn’t actually need to ask the question out loud.

Rio moves casually past her, pulling her luggage behind her to drop it on the floor in the middle of the room. “You wouldn’t fall asleep in anything under four stars, Agatha.” She gestures around the room with a flourish of her hand. “This is what passes for four stars in this town, and it was the last room they had. Half of the airport is here.” She shrugs. “I’ll sleep in the bathtub. Easy.”

Agatha flips the light switch in the bathroom. There indeed is a bathtub, though not the kind Agatha would consider actually having a soak in. She has standards for baths, and the black spots in the grout do not meet it. Neither does the plastic shower curtain.

She glances at the bed. It’s a king, more than wide enough for two people. More than wide enough for two people to sleep without touching.

“Don’t be ridiculous,” she says with a scoff. “You’re no use to me if you’re all cramped up in the morning. There’s enough room for us both.”

She clears her throat. God, she’s such a cliché. Half the office already thinks she’s sleeping with her assistant. Her much younger, very attractive, very dedicated assistant. There’s no way Rio isn’t aware of the rumors.

And now Agatha is offering her half of the bed. Fantastic. If HR ever gets a whiff of this, she’ll never hear the end of it.

But the corner of Rio’s mouth only twitches. “Fine. I’ll go get us something to eat. The hotel restaurant will be a two-hour wait, but there’s a pizza place on the corner that looks like it semi-passed the health inspection.”

Agatha snorts. “Sounds gourmet.”

Rio fiddles with the thermostat for a few moments. “Pizza is pizza. And it’s not like you haven’t been known to demolish a slice every now and then.”

She doesn’t look at Agatha when she says it, but her voice is tinged with amusement and something else Agatha doesn’t recognize. She swallows as she remembers that night. The never-to-be-spoken about night.

That cheap pizza at two in the morning might have been the best damn thing she’s ever eaten.

This is the first time in two years they’ve come even close to talking about that night.

Rio doesn’t press the subject. She reaches for her bag and throws it over her shoulder.

“I’ll be back soon. And I’ll have them send up some extra pillows.”

Agatha nods, feigning indifference. “Fine.” She doesn’t comment on the fact that Rio knows she has to have at least two pillows to sleep comfortably.

When the door clicks shut behind Rio, Agatha stands barefoot in the middle of the room for a beat, worrying her bottom lip with her teeth, wondering why the hell her stomach feels like she’s dropped too fast down a hill on a rollercoaster.

Finally, she moves, as the sensation of being too aware of how gross and sweaty after today’s ordeal she is sets in. She grabs her toiletry bag out of her suitcase, drops her jacket on the back of a chair, and checks the thermostat.

It’s already set to her preferred temperature.

Clutching the bag maybe harder than necessary, she licks her dry lips and shuts herself in the bathroom.

Her assistant smiles. It’s the kind of smile that could topple empires, always has been. Sharp and wicked and irresistible. “Yeah, with my couch and my The L Word rewatch. You ready to get out of here?”

Agatha ponders the question. She doesn’t really want to drink anymore. She doesn’t even particularly want to be upright.

She’s just so fucking tired.

“I don’t know…” She hiccups, and it annoys her more than it should. She’s not herself like this. So helpless, so vulnerable, with not enough wits left about her to put her armor back up. “I don’t know if I can walk.”

She’s still wearing her stilettos. They clink against the metal legs of the barstool.

Rio simply raises a bag she’s been holding this whole time. Agatha had not registered its existence until it was in her direct line of sight. “Brought you a change of shoes. They’re mine. They should fit.”

Agatha would be floored if she had enough mental capacity for true surprise tonight. And even if she did, after four years things like these don’t hold as much shock factor anymore. Rio has always been awfully good at anticipating her needs.

“Hm,” is all she says. Then she realizes she should attempt to change out of her leg-breaking footwear, but if she bends down, she might just throw up all over the Louboutins. 

Which puts her in a bit of a pickle, because she won’t be able to stand up from the chair until she’s out of the beastly shoes, and she can’t coordinate herself enough to take them off. She might have to stay right where she is all night.

It doesn’t seem too bad in the moment. She can just rest her head on the bar and sleep. Sleep is good.

Except then Rio is bending down - no, she’s kneeling on the floor, and her hand is around Agatha’s ankle, and her touch is warmer than the buzz the alcohol had brought with it. She pulls Agatha’s heels off with ease and replaces them with her bright green sneakers.

Agatha simply stares, because somewhere in her mind, a distant voice is yelling that her assistant should not be kneeling on the dirty bar floor at 10 PM on  a workday, tying Agatha’s shoelaces. And Agatha definitely should not be allowing her to do that.

But she’s so tired, and Rio is here with her ridiculous Converse, and Agatha’s heart beats so fast in her chest that for a moment she thinks she might die.

She must voice the sentiment out loud, because Rio rises to her feet, a breathless chuckle on her lips. “You won’t die, Cinderella. Now come on. Let’s go and get some greasy food in you, yeah?”

The nausea she’d felt has receded by now. Suddenly, with Rio towering over her - because Agatha is half-slumped over the bar, she realizes, as if she can’t quite remember how to keep a straight posture - she feels like maybe she can actually get up. And eat something.

She really could eat.

“Pizza,” she mumbles. Her brain semi-registers that Rio’s arm is draped around her waist, and her body is pressed against the side of hers. It gives her enough support to slide off the barstool. “Can we get pizza?”

Rio’s breath is hot in her ear. “Yes, Agatha. We can get pizza.”

***

“Everything okay?”

Agatha is snapped out of the memory by the sound of Rio’s voice, interrupting the images and sounds from that night. She clears her throat.

“Of course it is. Everything’s just peachy.”

She doesn’t sound convincing even to her own ears, but Rio doesn’t press the issue. Her eyes simply rest on Agatha, dark and calm, as she pops the last piece of the crust in her mouth. Her posture is loose where she slumps in her chair.

Agatha, on the other hand, feels herself go rigid, guarded. Suddenly spending time with her assistant in just her satin pajamas doesn’t seem like such a good idea anymore. She rests one of her feet on the seat of her chair and pulls her knee up to her chest.

That night two years ago had been locked away for so long, tucked away safely in the deep of her closet along with the bright green Converse, never to see the light of day again. She’d made sure of it, or at least she thought she had.

So why is it coming out now?

It’s Rio’s fault. She brought it up, however vague the reference may have been, and now everything reminds Agatha of that night. The ache in her feet, the grease on the pizza. Her pajamas, too.

The memory still laps at her feet, like a lazy tide, but she refuses to give it any more attention. She doesn’t need to dwell on where the night led them after they’d left the bar.

Desperate to regain some sense of control, to stop feeling like she’s the one being analyzed by the other woman’s careful gaze, she takes a swig of beer and pushes a question out of her throat through the lingering bitterness of the drink.

“Why are you still here?”

That’s better. Her tone takes on its signature bite, which makes her feel safer. She clicks her armor of acidity into place and pushes her chin up in a challenge.

Rio smirks. That smirk used to drive Agatha nuts, that one corner of her mouth rising asymmetrically, a puff of air escaping through her nose, as if chuckling silently at a joke only she was privy to. “It’s a hurricane, Agatha. I can’t really leave.”

Agatha raises an eyebrow, pressing her lips into a thin line. “You know what I mean.”

Rio holds her bottle by the neck. She swings it in the air in front of her lightly, like a pendulum, back and forth. “Where else would I be?”

And that’s another one of those Rio-isms that make Agatha feel like she’s in free fall. Like it’s an obvious thing that she’s here with Agatha. Like it’s an obvious thing that she knows Agatha’s allergies and food preferences and her preferred thermostat settings and her optimal pillow configuration and everything else about her.

Like it’s an obvious thing that she’d come to a random bar late at night to pick up her drunk boss.

Agatha rests one of her hands on her knee. “No one wants to be an assistant for six years. It’s a placeholder job. Something you do until a better opportunity comes along and you can become the person who has an assistant.”

“Maybe I like to be an assistant, Agatha.”

“Bullshit. No one likes to be at the bottom of the ladder.”

“No,” Rio counters. “You don’t like to be at the bottom of the ladder. You don’t like to be in the middle either. It’s the top or nothing for you.”

“Yes.” There’s no point arguing: they both know it’s true. Who could settle for anything less than the apex of the food chain? “And you’re telling me you’re content cleaning up my messes when you could be focusing on your own career?”

Rio stills the bottle. She sets it on the desk and leans forward, resting her elbows on her knees. “And miss having the front seat to your rise to power? Not a chance.”

She grins as she says it, wide and maybe a touch unhinged. “If I can help you do that, one lunch order at a time, then that’s what I’m gonna do. It's as simple as that.”

Agatha’s heart stutters behind her ribs. Rio is close enough leaning forward like this that a subtle hint of cedar reaches her nose. Even if the sentiment is so completely foreign to her - you always look out for number one: yourself, is the lesson she’s carried with her all these years - it sounds earnest coming from Rio’s mouth.

Rio licks her lips. “It’s not like I haven’t had other offers, you know. After the first year, people started to notice that I’d survived longer than anyone working for the biggest bitch in LA.” She makes mocking air quotes with her fingers as she says the last part.

That other people in the business would try to poach her assistant doesn’t come as a shock. Agatha may be a shark, but she’s swimming in shark-infested waters too. What surprises her is the fact that she hasn’t heard of any of those offers.

What stings is the sudden pang of bitter jealousy at the thought of Rio leaving, or even considering leaving to work for someone else.

“But you didn’t take any of them.”

Rio shrugs. “What can I say? Being assistant to the biggest bitch in LA has a certain ring to it. Who else would be willing to go to the lengths you’d go to? You’d stab anyone in the back if it would get you that promotion.”

It’s a damning indictment, and if it had come from anyone else’s mouth, Agatha would laugh and agree. She’s backstabbed people before, and she’d do it again. She’d lie and manipulate and use anyone if it was convenient for her. It’s who she is, and she’s never apologized for it or pretended to be what she’s not.

But Rio is perhaps the only person in the world whose opinion… matters, and for a brief, strange moment, Agatha isn’t sure she likes the image her words conjure up.

Except Rio leans back in her seat and shakes her head, looking up briefly at the ceiling before her eyes return to Agatha. She smiles. It’s a smile of very many things, admiration at the forefront.

“It’s fucking spectacular.”

A surprised, breathy laugh escapes Agatha at that. Something in her chest feels lighter, as if a weight had settled there briefly before disappearing.

“Besides,” Rio adds, taking another slice of pizza and biting into it with gusto, “I’m probably the only person in the world who knows that it’s not true what they say about you.”

“Oh?” Agatha feigns intrigue. “Two seconds ago you seemed to agree with everyone else’s sentiment.”

Rio shrugs. “I mean, sure. You’re ruthless. You don’t let anyone stand in your way. But you’re not nearly as cold and unfeeling as you like people to believe.”

Agatha smirks. “Yes, I’m a veritable bleeding heart.”

“If those charity donations are anything to go by, you kind of are.”

Agatha freezes. Her eyes must go comically wide, because Rio looks unreasonably pleased with herself. “How the hell…”

“They send thank you letters to their biggest donors at the end of every quarter, Agatha. Letters that come straight to my desk. Mental health support for LGBT youth, child abuse prevention initiatives… You donate to them regularly. I’ve seen the evidence. The letters are very effusive in their praise.”

She might have to actually murder someone for not telling her there would be physical evidence of any of her… more morally fibrous actions. “I’ve never seen any of these letters.”

“Yeah, because I throw them away. I figured you wouldn’t want anyone to know.”

“Anyone except you, apparently.”

Rio shrugs. “Your secret’s safe with me.” In her mouth, it’s less a platitude and more a fact. “Just like the matter of my paycheck.”

“I have no idea what you’re talking about.”

“Oh, so me being the highest paid assistant in the building doesn’t ring a bell?”

Agatha looks away. The box the pizza came in, stained with grease, suddenly seems very interesting. “You can’t possibly know that. HR keeps the payroll under lock and key.”

“Oh, but assistants talk, Agatha. Sharon and I compared notes and I know for certain I make more than she does after thirty years in the company.”

“Who’s Sharon?”

“She’s been at Herb’s desk since the 90s, Agatha.”

“Doesn’t ring a bell.”

Rio laughs. It sends a shiver through Agatha, how delighted it sounds. “Of course it doesn’t. Just like you taking a cut of your own paycheck to raise my salary doesn’t.”

Agatha arranges her face into a careful mask of you’re talking nonsense. “Your attempts at making me out to be a decent human being are charming, but even you must hear how ridiculous that sounds.”

“Really? Because there is a set budget for assistants. And I paid off one of the girls in accounting to let me take a look at the paperwork. You took a five percent cut three years ago, and you haven’t gotten a raise since. But I have. Every year like clockwork.”

Fuckfuckfuckfuckfuck.

Fuck.

“That’s confidential information.”

“As if that would matter to you in the slightest under any other circumstances.”

Agatha rarely feels trapped. She’s usually too well-prepared for most scenarios and too good at improvising for the ones she doesn’t see coming. But now, sitting in this suddenly too-small room, she finds herself backed into a corner.

Rio’s eyes are searching, searching, searching for something Agatha doesn’t know how to articulate or give.

So instead, like any trapped animal would, she attempts to gnaw her own leg off.

“It’s self-interest,” she says, forcing her tone to remain casual. Haughty, even, utterly uncaring. “You had that roommate three years ago that wouldn’t let you get enough sleep. More money meant you could find a better place. You’re more useful to me if you’re rested. It was purely an investment.”

Rio shakes her head. “I gotta admit, that’s pretty good. You might want to try it a couple more times. You might convince me yet.”

“Last time I checked, I don’t need to convince you of anything. You work for me. I made a business decision. End of story.”

She stares Rio down, willing her heart to settle. It’s too much, being called out on something she intended to remain between her and her bank account only.

She’s not a good person. She’s never been a good person. And she wouldn’t take a cut of her own paycheck for an assistant simply because it was a decent thing to do.

The fact that it wasn’t just a business decision is neither here nor there.

Rio tilts her chin up, raising her hands in surrender. “Fine. Have it your way. But why do you let everyone believe that you’re just this one thing, just this heartless monster, when you’re so much more than that?”

“Come on. You’re a big girl. Even if I was… anything more than that, people aren’t very interested in nuance. They want either a saint or a bitch. If you’re a saint, they’ll stone you the moment you slip up. It’s easier if they see you as a bitch. At least you survive if you’re a bitch.”

She can’t quite keep a bitter note out of her voice, but it’s the harsh truth. She’s known it for years, and she’s accepted it. Love is too fickle an emotion to actually get you anywhere. Respect, even if it’s born out of hate or fear, is much more reliable.

Rio opens her mouth like she wants to counter that, or maybe ask another question, but she doesn’t get a chance to. A knock on the door interrupts her.

Her eyes linger on Agatha for a moment longer before darting away.

“That’ll be the pillows,” she says. “I’ll get them.”

She rises from her chair, and Agatha, for a flash, feels suddenly bereft.

Rio deposits the extra pillows on the bed a moment later. She fluffs them out for good measure and gives a satisfied hum. Agatha watches her from her seat. Dark hair falling down her back over the wrinkled shirt. The single-minded dedication with which she arranges the pillows on the bed.

If I can help you do that, one lunch order at a time, then that’s what I’m gonna do. It's as simple as that.

“I’m gonna hit the shower,” Rio says. “Do you need anything else?”

Agatha shakes her head. “No, thank you. Go ahead.”

Rio pauses, for half a heartbeat, in the middle of the room. Then, she walks into the bathroom and closes the door behind her.

It takes Agatha a few minutes, listening to the sound of running water, chewing on her perfectly manicured nail, to realize she said thank you.

Outside the window, a flash lights up the quickly descending darkness. Moments later, quiet pattering on the glass joins the sound of the shower.

With lightning and a crack of distant thunder, the storm begins.

It must be well after midnight by the time Agatha accepts that she’s not going to fall asleep.

Insomnia has always been something that’s existed in her life, an unwelcome visitor that would drop in seemingly at random, taking up residence in her brain for days or weeks at a time. Sometimes it was due to stress; other times, her mind simply would not settle even if there wasn’t anything worth fretting over. 

Normally, she has her ways to deal with it: pills or booze, or, in her worse moments, a little bit of both.

But she didn’t bring the pills on this trip, since they should have been home by now, and the only supply of booze she currently has access to is beer that’s both shitty and warm after sitting on the hotel room desk for the last few hours. 

If she was desperate enough, she could go down to the hotel bar for a glass of something stronger, but the idea of having to interact with anyone makes her bones feel too heavy to move, like someone’s poured liquid cement into them and let it set, weighing her down.

So she lies in bed and stares at the ceiling, the urge to toss and turn squashed by the acute awareness of the body sound asleep next to her.

She makes a point of not looking at Rio’s sleeping form, and the small room does not have much else to look at, so ceiling it is.

The rain roars outside, absolutely torrential. The windows muffle the worst of it, designed to keep the noise of the outside world away from the sensitive ears of four-star hotel dwellers, but it still provides a background to Agatha’s restless thoughts. When lightning flashes through the not-quite closed curtains, she counts in her head until thunder cracks in the sky.

It’s not sophisticated entertainment, but it allows her to focus on something else for a few seconds. Something other than the tangled web of memories and anxieties and desires that she can’t push away no matter how hard she tries.

She’s always been good at compartmentalizing. It was necessary in order to survive in that dreary little town in that awful house that had never been a home, when the only thing that kept her from succumbing to despair was locking it away in a deep dark corner of her mind and focusing on an escape plan instead. 

In LA, throwing herself into work and climbing the ladder has been the only thing that mattered; that she’s allowed to matter. First, it had been about finding a job, any job that would keep her off the streets. Then, it had been about turning a shitty internship into a slightly less shitty entry level clerk position, and then about going from a cubicle to an office, and then to a bigger office with a nicer view. 

It had been about securing a place to live, and then upgrading to an apartment that didn’t have one bathroom and five people sharing it, and then to one that was bigger and infinitely more expensive than that house whose smell still haunts her nightmares. Incense and moth balls. One could suffocate on it.

Her apartment smells like cashmere-scented candles and clean linens. It’s all open concept space and modern furniture, a far cry from the heavy dark wood and never-ending quilts of her shitty childhood. The Harknesses had lived in that house since Pilgrim times, or at least it had felt like it. 

Agatha knows perfectly well she has been written out of the will and that the house will go on sale once Evanora finally kicks it. She also knows she will take great pleasure in buying it and demolishing the whole thing down to the last bloody foundation. 

She will also make sure Evanora gets a grand old headstone. Perfect for her to go and spit at, which will be the only occasion she’ll ever set a foot in that town again.

That thought does make her smile, however briefly.

But underneath that pleasant idea, there is still a whole choppy ocean of things she really would rather not dwell on, and yet it keeps flooding that precious part of her brain that controls sleep, pushing the release of it out of reach despite her tiredness. As if all those things she’s so carefully kept under lock and key have decided they have had enough.

All these years, compartmentalizing has served, first and foremost, one purpose: to keep her mind away from the fact that she’s lonely.

Agatha squeezes her eyes shut.

Fuck, she’s so lonely.

Sure, she has so-called friends, and no shortage of interested partners if she deems it worth her time to go out and look for one for a night. She has people at work whose names she sometimes cares to remember. She has her hairdresser and that guy from the Mexican place next to her apartment who has her order memorized.

That counts for something, right?

And yet, in the dark, with the storm steadily lashing the windows, she knows it’s pitifully little to show for after forty years of life. Career-wise, she is thriving; even if she’s not at the top yet, she will be. She just has to bide her time, and she’s always been good at that. Work and plan and scheme and wait, and eventually everything will fall into place. That’s how she’s always operated, and it’s always worked out for her.

She’s so close to it now. She can practically taste it.

And it’s not enough.

The truth is, if she dropped dead tomorrow, it would be a few days before anyone would notice. People at work, eventually. Though Agatha doubts any of them would show up to the funeral. The company would send flowers, maybe. 

All in all, she would get a lesser turnout than her mother, who could at least count on her church friends and neighbors to attend, all those holier-than-thou people who never did a damn thing to help Agatha.

There would be no one to cry over her, and she’s never given a damn about that, because why would she care what happens when she’s no longer here? It was what she could achieve while she was alive that mattered.

Maybe that’s what’s bothering her. That no matter how high she climbs, it won’t be worth anything once she’s gone. That no one will stand over her grave and remember.

Well, except for one person, but that’s exactly who Agatha is trying not to think about.

And isn’t that just fucking inconvenient that that same person is snoring quietly mere inches from her. Close enough to touch if she’s not careful.

She’s never been careful about anything in her life, and yet she’s lying here, practically paralyzed, every nerve ending on alert from Rio’s proximity. It tickles her skin like a light electric charge. Not strong enough to hurt, but enough to keep her wide awake, fingers twitching restlessly at her sides. 

It’s not the first time Rio has been this close. Like thread from a yarn ball, her thoughts keep unspooling in the direction of that night. Her heart picks up in her chest, even though the memories are fuzzy, blurred by how much she had drunk that night and how hard she has tried to forget for the last two years.

But she can’t forget.

And tonight, she can’t not remember, either. 

They’re two very different things, in the end.

***

Time is more a suggestion than a fact that night.

The fresh air outside the bar and the awkward movement as she walks half-slumped against Rio help sober her up a little, but she still feels like she’s floating in a realm of not-quite reality. One moment, they’re in the street, the neon signage of the bar illuminating Rio’s profile; the next, they’ve moved several blocks over, passing by a club with music blaring through the door thrown open to release a wave of people onto the sidewalk.

Rio’s smell tickles Agatha’s nose. Her body is warm against her own, and surprisingly steady, showing no signs of strain even when Agatha’s legs forget how to work and Rio has to practically drag her.

It must take forever, and it must be the most pathetic she’s ever acted, but in that moment, she really has no capacity to care. Even her eyelids are heavy, and blinking takes far more effort than it usually does.

Her throat is raw from all the booze. She doesn’t think she can speak, but she must, because then it starts to hurt even worse. They have to stop near an alleyway when she dry heaves, certain that she’s actually going to throw up this time.

She doesn’t, but Rio’s cool hand is on the nape of her neck, holding her hair as Agatha doubles over, gasping for air.

And then, time warps again, folds onto itself, and Agatha blinks, and they’re in a pizza parlor.

She’s sitting on an uncomfortable chair with a faux leather seat and a metal back that’s digging into her spine. The ceiling lights are so bright that there are rainbow halos around them, and the smell of oregano and baked dough fills her nose. Her vision takes far too long to zone in on Rio at the counter, swiping her card to pay for her order.

“Okay,” she says, joining Agatha at the table a moment later and sliding a bottle of water towards her. “Drink this. The whole thing.”

Under any other circumstances, Agatha would not take too kindly to being ordered around. Even if it was for her own good.

Especially if it was for her own good.

She fumbles with the cap. Rio unscrews it for her, and Agatha gulps the water until the cheap plastic of the bottle begins to collapse onto itself. The cool liquid goes down easily.

Agatha has drunk things five hundred times more expensive than this, but the top-shelf liquor in her collection has nothing on the relief this quite-possibly-bottled-tap-water brings with it.

Rio is watching her, her elbows resting on the sticky table. Her face is clean of any of the makeup she likes to wear during the day, the heavy eyeliners and dark lipsticks gone, and she looks so young in these too-bright lights all of a sudden. Her brow is furrowed in concern.

“Better?” she asks when Agatha sets the crumpled plastic down. 

Agatha grunts. She’s not sure if it’s supposed to be a confirmation or not, but Rio seems to take it as one.

Then again, she pays Rio to anticipate her needs, so perhaps she’s become fluent in grunting over the years too.

She’s so tired. Even the table between them looks like a good enough place to nap, and on a better day she wouldn’t go anywhere near it without a healthy dose of sanitizer.

Truthfully, she wouldn’t go anywhere near it, period. This place would not even exist on her map of LA.

And it’s there, of course, that she feels a tear slide down her cheek.

Once again, something she’s wanted so badly, that she’s busted her ass for, has passed her by. Her whole career that she’s built from the ground up stalled by the whims of some asshole in a suit.

And now she’s drunk as fuck and sniffling in a pizza parlor that probably has two stars on Google, with her assistant holding her hand.

Her brain stops. Rewinds. Analyzes that last piece of information.

Rio is holding her hand. 

Agatha blinks down at their intertwined fingers in the middle of the table. She has no idea how it happened, or who initiated it. Only that Rio’s thumb brushes over the back of her hand soothingly, just like it had stroked the back of Agatha’s neck when she was not-quite vomiting in an alley ten minutes ago.

Maybe it’s been twenty minutes. Maybe forty. Agatha finds she has absolutely no clue.

She only knows that everything is too bright, and her head hurts, and she’s completely and utterly exhausted, and Rio’s hand feels like some sort of a lifeline, keeping her just barely together.

Agatha has no idea when she’s last been held, in any sense of the word.

But then someone calls out Rio’s name, and she lets go to get up from the table. Agatha stares at where her hand had just been, the pounding of her heart in her ears drowning out any other sound.

Time spins. The cool air out in the street wafts over her overheated cheeks and forehead. An Uber pulls over, and Rio, now holding a cardboard pizza box, guides Agatha to it. She sinks into the backseat, and all her muscles seem to give out at once, as if they’ve had enough of holding any part of her upright.

She must fall asleep, because then Rio is shaking her awake, and they get out  of the car, though Agatha pretty much falls out of it. Rio walks over to her side to haul her onto the sidewalk, and when Agatha finally finds her footing, green sneakers on concrete, she looks up to find her apartment building in front of them.

Home, or the closest thing to it she’s ever had.

“Keys?” Rio asks, and Agatha realizes Rio has been carrying her bag around on her shoulder. Probably for the best, since Agatha can barely carry herself.

“Inner pocket,” she says. Rio unzips the bag, somehow managing to do it while holding the pizza box in her other hand, and after a moment she fishes out a bunch of keys held together on a flimsy keychain. She takes a look at it and smiles.

The keychain is a cheap souvenir from a random gift shop Agatha found on her first day in LA. She spent two dollars on it for good luck, the young and bright-eyed thing that she was.

She still has it, though the design is barely recognizable after so many years of wear and tear. But in its own way, it seems to have worked, and she feels strangely apprehensive about getting rid of it.

She doesn’t allow herself too many irrationalities, but this is one of them. 

When they get to Agatha’s front door, through a haze of brightly-lit and empty hallways, Rio opens the locks with only minimal fumbling, as though she knows exactly which key fits where, despite having never been here before. She pushes the door open, and the familiar clean scents wrap around Agatha.

It makes her feel better, as does the blessed darkness of the apartment.

She drags herself to the couch and flops down on it, limbs awkward like they’ve turned to jelly. She, quite possibly, has never been this drunk before. The pounding behind her eyes must only be a preview of what’s to come in the morning.

“Here,” Rio says. Agatha cracks one eye open. A glass of water and a plate with two giant, greasy pizza slices are being offered to her. Her stomach makes an uncertain noise at the amount of fat piled up on the plate.

Agatha devours it in approximately five seconds. The grease stains her fingers. The cheese tastes like it’s a year past its due date, though with the amount of chemicals in it, it’s probably making no difference.

It’s the best thing she’s ever eaten, burnt crust and all.

She eats the pizza, and she drinks the water, and then she blinks and realizes she must have dozed off.

It’s still dark outside, just as it was before, but she’s sure some time must have passed. First of all, the pizza has settled in her stomach against all odds, and the pounding behind her eyes has subsided ever so slightly. Second of all, she really needs to pee.

Third of all, Rio, who has been moving around the apartment silently like a helpful ghost, has settled down on the couch next to her, and the TV screen is quietly playing something that Agatha after a few seconds of squinting recognizes to be The L Word. Her assistant has shed her shoes, feet resting on Agatha’s coffee table, and an empty plate covered in grease and crumbs is resting in her lap.

Agatha has had overnight guests in this apartment before, certainly. But none of them has ever dared to look so at home in it.

“You’re still here,” she croaks. Her voice sounds like her throat has been run through a paper shredder. Her neck is stiff from her sleeping position as she tries to sit upright.

Rio hums in response, not taking her eyes off the TV. “Someone has to make sure you’re okay.” It’s not an accusation, more of a duh statement. A Rio-ism, and the worst kind, the kind that makes Agatha realize that someone cares.

Agatha tries to muster up a sarcastic reply, but nothing comes. She’s all clumsiness and regret tonight. “My head is killing me.”

“I figured,” Rio says. “But you should wait a few hours before you take painkillers. If I came near you with a lighter right now, it’s still 50/50 on whether you would catch fire.”

“Very funny.”

“I’ll be here all night.”

“Good news,” Rio says, striding into view with two cups of coffee in a cardboard holder, fully dressed and typing on her phone. “We’re on the next plane out of here. And I got Starbucks.”

Agatha sits up in bed, letting the sheet pool around her waist. She can tell without looking that her hair has turned into a hopeless bird’s nest over the course of the night. “Good morning to you too.”

In the light of day, a wave of uncertainty creeps over her. As if last night may have been nothing but an incredibly vivid, storm-induced dream. Now that she’s naked and unkempt in bed, and Rio is already on the move, leather jacket on, dark lipstick in place, she finds herself at a disadvantage.

But before she can spiral, and possibly do something stupid - an Agatha-ism, if you will - Rio hands her one of the coffee cups and plops down on the side of the bed. One sip of sugary caffeine is enough to pause the incoming meltdown.

Rio looks at her as she takes a sip of her own drink. Agatha wonders if she can sense the panic churning in her stomach.

She tries to think of something to say, but all of this is too new, too terrifying, and she’s constantly tongue-tied by the sheer unfamiliarity of it. The desire to say the right thing, and the fear of messing up.

“Blonde vanilla latte,” she blurts out, and her mind promptly short-circuits.

Way to fucking go, Agatha.

Rio pauses mid-way to another sip. She raises an eyebrow.

Agatha stammers, decides to shut up, but her mouth is already moving again. “Blonde vanilla latte. With oat milk. Your favorite.”

She feels pathetic, half-naked and spewing nonsense. I know things about you too. I can learn, see? I can learn.

Slowly, Rio grins. She leans forward and kisses Agatha, deep and slow. It tastes like vanilla syrup. 

Agatha’s heartbeat picks up, then settles. Last night had not been a dream. Rio woke up in the morning and didn’t change her mind.

When Rio pulls back, she taps her fingers lightly against Agatha’s collarbone. “Now chop chop. The flight is in two hours.”

Agatha hums. “Someone’s bossy today.”

“Learned from the best.”

She chucks a piece of dark fabric at Agatha’s head. Agatha peels it away from her face and wrinkles her nose in disgust.

Across the polyester-blend T-shirt, I ❤️ Florida is splattered in Comic Sans.

“You’ve got to be kidding me.”

Rio snorts. She unzips her jacket to reveal an identical T-shirt.

“We needed some fresh clothes. You should feel lucky I didn’t get you the underwear to match.”

“Remind me to never come back to this place again.”

“Already sent the memo to your email.”

Agatha untangles herself from the sheets. When she passes Rio on the way to the bathroom, she stops and hesitates.

Just try.

“Um.” She looks at Rio, who looks back at her. Calm, searching, waiting. “Do you want to come over tonight? My place?”

Rio’s lips split into a grin. “Agatha Harkness. Are you asking me on a date?”

It’s teasing, but Rio’s eyes are soft, and Agatha swallows down the self-conscious jab on the tip of her tongue. “You got other plans?”

Rio shrugs. “I should be free. Unless my boss keeps me working overtime.”

“That boss of yours sounds like a handful.”

“Oh, she is.” Rio’s gaze travels down. “But she does have a great ass, so…”

Agatha feels herself grin back. She’s never been one to turn down a compliment. “Okay, then.”

“Okay.”

When she closes the bathroom door behind her, still holding the vile T-shirt, she sees her reflection in the mirror.

Her hair looks tragic. There are bags under her eyes after just a few hours of sleep. There’s a bite mark blooming purple on her shoulder. 

She looks happy.

She is happy.

As they board the plane to California an hour and forty minutes later, the polyester-blend grossly sticking to her back with the humidity, she can’t even remember to be annoyed. 

Not when Rio’s hand lightly sneaks into hers, lacing their fingers together on the arm rest between their seats as the plane takes off.
