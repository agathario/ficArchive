Chapter 1: Tech’s Next Big Thing

TECH’S NEXT BIG THING

By Agatha Harkness

It’s not everyday a billionaire buys you a cup of coffee.

But Rio Vidal, as polite as she is rich, insisted on buying my cappuccino for me. Even as I flashed my corporate card at her she shook her head and tapped her black Amex on the screen reader - not forgetting to add a 100% tip in the process.

Money talks, they say, but wealth clearly treats.

After some polite small talk about the difference between a flat white and a latte (Vidal has ordered the latter and made it matcha), we head on over to a small table in the far back.

A few heads turn in our direction. I’m unsure whether it’s just the polite interest of the early crowd of Felix Roasting Co. in Midtown Manhattan on a random Wednesday morning or if people know one of the richest women in the world is in their presence.

But still, people look. Perhaps Rio Vidal is just that recognisable in her forest green bomber and black cap, or perhaps it’s the Bottega Veneta boots that stand out to the casual observer.

Either way, there’s something about Rio Vidal. A quiet confidence, I think, as she offers me the bench seat and slips into her own only after I’ve sat down. Then again, you’d be hard pressed to find someone who wasn’t confident when they are the sole heiress to a $125bn fortune.

“I’ve not been here before,” Rio admits, her dark eyes scanning around the art deco splendour of the Manhattan cafe.

I say nothing as I set up my dictaphone and remove my notebook from my bag. Rio continues to scan the space.

“I didn’t even know it existed.”

I raise my brows and Rio, turning back to me, smiles. “Poor little rich girl, unaware of what’s actually going on around her.”

I take this for what it is, a show of modesty. Faux-modesty.

I ask her if she regularly walks the streets of Manhattan, and if so, where she would usually go.

“Oh, not really,” Rio admits quietly, a shrug suggesting she might not like her own answer. “I work a lot so don’t get much time.”

This, as far as I can tell, appears to be true. Since Rio took over from her father, the tech-entrepreneur Diego Vidal, not two years ago at the tender age of 26, she has transformed the industry with her visionary thinking and socially-conscious promises.

Though some, this author included, are reluctant to trust a billionaire to be as altruistic as she seems. But maybe a cappuccino and a generous tip are good signs.

We make some more small talk and Rio seems to know a surprising amount about architecture, which doesn’t lend to the most riveting conversation but does lend to a picture being built.

It turns out that Rio Vidal is a bit of a nerd. She knows a lot about a lot and takes a great deal of pleasure in announcing this in the form of “fun facts” and “Agatha did you know”s.

It takes a while until I can get to our real reason for being here: my writing a profile on her. Rio seems insistent in teaching me about the origins of the word ‘felix’, as in the place we’re now sat and the Latin word for ‘luck’.

I refrain from telling one of the richest women in the world that my rudimentary Latin has me aware of its meaning and instead let the heiress talk herself out of the nerves she’s clearly experiencing; her leg bobbing under the table, her eyes unable to settle on anything in particular, let alone my face.

When asked if she believes she’s a lucky person Rio laughs, a soft, unexpectedly modest noise.

“For being born into my family?”

I shrug and suggest the families we’re born into are down to luck.

Rio, now established as a thinker, is silent for a while and her gaze settles, finally, on my face as she considers her answer.

“I suppose,” Rio utters, a steadier cadence from the rapid-fire knowledge-sharing of before, “I suppose it is luck. But I work hard too.”

And it’s true, I consider. Rio Vidal was born into an incredibly rich family. She went to all the best schools, did her undergrad at MIT where she studied Computer Programming and pursued a minor in Economics. She’s since published papers on Quantum Computing as well as spearheading research into Computational Neuroscience that has placed Vidal Systems ahead of most of its competitors.

So I don’t doubt that Rio Vidal is a hard-worker, or that she very well might be a genius. But I push her on the “luck” of it all.

“Do you think you’d be where you are now if your parents weren’t who they are?”

Rio, though more settled than she had been upon first meeting for all of ten seconds, now looks uncomfortable again. No longer quietly confident with her rounded shoulders; she looked positively horrified, in fact, if you can believe that of such a powerhouse of a woman.

“Well,” Rio begins, scrambling for words that came so easily when spewing off facts earlier as I quietly listened, “I suppose… it’s hard to say.”

Rio scrambles a little while longer and somewhere in the scrambling manages to spill her matcha. A man rushes forward, one I hadn’t noticed until now. I realise, as Rio quickly dismisses him and blushes whilst doing so, that this is not a man I was supposed to notice.

And, with my journalistic integrity intact, I turn to sweep my eyes around the space as Rio cleans herself up as best she can and apologises profusely while doing so. There are three men, by my count, that I am not supposed to notice.

Lucky Rio Vidal doesn’t have a harem. She instead has a caravan of muscles following her wherever she goes.

She explains without my having to ask that the man who offered help is James, the guy by the door is Steve, and the man with the largest arms I’ve ever seen is Thor. Which sounds fake, but Rio assures me is not.

“I guess if my parents weren’t who they are I’d be lucky to be able to move through the world without eyes on me at all times.”

Touché. A glimmer of humor beneath the shaking, nervous exterior of a woman who it seems would be more at home defeating anyone at Trivial Pursuit than sitting in a coffee shop with a journalist answering questions about her life.

Then again, she agreed to this profile.

When I voice my suspicions of how she might prefer to be spending her Wednesday morning Vidal shrugs again, a gesture I’m quickly realising is defensive as much as it is explanatory.

“Everything I do is for the company. I figure as I’m one of the most recognisable faces of it, the least I could do is be open enough to do a profile.”

Vidal seems unsettled by her own words and as a member of staff rushes over, clearly sent by either James, Steve, or the unbelievably named Thor, I watch her blush and avoid eye contact with the young man with a purposefully bleached eyebrow.

Maybe it’s an aversion to being helped or made a fuss of, but being who she is, I know that can’t be quite right.

Rio Vidal, at twenty eight, is leading a company that many people twice her age could only dream of. She has more letters after her name that most people can even fathom. She has more digits on her bank balance than most countries' GDPs. She’s exceptional in every way.

Not just for buying me, a lowly journalist, an overpriced cappuccino. But for her plans to transform Vidal Systems.

Until recently, the company has come under fire for being progressive in name only, for treating its employees worse than poorly, for not upholding its promises of “Reinvent[ing] The Standard”.

And Rio, as I watch her now, looks nothing like the kind of woman - lucky or otherwise - who will be able to turn things around. She appears to be a cog in a machine, not the engine.

“Where were we?” Rio asks, clearing her throat and shuffling her seat closer to the table. Cap still on, the impact of Rio looking up from beneath the peak is immediate.

Because another thing Rio Vidal is known for, is her ability to look less than the sum of her parts. And, even makeup free and dressed down (expensive boots be damned), she’s a head-spinning mix of wide-eyed coquettish charm and femme fatale beauty. That is, in the right light.

I catch a glimmer of this as she looks up, before her expression slips back to vaguely frightened and totally shy.

Before any more spillages can happen, instead the conversation steers back to Vidal Systems.

She is no more comfortable on this topic than any other we’ve broached.

In fact, it appears as if she’d rather go back to her etymological interests instead.

Still, I power on, and hope she’ll catchup. I ask about the recent redundancies made, of which she refers to as “unfortunate, unhappy, a necessary evil.”

For a while Rio talks about a “sacred balance” of trust and forward momentum. She talks of this long enough that I start to wonder if she’s been rehearsing this speech for a while. I briefly check to see if she’s somehow smuggled it in on a napkin.

But no, it appears, rehearsed or otherwise, to be a genuine rally for CEOs everywhere who are simply “doing their best” for their company and the people they have left.

When asked about the public perception of Vidal Systems Rio simply shrugs and says “we cannot control what the press says about us”.

Interesting, then, that a young genius, a nepo baby of great renown intent in turning her company around has come to said press to have her say when she doesn’t seem to be saying much of anything.

I ask about this, about why a profile, why now, why me. After all, I have peers more qualified, with more experience, with a greater knowledge of technology or the business world.

Her response is surprising but not entirely unexpected: “We’re around the same age. I thought it might be easier to chat with you.”

When I ask if she’s this shy at work, Rio seems to take offence. For the first time since our arrival, she sits up straight and lifts her face fully from under the protection of her cap. A cap that, by the way, reads: ‘Directed by David Lynch’.

“I think just because I’m introverted doesn’t mean I’m shy.”

I take a sip of my cappuccino and give her a second to add to her words but she says nothing and clears her throat.

This wildfire CEO, ‘Top 30 Under 30’ in each year of her twenties so far, blushes.

When I suggest that her introversion could be construed as shyness, Rio clears her throat again and utters: “I do my job and I do it well. I don’t need to be a people person to succeed.”

A pull quote if ever I heard one. I smile and Rio, bewildered, smiles back. I ask how she manages C-Suite pressures and stakeholder management. To which she responds that she has a great team around her and good advice.

Her father is still pulling the strings, it seems.

Though this idea makes Rio’s tentative earlier smile seem like a figment of the imagination. She looks guarded again, and briefly disappears behind her cap before she responds.

“I am the CEO. It is my company. My father has given me the reins.”

Given, perhaps, but they appear to be holding them together. This is suggested to her and again Rio disappears briefly before she is back, sitting so straight in her chair now she looks uncomfortable.

“I have a vision. I intend to see it through.”

Just last year, at an annual tech stalwart event called the ‘Open Source Summit’, Rio had been booked as one of the headline speakers. But a strange mix of jet lag (though she’d flown from only across the country), stomach flu (though she’d been seen out for breakfast that morning), and work commitments (why agree to headline if you have work commitments?), meant that Rio pulled out of her slot not five minutes before she was due on stage.

I ask her about this and Rio is suddenly unable to make eye contact again. Her fingers twitch in such a way that I can see a new matcha being needed shortly.

“Regrettable,” Rio says, in her best, media trained tone. She sticks her tongue into her cheek and I watch it poking it there for a second before I press on the matter.

Rio shakes her head and before anything else happens, I know our meeting is over.

“It’s been a pleasure,” the genius across from me says with, so far as I can tell, a genuine, though shy smile. She holds her hand out to me across the table and when I take it it’s warm and provides a soft squeeze as she shakes my own.

With that, the billionaire tech entrepreneur releases me. Her matcha lays untouched on the table and I watch her go, flanked by the three men all hired to protect her.

As I gather my thoughts, turn off my voice recorder, and scribble some notes, the waiter with the bleached brow approaches me. He asks if I knew who that woman was, if I work for her, and if I could get him a job.

I question why and he simply says: “She was here, like, ten minutes before you. She slipped like two thousand dollars to my colleague and told us to share it out among ourselves.”

I can’t help but wonder if she had the same generosity when dealing with her own staff’s redundancy packages. Perhaps not, I think.

Later, as I gather my thoughts on the interview that lasted barely half the time I’d pencilled into my diary, I realise that Rio’s impromptu lesson in Latin lasted almost half the time we had.

So feliciter velim, Rio Vidal. I think you’ll need it.

The first thing that woke Agatha up was the sound of a crash as her phone fell off her nightstand onto the thin carpet of her Manhattan rental.

She cursed and scrambled first for her glasses and in doing so knocked them off the side too.

“Fuck!” She shouted louder this time, scrambling more desperately when her phone begs to protest against being ignored on the floor by vibrating aggressively again.

Feeling around in the dark she found her glasses first and put them over bleary eyes before she grabbed her phone.

It continued to ring. An unknown number. Agatha scowled at it.

She slid to accept.

“Yes?”

“Did I do something to piss you off?”

Agatha blinked into the dim light of her small bedroom and sat back against the headboard. The voice sounded familiar enough to trigger something in her brain but she couldn’t place it.

“Who is this?”

“It’s Rio,” the voice said, and then, after a beat, “Vidal. We met for coffee a couple weeks ago.”

Agatha looked at her alarm clock on the side. Reflecting her bad mood the angry red letters read: 03:33. She groaned and rubbed the back of her neck.

“Do you know what time it is?”

“Oh.”

Agatha groaned, not quite in her right mind yet. Was this a dream? No, she thought as she yawned and stretched below her thin duvet, dislodging the laptop that sat on the very edge of her bed.

“Fuck! Jesus.”

“What happened?”

Agatha dropped her cell and scrambled over her bed, nearly toppling over herself. Her glasses fell to the tip of her nose and she groaned once more as she supported herself - now half off the bed - with one hand and threw her laptop back up with the other.

Once righted, and angrier than before, she grabbed her cell back again.

“If this isn’t a good time I can…”

“Why are you calling me at three in the morning?!”

Agatha, blind again, not because she had no contacts and wasn’t wearing her glasses, but this time from rage, ripped her laptop open as she pinned her cell between her ear and her shoulder.

She could hear a lot of stuttering but not much answering down the line.

“What? Suddenly developed a stutter? Some kind of w-w-w-werewolf?”

She heard Rio clear her throat and answer in a quiet but even tone: “I’m in England at a conference. I forgot about the time difference. I’m sorry. I’ll let you get back to sleep.”

Agatha arched a brow as she tapped her nails on the edge of the faux-marble-actually-plastic countertop.

“Do you know the etymology of ‘incest’?”

Silence, filled only my Agatha’s gentle tapping.

“Yes, it, um, Latin again. I guess most things are Latin. Incestum. Or incestus. Impure, unchaste.”

Agatha hummed, only vaguely impressed but unwilling to admit it.

“My kettle takes a while,” she offered by way of response, still tapping her fingers as she stood in the four by four space of her kitchen. Enough room for a fridge, a sink, an oven, and one free space of counter that housed her dwindling coffee stash, a couple empty boxes of herbal tea, and her shitty kettle.

“I bought you your cappuccino, you accepted that.”

Agatha huffed but was saved from answering because her kettle began to boil.

“Have you woken me at three in the morning to bribe me, Rio Vidal? I should warn you that since we’ve not established that it isn’t, this call is technically on the record.”

More stuttering. Agatha stopped her tapping and smiled.

“Could it be off the record? Please?”

She hummed, enjoying that she had some power over this apparently powerful woman.

Her mug was full. She loved the kettle down.

“Fine. Off the record. Why have you called me?”

No stuttering now. Silence instead.

Agatha picked up her phone and walked the five feet to her little two-seater couch.

By the time she’d curled her legs beneath herself and placed her cell on the arm, Rio had found her voice again: “Did I do something to piss you off?”

“It’s just…,” Rio interrupted, “It’s just I come across a bit weak or, or ineffectual maybe. Like I’m not leading my own company.”

“Are you leading your own company?”

Silence. Agatha counted to ten in her head before Rio responded.

“Did you know that since 2022 we have made more discoveries in the world of quantum computing than in the past twenty years combined?”

Agatha blinked. She reached for her mug of steaming, bitter coffee. She did not know that. The basic research she’d done on Rio’s company told her that, yes, Vidal Systems was impressive. Rio was impressive. In real terms, in technical terms, Agatha didn’t really know what that meant.

But she believed her.

“Sure,” she said enigmatically, what experience she had telling her that sometimes it was best to let the person you were speaking to explain before you had to ask. People loved to explain themselves.

“Quantum computing comes from a concept from the fifties. A physicist named-...”

“I know where it comes from,” Agatha lied again. Apparently Rio liked to explain herself too much.

“Why don’t you tell me why you think I ‘slaughtered’ you?”

“Well,” Rio said, clearly taking the time to think over her words as Agatha adjusted the hold on her thin mug where it was beginning to burn her fingers. “I lead by example. I work hard. I am polite and I listen to other people, even if they’re more junior than me.”

“Everyone is more junior than you,” Agatha reminded, “You’re the CEO.”

Rio remained silent for a while and Agatha took the opportunity to leave her phone where it was on the arm of the couch. Her apartment was small enough that if Rio spoke again she’d hear it anyway.

Her bedroom was less a bedroom and more a nook in the corner of the living space. It was just wide enough for a queen bed with about a foot to spare for her nightstand which was covered in books and research she’d printed off using someone else’s credits at the office.

She grabbed her laptop - company-owned - and went to sit back down on the couch.

Rio still was silent.

Gently tapping her black phone screen to see if the connection had dropped, she wasn’t surprised to see it hadn’t, and that their time was still ticking on.

“You okay over there, bigshot?”

Rio cleared her throat. “Fine. I was just… have you ever been to London in the fall?”

“Never left the country,” Agatha answered, booting up her laptop.

“Ever?”

“Nope. Only got a passport a year ago. Never could afford it.”

Silence again, a little heavier this time. Much more uncomfortable for Agatha.

“Back to you,” she was quick to say before Rio could offer any pity she did not want. “Why are you uncomfortable with being the leader of your company?”

“I’m not,” Rio uttered, sounding as defensive as Agatha felt. “I’m telling you I do lead.”

“No,” Agatha huffed, her defensiveness turning to impatience. She needed her coffee. Badly. “You said you lead by example. That’s not the same thing. That’s passive. That’s nothing.”

“It’s not nothing,” Rio responded, and Agatha was at least a little pleased her voice sounded stronger now. “Studies show that-...”

“Fuck studies.”

It was a little harsh, maybe. But Agatha wanted Rio to raise her head up and out of a book for a second. If she were in front of her she might have snapped in her face.

“Tell me how your staff interact with you.”

Silence. Agatha filled it by tapping into Google Rio’s name. Her article was one of the first results that came up and she clicked into it again. It had been published just hours before and the reaction had been immediate.

Her boss had called her into his office to congratulate her on such a subtle, but worthy takedown. Getting the gig in the first place to interview Rio had been a huge win for her, but getting to write her up as shy, ineffectual, barely any mouth and barely any trousers, was enough to keep her on staff for a little longer.

It was what she needed; a steady gig with a steady paycheck. She was using the article and the waves it created to bargain for a raise.

“I… stick mostly to my office. I’m working late hours. I get in before most people are out of bed and I… I leave usually late into the night. I’m not a people person.”

“Neither am I,” Agatha countered.

“Your job is talking to people.”

“So is yours.”

“No, no my job is computers. My job is my research and my-...”

“Actually,” Agatha said with a soft huff. Her cursor was hovering over Rio’s face on the webpage. “You gave that up when you agreed to become CEO.”

The little black arrow circled one of Rio’s eyes. It was a flattering picture. Not that it needed to be flattering. Even a bad picture would be a good one of Rio Vidal. She was stood in a beige blazer with her hands tucked into the pockets of her matching pants.

To Agatha’s eye she looked vaguely uncomfortable. But to anyone else she would have just looked like a young, attractive woman being photographed at some industry event. At least she was smiling, Agatha supposed. A sign that she was trying.

“You asked me before about whether I thought I was lucky,” Rio said as Agatha opened a new tab and tapped in ‘Rio Vidal’. “It wasn’t luck that got me this job.”

“Is that right?”

Agatha clicked on ‘images’ in the google search results and suddenly her whole screen was full of Rio.

“The company was about to go under. My dad was incredibly unpopular. The board had lost confidence, we were hemorrhaging money. It was me or have us stripped for parts and sold to the highest bidder.”

“How noble of you to prevent that from happening. Did you tell that story to the people who lost their jobs?”

Rio was silent for a while as Agatha scrolled through endless pictures of the woman who’d sat shyly across from her in the coffee shop two weeks ago.

“Seven thousand six hundred and seventy one.”

Agatha hummed in confusion, tearing her gaze away from a picture of Rio grinning. It looked candid, like she hadn’t known the photo was being taken. And Agatha wondered who or what she was grinning at.

“That’s how many people lost their jobs in the last round.”

“Oh,” Agatha said quietly, staring at the black screen of her phone. She reached around for her coffee again and sat back, careful not to spill any on her laptop.

“I boosted as many packages as I could with my own money. Starting from the lowest payouts.”

Oh.

Agatha cleared her throat, staring still at the grinning Rio on the screen.

“Are you calling me to clear your conscience?”

“I… I’m calling because…”

Rio went silent again. Agatha blew absently over her hot coffee and braved a small sip. It was hot still, too hot for most people probably. But after years of late nights writing shitty copy she was used to the burning so it didn’t bother her enough not to take another sip.

“What would you suggest I do?”

Agatha paused, unsure she’d heard correctly. She glanced at her phone and then back to her laptop screen.

If she couldn’t taste the bitter coffee on her tongue of feeling the warmth of her old laptop probably frying her ovaries in her lap, she’d think again she was dreaming.

“You’re asking the advice of a journalist how to run your company?”

“You think I’m doing a bad job, which suggests you must know how I could do a good job. So… I’m all ears, Agatha.”

Agatha raised her brows and scrolled further in the search results. Rio shaking hands with some man in a suit. Rio smiling for more pictures she clearly didn’t want to be smiling for. Rio on some panel looking a little pale. Rio carrying heavy textbooks and wearing glasses on a college campus.

“Why did you take the job?”

“I told you, the company would have-...”

“I heard you the first time. But you didn’t say why that would be a bad thing.”

Agatha let Rio think for a minute as she scrolled absently through the pictures available on google. Even in 2D, it was clear Rio was shy, was more at home holding her huge textbooks or behind her computer than she was at any event or on any panel. Or, for that matter, being interviewed by a journalist.

“Are you sure I can’t get anything sent over to your apartment?”

Agatha laughed, “Like a muffin basket?”

“If you want.”

Rolling her eyes, she took another small sip of coffee.

Rio Vidal: Making Waves

By Agatha Harkness

It’s been two years I’ve seen Rio Vidal. Two years to the day, in fact, since I’ve seen her.

The pub is called The Woolpack, and boasts a semi-decent fish and chips for, I’m told, a semi-decent price of £15. It is my first time in London and Rio’s first time at this pub.

The locals don’t seem interested in an American with a dictaphone opposite another in a navy cap. And Rio’s security, as muscly (and in one case, as blonde), as they are, don’t draw attention either.

“I wasn’t sure what you’d like,” she said, “but I figure no one hates champagne.”

The result is a bit of a mess, with Rio spilling her own glass of champagne and managing to smash the glass without delivering either the hug or the kiss. I don’t mention the accident and someone comes to clear it up before Rio can blush more crimson than one of the red post boxes not ten feet away from the entrance to the pub.

“To take us home.”

“The wine won’t take long. They’re good here.”

Agatha nodded and bit down on the corner of her lip.

“Are you hiding a bald patch under there?” She indicated Rio’s cap and arched a brow.

Rio stuttered and Agatha smiled as she did. “N-no,” she said quickly, almost throwing the cap off in her haste. She was still in her jacket too, even though she’d taken Agatha’s when they’d first stepped in and hung it up for her.

“I, uh, I’m just going to change. I hate suits and… yeah, I’ll be right back.”

“I couldn’t pick a sweater and the concierge knocked so I… sorry.”

“It took you twenty minutes to decide on a purple sweater?”

“Purple’s your favourite colour,” she said quietly, trimming off the seal on top of the bottle with the small blade.

Rio cleared her throat and glanced Agatha’s way.

“I do my research too. I might not know a lot but… I took a shot at the purple.”

“If I told you my favourite colour was actually green would you run back and change?”

When she removed it she said: “You’re a nerd.”

Rio shrugged and offered a soft “maybe” as she went back to the task of opening the bottle.

“So… was I right? Do you like purple?”

Agatha hummed, trying to play it off as she cast her eyes about the room and Rio poured their wine.

“First class flights, a crazy expensive hotel, champagne, a muffin basket, a purple sweatshirt, are you trying to make sure I write you a flattering profile, Rio Vidal?”

Agatha saved her from another spill by reaching across to take one of the glasses.

“No, no,” Rio spluttered, “No, I… no, not… not that.”

So she gently clinked their glasses and then took a sip of her wine.

“Relax,” she said after the fact, “I like the sweater.”

Rio smiled shyly but wouldn’t look around at her and Agatha debated on what to do next. People weren’t usually this timid. Or, if they were, Agatha wasn’t backwards about going forwards.

On any other night she might already be feeling underneath that sweater. She might already be finding out what Barolo tasted like on Rio’s tongue.

“It’s more fun if we eat on the floor. If, obviously, that’s okay with you?”

But she moved it back quickly, unable to fully commit just like with the arm around her shoulders. “Dinner?” She barked so suddenly and loudly that Agatha’s arm, high above her head in the air, twitched for the shock of it. She brought them down.

“You flew me almost halfway across the world,” Agatha said, when Rio’s face flushed a deep crimson. “Maybe you are my Superman.”

Rio stuttered and Agatha had no idea what she was trying to say but now, she knew, was the perfect time for her to do something else.

Something that required her lips, but not her words.

Kiss me. Kiss me. Kiss me.

She’d calmed down through their evening together but she was still shy, she was still being hesitant.

And that was it, Agatha realised. If she’d gone back to the hotel room of any other woman where the power imbalance was so vastly swayed in her direction, and not Agatha’s, she’d have tried to have Agatha naked and on her back the moment she’d stepped inside. “Thank you for tonight,” Agatha whispered, awestruck as Rio’s hand wrapped tenderly around her wrist and her eyes fluttered closed. They rested their foreheads together and Agatha breathed Rio in.

They’d crossed a line and Agatha couldn’t step back. Not until Rio did. Not until Rio let her go and physically moved.

And when she did she was smiling. Not a shy smile anymore, but something different. She looked entirely changed, almost relaxed, but more just… happy.

Reality had crept in at the sides, suddenly unencumbered by Rio’s nervous energy that had kept it at bay all evening.

Rio was rich. Crazy rich. She was also famous, or at least, known. She was powerful. She was also a person Agatha had interviewed twice now. She was a person her boss wanted her to keep interviewing.

She was a person with a cute smile and soft hands and endless, ridiculous facts.

She wouldn’t be smiling if she couldn’t keep up interviewing Rio - her highest profile interview to date. She wouldn’t be smiling if everything she’d worked hard for was taken away for the sake of hearing some more fun facts and finding out if Rio would ever manage to wrap her arm around her.

Enjoy your day in London! Feel free to charge anything you want to the room. Excited for dinner. Your Superman :)

Credit card significantly damaged from the impromptu decision, on the plane she exorcised her sins by writing up Rio’s profile.

RIO VIDAL: RELUCTANT SUPERHERO

By Agatha Harkness

Five years ago, when I first met Rio Vidal, she was nervous. But this time, it’s me who’s nervous.

Perhaps it’s because the past two times I’ve interviewed Rio we’ve been in a coffee shop in Manhattan and then an inconsequential pub in London.

But now? Now it’s rush hour and I’m on a sweaty, packed subway on my way to the downtown offices of Vidal Systems where a press conference is to be held about a new computational discovery or something equally as technical and complicated.

(Editor’s note: For more information on Verdant, see Peter Parker’s article Vidal’s New Verdant Venture.)

It’s a much more formal setup than I’m used to when running profiles of the very rich and very famous. But I’ve made an exception for Rio, given she’s been reluctant to do any profiles since our last.

Rio, reluctant as she is brilliant, has, as far as the world and media knows, kept herself locked away in her high tower these past three years. Rarely spotted, hardly ever heard from, she’s taken her privacy seriously these past few years.

In fact, when I got the call to attend this press conference I was surprised my name was even in the list. Colleagues from different outlets are all business or tech writers. And then there’s me, a lowly culture writer, sitting at the big boys table.

It should be noted that due to the unusualness of my attendance at an event like this, I had to rush out the night before and buy a suit. And here is where I should note that I did it the night before not because I was procrastinating, but because the invitation only came through the day before.

Apparently I was a last minute addition to the party.

The check in process at 370 Park Avenue is a long and arduous one. I’m part of a queue of similarly dressed people, some I recognise, some I don’t. We all make small talk and it’s clear there’s anticipation in the air about this new project of Vidal’s.

It takes nearly twenty minutes for me to receive my pass and another five to take my place in the second queue for the turnstiles. When successfully through I’m pulled aside by a woman I know to be named Alice, Rio’s assistant - I’ve done my research.

Alice isn’t the kind of assistant one might expect of a high-powered, billionaire CEO. But she’s exactly the kind of assistant you’d expect of Rio.

She’s dressed professionally, but not corporately. Her hair is shaggy, cut to look like a seventies rockstar with a red streak at the front; she’s Joan Jett in a pantsuit.

Alice greets me warmly, but I sense that she’s nervous, and while I assume it’s the pressure of today I catch the way she eyes me and understand she might be wary of letting Rio be profiled on such a big day for her company.

I’m informed by Alice that I’ll have time with Rio after the press conference. I’m also told that I’ll be the only journalist allowed time with Rio after the press conference.

This surprises me but given how out of my depth I already am, I say nothing to reveal that fact. Alice, shrewder than she looks, seems to understand already and agrees with me that it is surprising.

She sends me off to join the crowds and soon I’m just a tiny cog in this strange, journalistic machine.

Vidal Systems, unusually for such expensive real estate in Manhattan, owns this entire building. Over fifty floors of little worker bees in a glass hive.

From reading up on it, I know that a lot of these floors are reserved for the people who do the real work: the programmers and technical wizards who keep the whole operation running. We then have the business people, marketers, salespeople, product teams and other general operational staff.

It’s widely known that Rio herself likes to sit on the same floor as her programmers. She has a small office, no larger than any other head of department, on the forty seventh floor. It’s a sign of her humility, and her desire, above everything, to be hidden behind a screen instead of in front of a podium.

When we arrive on the fiftieth floor of Vidal Tower, we’re greeted by staff showing us to our seats and to a large table full of self-serve coffee airpots. I help myself to a mug of black coffee and take a seat in the back.

It doesn’t take long for the seats to fill and I’m treated to mingled conversation about what Verdant could mean for the tech industry, what it could mean for Vidal Systems, and what I could mean for the financial backers of certain media companies.

It's clear to me that today is a big day. The excitement I noticed earlier is not only excitement; it’s also nerves.

Rio Vidal, shy and unassuming, is disrupting the sacred balance.

It takes almost thirty minutes for the room to hush and the first speaker to step on to the stage. The speaker in question happens to be Diego Vidal, much to the intrigue of the journalists around me.

Diego speaks eloquently about his daughter and her work. He uses phrases like “a beautiful mind as complex and intricate as the systems she creates”. But it’s clear in the room that people are impatient for Rio to emerge. The energy shifts as Diego finally announces his daughter and there’s an awkward moment where no one appears from the direction he’s indicating.

I wonder if Rio’s used one of her old excuses (jet lag/stomach flu/work commitments), but she hasn’t. She’s maybe instead learnt the art of a dramatic entrance, as she stalks onto the stage in a forest green pantsuit, looking every bit the confident CEO.

If you’re reading this profile for technical information or astute observations about what the market might do with the birth of Verdant, I suggest you stop reading now.

We’ll focus instead on the way Rio shuffles her papers, the way she keeps her gaze down, the way she adjusts her glasses.

This is the Rio I recognise from our last two meetings. She’s nervous, and looks it. But she takes a moment, she allows the applause to peter out.

And then she looks up, she surveys her captured audience. She removes her glasses and begins to speak. The room listens.

“Welcome,” she says, “thank you for taking the time to be here.”

It seems that Rio Vidal has come into herself in the three years since our last conversation. She stands tall, barely fidgets, and everyone, including myself, is enraptured by her.

As she continues, she remains calm and speaks clearly, without faltering. Her eyes sweep over the crowd in a way that suggests she’s been practising how to do this. As most public speakers have to.

There is, however, one moment of hesitation. It happens when her eyes land on me, the one outlier in this group. I smile, and try to look like I belong. And Rio looks away the moment my lips twitch into what I’m hoping is a professional and businesslike smile. This is so clearly not my arena.

She stammers, just once, and it’s clear that Rio as a CEO is powerful and confident. But Rio as a person, as someone who is about to be interviewed about her, very private, private life, is less so.

She puts her glasses back on and continues.

As the speech goes on, the crowd begins to respond to certain buzzwords or images on the screen behind Rio. She never pauses or acknowledges them and in this way gives herself away. The speech has been memorised and practised to a tee. Any pause for breath at any point that hasn’t been rehearsed would throw her off completely.

So she powers through, delivering, from what I can tell, an incredibly successful and information spiel about Verdant. When she’s done she doesn’t hang around at the podium, despite the calls for questions from the energised audience.

Instead, having done her job, she takes a seat at the back of the stage and one of the Project Supervisors takes her place. This man is Stephen Strange, an apt case of nominative determinism. It would be like if Rio were named “Rio Genius” or I were named “Agatha Not Really Sure What’s Going On Here”.

The conference goes downhill quickly when Stephen steps up. My peers begin to clamor for attention from, not Stephen, but Rio. They shout her name and yell questions at her about the size of the undertaking, the cost to consumers, the technical aspect of the project that are too complex for me to transcribe even after listening back to my recording several times.

Stephen tries his best but it’s a no-win situation. The crowd demands Rio and the crowd eventually gets what it wants.

There’s a high-pitched speaker sound that rings out through the large room and silence descends.
