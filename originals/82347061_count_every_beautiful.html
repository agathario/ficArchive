Chapter 1

Of all of the places Jen expects to see someone she personally actually knows, Bloomington is not it. But there’s no mistaking the dark hair (albeit shorter) and eyes. She maneuvers her way to the bar.

“April? April Ludgate?” She knows it’s her, but wants to pretend that she isn’t  sure.

April turns, brow furrowed. “Jen Barkley?”

“In the flesh.”

“Why?”

“What?”

April shakes her head as Jen squeezes into the space next to her. When the man sitting on the barstool turns to glare, she raises an eyebrow. “Do you mind?” It takes a few moments of staring, but ultimately he huffs and vacates his seat. She waves a hand at the bartender even as she mentally calculates what the wine selection here might be. 

“What are you doing in Bloomington?” April asks.

“Mayoral campaign.”

April laughs. “In Bloomington?” She shakes her head slightly. “Is this another Bobby Newport situation?”

She smirks, ”Slightly smarter, deeper pockets.”

“And who are you to turn down money, right?”

“Unfortunately it makes the world go ‘round.” The bartender finally approaches and she plays it safe with a vodka soda (the highest shelf they have). “What are you doing in Bloomington?” she turns the question back on April.

“Vet school.”

Jen is genuinely surprised. April never seemed to have much ambition beyond being a chaos demon (she did work hard though, at least when Ben needed her to).

“You don’t have to look so shocked,” April deadpans.

“I’m - I’m - ” She decides not to lie (even though she is very good at it). “I wasn’t aware you were that into animals. Although that husband of yours - Alan, is it? - always did remind me of a golden retriever.”

“Ex.”

Jen thinks she said ex but it’s rather loud in here. “What’s that?”

“Ex. Andy and I are divorced.”

“Oh, I’m sorry to hear that.” Another genuine emotion. Alan - Andy, she mentally corrects herself - and April had seemed to really love each other. 

April has another sip of her drink and shrugs. “Turns out that moving several hours away and starting an intensive graduate program is not good for a marriage.”

The bartender sets down Jen’s drink. “He didn’t come with you?”

She shakes her head. “No. It would have interfered with his dream of becoming a Pawnee police officer.” She downs some more of her drink. “It’s whatever. We’re still friendly. He’s got a new girlfriend.”

“And you?” 

“Me what?”

“Do you have someone new?”

She snorts. “Sure. Didn’t have time for a husband but totally have time for a boyfriend.” She pauses and adds, “Or girlfriend.” Jen thinks there is more to that than April is letting on, but lets it go.

“Makes sense.” She lifts her drink. “To old friends in new places?” she offers as a toast.

April rolls her eyes, but lifts her own glass and clinks it. They both take a sip.

If Jen spends five out of the next 10 nights at that same bar, well, that’s nobody’s business but her own. However, she doesn’t see April again until three Thursdays later. In the Kroger. She’d stopped to replenish her bottled water supply (her time in Pawnee had made her wary of all Indiana water). 

As she hefts several large jugs (she’s even being considerate of the environment but not buying a case of plastic bottles), a voice says from down the aisle.

“I was going to ask if you needed help, but…”

She turns and manages (barely) to repress the smile that wants to burst from her. “But what?” she asks with a raised eyebrow and a straight face.

April pushes her cart closer. ”But you look as if you’ve got it under control.” 

“Hmm.” She tells herself that April is not giving her an appreciative once over. Because that would be ridiculous. (Right?)

April peers into Jen’s cart. The only contents are the aforementioned water, a few frozen meals, and some fruit. “Wow.”

“What?” Jen’s eyes narrow.

“It’s just…that…” April waves her hand. “It’s sad.” 

When she really looks, April’s cart is filled with actual adult food. “I eat a lot of takeout.”

“I’m sure you do.” April stares for a moment before sighing deeply. “Tomorrow night. My place. I’ll cook you dinner.”

“What?” (Wow, did she know her voice went that high? She’s not entirely sure.)

“Come over for dinner tomorrow night.”

Jen just blinks.

April reaches into her pocket and holds out her phone. And then smirks when Jen just stares dumbly. “Put your number in. I’ll text you my address.”

She reaches for it, still not entirely sure what’s going on, and enters her phone number. After handing it back, her own phone buzzes from somewhere in her bag. Glancing at her watch, she sees: It’s me. 7:30. Followed by an address.

“Great talk,” April deadpans and then disappears out of the aisle with her shopping cart.

And that’s how it goes for the next month. Dinners at April’s place (she was a surprisingly good cook considering the stories she’d heard about Ben’s time living with April and Andy), nights at the only decent bar in Bloomington, even a movie once. 

Slowly, Jen came to realize that she had a friend. Something she hadn’t had since middle school probably. The girls had turned cutthroat and she, with her nose and her wild hair and her braces, wasn’t cool enough. (She pretended she didn’t care, worked hard to get into the right college, get into the right internships, and well, look at her now.) But she and April are friends. 

She’s over at April’s place again. April’s nearing midterms so they’d ordered Chinese food (Jen had been skeptical at first, but it was surprisingly good. The owner had emigrated from Guangzhao and was delighted when she conversed a bit in his native language.) Now April is at one end of the couch, intently reading, a highlighter squeaking over the page every so often, and Jen is at the other, laptop balanced on her lap, typing furiously as she mutters about incompetence. Champion is curled up on the rug. Suddenly, April slams the book shut and lunges at Jen. Jen isn’t sure what is happening at first. Her readers make everything look huge and April’s eyes are wide. 

But then April is kissing her. She senses rather than sees her laptop tumble to the floor and Champion whine and hobble away. April’s hands grip her hips, pulling her closer, and her bottom lip slots perfectly between Jen’s. And…that’s her tongue tracing Jen’s lips. In her surprise, she opens her mouth and April explores. She’d be lying if she hadn’t thought about this. But April hasn’t given a single indication that she likes Jen. Not like that.

While her hands seemingly have a mind of their own, sliding up under the hem of April’s shirt, her brain screams out a warning. Friends. You are friends. Friends don’t do this. She wants to tell her stupid brain to shut up. Unfortunately, it’s right though. She and April are friends. So she pulls back even as April’s mouth chases after. Her hands slide out from where they’ve been exploring the smooth skin of her back and gently push her back slightly.

“Whoa, whoa, whoa. What’s going on here?”

April furrows her brow. And then frowns. And then flings herself back to the other end of the couch. “If you’re not interested…”

“April, I’d just like a little context.”

“What? You’re hot. I’m young. It’s got to be lonely in that big hotel bed of yours at night.”

It is, but April does not need to know that. She takes her readers off, folds the arms in, and leans forward to set them on the coffee table. Champion has come back over and she ruffles his fur for a moment before urging him toward April. He hops over and puts his head on April’s leg. “Wanna tell me what’s going on?”

April says nothing, just scratching Champion behind his ears. Finally, she stands up and makes her way over to the counter that separates her kitchen from the living room. She snatches something up and, on her way back, drops it in Jen’s lap. As April plops back down and resumes petting her dog, Jen picks up the envelope. It’s heavy, expensive. The writing across the front - April’s name and address - are calligraphied. She looks at April and then slides the card out from the envelope. It’s an invitation. When she takes in the names, she looks at April again. 

“He can do what he wants.” April tries to sound nonchalant, but Jen isn’t buying it.

“What the fuck kind of name is Mulwae-Tweep?”

“She’s a reporter for the Pawnee Journal.”

Jen thinks for a moment. “Oh, she’s an idiot.” She somehow refrains from adding Like your ex. (April would not appreciate that.)

April shrugs. “I already made a doll for her.” She murmurs something to Champion and he returns a moment later with something in his mouth. Jen realizes that this is meant to be a voodoo doll.

“Because you’re still in love with…Andy?” It’s the only thing she can think of.

“What? No!” She shakes her head. “I’m not still in love with Andy.”

“So you have a voodoo doll of his fiancee because?”

“Because I haven’t even made out with anyone since and - and he’s getting married!”

Jen doesn’t know what to say to this. It’s not a political problem for her to solve, it’s a personal one. And she actually cares about helping so no stupid throwaway line. She takes a deep breath and slides a bit closer. “What can I do?”

April’s eyes are huge and watery (and she doesn’t know what to do with that at all). Deciding that she is going to channel her inner Leslie Knope, she wraps her arms around April and hugs her. April fidgets for a moment, but before Jen pulls away, collapses into her. 

“It’s gonna be alright,” Jen murmurs as she rocks April slightly and rubs circles on her back. “We’re gonna find you a hot guy and you’ll take him to Andy’s wedding and rub him in everyone’s face.” It’s not her best idea, but it’s also not her worst. 

Finally, April pulls back and wipes at her eyes. “Will you go with me?”

“W-what?”

“You’re hot. And successful. And people in Pawnee know you. Come with me.” A pause. “Please.”

She’s helpless against the sight of April Ludgate saying please. So she nods. “Sure.”

Pawnee, unfortunately, is exactly how she remembers. It’s a few days after Christmas and the town looks like Santa threw up on it. While April is staying with her family, Jen is stuck at the hotel that is also hosting the nuptials. 

“Jen? Jen Barkley?”

Jen would recognize Leslie’s shriek anywhere (but especially here in Pawnee). “Hi, Knope.” She glances around. “Where’s your other half?”

“Ben is parking the car. He’ll be right here.” Leslie narrows her eyes. “Bobby’s not running again, is he?”

She cackles and then shakes her head. “No. I am not here on any official business.” 

Leslie’s brow furrows. “Then why are you in Pawnee?”

“She’s here with me,” April says from behind Jen. Once she’s next to her, she slides an arm around her waist. “We’re dating.” (This is news to Jen. But she is experienced in schooling her features and hiding her thoughts.)

“Who’s dating?” Ben asks from where he’s appeared beside Leslie.

“Um, April and Jen. Apparently.” Leslie looks very confused. 

“Since when?” Ben asks at the same time Leslie inquires, “How did you meet?”

Jen glances at April and gets a nod. “Well, I was in Bloomington working on the mayoral campaign and we ran into each other.”

“No more questions!” April barks. “We should take our seats.” She steers Jen toward the banquet room. 

”We‘ll catch up later!” Leslie calls after them.

“I was unaware that we’ve been dating,” Jen whispers as they take seats on the ‘groom’s side’ of the hall. The chairs are cheap and creak ominously when she moves. 

“It’s just easier to explain.”

Jen lifts an eyebrow. “Easier to say we’re dating than we’re friends? Leslie’s practically glued to the nurse’s hip. I think she’d understand.”

“I thought I was supposed to be bringing a hot date and rubbing it in everyone’s faces.”

“Well, yes, but - ”

“But nothing. You’re the hot date.” Then April shushes her as canned music plays from somewhere. Andy takes his place up front, hands tugging at the collar of his tux. He also fidgets with his jacket buttons. Jen does not recognize the man standing beside him. The music changes to something more traditional and everyone rises to face the back. Andy’s bride-to-be is swathed in acres of tuile and lace. Without looking over, Jen reaches for April’s hand and gives it a squeeze. Rather than squeezing back, April laces their fingers together. 

Jen pays not one iota of attention to the bride and groom. (It does register when the groom is handed a guitar, but she tunes out whatever he’s actually playing.) She is too focused on April. (Although she can’t outright just stare.) She watches her out of the corner of her eye. She seems dry eyed. When Jen takes a quick glance around, she notices that Leslie and Ben are sitting closely with Ben’s arm around Leslie. So are the nurse and Chris. As well as that older man (Barry? Larry? Garry?) and his smoking hot wife. So she lets go of April’s hand and stretches her arm across the back of April’s chair, letting her fingers graze the bare skin of April’s arm. 

“You may kiss the bride.”

Andy does a fist pump and then lays one on his bride. Complete with tongue. Jen is surprised they don’t fall to the floor together. Finally they break apart. 

“Mr and Mrs Mulwae-Tweep-Dwyer!” he shouts, raising their arms in the air.

Jen hadn’t meant to leave April alone for so long. But Ben had asked her opinions and she’d lost track of time. She’s half-way through an answer on why their current Congressman is an idiot when she spots it.

April. Near the corner of the room. Standing entirely too close to Andy. Actually, it’s Andy who seems to be standing too close to April. Jen wants to rush over and fling him away. He doesn’t deserve a moment of April’s time. (She’s secretly wondered all of this time what kind of idiot invites his ex-wife to his wedding.) She doesn’t even finish her thought and hurries to the far side of the room. 

“I just don’t get it,” she hears Andy say. “She’s so…mean. Like, she was so mean to Leslie.”

April shrugs. “She can be mean. But she’s usually right. And she’s funny. And so smart. And when she really laughs, she does this snort at the end. It’s so - ” Suddenly April breaks off. “Oh, hey,” she greets with a little chuckle. “I thought you were talking to Ben.”

“I was. But we finished.”

April nods. “I was just telling Andy that I’m happy for him. And congratulating him. He and…” She pauses for a moment before coming up with, “Shauna are moving to Indianapolis.”

It takes every ounce of self-control not to show her surprise. “Oh, that’s interesting.” (She really wants to beat the shit out of him. He wouldn’t move for April, but will for Tweep?)

“Yeah, Shauna’s got a job at the Indianapolis Star.”

Jen opens her mouth, but April interjects. “That’s great. I wish you all the best.”

Andy glances from Jen to April. “Yeah, you, too. I’m glad you’re happy.” His voice goes up at the end like it’s a question.

April smiles. A genuine smile. “I am.”

This seems to satisfy Andy because he grins and then pulls her into a hug. “You’re the best. Thanks for coming.” Jen isn’t sure if the growl she makes is audible. (She’s going to hope not.) April pats him on the back. He lets go. “Thanks for coming, too,” he says to Jen. “And for making April happy.”

“My pleasure.”

Then he’s gone.

“What do you need?” Jen asks. April says nothing for a moment so she continues, “A drink? An exit strategy? Some waffles? Your voodoo doll? A hit put out on him?”

April guffaws. “No.” She shakes her head. “I don’t need any of that. I’m fine.” Jen is skeptical and it must show. “Really, Jen. I’m fine.”

She side-eyes April. “Well, if you change your mind…”

“I’ll keep that in mind.” Then her brow furrows. “Did you pack the voodoo doll?”

Jen shrugs. “Thought you might need it.”

Another laugh. “One of the many things I love about you.”

(What? April did not just say that, did she? No, she didn’t. Jen is having auditory hallucinations. Isn’t that a sign of something? A stroke maybe?)

“Hey, I didn’t mean it like that. I meant…” April inhales and then exhales. “I’m glad you’re here. That’s all.”

“Oh. OK. Right.” Jen waves her glass. “I, uh, I need a refill. Be right back.”

So what if the trip to the bar includes a detour to the ladies’ room? And if the detour encompasses the last thirty minutes? Jen is standing at the row of sinks, hands braced at the counter, staring at herself when the door flings open. 

It’s Leslie Knope. 

Who immediately shoves into a stall and, from the sounds, starts vomiting spectacularly. If it wasn’t so gross, Jen might be a little impressed. The door opens and Leslie emerges, patting at her face with a square of toilet paper.

“Alright there, Knope?”

“Yeah, I - uh, some bad - ” Jen wants to know what bad thing she ate so she can avoid it. (Although it’s Pawnee, it’s a hotel, and April’s ex and his new wife are idiots. Jen isn’t certain she should eat anything here.) “I’m pregnant.”

Leslie turns on the water, cupping her hand beneath it, and rinses her mouth. She leans in and examines her face in the mirror. Satisfied with what she sees, she turns back to Jen. “So you and April?”

“It’s…new.”

“Well, she seems happier than I’ve seen her in a long time. So thank you for that.”

Jen blinks. “She - she does?”

“Yes. I know she tells me she’s fine when we talk on the phone, but she’s not the same. But so far today, I’ve seen her smile a lot. Mostly in your direction. Real smiles. Not just the ones where she pretends to smile and just bares her teeth.” (Jen is familiar with those smiles. April has deployed them in Bloomington. But never on Jen.)

Leslie stares for a long moment. So long that Jen is overcome with the urge to look at herself in the mirror to make sure there’s nothing on it. Or in her teeth. “If you hurt her - ”

As much as she’s curious to know exactly what Leslie Knope will threaten her with, she interrupts, “I’m not going to hurt her. She’s had enough of that.”

Leslie squints at her. But before she can say whatever is on her mind, her face drains of blood and she bolts into the stall. This time, Jen doesn’t stay to listen to her.

“Where’s your drink?” April snarks once she returns to their table.

“Look, I know I freaked you out before. And I’m sorry. I didn’t mean -”

“It’s 8 degrees outside.”

April’s right. It’s freezing out. And her puffy coat (back in her room of course) does not go with this dress and heels. “I don’t know. Can we dance then?” She stands up and holds out her hand. It takes a long moment before April, ignoring the hand, stands up, too. She heads toward the dancefloor and Jen is left to follow. (Everyone’s eyes are following them. She can feel it.)

The song, luckily, is slow. Jen holds out her arms and April steps into them. Between the shoes they are both wearing, they are close to the same height. “So,” April begins, “what did Leslie say?”

“That - that you seemed happy.”

There is a derisive little snort that she’s almost 90% sure comes from April. 

“Are you?” Jen asks.

“I mean,” April shrugs under Jen’s arms. “I’m not totally miserable.”

“I am.” April’s face falls and, quickly, Jen shakes her head. “No, I’m not - not miserable. I mean, I’m happy.”

And that’s it. 

Just blinking.

No words.

Look, there’s more!

April hates parties. She always has ever since elementary school when the only reason she was invited was because she had to be. (And her mother made her go. At least until she hit middle school and outright refused.) The only time parties had ever been tolerable was when she was with Andy. They would pretend to be other people and not care what anyone else thought. Tonight, though, she’s going to this party. Because she is not going to be the pathetic loser pining away at home for her…someone. (They haven’t defined what they are aside from making time to spend at least one weekend each month together. April’s definitely not going to do that on her own. She won’t be able to take another ‘awesome sauce’ incident.)

She slides on her blazer (black obviously) just as someone knocks on the door. She’s not expecting anyone so it takes a moment to react. There, through the peephole, is her…someone. She furrows her brow and unlocks the door.

“Jen? What are you doing here?” She’d been pretty clear two days ago when she abruptly cancelled their weekend plans for a ‘work emergency.’

“Hi,” Jen says, leaning in for a kiss.

April turns her head at the last minute and the kiss lands on her cheek. She doesn’t miss Jen’s grimace. Which gets even more pronounced when she looks April up and down.

“Are you going out?”

She nods. “Yes.” That’s all she’s getting.

“Where?”

It takes a moment but she relents, “A party.” And immediately regrets it when Jen laughs. Right out loud.

“Oh, oh! You’re serious?”

Another nod. “Yes.” There is a brief staring match until Jen blinks first (April had long ago trained herself not to blink for an inhumanly long time). She considers, discards, and then reconsiders before asking, “Do you want to come?”

“To a vet party?” There’s that laugh again. (It’s the fake one that Jen uses professionally. With April, she usually makes her real laugh, the one with the little snort at the end.)

“Yes.” She gnaws on the inside of her cheek for a moment. “Does this mean you’re not coming?”

“April,” she kicks off her heels, putting them and her wheel-y bag near the door. “I came here to see you.”

“Well, I’m going. So if you really want to see me, you can come.”

Jen shakes her head. “I just got off a plane. I don’t really want to go to some lame party.”

April shrugs. “See you when I get back then.” She grabs up her keys and phone, checking that she has some cash and a credit card in the pouch on the back of her phone and stowing them in various pockets. Hand on the knob, she turns back, “Don’t wait up.” It’s mean, but then again Jen can be mean sometimes; April thinks she will appreciate it.

It only takes ten minutes before she remembers that she hates parties. This is filled with veterinary students (mostly younger than her) drinking some concoction out of a trash can. It’s electric green and one sip has her gagging (she feels slightly nostalgic for Snake Juice, at least with that, you didn’t notice how potent it was as you drank it).

So she clutches her red cup, keeping it half-way filled and pretending to take a sip every now and then. She moves from group to group, listening to conversations, saying something completely out of pocket and moving along. It’s not quite the same as pretending to be someone else, but it entertains her. 

“Hey.” A bear of a man leans down. “You’re in my dentistry class, right? Aggie?”

Bill takes it. “I’m Bill. I have dentistry with April.” While he also swallows up Jen’s hand, she can see that Jen is squeezing as hard as she can. Bill snatches his hand back, shaking it slightly. 

“Learning how to clean cat’s teeth and extract dog’s, that kind of thing?”

Bill nods. “Yeah.” He squints. “Are you a student?”

Jen laughs (the fake one). “No, no. Too squeamish for medicine. I’m a consultant.”

The squint becomes more pronounced. “Consultant for what?”

April finally intercedes. “It was nice to meet you. If you’ll excuse us.”

“See you in class!” Bill calls after them. April just waves over her shoulder.

She leads Jen out of the house onto the back deck. Jen plucks her red cup from her hand, inspects the contents, shrugs, and takes a long pull. “Shit,” she makes a face, “that’s terrible.” Clearing her throat, she asks, “Is this what I missed in college?”

April shrugs. “Probably. I don’t know.” (Both she and Jen had unusual college experiences. April lived at home and had 2 boyfriends for much of it. Ben and Derek weren’t into partying. Jen focused exclusively on her classes and internships, determined to make the most of herself.)

Jen hands the cup back and her face softens, “Look, I’m…”

April waits much longer than she thinks she’d be able to, but nothing else is forthcoming. “Why are you here?”

“You said that if I wanted to spend time with you, I had to come to this party.”

She shakes her head. “No, why are you here? In Bloomington? You said you were busy.”

Jen looks down at her feet (which are in flats, another first). “I was. But I fixed it earlier. So I could come here,” she finishes, looking up hopefully.

If Jen is expecting her to be charmed or to swoon, she is sadly mistaken. “When you went back to DC three months ago, we promised we’d make time for each other.”

“I have!”

April glances around, but no one is paying attention to them. She sighs, “I think that maybe this is too hard. I tried this once already.” (She’s not trying to rub Andy in her face, but the truth is the truth.)

“I’m not your stupid ex-husband who wouldn’t uproot his life for you, but did for that idiot reporter!”

April just lifts an eyebrow. “Well, you’re sure as hell not here.”

“Of course I’m not!” Jen swallows hard. “Because you’re not going to be here after next year.”

“What?”

“April, we both know that you are not going to stay in Bloomington after you graduate.”

This is true. She’s not planning on practicing in Bloomington (there are already too many vets in town). But that’s not what’s weighing on her mind. “What are you saying?”

Slowly, Jen reaches out and takes her hand. “I’m saying that once you figure out where you’re going, then I can - ” She purses her lips. “I can figure things out too.”

“Like move somewhere with me?” April blurts out.

Jen shrugs. “Maybe. Or at least move closer. I don’t know.” She laughs (it’s real, but wet sounding and a little sad). “I’ve never done this before, you know.”

Inwardly, she curses herself. Because sometimes she forgets that Jen is a lot less experienced in this type of thing than she is. April’s been married and divorced; Jen has rarely had a long term relationship. So she takes a deep breath, exhales, and nods, “OK.”

“OK?”

April intertwines their fingers. “I’m OK with long-distance. At least until I graduate and figure out what’s next.”

Jen’s eyes widen. “Really?”

She nods. “Really.” A gentle squeeze. “C’mon, let’s go home. I want to spend time with my girlfriend.” (She can’t wait for Jen to say it.)

A slow smile spreads across Jen’s face. “Your g-girlfriend would like that.” 

Chapter 3

“I don’t suppose April has mentioned the awesomesauce incident, Jennifer.”

Jen, from her spot in the corner, near to but definitely not leaning against the wall, glances over. “Pardon?” she says to Ron. (To be honest, he scares her. He says exactly what he means and appears to play no games. While she knows that April cares about him deeply, she usually tries to avoid him.)

“Awesomesauce?” He raises an eyebrow, but no other part of his face moves.

“What is that? Some kind of Pawnee thing? Do you put it on waffles? Or steak?” (Ron doesn’t seem like the waffle type.)

“No, it’s not an actual sauce.” He sips at the amber liquid in his tumbler. “When April first told Andrew - ” She really doesn’t want to hear this and, judging from the look on Ron’s face, he knows it. Despite this, he continues, “When April first told Andrew that she loved him, he responded with - ”

“Awesomesauce?” She snorts. “Of course that moron did.”

“The point I am trying to make is that April is unlikely to make the first such move again, Jennifer.” Another sip. And then she wonders if she can go. (She’s not that lucky.) “So if you have similar feelings for April, you will need to tell her first.” One last sip. “That is all.” She’s finally free as he ambles away.

The victory is short lived as Ben comes over holding one of the babies. “Would you like to hold her?”

“I’m good.” As an afterthought, she adds, “Thanks.”

“I have no idea how they are going to take care of three babies,” April says, slipping off her shoes and leaving them in the middle of their hotel room floor.

Jen picks them up and stows them in the closet along with her own heels. “They’re outnumbered. And I don’t know how anyone takes care of even one baby. All of that ” - her nose wrinkles - “mess.”

“So you’re saying that you don’t want me to bear your spawn someday?” April flops on the bed, starfishing.

Jen thinks she’s joking (but she’s not certain, not 100%). “Is - is that something you want someday?”

She laughs. “God, no. I do not want to be anyone’s mother.” She pops her head up off the mattress just enough to catch Jen’s eye. “You?”

“It’s not something I ever envisioned as part of my life.”

“Good.” She lets her head drop back on the mattress. More quietly, she says, “Andy really wanted kids.”

If it were anyone else, she would have gladly called April’s ex an idiot. Now, however, she just perches on the edge of the bed, one leg dangling while the other is bent so her foot is tucked beneath her. 

“Thank you for coming with me,” April says. (Sincerely. That doesn’t happen often.)

“Like I wouldn’t come,” she rolls her eyes.

April shrugs as best she can while lying flat on her back. Without looking, she reaches for Jen’s hand and twines their fingers together. 

Awesomesauce, Ron’s voice whispers in her head. 

Shit.

“Um, April, you - you know that - ” OK, she is beginning to maybe see the merits of awesomesauce. Because this? Putting herself on the line? It’s hard. “You know how much you mean to me, right?”

April sits up and stares. “Are you dying?”

“What?!” She shakes her head. “No, I’m not dying. What - what would make you say that?”

“You’re being weird.”

“I’m not - I’m just - I’m trying to tell you that - that you’re important to me.”

“Why?”

“Why you’re important to me?” She shrugs. “Well, you - you see a side of me that no one else does and you haven’t run away.” She adds with a laugh that she doesn’t mean. “Yet.”

April scooches closer. “Are you sure you’re not dying? Or - oh my god, do you actually want kids?”

“Ew. No.”

“Then I don’t get why - ”

“I’m trying to tell you that I love you!” 

April’s mouth drops open and her eyes go wide.

Awesomesauce.

Jen shakes her head. “You know what? Just forget - ” The rest of her sentence is cut off by April’s mouth. It’s not much of a kiss though because April is grinning. 

Finally she pulls back just enough to look at Jen. “I love you, too.”

“You - you do?”

She rolls her eyes. “Of course I do. You see a side of me that no one else does and you haven’t run away either.”

“I - I’m not planning too.”

“Good. Neither am I.”
