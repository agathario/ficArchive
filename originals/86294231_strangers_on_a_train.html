Getting onto the train was, all things considered, pretty easy. The poor, now probably jobless, clerk had even written her name on the ticket.

Well, a made-up name, but it was hers for the time being. All that had been needed was a kind smile, a convoluted sob story and a well-placed electric pulse to his prefrontal cortex, and voilà! Agatha was climbing the last few steps into America’s Pure-Sang, without a dollar spent. 

A luxury train with the most technologically advanced engine the free world could engineer. It was set to zoom across the country in just under five days. A sight to behold truly, this brand-new train, with its shining chrome locomotive patiently waiting to unleash its full power.

Agatha didn’t care about any of this. Sure, the amenities would be nice, but what mattered to her the most was its speed. Or potential speed. Speed is energy, energy is fuel. Fuel for power.

That same unfortunate ticket clerk had said the train would average around 90mph.

“Can it go faster?” She had whispered, conspiratorially, through the desk’s window.

He eagerly nodded, “150 miles per hour, but I heard it can go even faster than that.”

“You don’t say!” She had sealed the deal with a wink and the clerk had attached a voucher for a complementary drink to her ticket. 

That drink is now sitting just before her, on a delicate paper napkin, the olive swaying gently in the clear liquid to the train’s vibrations. They’re still pushing past the urban projects of the outer boroughs. All of New York’s most wretched corners are suddenly put on display, rolling by her window. All the damn alleys and condemned buildings rot away before her eyes. 

It’s a sight the company hasn’t yet managed to sanitise, and Agatha can’t help but find some wicked joy at all those refined passengers having to witness this ugliness. 

Agatha had been busy in New York. The end of the war had brought all kinds of people to the city. Traumatised young men who had been the perfect subject for her experimentations, thousands of immigrants with their suitcases full of ancient European artifacts and head full of stories. And if Agatha loves one thing, it's stories. 

She’d listen to countless myths, legends, old family tales and more rarely, odd personal accounts. Most of them were useless, strings of words meant to scare children, or already well known. But sometimes someone would unknowingly share something old and true. 

Something that could be used.

“I simply cannot wait for us to reach the lakes!” A shrill voice tears her away from her contemplation of the grey landscapes.

She has the audacity to sit just opposite Agatha with a blinding smile. She extends her hand as if her presence could be considered a blessing. “Mrs. Richardson” She offers, “But please, call me Susan .” 

She won’t, Agatha immediately decides. 

She is middle aged, if she had to guess. Because Agatha decided, centuries ago, to let the roundness of her cheek melt away and allow fine wrinkles in her skin to settle, she is often associated with being middle aged. If that decision allows her maximum freedom to do as she pleases – hard to argue with a grieving widow, she often forgets that it has the unfortunate side effect of attracting the sympathies of other grieving widows. Evidently, this pastel wearing, Chanel smelling broad assumed that her and Agatha would make fast friends. 

“We’re sharing a cabin,” she so cheerfully announces and Agatha feels like she just dropped from a five-story building.

“No.”

“You’re staying–” why does she say "staying”; this isn’t a hotel, “in 3901, are you not?”

Agatha blinks at her a couple of times before summoning her most syrupy smile. “There’s obviously been some sort of mistake–”

There isn’t. 

After checking in with a steward at first and then the train conductor, Agatha has to admit that that dim witted ticket clerk at the station played her. That, or she wasn’t paying attention to whatever he was saying, which is beside the point.

Now sitting opposite each other in the plush armchairs of her their compartment, Agatha is contemplating murder. Unfortunately, if reading those dreadful crime novels that the world has now been craving since the late 19th century have taught her anything, it’s that it is unreasonably difficult to get away with murder in a closed space. It’s finally quiet in there, as Agatha sets up her bed. There’s a tiny speck of blood from where Mrs. Richardson died just a couple of hours ago. Agatha adjusts her fitted sheet and fluffs her pillows.

Her bunk’s mattress is more comfortable than the last few beds she’s been in and with the rhythmic roll of the train, Agatha is soon lulled to sleep.

The morning is aggressively sunny, in the way fall mornings can be, when summer still tries to cling on. The sun is blinding but its heat is quickly dispersed by a crisp and sharp wind, cutting away the warmth from Agatha’s body.

She’s standing on the platform, a too thin wool coat wrapped around her, an unlit cigarette pinched between her fingers. A couple of carriages away, a group of train employees are huddled around a gurney. The police are there too. 

They’re unloading Mrs. Richardson’s body.

It’s not guilt that made her step out of the train, more the need for fresh air. Even if the train was engineered to always offer clean air, Agatha never did great when cooped up for too long in confined spaces. 

She feels that goddamned familiar buzz brushing against her magic before a presence steps in beside her.

“I knew you wouldn’t just leave me alone.”

“As you said, I’m persistent.”

She glances at Death and is surprised to find them without their usual cloak. The being is dressed in a men’s dark wool coat and, she guesses underneath, a gray three piece suit with a silk tie. It very annoyingly suits them ridiculously well.

“That’s different.” She raises an eyebrow. The crown is also gone, leaving their head bare and their dark hair slicked back. She last came across Rio in the mid 30s, the being had then adorned a more feminine style, complete with long slender dresses and high pointed heels. 

From their coat pocket, Death fishes out a silver lighter and flicks it open, the flame dancing dangerously in the morning wind.

“One has to keep up with the times.”

“Right.” Agatha considers the light but keeps her distance, she resists telling them how good the suit fits them. “No one here died today.”

“No.”

“Well, aren’t you busy?” Agatha asks, sharp.

Rio stays ramrod straight, flame still flickering, “Sure, I’m always busy.”

Agatha knows that Death doesn’t understand time like mortals do, but she still grits her teeth at the comment. “Your business is done here.”

“Is it?” They raise an inquisitive eyebrow. 

Agatha is torn between slapping them across the jaw or tearing their obscenely nice clothes off. “I don’t understand why you’re here in the first place, she wasn’t anything special.”

“Contrary to your beliefs, I don’t only show up for your gifts, dear. I come for everyone.”

“Maybe, but you only stay for me.”

The entity tilts their head and Agatha sweetly smiles, she guessed correctly.

“You seem…” Rio starts.

“Happy? Glowing? Glorious?”

“... Oddly civil.”

“Ah– you’re wondering why I haven’t jumped at your throat yet.” She certainly wants to.

“Our last encounters were more combative.”

Agatha scoffs, “I was in a dark place then.” She considers the lighter and its flickering flame. Cathartic as may have been to throw each other around in the forest, hoping to draw blood, she can’t have that now. “Your presence here is unwarranted, go do your job you so dearly cling to.” She spits.

Rio shifts, minutely, almost invisible to the untrained eye. But Agatha is well accustomed to their movements. She knows those designed to seem human, those based on her own and the rarer, more impressive ones of a cosmic entity. There’s darkness to those, it seeps through the cracks, and even if flesh remains on their beautiful face, there is no doubt that Death is speaking when Rio next opens their mouth.

“I’ll be here, for the time being, until I understand what is happening.” And Agatha shivers at the sheer power lingering behind Death's cold and flat tone. “Tell me now and I’ll leave you alone.”

If Agatha’s eyes flick down to Death’s lips, it’s no one's business but her own.To regain some of her composure, she finds a scrawny set of matches in her pocket and lights her cigarette, ignoring Rio’s flame. Death nods and flicks their lighter shut.

The mortuary gathering on the other end of the platform seems to move and pull Mrs. Richardson’s gurney away. 

Agatha angles her face towards the sun, feeling its timid heat on her skin. She hates to admit it but Death’s presence at her side is oddly calming. Like that of a particularly large hound, one that could easily bite your face off but gently lies its head on your knee instead. 

She hasn’t been in Rio’s company in more than a decade. And if time, for Rio, is convoluted, Agatha did kind of miss their back and forth. 

“Did you find that tie in a mobster’s closet? It’s offensively tacky.”

But when only the station’s cold wind answers her, Agatha understands that she is alone, once again. She tells herself that she has her peace again, tries to sound convincing about it too as she climbs back into the train.

 

The police, it turns out, are more easily convinced of her innocence than that conductor. They’re quick to judge her harmless. She plays, afterall, the perfect respectable wife, a bit fragile, a bit clingy. In the end they’re more than happy to let her go if it means she’ll finally stop talking.

Sometimes the manner of speech is as much a weapon as the words themselves, Agatha learned.

With her interrogation done and their clumsy investigation wrapped up, the train can finally depart once again, and Agatha is free to go back to her solitary cabin.

 

 

The cabin is pretty luxurious for one person. Her bed has been converted back to a couch for the day. There are two of them flushed with the walls on either side of a large window and separated by a small table. Opposite the window, left to the sliding door, there is a tiny bathroom, equipped with a shower, a toilet, a small sink and a mirror. Agatha is above all relieved to not have to share her shower with anyone else. 

The walls are decorated with some intricate marquetry, a testament to some incredible craftsmanship. It’s all very tastefull, very clean. 

Agatha takes out her knife and starts carving runes into them.

One for discretion, she wouldn’t want any of the staff to suspect anything amiss. One for protection, she might not need it but better be prepared than sorry. One for silence and the cabin is immediately much quieter. 

And a last one for focus, her buzzing mind finally calms down as she feels her intention sharpen. 

She should get it tattooed really, it makes such a difference.

The floor gets a special treatment. It’s covered in an elegant carpet. Armed with her knife, Agatha cuts along the sides of it to peel it back, revealing cold bare metal underneath.

Pricking the tip of her finger with the blade, she draws a simple summoning circle. It’s a blank canvas for anything she’ll likely need in the near future. Her magic hums happily at that, seeping through her blood and infusing the circle with power. It’s a beautiful thing to watch, the red liquid is almost instantly absorbed by the metal as the elements bond together. 

Happy with her progress, Agatha rolls the carpet back over the floor, it’s crudely torn in some places but the discretion rune should suffice to hide most of the damage.

She sighs, this feels more like it. The cabin finally resembles something worthy, something her magic has begun to smoothly take over. 

Her books remain tucked away in her suitcase under her bed. She finds her notebook and settles at her table. It’s an unassuming journal. Something no one would look twice at. Inside, though, it holds all her research of the past few years. Hours and hours of restless nights poured over ancient grimoires have been summarized in this purple-covered book. There are a couple of pages at the end she’s particularly proud of. 

A spell she theorised months ago and is– evidently, still perfecting. It’s a way of bypassing the traditional mind-controlling spells that are very draining even when working on feeble minds. 

It worked, in theory. Well, it had worked on the ticket clerk in New York. He had become more receptive to her demands once she jammed the part of his brain in charge of making decisions. 

It hadn’t worked as well with Susan, perhaps her frustration had bested her and she’d nudged too strongly. Or perhaps she had aimed too clumsily and overwhelmed the poor woman’s brain into seizing.. Either way, she can’t afford to make the same mistake again. Not with Death already snooping around.

Sometimes, not often, Agatha will stand in front of a mirror and look at her face. 

No, that isn’t right. Agatha frequently looks at her face. She likes her face. Likes what it can do, likes how others see it. What Agatha doesn’t often do is look at her face. 

The first time she had done so, she couldn’t have been older than five. She had dragged a heavy stool to her mother’s room where a small silver mirror hung. There, balancing on her uneven perch, she had tried to recognize the woman who had birthed her in her features. 

She hadn’t seen the similarities then, sure the clear eyes were similar, but Agatha’s were blue when her mother’s were green. She also had darker hair, but Evanora had said hers were lighter before they turned grey. Her rounded cheeks, pink lips and long nose were the opposite of her mother’s angular face. 

Maybe it was one of the reasons her mother often scolded her. Agatha had wished for a different face then, one that was leaner, more serious with a smaller nose. One where Evanora could see herself when she looked at her daughter.

It’s only a few years later that Agatha truly looked again. In the room of an inn, above a noisy tavern. The dirty mirror had shown her the face of a young lady, sharper that the one of a child but with softness still clinging to it. Skin flushed by the warm meal she’d just eaten, hair damp from her bath, she had leaned closer. 

Evanora was gone and Agatha had taken comfort, over the years, at how little she looked like her mother. But now, alone in a world filled with strangers, she looked at all the features that set her apart from Evanora. Her eyes, her hair, her mouth, her nose. She had imagined the face of a third person, then. Someone unknown, unnamed. Another parent. 

Agatha never searched for them outside of her own face. Sometimes she imagined a benevolent presence, one with magic close to hers that could teach her its intricacies. More often, though, the thought of someone Evanora would be shameful about plagued her mind. Someone cruel or wicked, someone that was casted away from their coven. Someone so bad that no one ever talked about them. But worst of all was the thought of them being magicless; a man from town, forgettable and insignificant. Someone whose life wasn’t even worth mentioning. 

Now, when Agatha looks at her face reflected to her in the large window of her cabin, she doesn’t look for Evanora or her other unknown parent. When her eyes brush across her dimpled chin, her thick eyebrows and the slight bump in her nose, she looks for her son. 

And when she leans forward and looks, really looks at the smooth surface and forgets the dark landscapes beyond, she begs the reflection to change, to move. To show her something else than her glassy blue eyes, her dark hair, her pale skin. 

Behind closed lids, she can almost feel his straight hair under her lips, see his brown eyes, shining like melted honey in the sun. And everywhere that she doesn’t see herself in Nicky’s face, she finds Rio’s. 

For all the grief and rage that ensnared her for so many years, there is no denying that Nicholas was fully theirs. 

Their boy.

She doesn’t understand what he is saying but he keeps talking, brows furrowed and hanging skin under his jaw flapping about. She doesn’t care to listen to what he has to say but he is expecting an answer, one she doesn’t have for him.

What’s to be had for dinner? Who will take his daughter to prom? Will his son get into college?

He longs for an evening with the wife, and when they’ll sit together on the couch in a few days to watch Lucille Ball scoff at her husband’s antics, he’ll laugh and forget all about this train.

Something stopped for Agatha in 1756.

Pain didn’t make her more human.

Pain turned her into something else.

When Agatha looks at this man, his sweaty moustache, his serious frown, she can’t help but feel oh so far away from him.

Agatha may be mortal but she stepped out of humanity when her boy died.

He mentions the police, waiting for her at the next stop. Tomorrow she’ll be escorted out, a brigade will be glad to process her. 

Agatha smirks. Sure.

“What-”

Death is gone. 

Death is gone and Agatha is alone kneeling next to the broken body of the poor man whose only crime was to do his job.

She has no choice now.

Tomorrow at noon, Death will end.

Stubbornly, Agatha tries again and again until her muscles burn with exhaustion and her magic angrily pulses. 

She falls to her knees, “I can’t.”

“Why?” Rio looks genuinely confused, her brows furrowed and that damned gap in her teeth showing. 

Our griefs are easier to bear together, she doesn’t say.

I don’t want to be the only one remembering him, she doesn’t say.

I still love you, she doesn’t say.

“I don't want to hurt you,” she sobs.

“Oh,” is all Rio answers. Stupid fucking Rio with her stupid fucking face and her stupid fucking smile. Pulsing through the bond radiates a warmth that melts all of Agatha’s muscles. 

She falls forward, confident that she will be caught. Rio doesn’t disappoint, her arms wrap around Agatha’s shoulders and hold her. She feels so solid against Agatha’s trembling form. So real against her aching body. 

The witch buries her nose in that familiar neck, breathing in Death’s unique scent that she so dearly missed. She lets Rio whisper soothing words in her ear.

The pain is still there, of course it is. It bites at her raw nerves and makes her want to douse the world in gasoline and flick a match. But it’s more manageable. Its weight is alleviated, and its edge is duller. 

Agatha decided, months ago, to set her plan in motion because Rio’s pain bled through their bond. She realises now that if she allows her own pain to be shared, the whole burden is easier to carry. 

Rio must sense her musings because she tightens her grip on Agatha. 

“My love, let me be here.”

Agatha curls into her arms, and she forgot how easy it felt to be held. She forgot how simple it could be. 

“I’m still angry,” she says between muffled sobs. 

“I know.” Rio keeps stroking her hair, “let your rage burn, but allow me to be by your side, please.”

For the first time in decades, Agatha doesn’t turn to the past to look for answers or bitter memories. For the first time she considers a future, like a hidden door that just made itself be known, Agatha didn’t know this was a path that could be followed. 

Suddenly there is a perspective, one outside of this train, it won’t be painless or peaceful but at least it’s there.

She doesn’t know how Rio would fit into it. But sharing their pain is a solid first step.

“Okay,” she sighs, melting into the arms of her love.

The train violently rattles, and her magic, as if moved by a sentient force funnels back towards her. Its enormous amount of energy blisters the walls of the cabin and flames spur from the synthetic cushions. Rio grips her tighter as purple light surges exponentially, blinding. 

Agatha screams, the spell can’t be shut down. She’s amassed too much energy to take it all in. And even as she tries to control it, her magic buckles and bends like a wild horse. 

It happens all at once, the windows shatter outwards and the walls cave out under her power. 

let it out, let it all out

Rio pushes in her mind, a sense of calm takes over her nerves. As Agatha raises her eyes to Rio, she’s met with the stoic face of Death, flesh stripped away and eyes hollow. A face she’s certain to only see right at the end. Agatha smiles, she finds comfort in that face. 

“I’m sorry,” she just manages before her magic explodes fully.

It’s incredible, infinitely strong, and unstoppable. It pulverises the train in a deafening screeching of metal and fire. Agatha is sent back, body arching under the force. She lets the magic run its course, unobstructed. It flows freely, beautiful and terrible. 

She watches in awe as time stretches and contracts around them. Her magic laps at the edges of reality, trying and failing to find a grip. 

Before she loses consciousness, Agatha feels solid hands cradle her head and her back. And the witch turns towards this strong form, happy to be held even as the world is exploding into a million pieces.

 

 

Cold touches land on her cheeks and on her eyelids. Agatha pictures soft kisses laid on her face, gentle and patient. There is nothing but the sound of the wind, distant but present. And the delicate landing of snowflakes against her skin. 

She opens an eye. 

Absolute white greets her and Agatha sighs, peace and truth, Rio said. She feels  nothing now, not even the familiar pull of her pain. A bird crosses her field of view, it’s small and clumsy in the cold air.

But as it flaps its wings, Agatha follows it with her eyes. Mountains. Grand, sharp. Snow falls delicately, flakes dancing together in the wind. Peace, she feels it now. Simple, warm. A snow flake lands in her palm, it melts quickly and leaves a pleasant tingle in her hand. 

The pressure that lives with her in the last few days is gone. Her magic is back to its usual weight. Comfortable, manageable. She feels purr in her veins, content to have been let out with so much strength.

Not too far below her lies the wretched carcass of the train.

It stands out against the light rocks, a tangled mess of steel bars, their jagged edges pointing menacingly to the sky. Some places in the rubbles are smoking where fire is already eating at the remains of this metal beast. 

There are no survivors. Agatha knows it like she knows how to breathe. 

Agatha also knows that she should have been with them, twisted and crushed beneath tons of steel. 

The witch takes a deep breath, tries not to focus too hard on the kind arms that pulled her out of her own mess. She doesn’t linger on the impossibility to have been saved by Death.

The aches of her body begin to wake. Her muscles scream in protest as she shakily walks down the plateau towards the train. Agatha must have twisted her ankle somehow because her foot badly smarts. For some reason her magic doesn’t alleviate the ache like she’s used to, but maybe it’s for the best. She hops to the crash site and with a pointed stick, looks through the mess. Maybe something of value did make it.

She rummages for a while until exhaustion makes her pause. Ash stings in her eyes and sweat pearls on her forehead. Her back is a mess, her ankle hurts like a bitch. She’s hungry, she’s cold and she is, above all, drained. But, Agatha is alive. It bewilders her maybe the most. She failed but she is alive. 

Out of the corner of her eyes she catches it. Her bag, sitting atop the remnants of the bar in the former lounge area. Purple and notably, undamaged. 

Agatha doesn’t resist hopping to it and opening its brass zipper. Her coat she immediately pulls out and wraps around herself. It’s still too thin for the season but its fur collar brings her some sort of comfort. Her notebook falls from the folds of her coat, undamaged even though Agatha knew full well that it was lying on the table during the explosion. She tucks it in her pocket with a relieved sigh. At least all her research survived.

But the bag is still heavy.

She knows of it before she finds it. Beneath a few stray articles of clothing, Death’s rib is waiting for her.

Agatha doesn’t dare to touch it at first. It sits between her silk scarfs like it was always expecting her. She brushes an almost shy hand over the bone and it pulses faintly in response. Warmth radiates through her, from her arms to her toes, it’s like taking a long hot shower after a tiring day. 

Most importantly, the distinct feeling that she isn’t alone washes over her. Hers and Rio’s bond hums.

She sighs, considers leaving the rib here. She could still walk away from all that. It would hurt but it would be familiar at least. 

Now, she doesn’t have to focus on it to feel Rio’s heartbeat mirroring her own, even if she is universes away. Agatha pushes a magical pulse through the bond, a reflex more than anything else. She doesn’t expect anything else in return but when nothing comes, Agatha fights the tears stinging her eyes.

She tilts her head back with a sigh. It’s fully snowing now. Heavy snow flakes are catching on her lashes, in her hair. She tucks the rib between the lapels of her coat.

As fresh white snow settles on the torn rubbles of the train, a playful pulse radiates once, then twice in her chest.
