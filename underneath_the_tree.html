Summary

Rio is back in her hometown after years of disappearance. Having always been seen as the black sheep for going three decades without meeting her soulmate, she finds herself awkwardly going through the motions of a rather disastrous family Christmas. But when a rather hasty brunette runs into her in town, Rio finds herself happy to spend as much time getting to know her before returning to the cruel reality of the holiday season.

Chapter 1

A world where soulmates exist seems ideal. It removes the need to seek another out, to put oneself in the midst of a heartbreak for someone who may not be ‘the one’. Oftentimes those whose souls are bound meet within the first decade or two of their lives. There are rules the world follows where one solely communicates with a soulmate through the usage of a journal prior to meeting. No names or locations are able to be written.

Fate is what drives two souls together, not free will.

In rare cases, soulmates never have the pleasure of meeting. And when they do, often living in close proximity with one another, it is only the matching journals, filled with countless pages of conversations, that show one another they belong together. A difficult task it is to successfully find another’s half, yet truly fulfilling.

Sitting at the desk in her dimly-lit room, Rio sighs. It is often that she finds herself in said predicament, coffee near her dominant hand, a journal spread open before her with endless pages dating back decades, and glasses perched at the tip of her nose. She sighs, an almost trademark part of her nights, upon staring down at the blank page before her.

Already in the depths of adulthood, she was unsure about her chances of meeting her soulmate anytime soon. For years she has fought hard in a frail attempt to solve the riddle of who her soul was bound to, but as always, it ended in deep disappointment. Currently she finds it easier to humor the notion of spending the rest of her days alone. But each time another word appears in her notebook, she falls for the phantom person once more.

Good evening, sweets. How are you?

Tired. I’m glad to be on break until the upcoming semester. I almost popped a vein grading all those shitty papers and exams last week. Rio writes back in green pen, a color which deliciously clashes against the purple that materializes on the page. During the Spring and Fall, she finds herself drowning in lecturing classes as a psychology professor at Carnegie Mellon. Although the coziness of the Summer and Winter leaves her without many students to interact with, they are filled with continuing her research while jumping from conference to conference. At least for the blissful period that was Christmas and New Year’s, she basks in the peace she rarely achieves. How was the hospital today?

I had a rough time with a patient, it’s a long story. I’d rather not talk about it for now. It was…heavy to say the least.

They are not able to tell each other much when it comes to identifying one another – the ink dries and turns transparent if they do. Yet as years go by, little fun facts are dropped here and there. That is enough to keep Rio excited for when they inevitably meet…at least she hopes enough.

I want us to meet. Not a week goes by where the same words are not written on the page. It hurts.

Even if she did not know the woman’s identity, Rio’s heart swelled for her. She shakes her head and looks away momentarily. Sometimes she entices the idea of never writing back, to instead disappear and take fate into her own hands. There is difficulty in communicating with someone whose status is nearly unknown. Most peers and family members of hers have met their other-halves within years of writing back and forth on the journals, but not Rio.

Of course, out of all people, she is the one to suffer through the brunt of never meeting her soulmate.

I hate Christmas.

They never miss her in the same manner; she refuses to give them the benefit back.

That she has finally found someone who gives her the affections her family often lacked growing up.

The stench of coffee on her shirt is forgotten, instead replaced by the mental image of the woman she would see on the following day.

But as she remembers Agatha, a sweet brush of color in the monochrome taint her world had become, Rio finds herself realizing that maybe there is something, someone, worth staying for.
