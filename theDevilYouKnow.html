the devil you know

Rio bit back a yawn as she rose with the sun.

One would think that after years of training, such early rousings would come more naturally. Rio had always been more inclined to dusk than dawn though, and it was with great reluctance that she left the warmth of her bedding.

After splashing cold water on her face, Rio prepared for the day as she had the last fifteen years of her life. Leather pants, loose blouse, fitted bodice. Her belt to hold her tinctures and knives came next, along with the sheath for her dagger and the leather guards for her arms. Last came her crucifix, a gleaming silver piece that was the last remnant of the Vidal’s legacy. That, and Rio, of course.

Rio pressed her lips to the cool metal before she slipped it over her head, letting the pendant rest right over her chest. 

As she stepped out of her cottage, Rio combed her fingers through her hair, pulling the upper half into a bun to keep it out of her way. Around her, the rest of the village bustled about with morning chores.

Rio slipped past those carrying laundry or gardening tools, heading straight for the large stone building at the center of their village. Several greeted her as she passed by, and Rio gave them each a respectful nod. Those who lived here had either known Rio since she was small or grown up with Rio as a staid fixture.

A small boy broke away from his group, running up to Rio with a wide, toothy grin. He greeted her with a wave, chirping, “Good morrow, Rio!”

“Felix,” Rio replied, inclining her head. At ten years old, Felix was still rather short for his age, but he was no longer the frail boy Rio had found a year ago. She let her hand rest on his head briefly. “How is your training going?”

“Well enough,” Felix answered. He practically bounced in place as he explained, “I hope to practice with a real dagger soon.” He nodded to the one strapped to Rio’s thigh. “Like yours.”

“Phineas will give you a wooden one first,” Rio reminded him. Phineas was one of the best skilled hunters in their guild, but after a particularly brutal slaying that injured his leg, he kept to the village and trained their youths. Rio knew how it pained him at times, but she’d seen more than one student flourish under his tutelage. Hopefully, Felix would be another.

Felix waved off her reminder, saying, “Yes, but then I can have a real one.” 

Rio smiled fondly. “Yes, once you’ve proven you can be trusted, then you can have a real dagger.” She noticed the sun beginning to creep over the horizon. She ruffled Felix’s hair and said, “Study diligently and practice fiercely. I know you will do well.”

Felix beamed at her and said, “May fortune find you in favor.”

“My thanks,” Rio replied, amused to hear the old hunters’ saying from such young, inexperienced lips. “Go along now.”

Rio watched as Felix scampered off before she continued to make her way to their guild’s base. It was a stone manor, just large enough to host guests and a select few, high-ranking hunters. Rio had been invited several times to move in, but each time, she gently rejected the offer. She preferred her own space in her cottage. 

However, as much as she avoided actually living in the manor, Rio was never shy to help herself to the food there. This morning, Rio helped herself to a plate of soft-boiled eggs, sausage, and warm bread. She thanked the cooks for their work, and she walked with her food to the far end of the manor where the war room was.

The war room was poorly named, but in truth, Rio was relieved that it had never lived up to its name. They were unlikely to survive if their hunt against vampires truly came to war. Still, everyone called it the war room, the cavernous room large enough to fit a long table and several crates of weapons and shelves of books. Some evenings, Rio enjoyed hiding in here and reading whatever happened to find its way into her hands.

Already there was Theodore, who insisted everyone call him “Theo”. He was forty-five years old, one of the oldest of their hunters. He was broad-shouldered and scarred, with dark skin and black hair that he wore cropped short. Rio still remembered when some attempted to call him Lord Theodore, but that had been swiftly abandoned when Theo had punished such attempts with miles and miles of endurance training. Commander was the closest Theo came to formalities. 

“Took you long enough,” Theo grunted when he saw her. He only had a cup of tea in his hand. Maps took up most of the table in front of him. “I thought you would sleep until the sun was high.”

“The sun’s barely kissed the sky,” Rio replied, sitting down at the opposite end of the table. “Besides, is what we’re to discuss really of such urgency?”

“Depends on how you view the seven deaths of innocents.” 

Rio’s mouth snapped shut. She ducked her head, chastened. “My apologies,” Rio said. “Please inform me of the circumstances of the deaths.”

As Rio ate, Theo explained to her the events in a far-out village, nearly a three days’ ride from here. Over the last two months, once a week, someone had gone missing. Only men. There was no doubt it was a vampire either. Every time the missing men were found, it was with two punctures at their throat and their bodies drained of blood. 

It unnerved Rio to hear that last detail. This vampire wasn’t even attempting to hide their true nature, flaunting it as if to goad hunters to come get them. Rio’s grip tightened around her fork. It was sickening.

Even after all these years, Rio still found vampires unfathomable. They defied the laws of nature, in Rio’s eyes. They were never meant to live again, let alone with the life blood of others to sustain themselves. They were abominations. How else could one explain the careless way they wielded life and death? 

There was only once Rio had ever seen a vampire partake in mercy. She was living proof of it. 

It only made Rio loathe them more.

“When do I leave?” Rio asked.

“Tomorrow,” Theo answered. He walked around the table and handed her a map with the directions to the village. “Spend today preparing all of your necessities.” When Rio went to take the map, Theo withdrew slightly. He waited until she met his eyes to say, “I know this is ill timing for you. Do you wish to have someone accompany you for this?”

Flashes of fire and screams flared behind Rio’s eyes. She blinked hard. Rio shook her head and said, “I require no assistance.”

“Fortune does not shine on those too stubborn to admit when they need help,” Theo said.

Rio clenched her jaw and snatched the map from his hands. She stood abruptly from her chair and snapped, “Lady Fortune is a fickle mistress. She will pick and choose as she pleases. Having someone shadow me on such a task will have no bearing on the outcome.”

There was a beat of silence before Theo asked, “Are you done acting like a child?”

“Are you done treating me like one?” Rio shot back. For a moment, she swore she could feel the licks of flames against her skin. She swallowed. “Am I dismissed, Commander?”

Theo stared at her, dark eyes piercing. Rio held her ground though. Such a gaze lost its effect when one faced it since one was twelve. Eventually, Theo sighed and said, “Dismissed. I expect to see you before you leave tomorrow. Dawn.” 

“Yes, sir,” Rio muttered. 

There was much to prepare for an indefinite hunt like this. Rio could be gone for one day, or she could be gone for one month. Because of this, Rio knew it was best to prepare for a lengthier stay. She stopped by the blacksmith to sharpen her weapons, the stables to ready her horse, the apothecary to gather the necessary potions and herbs, the storehouse for food, and finally, the wash house for one last bath. There was no telling the amenities a village that far from others would have. 

Thanks to her station as a hunter, Rio walked past the larger bathing area for the majority of villagers. She stepped up to a door and smoothed her thumb over the rune carved right above the door knob. The rune glowed gold briefly, followed by a click. Rio stepped inside the smaller bathroom and went about heating the water. It was nearly midday now, and there were few bathing at this time. Not to mention, this was solely for hunters who were women, and there were even less of them to run into here.

Sinking into the steaming water, Rio knew it would be wise to begin to craft her strategy. Despite this, Rio found her thoughts drifting. As much as she didn’t want to admit it, Theo was right. Sending Rio off on a hunt during this time was risky. Each year when the day of her family’s deaths came, Rio grew tetchy. It wasn’t just that she was rude to others. Her thinking grew clouded. Her actions grew impulsive. Rio would have to monitor herself carefully as she went to this village. Losing herself in her memories could prove fatal. 

Once Rio finished her bath and dressed, she finished the last of her preparations. It would be wise to go home and retire for the evening. If she did, she would be well rested for the long travel ahead. Instead, Rio made her way out to the nearby field. Rows and rows of crops greeted her, but all of its caretakers had already gone home for the night. That was fine by Rio, who picked her way between the plants and settled herself among the budding fruits. 

This was something Rio had done since the hunters’ guild took her in. The grief and guilt that clung to her did not feel nearly as suffocating out here. Perhaps that was why Rio came out here tonight. Laying against the cool dirt, Rio pulled her crucifix from beneath her shirt and held it above her face, letting the moonlight cast a glow against the metal. 

Rio still remembered when she first received this. At six years of age, Rio had seen the shining jewelry adorning her grandmother's neck. She clambered into her lap and asked what it was, tiny fingers running along its edges.

“It is protection,” her grandmother had told her. 

“From what?” Rio had asked.

Her grandmother had stared at her with weary eyes and said, “Devils.” When Rio startled, her grandmother had taken off the necklace and put it around Rio’s neck. “There,” she’d said. “You will be safe now.”

“What about you?”

“I will be safe so long as I have you, my dear.”

Her grandmother hadn’t been safe though. No one had. To this day, Rio wondered if it’d truly been this crucifix that saved her life that night. She wasn’t sure if she could handle it if it was, knowing that her grandmother’s kindness had also been her death. She also wasn’t sure if she could handle it if it wasn’t, knowing that her life was some kind of cruel coincidence. 

Rio pressed her lips to the crucifix and went home.

. . .

Not even the sun was awake when Rio left the following morning.

Thanks to a sleepless night, Rio was able to leave even sooner than planned, but as requested, she still stopped by the manor before she left. She strode into the building dressed as she had the day before, but to fend off the chill of the autumn morning, Rio slipped on her cowl and cloak as well, securing the chain of her cloak across her chest and pulling its collar up around her neck. 

“Dramatic,” Theo had always said in amusement.

This morning though, there was no humor in Theo’ demeanor. He peered at her in the dim glow of candlelight, scanning her person to ensure she was appropriately armed. He nodded after a moment and gestured for her to follow him outside to where Rio’s black horse, Dahlia, was waiting. 

“Be careful,” Theo said. “Take no unnecessary risks.” He stood tall with his hands behind his back, but Rio knew him too well. There was a worry that made his brows furrow ever so slightly. Rio wished to assure him, but she knew nothing would soothe him except her safe return.

“Of course, Commander,” Rio said. 

Theo sighed and placed a warm hand on Rio’s shoulder. Even though things were tense between them, Theo still told Rio, “May fortune find you in favor.” It was considered bad luck not to exchange the words before a hunt, as if condemning the hunter to a luckless death.

“My thanks,” Rio replied.

“I hope this will prove a simple matter,” Theo said, squeezing her shoulder once. “Nonetheless, don’t be rash.”

“Who, me?” Rio said with a small grin. 

Theo rolled his eyes, and it was with a final pat on Rio’s shoulder that he sent her off.

Even as the sun began to rise, Rio’s route was quiet. That was unsurprising though. Many would be traveling in the opposite direction as her, heading towards the clustered villages that came before the city, rather than further out into the wild. As she rode, Rio kept an eye out for anything suspicious, but nothing struck her as strange, even as she grew closer and closer to her destination. 

However, just a mile out from the village, Rio came to an abrupt stop, feeling the back of her neck prickle. She waited a moment, but the sensation remained. She climbed down from her horse and pulled her cowl over her mouth and nose. With slow, deliberate moves, Rio unsheathed her dagger and prowled forward.

Her eyes scanned the area, but all she saw were trees. Turning slowly, she sought out any sign of others, but all she saw was a figure on the ground a little ways away. Rio adjusted her grip on her dagger as she crept forward, careful not to step on any stray branches. 

It could be a trap. Someone pretending to be unconscious, or perhaps, it was a decoy with someone waiting elsewhere to ambush her. 

Rio kept all of her wits about her, but when she drew close, she saw it was nothing but a dead deer. Rio inspected the pitiful creature with its throat torn out. She muttered a prayer to Lady Fortune, wishing for a swift return to the earth for the deer and a better fate for herself. 

Glancing around, Rio determined no one would be surprising her here. She knelt beside the deer and examined its throat. It was difficult to know whether it was a wolf or a vampire; both were brutal in their bites, and this particular wound was so messy that Rio couldn’t gauge the size of the mouth or teeth. 

Rio shook her head as she stood again. She had an awful feeling about this. She needed to make sure she was prepared. She looked around the woods again and decided there was no better time or place than here or now. Rio shed her cloak and rolled up her sleeves. With the practiced ease of years of experience, Rio pressed the tip of her dagger into her skin, whispering under her breath as she did. Rather than draw blood, a reddish glow trailed after the dagger as Rio carved runes along her arms. 

It was a well-kept secret of hunters, these runes. People knew of runes to set traps or start fires, but few knew of the ancient runes meant to give hunters heightened strength and speed to fight against the unnatural. No one could say where the runes came from, only that they were a rare advantage that only hunters knew.

Rio only learned of it herself when she turned eighteen, the acceptable age to begin using them. It took diligence and skill to correctly draw and activate runes, but they had never been a problem for Rio, who accepted the way they burned her flesh without so much of a blink. That had always concerned Theo, but it was undeniably Rio’s greatest strength as a hunter, her tolerance for more runes than any other. 

For now, Rio only carved three runes into her skin: strength, speed, and perception. She would add more after she determined the true danger of what she was dealing with. Rio put her cloak back on and made sure her sleeves covered the runes. They made others skittish.

Mounting her horse once more, Rio traveled the last stretch before she reached the village. 

Given the violent reports they received, Rio expected a rather sedate, hushed atmosphere, but the village bustled with the usual energy of any other village. It was strange to think of them as unaffected by the string of murders. Seven men dead could easily be a third of the men in a village. Yet, no one seemed especially worried or scared. They continued on, as if the deaths were just another part of life.

The villagers’ strange behavior made Rio itch with the urge to immediately go and investigate, but courtesy and manners dictated she see the head of the village before she did anything. With great reluctance, Rio made the trek to the large house on the hill that casted a shadow on the rest of the village. Eyes tracked her as she went, followed by whispers. Rio did not need to strain her hearing to know what they were saying. Nightwalker .

Rio loathed the name her people had gained over the years by the common masses. It was too much like something one would call the vampires. However, the name grew in its popularity, as did people’s distaste for hunters. Due to their progression, they kept to themselves and traveled most often when the moon was alight and high in the sky. That, coupled with their ability to match a vampire’s prowess, left an unsavory taste in many mouths.

Despite being necessary, hunters were treated only a fraction better than the monsters they killed.

When Rio finally arrived at the village head’s house, a meek servant greeted her and took her cloak before she led her into the sitting room. A small fire burned, making the room just warm enough to make Rio shift uncomfortably. Sitting on one of the couches was the village head, a fat man with a carefully trimmed, brown beard and a ruddy complexion.

Lord Edmure Harkness was not of any bloodline Rio knew of, but Rio did not take any kind of particular care to memorizing the households of nobility. Her time was better spent sharpening a blade or even cleaning out a horse stall. This was why Rio was less than impressed when Edmure stood and exclaimed, “The Nightwalker has arrived!”

“My lord,” Rio said flatly. She fell into the expected bow, but she did not linger in it. The greeting was warmer than she expected though. Many were not so welcoming to have a hunter in their midst. Some believed them to be an ill omen, even if they are the ones to request their presence. The paradoxical nature of the mythos that surrounded hunters made Rio roll her eyes often. 

“Sit, sit,” Edmure said, waving at the couch across from him. “Have you eaten yet?” Before Rio could even answer, Edmured continued, “Let us eat. I know the journey must have been long, and my cook has just brought out freshly made biscuits.”

Rio bit back her annoyance and said, “Thank you, my lord, for your generosity, but I would prefer to begin my investigation while there is still an abundance of daylight.”

Thankfully, Edmure took no offense. He chuckled, saying, “I suppose the stipulations that Nightwalkers only live and breathe to continue on to their next kill are true.” Rio’s eyes narrowed as Edmure said, “If you insist on traipsing through the woods, I must insist my daughter accompany you.”

“Your daughter?” Rio repeated, caught between confusion and exasperation. What was this vapid noble going on about now?

Edmure nodded. “Yes, I think she would be of great assistance to your cause.” He glanced over his shoulder and waved his hand once more. “Rio, this is my daughter, Agatha.”

A slight woman stepped forward, dressed far too elaborately for a humble village miles out from the nearest trading post. Agatha appeared to be the only daughter of the minor lord though, so perhaps, in their insipid brains, they saw it fit to deck her out in the finest silks they could afford to maintain some kind of misguided superiority over their villagers. It wouldn’t surprise Rio if it worked.

Agatha had the kind of classical beauty that nobility passed on from generation to generation, with high cheekbones and a sharp nose. Dark hair was swept into an updo, but long strands hung around her face, framing it. Her skin was pale, as was fitting for a woman of her station, and her eyes were a startling blue, the kind that looked unnatural. 

Her clothing only served to accentuate her beauty. Agatha’s dress consisted of a black bodice and skirt and a matching blouse, lined with a blood red fabric that dipped low, toying with propriety. The most eye-catching part of her finery was her necklaces, black jewels that swooped along collarbones and clustered around an oval, purple stone. For a moment, Rio thought there was something engraved, but when she blinked, all she saw was smooth violet.

Giving a short bow, Rio said, “How do you do, Lady Harkness?”

“Thank you for coming so quickly,” Agatha said. Her voice was lower than Rio expected. It wasn’t the usual simpering but rather it was low, almost rough. An odd dichotomy against her dress. “I hope to aid in your hunt however I can.”

“As generous as your help is, it is best that I work alone,” Rio said. There was an underhandedness to the kindness. They did not truly trust her. Why else have someone so ill-suited to the task to follow her?

“Would it not be beneficial to have someone who knows the woods at your side?” Edmure countered. “Someone to help guide you through our village and provide insight into the families of the victims.”

Perhaps if it were a competent hunter, who was not an obvious play to keep an eye on her. Rio made sure to keep her voice even as she said, “My lord–”

“Agatha will attend to your needs,” Edmure said firmly, and Rio wasn’t sure if she was imagining the amused curl to said woman’s lips as he said this. “She will provide the necessary particulars and inform me of all that you discover.”

“Is it not,” Rio struggled to sound like she was not passing judgement on his propriety, “unconventional for the lord’s daughter to partake in such activities?”

“We are not so small-minded simply because we live far from the city,” Edmure admonished. “I would have thought a Nightwalker such as yourself would understand the need for a young lady to take advantage of the opportunities afforded to her, no matter how unsightly to others it may seem.”

The thought of any kind of parallel drawn between her and a lord’s daughter was laughable, but Rio inclined her head and said, “My sincerest apologies, my lord. I would be honored to have your daughter accompany me.”

Edmure beamed at her and said, “I knew you would understand. Please allow Agatha to break her fast before she joins you.”

“Of course,” Rio agreed. “I will be in the village in the meantime, speaking to your people, if you will allow it.”

Once the permission was granted, Rio excused herself. As she stepped back out into the brisk morning air, she heaved a heavy sigh. She gave a resentful glare over her shoulder, wishing that someone else was given this assignment after all. Looking after some nobleman’s daughter felt akin to punishment, and if she didn’t know any better, she would guess Theo planned this.

With a resigned air, Rio set off to explore the village and its strange inhabitants. 

In the two hours it took for the Harknesses to complete their meal, Rio wandered the village, speaking to a few villagers who were out to gather their thoughts on the recent murders. Many did not have much to say, merely expressing their worries and fears to her. That was mostly the women though. The men refused to acknowledge her presence, as if her being here was some statement against the capabilities. It was a vain practice of pride, as if any of them here would be able to hold their own against anything in the realm of a vampire.

It was almost a relief when Agatha appeared alongside a servant, saving Rio from one of the blacksmith apprentices, who’d taken it upon himself to lecture her about the finer details of caring for silver weaponry.

Rio felt her eyes widen as she took in the sight of Agatha, newly prepared for her endeavor with Rio. It appeared that the length of time was not simply due to eating but also changing her apparel. Gone was the luxurious gown, and in its place were riding leathers, a blouse, and a fitted bodice with laces up the front and embroidered with gold. 

“No need to look so surprised, Nightwalker,” Agatha called out to her. “I am not so foolish as to travel the woods in silk.”

“One never knows with ilk of your kind,” Rio muttered under her breath. 

Rio forced herself to take another breath as she watched Agatha dismiss her servant. This was of no issue. Rio had taken Felix on a hunt before, and that had been tolerable. Of course, that had been a hunt for deer, and Felix was far more obedient than a spoiled noble. 

Agatha flashed her a smile that was far too excitable for someone who was about to venture into the same woods where seven bodies were found. “Shall we?”

“After you, my lady,” Rio said. “You are my guide after all.”

“No need to sound petulant, Nightwalker,” Agatha replied, but she still walked ahead, straight into the woods without any sort of hesitation. 

Rio followed her, thankful her back was to her, so Rio could express the true extent to her exasperation, poking her tongue out. Once the childish urge passed, Rio cleared her throat and asked, “What can you tell me of the victims? The reports your father sent only told us that they were all men.”

“Will you give me a knife?”

Rio blinked owlishly at the sudden question, unsure where it had even come from. Agatha had not even deigned to turn to face her to ask. Warily, Rio said, “I do not think that would be wise.”

“How am I to protect myself then?” Agatha looked at her now, nose wrinkled. 

Rio stated what should have been a given, “I will protect you.”

“Do you promise?”

Rio looked up sharply at Agatha then, meeting her piercing gaze. The other woman raised a brow, taunting Rio in her lack of an answer. Rio exhaled slowly through her nose as she chose her words carefully. This woman was more difficult than she initially seemed.

“As is my duty as a hunter of my guild, I swear to protect your life with my own,” Rio said. The familiar words allowed her to relax a fraction. It reminded of being twelve years old and reciting such words for the first time, inducting her into the guild. A simpler time. 

“Duty,” Agatha repeated the word slowly, as if tasting it for the first time. She turned to face Rio fully, walking backwards with ease despite the uneven ground.  “Is that all your life revolves around? Duty?”

“Is that so wrong?” Rio replied with a tilt of her head. “To dedicate myself to the protection of others?”

“And what of yourself?” Agatha asked. She stopped, allowing Rio to draw closer. It was only when Rio stopped herself, a few feet away, that Agatha said, “Do you not have anything else you desire?”

Rio’s throat bobbed at the way Agatha’s voice deepened on the last word. She licked her lips before she said, “This is not an appropriate topic of discussion.”

“Is this not what women discuss?” Agatha said with exaggerated innocence, eyes wide. When Rio merely shook her head and continued walking, Agatha sighed and fell into step with her. “Fine,” Agatha said. “Then tell me, Nightwalker, how does one kill a vampire?”

“I do not believe that is an appropriate topic of discussion either.”

At this, Agatha rolled her eyes. “Come now,” she said. ” “My father sent me here with you, did he not?” Rio did not answer. Agatha stared at her for a moment longer before she said, “Fine, I shall start. A wooden stake to the heart is a time-honored method.” Unable to help herself, Rio eyed Agatha, to which Agatha smirked and said, “Do not look at me like that, hunter. I do read.”

“Pardon me, but last I was informed, occult is not typically on the list for well-to-do women to read,” Rio replied. Her tone was dry and unamused, but for some reason, it only made Agatha’s smile grow.

“Was I wrong?”

There was no escaping this line of conversation, it appeared. Rio said, “No, it is a fair choice of weapon. It is also the most accessible to those untrained and unequipped for such dangers. One can fashion a wooden stake with ease.”

“And for those who are trained?”

“Silver.”

Agatha’s eyes dipped low before they flicked back up to Rio’s face. “Like your dagger?”

Rio felt the urge to use her cloak to cover the weapon. Her hands remained at her side. “Yes, like my dagger,” Rio said. “Now, my lady, tell me of the victims.”

Thankfully, Agatha relented and spoke all of what she knew of the seven men who were murdered. Four of the men were fathers, and three of the men were unwed. Their ages ranged from twenty to sixty years, and while none of the men were saints, only two of them were worthy of any scorn. All of the men were found in the very woods they were walking through, drained of blood but with little other injury.

“And this has never happened before?” Rio asked.

“Not that I know of,” Agatha answered. Despite Rio’s quick pace, she did not seem fatigued in the least. It made Rio ponder exactly what the other woman did during her leisure time because it certainly wasn’t sitting around. Any other noble would have been red-faced and panting by this point. “My father had our advisor look into the archives, but there was nothing of note there either.”

Rio hummed, considering all that she knew. It wasn’t unheard of for vampires to go into hibernations of sorts, coming out to kill only every so often, but that was rare. For the most part, vampires preferred regular feeding, so it was likely that a vampire recently came into the area. They most likely assumed the feedings would go unnoticed or unhelped considering how distant the village was from others. The real question was whether the vampire was still here or they moved on already.

Rio was deliberating the merit in setting a trap when Agatha suddenly pitched forward. Her petty glee at the other woman finally tripping was cut short by her reflexes. Rio stepped into her path, catching Agatha around her waist before she could hit the ground. She held her breath as Agatha stumbled into her. Agatha’s hands found purchase against her chest, and Rio could not remember the last time she felt another body so solidly against her own.

“How gallant,” Agatha said breathlessly. She made no move to free herself from Rio’s arms, peering up at Rio through thick lashes. Her eyes were bright and blue even in the overcast of a dull morning, and Rio was reminded of just how beautiful she was. “Perhaps I could be persuaded to find a new appreciation for Nightwalkers.”

Rio flushed. She opened her mouth to deflect when she suddenly tensed. Her ears caught the crackle of leaves just behind them. Releasing Agatha, Rio spun on pure instinct and launched a knife in the direction of the sound. The blade sank into the rabbit, killing it instantly.  

Adrenaline made Rio’s ears ring as she scanned the area for anything else. After a moment, her body relaxed. Just the rabbit. 

“I see why you were sent.” 

Rio stiffened once more, glancing over her shoulder to where Agatha eyed her with something much darker than the previous, playful admiration. “You are much more competent than I expected,” Agatha said.

“You flatter me,” Rio said, dismayed to find she was the one who sounded a tad breathless now. She rubbed her hands on her pants and turned away. “Let us continue.” She did not wait to see if Agatha would follow, but she heard the crunch of leaves a moment later. 

Rio shook her head minutely. Of all the things to distract her on this assignment, a nobleman’s daughter had not even been a consideration.

. . .

For once, Rio had never been so glad to see the setting sun. It was a sign to head back to the village, a place where she would no longer be alone with Agatha.

Rio remained on edge for the entire day, unable to free her mind of the feeling of Agatha’s body against her own. It was a foolish thing to linger on, but Rio could not bring her mind to cooperate. Several times, she would drift, recalling supple curves and coy smiles.

It was not uncommon for Rio to find women attractive during her travels, but never before had someone elicited such a reaction from her before. It was maddening, especially considering who it was that had captured her attention so.
