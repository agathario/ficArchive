Heart Beats like a Drummer

Rio Vidal was a rolling stone.

She had never stayed in one place for more than a year - maybe two, tops . Since she’d picked up her drumsticks at the tender age of four, tapping away at her Uncle Ludo’s old, beat-up drumset, she’d been moving. Her arms, her feet, her legs, everything. She kept moving, kept getting better at her craft, kept learning all she could about music and the people that played it, kept moving from place to place, lending her talent wherever it was wanted or needed.

She was the most sought-after professional drummer in the music industry now, lending her drumsticks to dozens of best-selling albums all over the world. She never worked with one act on a second album, never stuck around long enough to get attached. She just kept moving, kept drumming, kept breaking hearts with her music and her charm…

Until Agatha.

Agatha Harkness was different.

She was, in the opinion of most people around the world, a living legend. Her mother, Evanora Harkness, had been a well-known singer, as well, but she’d stuck mainly to church music, her songs going Platinum only amongst certain crowds.

When her only daughter started to show musical talent, as well, it was to the surprise of nobody in their inner circle.

But when Agatha Harkness joined a rock band as their lead singer and guitarist, Evanora’s fans were shocked and dismayed. Agatha’s new brand of music was deemed ‘sinful’ and she was shunned from her mother’s circle. Her mother had almost immediately disowned the sixteen-year-old.

But that was just fine with Agatha as her star began to rise. She wasn’t the only famous child in the band, after all.

Alice Wu Gulliver was on the keyboard, putting the talent she’d inherited from her world-renowned pop star mother, Lorna Wu, to good use. Jen Kale, her best friend since childhood, was on drums, completing their trio in their band Coven , which took off as soon as their first album was released.

After that, it was really only a matter of time before Agatha went solo, which she did in her mid-twenties, after some spat with Jen and Alice over ‘creative differences’. Alice and Jen’s careers seemed to peter off after that; the former continued to tour with her mother, but the latter turned to skincare and social media to keep her star from waning any further.

Agatha, on the other hand, became a global sensation. After leaving Coven , she found an agent in Lilia Calderu and continued to make music and tour the world, rarely taking any breaks if she could help it.

Rio had been following her career since she was sixteen years old, still part of her own first band. Her uncle had gotten her tickets to Agatha’s first solo tour, for which Rio would always be grateful because it introduced her to… Her .

Rio had heard of Coven . She had listened to the hit songs they put out on the radio, had bobbed her head along at parties and dances, her sweaty hands wrapped around other teen girls as they spun in circles in a way that could have maybe counted as dancing if you squinted enough. A song or two even made it onto a playlist on her iPod when she was walking to school in the morning. 

But she’d never truly appreciated the famous trio that made up the band. The vocals were sick, she had to admit, and the drums were decent. The piano riffs were also not too shabby, but Coven was just one of those bands that you let play in the background while you do your homework.

Agatha , on the other hand. Rio remembered her uncle giving her the tickets at her sixteenth birthday dinner with a wide grin. 

“Um, thanks,” Rio said, looking down at the two pieces of paper. “Who’s Agatha Harkness?”

“Who’s…the lead singer of Coven !” he’d huffed.

“Didn’t they break up?”

“Yes, but Agatha went solo. These are front row seats to her first show in New York!”

“Oh,” Rio said. “Cool, I guess.”

“You guess ?” Ludo grumbled. “Girl, give those back! I’m gonna take your tia. She’ll probably appreciate them, at least.”

Rio had clutched the tickets to her chest. “I appreciate them!” she insisted. “I’m just…surprised. That’s all.” She wrapped her arms around him in a tight hug. “ Gracias, Tio. Te amo .”

“ Te amo, mija, ” Ludo said, pressing a kiss to her temple. “So, which girl are you gonna bring with you?” He waggled his eyebrows at her.

“Girl?” Rio scoffed. “I’m bringing you , old man! Unless…oof, is the show past your bedtime?” She grimaced, teasingly.

“I am going to drop-kick you into next Tuesday,” he growled, wrapping his arms around her and picking Rio up as she squealed. 

Ludo had gotten them the two best seats in the house with his connections. The stadium had been otherwise packed to the brim with former fans of Coven and current fans of Agatha, all chanting her name as they waved around purple glowsticks, cheering for her as the lights began to flicker and she rose up from beneath the stage, glad in some purple, lacy, and very, very glittery.

Rio had been to other shows in the past. Her uncle was a famous drummer, and Rio herself was starting to make a name for herself. He’d been taking her to concerts since she was about eight, after he needled her parents into letting her accompany him to her first local rock gig. She had marveled mainly at the drummers, the way they wielded their drumsticks as they pounded out a rhythm. She had worn ear plugs to her first few concerts, her parents worried about the damage her hearing would take, but there was no way to really block out the vibrations of drums, which she swore she could feel in her heart.

But there was also nothing like the first Agatha concert that Rio had ever attended. She was past the eardrums thing (her parents had conceded that, being their daughter was a drummer herself, the damage was far more likely to come from her own doing, anyway) and had actually let herself get excited about the concert. She’d been listening to old Coven songs in the weeks leading up to Agatha’s solo concert. Ludo had blasted Agatha’s new EP on the way from Westview to NYC, singing along to the eight songs Agatha had put out since leaving her old band behind.

They were surprisingly good.

Then, when Agatha had appeared on stage in that glittery purple number…well, Rio had felt weak in the knees. 

She had always known that the lead singer of Coven was hot; that was not a surprise. Every member of Coven was hot, actually. But Agatha Harkness was…breathtaking. Rio may have actually forgotten how to breathe when she first saw her up on that stage, her hair wild and curly around her head, her lips painted burgundy, her eyes outlined thickly in liner, mascara, and deep purple eye shadow. Rio’s mouth had gone dry as Agatha strutted toward the front of the stage, her mic posed between long fingers, a wide, cocky grin on her face.

“Good evening, New York!” she shouted, to the roar of tens of thousands of her fans. Ludo nudged her as she walked straight toward where they were standing, but Agatha wasn’t quite looking at them yet. Her gaze was still roaming over the stadium, over all the fans standing on their feet for her. “How’s everybody doing tonight?” Another roar of applause and cheers, chants of her name. “Good to hear it! Thank you all so much for coming out to my first solo show ! Isn’t that insane !?!” More cheers. “I couldn’t have asked for a better first night, though. You are all so amazing ! Give yourselves a round of applause.” The sound was like thunder as every single person in the stadium began clapping for themselves, but Rio could barely move as transfixed as she was staring at Agatha.

“Before we begin,” Agatha said, “I wanna let y’all in on a little secret that I’ve been dying to tell you for a while. It’s not super huge or anything. It’s just this little thing that I’ve been carrying for a while and I’d love the chance to share with you right here, right now. You ready?”

Cheers of ‘Yes, Mother’ went up around the crowd. 

Agatha barked out a laugh. “ Mother ?” she echoed. “ Really ?” She shrugged. “I’ll allow it.” She gave the crowd a brilliant smile. “Okay,” she said. “Alright. So, here’s the thing.” She cleared her throat, crouching down at the front-center of the stage, just about ten feet from where Rio stood, still staring at her with wide eyes, her cheeks pink and rosy. “Y’all listening?” Cheers and murmurs and claps. “I am a woman,” Agatha started, “who enjoys the company of…other women.” 

In the crowd of thousands of people, Rio was certain she’d have been able to hear a pin drop from a mile away from the silence that suddenly shrouded them. Agatha’s gaze roamed over the room, and Rio watched the way that her throat bobbed nervously though she kept that wide grin plastered on her face.

Then, slowly, people began to clap. And then that was joined by scattered cheering. And from there it grew even louder, people going wild at Agatha’s confession. Rio’s eyes just grew wider, her jaw dropping slightly as she stared up at this woman, whom she didn’t know very well at all despite listening to her band in passing since she was a child.

And she was like her . She was like Rio , a woman who liked other women.

Rio had never been in the closet for even a day. She didn’t grow up thinking that being a girl who liked other girls was weird or wrong. Her uncle was a proud bisexual man who married a proud bisexual woman. Her parents had made it a point that she was allowed to like whomever she liked, whether they were male or female. She’d walked in pride parades with her uncle and his previous boyfriends. She’d waved a flag while she sat on his shoulders. She’d held a girl’s hand in the second grade, had even introduced her to her parents as the girl she was in love with (girlfriend apparently wasn’t strong enough for how Rio had felt for her at the time), and her parents had been ecstatic, cooing over the two girls, which was the only thing that even remotely embarrassed her.

Even with all that, Rio hadn’t missed the lack of representation in the media of women who loved other women. She hadn’t missed the hateful rhetoric spread by people like Agatha’s own mother, who went on homophobic tirades from time to time. That was probably the main reason why Agatha hadn’t come out long ago. Or maybe she hadn’t even known before that liking women was an option.

No matter the reason, the fact that Agatha was standing there, in front of a crowd of tens of thousands of people, telling them what was probably her deepest, most closely-held secret, was something that Rio felt deep in her chest may have actually changed her life.

And it did.

After that day, Rio Vidal was a die hard Agatha Harkness fan. She rose up in her career with the main goal of being the drummer for Agatha’s tour or being featured on one of her albums. Even just meeting the woman and getting to talk about their love for music would have been enough.

And now, she was there.

She was thirty-six years old, twenty years past sitting in the front row of Agatha Harkness’s first solo concert, and sitting in a recording studio, staring at a shiny new drumset. She tapped out a soft rhythm as she waited for the other band members to get there, closing her eyes as she imagined the notes of whatever song they would start with. Agatha had emailed her the music, directly, which had been their only contact since Lilia Calderu had called her, personally, to invite her on Agatha’s newest album.

Rio had thought it was a prank, at first, and had nearly hung up the phone.

But then Agatha’s voice had joined in.

“Rio Vidal?” It was unmistakable, so clear and unique and definitely not a prank. Rio’s voice had caught in her lungs for a second. “Hey, this is Agatha Harkness. Please don’t say no.”

So Rio hadn’t. She’d barely strangled out a yes, uh-huh-ing and humming as Lilia explained the terms of the contract, which she’d be happy to meet Rio and her agent, Sharon Davis, to talk over when she landed in New York, which would of course be covered by Agatha’s production team. 

Before Rio could even wrap her head around what was happening, she was sitting in a room with both Lilia and Sharon, who were discussing the proposed contracts over glasses of sparkling cider and a bowl of fresh fruit. 

Agatha Harkness had not been in attendance.

She beat out a simple drum beat as she played the song in her head, imagining the melody as it was written on the page. Rio had noticed that there were no drum notations on the PDF she’d sent, with her own hastily scribbled chicken scratch on the page. In the body of her email, Agatha had simply written, “See what you can do with this. See you on Wednesday!”

Rio had read and re-read those notes, imagining what the bass would sound like, the guitar, the piano, Agatha’s smooth and sultry voice…but she was having trouble imagining the drums. She wouldn’t really know where to begin until they played the song once through, anyway.

Rio jumped and spun around on the little stool, her eyes widening at the sight of the Agatha Harkness standing in the doorway to the recording booth, a coffee cup in hand, and looking far too casual to be an internationally renowned superstar in yoga pants and a loose-fitting hoodie, a pair of rose-tinted sunglasses slipping down her nose, her bright blue eyes peeking over them.
