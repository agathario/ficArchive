Summary

At 32, a single mother of two with a high-powered career, her life was already overflowing with responsibility and carefully constructed order. Love, especially something unexpected, felt like a luxury she didn’t have time for.

Then, at a Christmas party hosted by her best friend Wanda, Rio Vidal walked in. A confident, grounded doctor with a quiet warmth and an easy smile, Rio didn’t just turn Agatha’s head—she challenged everything Agatha thought she knew about herself.

In a single evening, Agatha felt the walls she’d so carefully built begin to crack. And maybe, just maybe, she wasn’t afraid to let someone in.

Agatha sighed deeply, her gaze sweeping over the controlled chaos of her living room.

Toys were scattered across the plush forest green rug, remnants of a morning spent keeping her daughter entertained while trying to tick off an endless to-do list. She could hear the thudding of little feet upstairs—her five-year-old son, Nicholas, was racing around in his underwear, giggling to himself as if it were the best game in the world.

“Nicholas,” she called, exasperation creeping into her voice, “please, for the love of all things festive, get dressed! We need to leave soon.”

A high-pitched, “I’m Spider-Man!” echoed back, accompanied by the sound of him leaping onto his bed.

Agatha pinched the bridge of her nose and sighed again. She loved her children fiercely. They were her world, her joy, her purpose. But that didn’t mean motherhood wasn’t hard—relentlessly, bone-deep hard. Days like this reminded her just how thin she was sometimes stretched, balancing the delicate, precarious dance of being a single mother by choice, while running a company that demanded her sharp intellect and relentless energy.

Her consulting firm wasn’t just successful—it was one of the elite. Harkness Consulting was a name whispered in the halls of Fortune 500 companies and political offices alike. CEOs and dignitaries paid her exorbitant fees for her and her teams expertise and she delivered results every time. Her wealth was the envy of many, her tailored suits a symbol of her power and precision, her confidence unshakable in the boardroom. But no amount of success could make up for the exhaustion she felt in moments like these, wrangling her children alone in a house that felt far too large and quiet after they were tucked into bed.

Agatha glanced down at Storey, her eleven-month-old daughter, who was perched on a playmat surrounded by stuffed animals. Storey was currently gnawing on a plush rabbit, her bright blue eyes—a very similar shade to her mother’s—darting around the room as she babbled happily to herself.

Agatha couldn’t help but smile at her daughter’s innocence, even as she reached down to adjust the baby’s festive red dress for the hundredth time that day.

“Alright, missy,” Agatha murmured, brushing a stray wisp of dark hair from Storey’s face. “You’re ready, at least. That’s one of us.”

She rose to her feet and glanced at the clock. They were supposed to be at Wanda holiday party in less than an hour, and she hadn’t even started getting herself ready. A glance at the large floor length mirror by the door confirmed her suspicions—her usually polished appearance was nowhere to be found. Her dark hair, normally sleek and styled, was tied in a messy curly ponytail, and she was still wearing an old Yale sweatshirt and black leggings stained with carrots from lunchtime.

“Perfect,” she muttered sarcastically.

She padded into the open plan kitchen, grabbing a sip of now-cold coffee from a mug she’d abandoned earlier, her mind racing through the rest of the evening’s logistics.

Wanda’s parties were always fun for the kids. Nicholas adored playing with Wanda’s twin boys, Billy and Tommy, and Agatha couldn’t deny that she enjoyed seeing Wanda, one of the few people who truly understood how much she juggled every day. Still, there was a nagging feeling at the back of her mind—an ache she’d tried to ignore for years.

She was alone.

Yes, she had Nicholas and Storey, and they were her everything. Yes, she had a career that gave her financial independence and the respect of her peers. But there were moments—quiet moments, when the children were asleep and the house was still—when the loneliness crept in like a shadow. She secretly wished she had someone to share the load, someone to laugh with, to lean on, to love. Nicholas had been just a few months old when she adopted him, and Storey had been nothing more than a few hours old when she joined their family. She didn’t have energy or time to date, which left her with beautiful children but no partnership to speak of.

Agatha rarely let herself dwell on it. There was no time for self-pity when there were diapers to change, clients to charm, and PTA meetings to attend. But tonight, as she wrangled her children in preparation for the party, the weight of it all felt heavier than usual.

“Nicholas!” she called again, making her way up the stairs with Storey on her hip to find him. She entered his room to see him standing on his bed, his underwear on his head like a hat, pretending to swing from invisible webs. Despite her frustration, she couldn’t help but laugh.

“Spider-Man, huh?” she said, crossing her arms and leaning against the doorframe.

Nicholas grinned, his dimples flashing. “I’m saving the city!”

“Well, the city’s going to have to wait because we need to get to Auntie Wanda’s party. Come on, bud. Let’s get dressed.”

After much coaxing and a promise that he could wear his favourite Christmas sweater, Nicholas was finally dressed and ready, bounding down the stairs with uncontainable excitement. Agatha, meanwhile, managed to pull herself together in record time, slipping into a chic black tailored suit accented with a festive red scarf. By the time they piled into the car, Agatha felt like she’d run a marathon—and the night hadn’t even begun.

As they pulled away from the house and through the electric gates, Nicholas chattered excitedly about all the fun he was going to have with Billy and Tommy, while Storey babbled happily in her car seat.

Yes, it was hard. Yes, she was tired. But as Agatha drove toward Wanda’s house, her children’s laughter ringing out like music in the car, she reminded herself that it was all worth it. The sacrifices, the late nights, the perpetual exhaustion—none of it mattered when she saw Nicholas’s infectious grin or heard Storey’s delighted babbles. She wouldn’t trade her life for anything.

But there were also plenty of unfamiliar faces—likely Wanda’s colleagues from the hospital, judging by the snatches of conversation about patient rounds and upcoming shifts.

“Some of the other doctors came,” Wanda said softly, as if reading Agatha’s mind. She handed her a glass of mulled wine, a knowing smile on her face. “I thought it’d be nice to mix things up this year. Plus, you never know—maybe you’ll meet someone interesting.”

Agatha gave her a pointed look. “Wanda…”

“What?” Wanda asked innocently, though the mischievous glint in her eyes betrayed her. “You deserve a little fun, Agatha. You’re always working or chasing after the kids. Let someone chase after you for a change.”

Agatha rolled her eyes, though she couldn’t entirely hide her smirk. “We’ll see.”

As the evening unfolded, Agatha found herself moving seamlessly between conversations with the other parents and polite introductions to Wanda’s colleagues. The other mums were warm and welcoming as always, swapping stories about their kids’ latest antics and exchanging recommendations for winter activities. But it was one of the strangers—a striking woman with dark eyes and an easy smile—who caught Agatha’s attention.

She first noticed her standing near the far wall, watching the chaos of the children’s games with an amused expression. There was something about her—an air of quiet confidence, the kind that didn’t demand attention but effortlessly commanded it.

Agatha found herself stealing glances at her as she juggled conversations and kept an eye on her children.

Unaware, at first, that she was also being equally observed, Agatha found herself caught up in the whirlwind of the party, drawn from one conversation to the next by Wanda’s circle of friends. But the subtle prickle of awareness eventually caught her attention.

Agatha was no stranger to the feeling of being watched—whether it came from boardroom stares or passing admiration. Her eyes flicked across the room, sharp and calculating as always, until she spotted her observer.

By the refreshment table, the same striking woman stood, holding a glass of mulled wine. Her dark, intelligent eyes followed Agatha’s movements with an unmistakable spark of curiosity. The woman wasn’t part of Wanda’s usual crowd, Agatha was sure of it. Her poised stance and the thoughtful way she sipped her drink suggested a quiet confidence, and the faint smile playing at her lips hinted at an interest that Agatha couldn’t ignore.

As if sensing the scrutiny, the woman’s gaze met Agatha’s. For a heartbeat, the air seemed to thrum between them, an almost electric pull that caught Agatha off guard. The intensity of it was subtle yet undeniable, and for a moment, Agatha felt rooted to the spot, unable to look away.

The woman’s eyes—dark and thoughtful, with a hint of something playful—held hers just long enough to make Agatha’s pulse quicken. Then, as quickly as it had begun, the moment broke. The woman looked away, her lashes dipping as a faint flush crept up her cheeks.

“Interesting,” Agatha murmured to herself, a small smirk tugging at her lips.

She sipped her wine, letting the warm spices of the drink linger on her tongue as her mind raced. Who was she? A friend of Wanda’s? A colleague? The effortless grace with which she carried herself hinted at intelligence and self-assurance, yet there was a softness to her that intrigued Agatha—a quiet vulnerability that made her want to learn more.

Her gaze lingered for a moment longer, taking in the woman’s relaxed posture as she stood listening to a man that had engaged her in conversation, her fingers lightly cradling a glass of mulled wine. She seemed comfortable in the lively chaos of the party, observing it all with a calm detachment. But Agatha couldn’t shake the feeling that beneath her composed exterior, there was more—something unspoken, waiting to be uncovered.

What would it be like to talk to her? Agatha wondered, leaning against the back of a chair as she watched the woman from across the room. Would her voice match the warmth of her eyes? Would she be one of those people who spoke with an easy, flowing rhythm that made conversation feel effortless, or would there be moments of shy hesitation, fleeting pauses that revealed the layers beneath her polished demeanor?

Agatha allowed herself a brief fantasy, imagining the two of them trading sly remarks and clever quips, their words laced with the kind of quiet humor that felt like a secret shared between kindred spirits. Perhaps the woman would surprise her, meeting her wit with her own sharp tongue, or perhaps she’d be the type who leaned in to listen, her attention unwavering, as if every word mattered.

She shook her head, laughing softly at herself. It wasn’t like her to get so caught up in a stranger—especially not here, in a room full of familiar faces and holiday chaos. And yet, there was something about this woman that called to her, a pull she couldn’t quite explain.

She couldn’t help but wonder—what was it about this stranger that had already captivated her? And, more importantly, would she get chance to speak to her?
