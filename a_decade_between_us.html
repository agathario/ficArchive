Summary

Just another divorced-wives AU.

It’s been ten years since Agatha signed the papers on the life she and Rio were building, convincing herself it was the only way to let Rio chase the career she deserved.

Now they’re in the same room for the first time since the divorce, at a fiftieth birthday party, and Agatha is fighting a war between what she wants, what she’s terrified to admit, and what she still believes everybody else needs.

Agatha arrived exactly on time.

Of course she did. She didn’t show up late to anything, even birthday parties in dimly lit bars that smelled faintly of stale beer, old wood and bad choices.

Alice’s place looked good. It always did. The bar sat low and neat along the left wall, bottles lined up like stained glass. Warm light pooled over the polished timber tables. Someone had gone to the trouble of hanging fairy lights along the exposed beams, a soft halo around the space. 

Jennifer Kale’s fiftieth birthday.

Agatha had celebrated her own fiftieth birthday in bed with a vibrator, a bottle of malbec, and her wedding video, though she hadn’t told anyone about the last part. Her friends had campaigned for months for her to have at least a dinner, but she hadn’t been able to find the fucks to give by the time the date rolled around.

She tugged at her silk blouse, straightening it and trying not to think about the fact that she had picked the most formal thing in her wardrobe for a casual birthday party. Tried not to think about the dress that was still hanging over a chair at home. The one she hadn’t worn because it showed too much cleavage, and because the last time she’d worn it had been their final anniversary dinner. Ten years later she still hadn’t been able to throw it out.

She did one slow sweep of the room. Alice behind the bar cackling at someone’s joke. Lilia perched on a barstool, already halfway through a cocktail and talking animatedly at Wanda and her sons. A cluster of familiar faces near the back.

No sign of Rio.

Something in Agatha’s chest unclenched, and she hated herself for it.

She moved toward the bar, smiling when Alice spotted her.

“Look who decided to grace us with her terrifying presence,” Alice called.

“You keep serving that nice expensive gin and I might even relax,” Agatha said, leaning forward to press a kiss to Alice’s cheek.

Alice pulled back to take her in. “You look good.”

Agatha gave her automatic answer. “I always look good.”

“Sure. Because you bathe in the blood of innocents. Drink?”

“Gin and tonic. Lots of lime, please. I am trying to pretend I am a calm, well adjusted woman in my fifties.”

Alice snorted at that. “I have never seen you calm a day in your life.”

She set the drink down. Agatha curled her fingers around it, grateful she had something to do with her hands.

“She’s not coming, you know,” Alice added, almost casually.

Agatha’s grip tightened by a fraction. “Who?”

Alice raised one eyebrow.

“We talked,” she said. “I told her you were coming. She said she wouldn't be able to be in town. So you can unclench your jaw a little.”

Agatha forced a breath out through her nose. “My jaw is fine.”

“Sure. Your eye did that twitch thing, but you are definitely fine.”

“Alice.”

“I’m just saying. You don’t need to be on edge all night.”

“I am not on edge. I’m here to celebrate Jen and, if I happen to do that by pretending the last ten years were a mature and dignified exercise in growth, so be it.”

Alice’s mouth curved. “You tell yourself whatever you need, honey.” She tipped her head. “Wanda’s over there. She was asking if you had arrived yet. Go say hi.”

Agatha took her drink and let the crowd swallow her.

She spotted Wanda at a long table near the middle of the room, red hair pulled back, eyes bright, and a glass of red wine in one hand. Billy and Tommy sat on either side of her, taller and broader than when she’d last seen them, their childhood written across Agatha’s memory in scraped knees, school concerts and birthdays.

Tommy saw her first.

“Aunty Agatha,” he said, already getting to his feet.

She barely had time to set the glass down before he wrapped her in a hug that lifted her off the ground. She let out a surprised laugh, half choked against his shoulder.

“When did you get so tall,” she said into his shirt.

“Couple months ago,” he said. “Puberty. It’s a whole thing, apparently.”

Billy stepped in next, less dramatic but just as affectionate. He smelled like expensive cologne and the ghost of the little boy who used to climb onto her lap during movie nights.

“You both look too grown up,” Agatha said, pulling back to look them over. “I am filing a complaint.”

“With who,” Billy asked. “Time?”

She made a face and nodded. “Oh absolutely. Stop growing up so fast.”

Wanda stood, smiling, and Agatha felt something soft settle in her chest. Familiar ground, that’s what she needed.

“You look incredible,” Wanda said, kissing her cheek. “I hate you.”

“Please,” Agatha said. “You have not aged since the boys were six. Whatever you are doing, bottle it and I’ll buy it. Heavily discounted, of course.”

“Chasing them and threatening to take their phones apparently keeps me young,” Wanda said. “Sit. What's been happening this week? How is the terrifying world of whatever law you practice?”

“Corporate. You know it’s corporate. And it’s still ruthless, and full of men who still call me ‘dear’ in meetings, which is adorable given that I could end their careers with a single email.” Agatha sat, smoothing her shirt again. “We are in the middle of a huge case that is eating my life, but it’s my name on the door, and I can’t risk a single thing going wrong. I might sleep if I ever get to retire.”

“Well, this is what you wanted,” Wanda said. “Are you happy?”

The question hit Agatha squarely in the chest, her heart knocking against her ribs. She took a sip of her drink to buy time. 

“I like winning,” she said finally. “And I’m good at what I do.”

“That is not what I asked.”

“I know.” She kept her eyes on the condensation on her glass. “I’m fine. That’s close enough.”

Wanda studied her for a second. Agatha could feel it, the stare that had seen her through a decade of breakdowns and bad dates, and the first night alone in an empty townhouse.

“You still thinking about–” Wanda started.

“Don’t,” Agatha said. Her voice was softer than she expected. “It’s Jen’s birthday. It’s been ten years. We’re not dissecting my divorce tonight.”

Wanda tilted her glass slightly in surrender. “Fine. How is your love life, then.”

Fucking nonexistent was the honest answer.

“Still having occasional dates,” Agatha said instead. “No one has tried to murder me, so that’s a step up from my twenties. Nothing has stuck yet, obviously.”

“Because no one is Rio,” Wanda said.

It wasn’t cruel, it was just true. Which somehow hurt more.

Agatha looked at her.

“I made my choice,” she said. “I can’t keep using her as the yardstick for the women unlucky enough to enter my orbit.”

“You left her, Agatha. You were the one that filed for divorce,” Wanda said quietly. 

Agatha’s throat felt tight. She took another sip and forced herself to swallow.

“We’ve done this time and time again over the last ten years, Wanda. I did what I thought was right,” she said. “For her. For her career. For… both of us.”

“And how is that working out for you,” Wanda asked, gently.

Agatha didn’t answer that question.

The music shifted to something jazzy and low, curling around the hum of conversation. Alice turned the lights down a fraction. People clapped near the bar where Jen was glittering in a sequined dress, loudly laughing and hugging everyone in reach.

Agatha let herself watch the scene, let the noise wash over her. Parties had always been easier when she had Rio at her side, one hand at the small of her back, thumb making slow circles when she got too tense. Rio was good with people. She made everything feel less busy, less risky.

Agatha set her jaw. This was why she was glad she didn’t have to navigate tonight with that particular issue in the room. They’d managed ten years of careful avoidance. Shared friends, sure, but separate events, separate dinners, separate lives. She had managed not to see Rio for a decade.

She could manage the echo of her ghost for the night.

Wanda was saying something about her latest translation project when Agatha felt the air in the room shift.

It was subtle, a ripple across the air. Voices dipped and then rose again. Someone laughed loudly. Agatha felt the hair on the back of her neck stand up.

The bar entrance opened behind her. The cold air from the street slipped in first, then confident footsteps. 

Wanda’s gaze flicked over Agatha’s shoulder and she went abruptly quiet.

Agatha didn’t want to turn. Every muscle in her back tightened, like if she stayed perfectly still, reality would rearrange itself and whoever had just walked in would be a stranger, a delivery driver, anyone else.

“Agatha,” Wanda warned softly.

Her voice had changed. There was the tone that Agatha was all too familiar with. The gentle, caring tone that had talked her through tears, through panic, through lonely nights.

Agatha’s fingers clenched around her glass, the condensation slicked under her grip.

“Don’t,” she whispered.

“Agatha,” Wanda repeated. “Look at me.”

She did, and Wanda’s eyes were full of apology.

Then they drifted past her, over her shoulder, and Agatha knew.

Her heart stuttered, then for a second, sound receded. The music, the laughter, the clink of glasses, all of it dropped away into a dull hum.

She turned.

Rio stood near the doorway, a few steps inside, in a particular pool of light that always seemed to find her without effort.

Shoulder length dark hair, a little messier than it used to be, waves pushed back like she’d run her fingers through it on the way in. Muscular, lean, lines of strength under the open collar of her forest green shirt. Black trousers and jacket slung carelessly over one forearm. The same sharp jawline, the same quick mouth. Older, and more settled, more herself.

Agatha felt the impact of Rio like a physical hit.

She looked better. Of course she did. A decade of a life in London, the career and the work she had always deserved. It had settled on her like the final strokes of a painting. There was a little more definition around her eyes, and a line at the corner of her mouth that said she had laughed hard and frowned harder. She looked good.

She also looked stunned.

Her gaze had already found Agatha. Of course it had, it always did. There was a brief second where they just stared at each other across the room.

Ten years collapsed in on them. Late nights in their cramped kitchen. Packing and unpacking suitcases for holidays around the world. The taste of coffee on Rio’s mouth in the morning, the taste of a good red on her lips in the evenings. A signature on divorce papers that had made Agatha’s hand shake.

All of it, at once.

Rio’s fingers flexed against the jacket on her arm. Her shoulders straightened, just a fraction, the old instinct to square up before walking into a difficult room.

Agatha couldn’t move. Her feet were welded to the floor. 

Wanda’s hand found her forearm, a light, anchoring pressure that Agatha barely felt.

Rio blinked first. A small, controlled movement. She dragged her gaze away, let it sweep the room, searching for somewhere else to land. Someone else to look at. She smiled at Alice in a way that didn’t reach her eyes. Alice’s answering expression was tight.

Agatha forced air into her lungs, the breath felt like swallowing glass.

“Thought Alice said she wasn’t coming,” she said. Her voice was much flatter than she had meant it to be.

Wanda squeezed her arm. “Maybe she decided she couldn’t miss Jen’s fiftieth.”

“Of course she couldn’t,” Agatha murmured. She looked back at her drink. The lime slice floated, bobbing against the edge of the ice. “Rio always did know to show up when it counted.”

“How long has it been?” Wanda asked quietly.

Agatha didn’t answer. She could still feel the weight of Rio’s gaze, felt it whisper across her skin.

All the composure she had built up on the walk over, in the mirror, in the weeks since the invitation, felt thin and fragile. Like one wrong word, one wrong move might shatter it.

She took a slow sip of gin and told herself that she was fifty years old, a named partner in a law firm that she managed, a woman who could stand up in court and dismantle entire arguments with a raised eyebrow.

She could survive one night in a room with her ex wife.

She just had to remember how to breathe around the fact that Rio was still the most incredible thing she had ever seen.

Agatha didn’t bolt for the door.

She told herself that counted as a win.

It would’ve been easy. Grab her bag, mumble something about an early meeting weekend in the morning, vanish before anyone could stop her. She could’ve been home in twenty minutes max, sweating, humiliated and alone with a glass of wine big enough to legally count as a bath. 

Instead, she stayed seated. Adjusted the fall of her top, and reached for an olive like she wasn’t seconds away from having a full cardiac event.

“Do you want to leave,” Wanda asked quietly.

Agatha shook her head once. “No.”

“Are you sure?”

“Absolutely not,” Agatha said. “But I’m not giving anyone the satisfaction.”

Wanda’s mouth twitched.

“Do you want me to run interference,” she asked. “Form a human shield. Tell her you died tragically in an accident, maybe a bookshelf fell on you?”

Agatha huffed something half laugh, half sob. “She wouldn’t believe you. I’m too stubborn to die.”

“That’s true,” Wanda said. “You’re a cockroach. In a very expensive blouse.”

“Thank you,” Agatha said. “It was very expensive.”

They both knew she was stalling. That was fine, she could stall.

Across the room, Rio was already being swallowed by a crowd of hugs and greetings. Jen flung her arms around her with a shriek that turned every head. Rio bent to kiss her cheek, smiling her easy, natural smile that always made people drift toward her without thinking.

Agatha watched the way Rio’s hand settled automatically at the small of Jen’s back, steadying the other woman when her heel caught on nothing.

That used to be Agatha’s hand to feel. 

Billy and Tommy had clocked Rio now. They shared matching looks, and peeled away from the table.

Agatha’s stomach dropped, and she sighed.

“Let them,” Wanda murmured. “They adore her.”

Agatha knew that. Rio had helped raise them too. Wanda’s boyfriend had bolted the second he heard the word pregnant, and for the first three years of the twins’ lives, Wanda, Rio and Agatha had all lived under the same roof, tag-teaming night feeds and scraped knees their own little makeshift family. 

Knowing all of that didn’t make watching them any easier.

The boys reached Rio just as she came out of another hug. Tommy launched himself at her like a human missile. Rio staggered, laughed, and pulled him in, pressing her face to his shoulder like she’d been waiting years for that moment.

Billy stepped in next, quieter, but his smile was wide, soft, and entirely unguarded.

For a second, the three of them were back in her living room, tucked in blanket forts with bad cartoons on pizza nights. Rio’s hand in Tommy’s hair. Billy saying something that made her throw her head back and laugh from her chest.

Agatha felt like someone had driven a fist straight through her ribs.

Rio had been there for the fevers, school plays and late-night homework crises. She’d watched Rio teach them to throw a baseball in the park, watched Wanda fall apart in her kitchen while Rio fed the boys ice cream and Agatha poured wine.

And then Rio had missed years of their lives. A whole decade.

You did that, she reminded herself. You filed. You signed the papers. You shut her out. You chose.

It didn’t make her ache smaller.

“Hey,” Wanda said quietly. “Hey.”

Agatha realized her hand was shaking. She set her glass down before she shattered it.

“I’m fine,” she said.

“You look like you’re watching your own funeral.”

“In a sense,” Agatha said. “A little bit.”

Wanda brushed her thumb against Agatha’s knuckles. The same gesture they had done for each other more times than she could count.

“Bathroom escape?” Wanda offered. “You can hide there till cake.”

Agatha almost said yes.

“I can’t,” she said instead. “If I’m hiding in the bathroom when she comes over, you’ll tell her, and she’ll know I ran.”

“True,” Wanda said. “You’ve always been deeply committed to the illusion that nothing fazes you.”

“Exactly.”

As the room slowly came back into focus, the music shifted to something low and brass-heavy as the laughter from a make-shift dancefloor filtered across the room.

Agatha lifted her chin. She could still feel the ghost of Rio’s gaze on her skin.

Fine. If they were playing avoidance, she could play that.

For a while, it worked.

People drifted in and out of the group at their table. Jen’s art friends, and old acquaintances. A couple of women who’d known her and Rio back when they used to host dinner parties with mismatched chairs and food that took hours longer than expected.

Agatha did what she did best, pleasant deflection. Curated anecdotes, and sharp quips. She laughed when it was socially required. She asked about other people’s kids, careers, vacations. She even told a story about a cross-examination that had the whole table laughing.

She didn’t think about how Rio used to kiss her jaw after a story like that and call her terrifying and gorgeous in the same breath.

Rio stuck to the opposite side of the room. Agatha could feel her, she would always feel her. She didn’t look directly, she refused to do that. She caught tiny glimpses, reflected in glassware, in the bar mirror, in the way eyes drifted past her shoulder.

A flashed jawline. The tilt of Rio’s mouth when she laughed. The way she pushed her hair back with two fingers, like she had always done.

It was absurd, how her body responded, like she’d been carved with a map of this woman and ten years hadn’t erased a damn thing.

Someone popped champagne that sprayed across a nearby table. Shrieked laughter followed. Alice shouted threats from behind the bar before Jen launched into a tipsy toast about survival, friendship, and turning fifty without being arrested.

Agatha raised her glass with everyone else. She didn’t look across the room. Not even once. She was absurdly proud of that.

“Old habits die hard,” Wanda muttered when the noise dipped again.

“What habit?”

“Your battlefield awareness,” Wanda said. “You’re refusing to look at her, but you’ve flinched every time she’s come within ten feet.”

“I don’t flinch,” Agatha said.

Wanda arched a brow. “You also don’t lie well, honey, but we can pretend.”

Agatha was gearing up with a retort when Jen swept in, drink sloshing against the slide of her glass.

“Is this table reserved for the people who remember VHS tapes?” Jen asked. 

“You’re the birthday girl,” Wanda said. “You can sit wherever you want.”

Jen flopped down across from Agatha, glitter flaring under the lights.

“I feel like I should make a speech at every table,” she said. “Personally bless each of you. Remind you how you traumatized me.”

“Please don’t,” Agatha said. “I’ve suppressed enough.”

Jen laughed, then tilted her head.

“You good?” she asked, voice dropping.

Agatha knew what she meant, everyone knew what she meant.

“I’m fine,” Agatha said. “Happy birthday, hon. You look like a disco ball.”

“Thank you,” Jen said, completely sincere. She squeezed Agatha’s wrist once, hard, like she was grounding her.

Before Agatha could reply, Billy reappeared, flushed and excited.

“Rio’s here,” he announced, as if this was breaking news.

“Obviously she’s here, darling,” Wanda said. “We’re all seeing the same room.”

Billy ignored her and looked straight at Agatha.

“She said London’s cold and full of boring people.”

“Sounds accurate,” Agatha said, aiming for casual and landing somewhere between brittle and homicidal.

“She asked about you,” Tommy said, plopping down with a plate of sliders. “We told her you still yell at drivers.”

“I don’t yell,” Agatha said. “I make constructive observations.”

“From your car,” Tommy said. “At strangers.”

“They can still benefit.”

Wanda snorted wine out her nose.

“What exactly did she ask,” Jen said. “Give me dialogue, boys. I crave accuracy.”

Billy frowned, concentrating. “She said: ‘Have you spoken to your aunty Agatha yet,’ and when we said yes, she did that thing with her face.”

“What thing,” Wanda asked.

“You know,” Billy said. “The like-someone-punched-me thing.”

Tommy nodded. “She looked like she might throw up.”

Agatha glared at Wanda and Jen when they both looked at her.

“I refuse to listen to the whimsical tales of children,” she said.

“We’re eighteen,” Billy protested.

“You’re idiots,” Agatha said. “It’s a developmental stage. You’ll grow out of it in your thirties.”

Tommy grinned. “Rio said the same thing.”

Agatha froze for a second. Something hot twisted under her ribs.

Of course Rio did.

Before she could decide whether to laugh or swallow her entire drink, a shadow fell over the table.

“Is there space here for one more over here?” Rio asked.

Her voice hit Agatha like a match to gasoline.

Agatha looked up.

Rio stood there, close enough that Agatha could see the faint scar near her eyebrow, the hint of her collarbone under undone buttons. Her eyes flicked over the table, then stopped on Agatha.

“Or is this the cool-kids section,” Rio added, mouth tilting.

“Birthday exemption for you, dork,” Jen said. “Sit.”

She tapped the empty chair beside Agatha.

Of course that was the empty chair. Of course.

Agatha considered flipping the table, then she considered climbing through a window and fleeing into the night, never to be seen again.

Instead, she didn’t move. She sat perfectly still, hands steady around her glass, even though her pulse was battering against her throat.

Rio hesitated, just slightly, then pulled out the chair and sat.

Her thigh brushed Agatha’s. Barely contact, barely anything. 

Agatha’s nerves lit up like someone had plugged her straight into an outlet.

“Boo,” Rio said softly, leaning in, just for her.

Agatha turned her head. It was too close, too familiar. She could see every tiny shift in Rio’s face, the laugh lines, the exhaustion, the history. She smelled like bergamot, she smelled like a home Agatha had been trying to forget for a long time.

“Hi,” Agatha said.

The word felt foreign.

They didn’t look away from each other. They couldn’t look away.

Then Jen clapped her hands. “Perfect timing. We were just talking earlier about the champagne incident where Rio almost blinded Lilia.”

“I didn’t almost blind anyone,” Rio said, dragging her gaze away. “The railing was unstable.”

“It was cement,” Lilia shouted.

“And it was unstable,” Rio insisted.

Laughter rolled around the table. Stories began to overlap. Someone eventually brought up the karaoke disaster that saw Islands in the Stream butchered by a very drunk Wanda and Rio, each fighting over Kenny’s part before sobbing their way through the key change. Tommy reenacted Rio showing up to a school parent’s or ‘special friends’ night when he was seven with briefing notes sticking out of her pocket for his teacher.

For everyone else, it was easy.

For Agatha, it felt like standing on the edge of a building, pretending the fall didn’t look tempting.

She did her part, she laughed along, and then corrected Tommy when he exaggerated something.

“That’s not what happened,” she said. “I never threatened to sue the entire restaurant.”

Rio’s mouth twitched, a ghost of amusement.

“How did it happen then,” Wanda asked.

“I told the manager that if he kept double-booking tables, I’d take my business elsewhere. I got really annoyed when they seemed to know how to take the reservation, but not how to keep the reservation,” Agatha said. “Then he cried. Not my fault.”

“You made a grown man cry,” Tommy said, delighted.

“He was twenty-three. And he was flirting with my wife—”

The word slipped. Silence dropped hard on the table.

Agatha stared at her glass, heartbeat loud in her ears.

Beside her, Rio inhaled sharply, devastatingly.

“Now ex-wife,” Agatha said, forcing the correction. “Obviously.”

Jen saved her with an overly loud story and the table snapped back into motion, noise filling the space again.

A few minutes later, Rio shifted a little closer, her voice dropping so only Agatha could hear.

“Agatha.”

Agatha didn’t look at her. She stared into her glass, mostly ice left. “Yes, Rio.”

A small pause. Rio exhaled once through her nose, quietly.

“You okay?”

“I’m fine,” Agatha said. “It’s loud.”

Rio nodded like she didn’t believe a word. Another beat passed.

“You know,” she murmured, “I didn’t mean to surprise you tonight.”

Agatha’s eyes flicked sharply to her. “No?”

“No.” Rio’s fingers tapped against the table, a nervous tic she’d never managed to unlearn. “I had to be here for work last minute, and my flight was booked earlier in the week. I just… decided that if I was in the city, I may as well make my way here.”

Agatha kept her face neutral. “How very impulsive of you.”

Rio huffed a quiet laugh. “You used to call it ‘chaotic.’”

“I stand by it.”

Rio’s mouth tipped at the corner. “I haven’t seen you in ten years.”

Agatha swallowed, her throat feeling raw. “I know.”

“I suppose—” Another soft breath from Rio. “Maybe I thought that if I didn’t come, I might not get another chance to see you again.”

Agatha’s pulse kicked hard. She kept her hands still. “I don’t know what that means, Rio.”

Rio didn’t look away. Her voice stayed low and steady. “It means ten years is a really long time to spend walking into rooms like this one, wondering if I’ll see you.”

Agatha’s breath stuttered, but she didn’t let it show. She couldn't let it show. 

Rio went on, gentler. “I’m not trying to pick a fight,” she said. “Not tonight.”

“I don’t want to fight either,” Agatha managed.

“Good,” Rio said. “That’s… good.”

Someone laughed loudly near the bar. The room blurred at the edges. The frame of the world shrank back down to the inch of space between their chairs.

Rio’s gaze dipped to Agatha’s mouth for half a second, barely a flicker, but enough to send heat straight through her. 

Agatha inhaled. She drew air in, all the way to her toes.

Then she set her shoulders, let the breath go slowly, and looked back toward the rest of the table like she hadn’t just been yanked ten years into her past.

Rio didn’t say anything. Her hand rested on the table, fingers tapping a slow, distracted rhythm. Every few beats, they brushed the back of Agatha’s hand.

Barely contact. Less than a touch. It didn’t matter.

Agatha’s whole body reacted like she’d been waiting ten years for exactly that nothing.

And she let it happen.

Minutes later, Jen was accusing Rio of abandoning Boston for a city with worse weather and arguably worse pay, and Rio shrugged, mouth crooking in the familiar, uneven way that always used to precede trouble.

“What can I say,” she replied. “No one in London yells at me nearly enough. Really makes you miss home.”

Her eyes flicked to Agatha as she said it.

The stupid grin landed first. The stupid look, second.

Agatha felt something in her chest lurch, sharp and unwelcome. The world narrowed around the curve of Rio’s mouth, the tiny scar at the corner she knew the history of, the way the light caught on her teeth.

For a second, the bar blurred. The noise dropped away. She could hear her heartbeat in her ears.

Her drink was in her hand. The condensation still on her fingers.

Then there was the memory of coffee. Iced, sticky, and running down her thigh.

The café was too loud and too busy. That was Agatha’s first thought.

Her second was that the tables were far too close together to constitute safe occupational practice.

Her third was that the iced coffee currently soaking into the expensive wool of her charcoal slacks was an active hate crime. Quite possibly a war crime.

“Oh my god. Shit. I am so sorry.”

The voice was low, rough at the edges, and far too close.

Cold seeped through the wool and hit her skin. Agatha inhaled sharply.

She looked down first. Brown spreading over gray. Ice cubes skittering across the floor.

Then she looked up. The woman in front of her held an empty cup, straw bent. Her other hand hovered uselessly.

Shoulder-length dark hair shoved back and a crooked button-up stretched over broad shoulders. Forearms dusted with fine hair, ink peeking out from a cuff. Bright brown eyes, mortified and too warm.

And the audacity to be annoyingly attractive.

“Are you alright?” she asked.

“No,” Agatha said, crisp. “I am not.”

The woman flinched. “Right. Fair. Yep. I am so, so sorry. I was trying to sit down without spilling and somehow I did the exact opposite of that.”

“You threw your coffee at me,” Agatha said. 

A couple at the next table tried not to laugh. The barista glanced over, decided this was above their pay grade, and disappeared behind the machine.

Rio didn’t. Rio was still watching her. Agatha felt it like a hand closing around her ribs.

Rio’s eyes flicked down her face, lingering at her mouth before lifting again. “I meant what I said.”

“Don’t,” Rio said quietly. 

Agatha shut her eyes.

Someone bumped into her shoulder and muttered an apology. Laughter rose somewhere near the jukebox. Jen began yelling about giving speeches.

Life continued and the party rolled on.

But the air between them stayed taut and fragile.

Agatha opened her eyes and met Rio’s.

Then Wanda appeared at Agatha’s elbow, thrusting a plate of cake into her hands with enough force to jolt her spine.

“Eat sugar,” Wanda said. “It’ll stop you from passing out.”

Agatha blinked at her. “I’m not going to pass out.”

“You’re two more emotions away from collapse,” Wanda said. “Eat the cake.”

Rio choked out a laugh.

Agatha looked at her, Rio looked back. The next fault line cracked open.

Jen spotted her quickly.

Of course she did. Jen had always had sniper-level accuracy when it came to Agatha’s moments of emotional instability. She beamed, waved a fork like a wand, and shouted across the room.

“Agatha Harkness, my terrifying queen, come here and say something meaningful before I pass out into my own cake.”

The room laughed. Agatha wanted to lie down on the floor.

“Absolutely not,” Agatha called back, but she was already being nudged forward by Wanda and physically herded by the twins like they were moving a skittish racehorse toward the starting line.

“I could ruin your life with a single sentence,” Agatha murmured.

Jen grinned. “I know, how exciting. That’s why I asked you.”

Alice snorted into her wine.

Someone shoved a half-empty champagne flute into Agatha’s hand. It wasn’t hers, but it didn’t matter. The room settled, expectant.

Agatha exhaled, straightened, and lifted her chin.

Then she found Rio across the crowd. She didn’t mean to look for her, except she absolutely did, she always meant to look for her. Rio’s eyes met hers instantly. 

Agatha looked away before she drowned in it, and raised her glass.

“Well,” she started dryly, “if I must.”

Laughter rippled.

“Jennifer Kale,” Agatha continued, pausing long enough for Jen to bow dramatically, “is the only person I know who could turn a fiftieth birthday into a cross between a bacchanal, a séance, and a public health hazard.”

More laughter, Lilia whooped as Jen curtsied.

“But,” Agatha said, the word softening as she continued, “she is also one of the most fiercely loyal, infuriatingly supportive and relentlessly kind people I have ever had the privilege of knowing.”

Jen made a dramatic gasp and clutched her chest.

Agatha rolled her eyes. “Please don’t make this sentimental. You’ll ruin my reputation.”

The crowd laughed again. She let it quiet slightly, then continued, her voice slipping into something gentler.

“You have been in my life longer than some small countries have existed. You’ve talked me through professional disasters, personal catastrophes, and at least three questionable hairstyles. You’ve fed me, yelled at me, made me laugh when I didn’t want to, and dragged me back into the world every time I tried to retreat from it.”

She glanced, deliberately, toward Rio.

For one heartbeat, they were the only two people in the room.

Then Agatha looked back to Jen, perfectly controlled.

“And you’ve created this family,” Agatha said. “This ridiculous, wonderful network of misfits who somehow all ended up loving each other. You’ve kept us together. Even when some of us made that nearly impossible.”

She felt more than saw Rio flicker. A tight line in her jaw as it clenched.

Agatha continued, lighter again. “So here’s to you, Jenny Kale. A menace, a marvel, and a woman who refuses to age appropriately.”

The room erupted. Jen lunged forward and crushed Agatha in a hug that nearly toppled them both.

Agatha laughed against her shoulder, muffled and unwilling to enjoy how grounding it felt.

“You sap,” Jen whispered in her ear. “You big soft marshmallow.”

“Fuck off and die,” Agatha muttered affectionately.

Jen released her, wiping at her eyes dramatically.

“Alright,” Jen announced to the crowd. “Someone get this woman another drink!”

Agatha stepped back, smoothing her blouse, catching her breath.

When she looked up, Rio was still watching her.

Always watching her.

Wanda slid an arm through Agatha’s just long enough to whisper, “That was beautiful.”

Agatha swallowed. “I need a very strong drink.”

Wanda nodded. “Yeah. You really, really do.”

And the party moved on.

But she could feel Rio’s gaze like a warm hand pressed between her shoulder blades, steady and unrelenting.

Agatha looked up immediately. “Baby, what’s going on?”

Rio stopped a few feet away. Hands stuffed into her pockets. Eyes avoiding hers.

“Agatha,” she said, just her name, voice thin around the edges. “I–there’s something I need to tell you.”

A cold, instinctive current ran down Agatha’s spine.

She set her papers down. “Okay, so tell me.”

Rio exhaled, bracing herself. “I got an offer.”

Silence. Agatha waited for the rest, nodding slowly.

Rio swallowed. “A job offer. In London. A… a big one. Research director in a climate policy think tank. It’s sort of… everything I’ve ever worked for.”

Everything.

The word echoed, dull and heavy.

Agatha kept her face neutral. “Okay, when did they approach you?”

Rio hesitated, that hesitation was the fracture. A small, nearly invisible crack, but Agatha felt the tremor of it all the way through her ribs.

“I, uh–” Rio looked down. “I applied, actually. A while ago. A few months ago.”

The world did not fall apart, it simply… flattened into a dull gray.

An old and familiar numbness swept through Agatha, smooth and absolute, like something in her chest executed a controlled shutdown.

“You applied months ago,” Agatha repeated, flatly, her own voice sounding unfamiliar.

Rio winced. “Sweetheart, I should have told you. I know I should have. I just– I didn’t want to disappoint you. I didn’t think anything would happen. And then when they reached out, I panicked. I didn’t know how to say it without making it sound like I was choosing–”

“Rio, are you taking it?” Agatha cut in with a surgical calmness.

Rio’s eyes snapped up, startled by the tone. “I… I don’t know yet. No. We have a life here. I haven’t even responded. I wanted to talk to you first.”

Agatha nodded, mechanically, body on autopilot.  

This was the moment she’d always feared, Rio being offered the life she deserved. The one Agatha was certain she would eventually resent her for not having.

The thought bloomed like poison.

“Okay,” Agatha said quietly.

Rio took a step toward her, hand outstretched. “Honey, wait, can we just–”

Agatha stepped back. Not far, just enough to put distance between them.

Rio froze.

Agatha forced a breath. “Thank you for telling me,” she said, polite enough. “I appreciate your honesty.”

Rio’s face crumpled like tissue paper. “Agatha, please don't shut down. Don’t go somewhere I can’t reach you.”

But Agatha was already gone. Inside, at least.

The switch had flipped. The armor was sealed.

And Rio, in her devastating sincerity, noticed. Her voice broke. “Talk to me, baby. Please.”

“I’m thinking,” Agatha said.

Lie. She wasn’t thinking.

Rio’s voice was a threadbare whisper. “I don’t want to lose you.”

Something inside Agatha twisted sharply, then folded in on itself. She kept her expression still, steady and unbearably calm.

Rio tried again. “We can try distance,” she said desperately. “London isn’t Antarctica.”

“Sure,” Agatha heard herself say. “That sounds good.”

Rio blinked. Hope flickered across her face so naked and bright that it made Agatha’s stomach drop.

“You’d really consider that?” Rio asked, stunned.

Agatha nodded. Her head moved through the lie easily, far too easily. 

Because the truth was she would consider anything if it meant Rio didn’t look at her like that. If it meant delaying the moment Rio realised she should choose the life she deserved.

“Yes,” Agatha said. “Of course.”

Rio exhaled shakily. She stepped forward, hands gentle on Agatha’s waist, searching her face like she wanted to commit every freckle, every piece to memory.

“God,” Rio murmured, voice breaking on the word. “You’re… I don’t even know what I did to deserve you.”

The numbness inside Agatha pulsed hard when Rio kissed her.

Not their usual kiss. Not hungry or teasing or confident.

A soft, careful kiss. Slow enough that Agatha felt her own heartbeat stutter against it.

It hurt.

Rio cupped her cheek, thumb grazing the skin just under her eye, and pressed another kiss to her mouth, even softer than the first.

Agatha kissed her back, automatically, and just as gently.

She wondered if Rio could taste the panic she was swallowing.

When Rio finally pulled back, she rested their foreheads together for a breath.

“We’ll figure it out,” Rio whispered. “You and me. We always do.”

Agatha closed her eyes and said nothing.

Wanda showed up a few days later unannounced.

Agatha had just gotten home from work, jacket still over her arm, hair pinned back but escaping at the edges. The living room was dim except for one lamp. She had not turned on the overhead. The big light was only ever to be touched in extreme emergencies.

Wanda opened the door and let herself in, without waiting for a greeting.

“Agatha, what the fuck are you doing?” she said. No preamble. No warm-up. Absolutely no foreplay. “You’re shutting down.”

Agatha inhaled slowly. “Hello to you too, dear. So long between visits?”

Wanda crossed her arms and stared at her with a level, razor-sharp calm. “Cut the bullshit. She told me about the job.”

Agatha froze, just slightly. Of course Wanda saw it.

“Agatha,” she said softly. “Talk to me.”

“There is nothing to talk about,” Agatha replied. “It is a good opportunity for her. It’s an incredible opportunity for her.”

Wanda’s mouth tightened. “And what does that have to do with you acting all withdrawn and like a ghost in your own home?”

Agatha walked past her. Put her jacket on the back of the couch. Poured herself a healthy glass of wine she wouldn’t drink. Wanda followed.

“Agatha, you love her,” Wanda said quietly.

Agatha swallowed. The wine trembled in her glass. “Loving someone is not the same as being good for them.”

“Oh for fuck sake,” Wanda snapped. “This again.”

Agatha didn’t rise to it.

Wanda stepped closer, voice gentler. “Agatha. Look at me.”

Agatha did not look at her.

Wanda tried another angle. “What did she say when she told you?”

“That she didn’t want to lose me,” Agatha said. Her voice was even. “That she loves me. That she wants to try.”

Wanda’s eyes softened. “And you said yes?”

“Yes.”

“And now?”

Agatha set the water down. She stared at her hands. Fixated on the gold of her wedding band and simple, emerald engagement ring. “She deserves someone who doesn’t hold her back.”

Wanda blinked. “Agatha. You don’t hold her back. She would choose you. She would choose this life. She has always chosen you.”

“That is the problem,” Agatha whispered. “She shouldn’t.”

Wanda stared at her like she had slapped her. “You cannot be fucking serious.”

Agatha kept going. “She applied months ago. She didn’t tell me. She hesitated because of me. My presence is already shaping her decisions. What happens when she starts turning things down. Missing opportunities. Regretting staying. Waking up one morning and realizing I was the one holding her back.”

“You are not holding her back,” Wanda said. “You’re her home.”

“Some people drown in homes,” Agatha said.

Wanda’s breath caught.

Agatha kept talking, her voice detached. “She is brilliant. She’s kind. Staggeringly capable. She will thrive in London. She can make real change. And I will not be the reason she looks back in ten years and wonders what she could have been.”

Wanda felt her throat close. “Agatha... don’t shut her out. Don’t make a choice for her, or for you, don’t do something that you can’t undo.”

Agatha didn’t answer.

Wanda reached for her wrist, gentle but firm. “If you want her, fight for her.”

Agatha’s eyes flicked up, finally. They were tired and empty, distanced.

“She deserves someone better, she has always deserved someone better” Agatha said quietly. “I’m not worth the trouble.”

Wanda’s breath went unsteady. “What if you look back in ten years and regret it?”

Agatha stepped back. 

“There’s nothing else to do, Wanda,” she said. “She’ll be happier without me, in the long run. I’m giving her the freedom she deserves.”

“That is not freedom,” Wanda whispered. “That is you once again martyring yourself for no reason other than furthering your own misery.”

Agatha’s face didn’t move.

Wanda closed her eyes for a long moment. When she opened them, she looked at her best friend of twenty years and saw someone slipping underwater.

“Please,” Wanda said. “You will regret it.”

In Agatha’s silence, the decision was already made.

The switch flipped quietly. Not in anger, or in fear.

In something colder and deeper, buried so far inside her it lived beneath her ribs, in the old, hidden insecurity she’d never outrun.

A clean, terrible numbness.

The next morning, she sat at her desk at the firm, eyes unfocused on a contract she could have drafted in her sleep. The city hummed beyond the glass. She no longer felt part of it.

By ten, she had her assistant pull the contact information for a family law attorney.

No one questioned why.

She met the lawyer that afternoon in a small conference room with beige walls and a box of tissues strategically positioned at the table’s center. The lawyer looked kind, which made everything much worse.

She went through the steps clinically, as if she were drafting someone else’s case. The date of marriage, “irreconcilable differences” as grounds, no-fault divorce petition.

She asked to keep the house and offered full buyout of Rio’s share. She declined requests for mediation or counseling. She signed the preliminary documents with a hand that trembled.

The lawyer paused. “Are you sure you want to proceed?”

Agatha nodded. 

Two days later, she received the finalized filing draft on her desk.

Her signature went at the bottom.

Her hand shook so violently she had to steady her wrist with her other hand to make the line of her name legible.

Her phone buzzed at that exact moment.

A text from Rio lit up the screen.

Can I grab you on your lunch break? I miss you. We can meet at our cafe?

Agatha stared at the message until the words blurred.

Then she turned the phone face down.

She didn’t reply. Her phone glowed again.

Rio: Baby, everything ok? Want me to pick you up after work?

Agatha sat perfectly still.

That night, Rio got home a little later than usual.

Agatha heard the key in the lock at 6:42 p.m. She knew the exact time because she had been staring at the clock on the wall for the thirteen minutes since she’d got home, listening to the second hand mark out every choice she was about to make.

The townhouse was too tidy. The cushions were aligned. The coffee table was bare except for the neat stack of paperwork in the center and the coaster under her glass of water. The lamp in the corner cast a warm circle of light that made everything look gentler than it was.

She sat on the couch, perfectly straight, hands folded in her lap. If she didn’t move, if she stayed very still, she could pretend she was preparing for court. That this was just another argument with a clear outcome and a clean exit.

The door opened.

“Hey, sweet,” Rio called, voice bright and a little tired. “Boys made it home alive. Billy beat me at basketball again. I think you are rubbing off on him. He heckles me now.”

Her bag hit the usual spot by the door. Keys in the tray. Jacket over the chair. The familiar rhythm of the life they had built over a decade.

Agatha’s heart pounded.

Rio walked into the living room, loosening the top button of her shirt with one hand. She stopped dead when she saw Agatha on the couch.

Something in her face shifted. The easy smile faded, replaced by wary curiosity.

“Hi,” she said quietly.

Agatha swallowed, but she couldn’t speak. 

Rio’s gaze dropped to the coffee table.

The stack of documents sat there, precisely aligned. There was a pen placed on top. Rio stared at it for a second too long.

Her eyes flicked back up. “What’s this?”

Agatha felt her spine lock. “Sit down, Rio.”

Rio didn’t move. “Agatha.”

“Sit, love,” Agatha repeated. “Please.”

The please came out wrong, raw around the edges. Rio heard it, her shoulders rose and fell as she took a slow breath, then she walked over and sat at the far end of the couch, leaving a polite, unfamiliar space between them.

Up close, Agatha could see the faint creases at the corners of her eyes from long days. The way her collar was slightly crooked. The tiny smudge of ink on her thumb.

Home, she thought. And then, viciously, she boxed the word up and shoved it somewhere deep.

“What’s going on?” Rio asked. Her voice was careful now. “You’ve been quiet for a couple of weeks. Did something happen at work?”

Agatha shook her head once. “This isn’t about work.”

“Then what?” Rio asked. Her eyes flicked to the papers again. “Because I’m getting a little freaked out by the murder-energy and the paperwork.”

Agatha reached for the stack. 

“I’ve been speaking to a lawyer,” she said.

Rio’s face went still.

Agatha kept her gaze on the papers. It was easier than looking at the woman she loved. “I think it is time we stop pretending. I think we should try to finalize a divorce before you leave.”

Silence shattered through the room.

For a moment, Rio genuinely looked like she had not understood the sentence, brows pulling together in confusion. Her brain seemed to reject it. Then her cheeks went a frightening sort of colorless, all the warmth draining at once.

“Pretending,” she repeated slowly. “Pretending what?”

Agatha forced herself to meet her eyes. It felt like peeling off her skin.

“Pretending this still works, or it will work,” she said. Her tone was clinical, perfect. “You have a new job in London. A whole career. It doesn’t make sense to drag this out or complicate matters. We should make a clean break before you move.”

Rio blinked. Once. Twice. Her hands flexed on her knees like she couldn’t quite figure out where to put them.

“What do you mean pretending this still works, Agatha?” she asked. 

Agatha swallowed. The numbness rose.

“It’s the logical choice,” she said. 

Rio laughed. It was a small, disbelieving sound that held no humor at all. “Logical,” she echoed. “You’re talking about logic. You said we could make it work. That we’d try the distance. We just talked about this a few weeks ago.”

“And I reevaluated,” Agatha said. “It's not feasible. We would be clinging to something that's already ending. Finalizing everything now will be easier for both of us.”

The words tasted like poison. She pushed through them anyway.

Rio stared at her like she was trying to see past the mask. “Stop, love,” she said quietly. “Don’t talk to me like I am a client. What is this really? What’s going on?”

“I told you,” Agatha said. “I spoke to a lawyer. The paperwork is ready. My offer is to buy out your share of the house. We can divide the rest of the assets fairly. Split the accounts. There’s no need to fight over any of it.”

“No need to fight,” Rio repeated. “You’ve got divorce papers in front of me, but no need to fight.”

Rio’s fingers curled into fists. “Don’t do this,” she said. Her voice wavered on the last word. “Agatha, please. What did I do? Tell me what I did. What do I need to do to fix it?”

“You did nothing, and there’s nothing you can do,” Agatha said. She was proud of how evenly it came out. “That’s the point.”

Rio flinched like she’d been slapped. “Don’t say that. Do not act like this just happened naturally. You know that’s not true. You don’t get to decide it is over.”

Agatha kept her eyes on the coffee table. On the date printed in the top corner of the form. On the neat black ink of her own name where it should never have been.

“Rio, I am trying to make this as simple as possible,” she said. “You leave for London, very soon, I might add, with nothing tying you here. No unfinished business.”

“I don’t want that,” Rio said. Her voice was rising now, panic scraping at the edges. “Baby, I’m not choosing a job over you. I would never do that.”

“You already did,” Agatha said.

The lie slid out smooth and devastating.

Rio stared at her. “What are you even talking about?”

“You applied without telling me,” Agatha said. “You got the offer and hesitated, not because you didn’t want it, but because of me. You’ve already been twisting yourself in knots around my existence. This way, you won’t have to.”

Rio shook her head, hard. “You can’t be fucking serious. I didn’t tell you because I wanted to consider it, think about it before I scared you. I hesitated because I didn’t want to hurt you. Agatha, you are my whole world. I want to be where you are. Please.”

“Regardless,” Agatha said. “This is happening.”

Rio’s breath hitched. “You’re lying,” she said. There was no venom in it, just raw, stunned hurt. “I know you’re lying. Look at me. Look at me and say you don’t love me. Tell me that this is what you want, Agatha.”

Agatha’s gaze dropped to her own hands.

They weren’t folded anymore. Her nails were digging crescents into her palms. Tear tracks shone faintly along the sides of her fingers where she hadn’t even felt them fall.

She closed her fists.

“Love isn’t the issue,” she managed. “Compatibility is. I want this, Rio.”

Rio let out a sound that was almost a laugh and almost a sob. “You are ending our marriage over something as insignificant as this? Agatha, we can work through this.”

Her chair scraped quietly as she pushed herself forward. She moved down the couch, closing the distance Agatha had built like a barricade, until she was directly in front of her.

Then she slid off the cushion and onto her knees on the rug.

“Baby,” she said, and this time her voice truly broke.

Agatha’s whole body wanted to reach for her. To grab her face in both hands and say I’m sorry, I’m wrong, stay, go, I’ll follow you, of course we will try, we should always try. 

She didn’t move.

Rio’s hands hovered just above her knees, fingers trembling like she was afraid to touch her and more afraid not to.

“Please,” Rio whispered. Tears were spilling freely, tracking down her cheeks, catching on her jaw. “Please don’t do this to us. We can figure it out, we always do. We can try and see how it feels. I’ll fly back every month, every weekend if I have to, I don’t care. We’ll find a way.”

Agatha stared at the top of Rio’s head.

She could see the familiar part in her hair, the faint silver beginning near her temple, the place where Agatha’s fingers always landed first when she was tired and wanted comfort without asking.

Her own vision blurred.

She pressed her tongue hard against the roof of her mouth to stop anything from shaking loose.

“Rio,” she said. It came out flat, like a stranger using her wife’s name.

Rio lifted her head. Her eyes were red and wet and blazing with desperate hope.

“What am I going to do, please,” she asked. The words tumbled out, unplanned, ruined. “What am I supposed to do without you?”

Agatha couldn't answer.

Because if she spoke, even once, she would take it all back.

She would say stay, I love you, don’t go, I am terrified, and I don’t know how to live without you either.

So she looked at the wall behind Rio instead and let the silence stack up between them like bricks.

It was the worst thing she had ever done.

The cruelty wasn’t in any insult or raised voice, it was in the absence. In the withdrawal of every soft thing she had ever given Rio. In the deliberate, careful withholding of comfort, because she had convinced herself that ripping the bandage off would heal cleaner.

After a long, shaking moment, Rio’s shoulders slumped.

“Okay,” she whispered. “Okay, sweetheart. I understand.”

She pushed herself up from her knees, unsteady. Her hands brushed her thighs, wiping away tears in a motion that was nearly angry.

She stared down at the paperwork on the table for a second, then at Agatha.

“You want the house,” she said. Her voice had gone quiet and hoarse. “Fine. Take it. Take anything you want. It is just walls. They’re just things.”

Agatha’s throat burned.

Rio’s mouth twisted. “I’ll sign whatever you want, Agatha. I’ll give you whatever you want. You know that. You have always known that.”

She walked to the door on legs that weren’t entirely solid. At the console table, she grabbed her keys with fingers that fumbled twice before closing.

“I’m going to go to Alice’s,” she said. 

Agatha didn’t tell her not to. She didn’t say let me drive you. She didn’t say stay and we’ll talk when we’re calmer, we’ll figure it out.

She sat very still and watched the love of her life put her jacket on with shaking hands.

Rio paused with her fingers on the doorknob.

Without turning around, she said softly, “For what it is worth, I have never once regretted choosing you.”

Agatha’s eyes slammed shut.

The door opened.

Then it closed, gently, and the townhouse fell silent.

She reached for her left hand.

Her rings dug into her finger, a familiar, comforting weight.

Agatha slid them off.

Her skin felt naked without her engagement ring and wedding band.

She set them on top of the papers, stared for a long, furious moment, then picked them back up and closed her fist around them until the metal bit into her palm.

Eventually, she stood, turned off the lamp, and went upstairs to a bed that suddenly felt too big, too cold, too quiet.

She lay on her side, eyes open, staring at the space where Rio should have been.

She had wanted to save Rio from resentment, from regret, from the weight of a life she thought she didn’t deserve.

Instead, she had given her the one thing she had sworn never to cause another person.

Abandonment.

Agatha didn’t cry. She stared into the dark and listened to the echo of Rio’s voice in the hallway.

What am I supposed to do without you.

She didn’t have an answer.

She just knew she would wake up in this house the next morning and continue to pretend, for everyone else’s sake, that this had been the logical choice.

Six months later, Agatha was colder.

Not visibly, and not in ways anyone would comment on. Her suits were still immaculate, her posture perfect, her voice controlled. She showed up, she delivered, she excelled.

But she didn’t laugh, not really.

The loss of Rio had settled in her bones like winter, like ice threaded through marrow. A quiet, permanent ache, a constant stillness that felt like frostbite.

And the world went on.

Emails. Court filings. Meetings that blurred into one another. Wanda checking on her too often. Jen dropping off food she claimed Alice “accidentally” made too much of. The twins hovering with an anxious, adolescent protectiveness that made Agatha both want to hug them and flee the continent.

Agatha stayed busy. She was fine.

Across the ocean, Rio stood in a tiny rented flat with peeling wallpaper. Half-unpacked boxes stacked in corners. A small kitchen she loathed. A narrow bed she never quite slept through the night in.

Her work badge hung on a hook by the door. She’d smiled in the photo, forced and tired. It hurt to look at.

The work was good, it was the kind of work she’d always dreamed of doing.

But some evenings she would sit on the edge of her bed, hair scraped messily back, forehead pressed to her palms, breathing like she was holding herself together by the thinnest thread.

Every once in a while, her thumb brushed the empty place on her ring finger.

On her last day in Boston, before her flight, she had walked to the townhouse. Stood at the foot of the porch steps for a full minute, unable to go further.

Then she’d taken out a small unmarked envelope and slipped it into the mailbox slot. 

Inside sat her wedding ring.

She didn’t include a note. Words felt useless.

She flew out that afternoon.

Agatha knew what it was without needing to open it.

She had taken it upstairs. Walked into her study. Opened the bottom drawer of her desk, the one she never used, and placed the envelope at the very back next to the box where her own rings sat.

She closed the drawer.

The announcement came in late spring, a year after their divorce had finalized.

Harkness, Greene & Meyer LLP.

A new plaque on the door. A bouquet from the board. Champagne in the conference room. Everyone congratulated her.

Agatha smiled exactly the right amount. Accepted the handshake from the senior partners. Gave the expected speech. Went back to her office and drafted two motions in silence.

Precision had always been her armor. But now it was also her companion.

She had stopped being updated about Rio somewhere around month four. Wanda had tried with gentle mentions, small check-ins, murmured updates about London, but Agatha had looked at her with such bare, frigid finality that Wanda never brought it up again.

She didn’t cry.

So the next fault line cracked open.

It started small.

Agatha tried to blend back into the edges of the party, the way she always did when she had met her social and emotional quota for the night. She handed Wanda back her untouched plate of cake, ignored the searching look she got in return, and slipped sideways toward a darker strip of wall near the end of the bar.

Music swelled as the DJ changed the track to something faster. People started to dance again. Jen had abandoned all semblance of composure and was now feeding Alice cake with her fingers.

It was chaos.

Agatha flattened herself against the wall and tried to pretend she was part of the architecture.

It might have worked if Rio had not followed.

Again, she felt her before she saw her. A proximity shift. The air nudging around her. She tried to pretend she didn’t, took a sip of the wine she didn’t want and focused on a framed print on the wall that she remembered Alice losing her mind over when she’d won it at auction.

“Agatha.”

Rio’s voice found the space between her vertebrae.

Agatha closed her eyes for half a second, then opened them again. “You really do make an excellent shadow, my love.”

Rio huffed out a humorless breath. “And you really do work fast in those heels.”

Agatha turned her head.

Rio stood a step away, hands in her pockets, shoulders set. Her expression was controlled in the way Agatha recognised from old fights. The way she used to look right before she said something she had been holding back for days.

“This is nothing,” Agatha said lightly. “I’ve seen you chase down a bus in boots. Barefoot, even.”

“Don’t do that,” Rio said.

“Do what.”

“Make jokes to dodge this.”

Agatha’s jaw clenched. “I’m not dodging anything. I’m here, at Jen’s party, doing my best impression of composed ambivalence.”

“You’re not doing it well, and you’re shaking,” Rio said.

Agatha’s fingers tightened around the stem of her glass. “I’m cold.”

“It’s seventy degrees in here,” Rio said. “You’re not cold.”

Agatha snorted. “God, I forgot how annoying you are when you act all high and mighty, and think you know me.”

Rio’s eyes flashed in frustration. “I don’t think I know you. I do know you.”

They stared at each other, the noise of the party a dull roar around them.

Agatha broke first. She pushed away from the wall. “I should find Wanda. Or Jen. Or literally anyone else.”

Rio stepped into her path.

“Move,” Agatha said.

“No,” Rio replied.

“Rio.”

“Agatha.”

Their eyes locked. It was a stalemate.

“You can’t keep running from me every time you think it’s going to hurt,” Rio said quietly.

Agatha laughed once, hollow. “You think this is about you.”

“I know it is about us,” Rio said. “You’ve been storming away from me all night like you’re running from fire.”

“You followed me,” Agatha said. 

“Yes,” Rio said. “I followed you. Because I am done losing you. I’m sick of it. I’m not willing to do it anymore.”

Something in Agatha snapped at that. It twisted hot in her chest.

“You didn’t lose me silently,” she said.

Rio flinched. “You filed for divorce. Not me.”

“I remember,” Agatha said. “But thank you for the reminder, hon.”

“You decided unilaterally that our marriage was over,” Rio said, voice rising for the first time. “You decided it was done and then you wouldn’t even discuss it with me. That was it. You signed the papers and you shut every door in my face. You just threw me away, Agatha.”

Agatha’s throat closed. “Don’t overdramatize it, I didn’t throw you away.”

“The hell you didn’t,” Rio shot back. “I begged you. I tried to talk to you for days, weeks, afterwards, and then you fucking blocked me.”

A couple brushed past them, laughing and oblivious. The lights felt too bright, too hot on Agatha’s skin.

Agatha set her glass down on a nearby ledge. Her hand was trembling. She balled it into a fist.

“I thought I was protecting you,” she said. “From being tethered to someone who would hold you back.”

Rio stared at her like she had grown a second head. “You weren’t protecting me. You were protecting yourself.”

Agatha’s laugh came out sharp. “Of course I was trying to protect myself. Have you met you? Have you seen what you are capable of? I watched you get offered the life you always wanted, one you applied for, Rio, and I knew if I asked you to stay, you would either hate me, resent me or both. I couldn’t handle either of those things.”

“You didn’t ask me,” Rio said. Her voice was louder now, cutting through the noise. “That’s the point, Agatha. You. Did. Not. Ask. You didn’t give me the chance to talk to you about it. You didn’t even say, ‘I want you to stay.’ You just decided for me.”

“Oh please, I wasn’t going to beg you, it's beneath me,” Agatha snapped. “I couldn’t stand there and watch you pick London over me.”

Rio stepped closer. “You didn’t even give me the chance to pick you.”

Agatha stepped back. Her back hit the wall. “You chose London the moment you applied for the job.”

Rio followed, hands still deep in her pockets like she was physically restraining herself.

“They asked me to apply, I never thought I’d get it. I never thought I’d go without talking to you first. I had only just been told when I came to speak to you. You think I wanted that job more than I wanted you? They practically drafted me, Agatha. My boss, he pushed me so hard.
