Chapter 1

Dance therapy was Rio’s last option.

Actually, not even that—more like a desperate shot in the dark. Nothing else was working, so when she came across this dance-therapy promising emotional and physical healing, expression, connection, listening to your body… well, it sounded like exactly what she needed.

Even if it also sounded terrifying.

It had been two weeks since she started, and so far it was going… okay? Maybe. Kind of.

She still struggled to express herself in front of others, even though the group was small—just six people, including her. As a 37-year-old woman, Rio found it deeply embarrassing to move in front of strangers. Especially when she felt like every part of her was awkward, heavy, offbeat.

And yet, here she was again.

Walking to the studio in leggings and a white sweatshirt, gym bag slung over her shoulder. As soon as she stepped inside, everyone turned to look.

Awkward.

“Oh! Hello, Rio!”

Wanda, the therapist—and a relentlessly sunny woman—beamed at her.

Rio offered a tight, polite smile. “Hi.”

“Come on in! We’re just about to start.”

She dropped her bag in the corner and quietly joined the others in the center of the room. A quick, subtle nod to the group. Arms crossed in front of her chest. Shrinking.

Wanda clapped her hands once. “So! Today we’re doing something a little different.”

People shifted, curious.

“We’re working in pairs!” she said, with a grin that made Rio’s stomach twist.

Dancing was already hard enough alone. Now she had to do it with someone else?

“Can we choose our partners?” Jacob asked, grinning like he was in summer camp.

“Well,” Wanda said, walking slowly across the floor, “I have an idea for that. Since there are six of you, let’s split into two groups—three on the right side of the studio, three on the left. Those on the right will choose someone from the left to be their partner. Sound good?”

A mix of excited and hesitant sounds filled the room.

Wanda smiled, like this was going exactly as planned. “Great! Take a minute and choose your side.”

After a bit of shuffling, Rio ended up on the left. Which meant she’d be picked. Or not.

Neither option felt good.

Across from her were Jacob (too eager), Benjamin (who looked perpetually lost), and… Agatha.

Agatha wasn’t looking at anyone. She stared at the floor like it held a secret only she could hear. She fidgeted with her fingers, a nervous storm wrapped in stillness.

Rio had been trying to figure her out since day one. Agatha wasn’t mysterious in some poetic way—she was tired. Not broken, but dimmed. Like a flame still burning, but barely.

Rio had once overheard her talking to Wanda. Words like “abuse” and “pain” Things Rio recognized.

She’s not going to pick me, Rio thought, that familiar ache of unworthiness curling in her chest.

Why would she?

“Alright,” Wanda said gently. “Those on the right can choose who they feel drawn to.”

Jacob darted forward to his friend, Beatrice, all enthusiasm. Benjamin hesitated, then wandered over to Rose.

Agatha didn’t move.

She was the last one on the right side. All eyes were gently waiting.

“Take your time, Agatha,” Wanda said softly.

Agatha slowly looked up. Her gaze moved across the room, checking the pairs. And then—

She met Rio’s eyes.

Only for a second.

But it landed deep.

Agatha wasn’t going to move any sooner. And that was okay.

Rio’s breath caught. Something about that look—raw, uncertain, real—pulled her in. Without letting herself think too much, she stepped forward. One, two, three small steps.

And now she stood in front of Agatha.

Agatha blinked, surprised.

“That was beautiful, Rio,” Wanda said, her voice full of warmth. “Really beautiful.”

She clapped her hands gently. “Now, take a few minutes to connect with your partner. This part matters.”

Around them, quiet conversations began. Rio stayed still, her eyes still on Agatha. And Agatha looked right back.

“Hi,” Agatha said softly.

Rio extended her hand. “Hey.”

Agatha hesitated but reached out and shook it.

Then she glanced toward Wanda. Wanda met her gaze, smiled, and gave her a thumbs-up, like a quiet nod of encouragement.

Agatha turned back. “Are you… okay?”

Rio hesitated, then nodded. “Yeah. I’m fine.”

They both knew that wasn’t true. No one came to therapy like this because everything was fine. But Agatha didn’t push. She just gave her a small, understanding smile.

When Rio opened her mouth to say something more, Wanda’s voice returned.

“Alright, everyone. Are we ready to begin?”

“But we don’t know what we’re doing,” Rose said, raising a hand.

“You’re absolutely right,” Wanda replied. “That’s why I’ll guide you.”

She walked to the center. “This isn’t about touching. It’s not about doing it right. This is about feeling. Staying present with yourself—and noticing the other person beside you.”

A few people nodded. Someone took a shaky breath.

“If anything feels uncomfortable, that’s okay. You can break eye contact. You’re in control of your own body. There’s no failure here.”

Rio looked at Agatha and noticed how tense she was. Her shoulders high, fingers restless.

“Are you ready?” Wanda asked the room.

Everyone nodded, or something close to it.

“You and your partner can pick a corner where you feel safe dancing. Go ahead.”

Rio glanced at Agatha. “Where do you want to go?”

Agatha pointed to a quiet corner near her bag. “There.”

Rio nodded. “Okay. Let’s go.”

They walked together, quiet.

Once everyone had found a place, Wanda began again.

“Feel your feet on the ground. The weight of your breath. Let it settle you.”

The room grew calm.

“Now, gently raise your arms above your head. Feel the stretch in your chest, your shoulders.”

They moved slowly. The quiet grew thicker, like mist.

They stretched and released for five long minutes.

And then—Wanda’s voice again.

“Our first activity: Emotional reflection.”

Rio raised an eyebrow and looked at Agatha, who looked deeply uncertain.

“In this activity,” Wanda said, “you’ll begin to move with one core emotion. Whatever you’re feeling most. Let it guide you. Let it live in your body. Your partner will mirror you. Not copy—mirror. They’ll witness. Respond.”

Rio swallowed hard. Her chest already ached.

Let herself be seen? Even just a little?

Agatha looked at her, and for a moment, they both stood there—two women with too many invisible wounds, about to speak without words.

Rio took a shaky breath and let her arms fall loosely to her sides. She focused on the tightness curling low in her stomach—an ache she’d carried for years. She allowed her body to sag just a little, slow and heavy.

Agatha’s eyes softened as she watched. Then, almost instinctively, she lifted her own arms—slowly, carefully—mirroring Rio’s weight and hesitation, but adding a delicate tremble, like she was testing the air.

Rio noticed. Her chest tightened again—but in a different way. Not fear. Something closer to hope.

She shifted slightly, letting her movements grow a bit freer, swaying like a fragile wave.

Agatha responded with the same gentle rhythm, their bodies slowly syncing—not perfectly, but honestly.

Neither spoke. Neither tried to fix or control.

They simply moved, sharing their invisible wounds in silence.

Wanda circled quietly around the room, her smile warm and encouraging, reminding them all that this was a space for honesty, not performance.

Rio’s breath trembled, her gaze flickering to Agatha’s.

Then, unexpectedly, Agatha whispered, “Is that sadness?”

Rio blinked.

She hadn’t expected words.

She didn’t even know how to answer.

But she nodded.

Agatha exhaled like she’d been holding that breath for years.

Then, without breaking eye contact, she mirrored Rio’s movement again—arms curling gently in and out, like tides pulling in something tender.

They kept dancing, but now… something had shifted.

They weren’t just mirroring anymore.

They were listening.

Agatha’s eyes didn’t leave Rio’s.

But something in them had changed.

The stillness she’d carried—tense and guarded—began to crack, just slightly. Her mouth parted like she might say something, but nothing came out. Instead, she moved again—barely. A slow, shaky gesture: her hand lifted toward her chest and then curled into a fist, pressing lightly against her sternum.

A gesture of pain.

Of something locked in.

Rio mirrored her—not exactly, but in essence. She moved a little closer, her posture soft, her breath calm.

Agatha’s movements grew disjointed, less like mirroring and more like leaking. Her hands fluttered, stopped. Her shoulders lifted, like she wanted to shrink away.

And then—

Her whole body trembled once.

Not violently, not dramatic. Just… enough.

Her arms wrapped around herself, and she turned slightly—not away from Rio, but inward, as if trying to hold something together that had been crumbling for too long.

Rio didn’t reach for her. She didn’t move forward.

Instead, she mirrored the rhythm of Agatha’s breath—slow, steady, anchored.

A tear slipped down Agatha’s cheek.

She didn’t wipe it away.

Rio exhaled through her nose and let herself settle into stillness, staying present—not watching, but witnessing.

Around them, the room was silent.

And then, from the center of the studio, Wanda’s voice returned—soft, gentle, like a warm blanket:

“Now slowly… let your body return to stillness. Notice your breath. Your heartbeat. The space between you.”

Rio stayed still, eyes on Agatha.

Agatha didn’t look back right away. But after a moment, she straightened, wiped her cheek with the back of her hand, and finally met Rio’s eyes again.

There was no smile. No “I’m fine.”

Just a flicker of something Rio recognized instantly:

Trust.

And it was enough.

They stared at each other for a while until Wanda broke all the thoughts in their own heads.

“Great job, everyone! I could feel everything by just walking next to you. The next activity is Trust Walk.”

Huh

Wanda started to walk around the room once again. “This one is more delicate, so it’s ok if you’re not ready for it.”

Everyone was quiet, waiting for the instructions.

“One partner will close their eyes and the other will guide them through the space. You can use gentle touch or verbal cues to guide your partner, being respectful of their boundaries of course. After a few minutes, switch the roles.”

Rio actually thought this idea was really interesting. It can help with self-reflection and reflecting others as well.

“So, are we ready?”

Everyone nodded slowly, except Agatha, who was now staring at the floor just like when session started.

Rio noticed that she was nervous. “I can start if you want.” she said with a small smile.

Agatha didn’t answer right away. “Yes.” was all she said.

“Okay, I’ll close my eyes then.” Rio closed her eyes and waited for Agatha’s guidance.

Rio felt Agatha move behind her, but Agatha hadn’t made any more moves.

The silence stretched, long and heavy, like something caught between them.

Rio kept her eyes closed, trying not to tense up. She waited… and waited.

Nothing.

So she spoke, voice soft but steady.

“It’s okay, Agatha. You can touch me.”

Another beat of silence.

Then a breath—not Rio’s. A shaky one. Agatha’s.

Still, no hands came. Just the warmth of her standing close, just out of reach.

Rio tilted her head slightly, smiling without opening her eyes.

“I trust you.”

Rio didn’t know where that came from. They have never spoken until today but it felt so right to say. So right to feel.

Agatha didn’t answer. But Rio could feel the hesitation, thick in the air between them. Like Agatha was standing on the edge of a cliff, unsure if she should jump.

Then—the faintest touch.

Fingertips brushed Rio’s shoulders like a question.

Rio didn’t move, didn’t flinch. She stayed still, and closed her eyes again.

Agatha's hands settled, finally—one on each shoulder. Light, like they might vanish again at any second.

Only then did they begin to move.

Agatha stepped forward.

Her hands gave the tiniest nudge, and Rio followed, blind but calm. She let herself be led, one foot after another, the world quiet except for the shuffle of their steps and the hum of nerves under her skin.

Agatha guided her around something—maybe a chair? Rio wasn’t sure. She felt the soft pressure of one hand lifting slightly, and she turned left. It was clumsy, a little offbeat, but Agatha adjusted immediately, steadying her with nothing but the curve of her palms.

And through it all—not a word.

But it didn’t feel cold. It felt like a secret. Like something important was being said in silence.

Rio smiled faintly.

“You’re doing good,” she whispered.

Agatha didn’t respond, but her hands got just a little more confident after that.

By the time they stopped, Rio’s pulse had slowed. She felt grounded. Held. She didn’t open her eyes right away—she just stood there, letting Agatha’s presence wrap around her like warmth from a fire she hadn’t realized she needed.

And Agatha?

She let go so gently it was like she was still there.

Wanda clapped her hands. “That was majestic! You all did a fantastic job.”

Rio smiled as she looked at Agatha—who felt tense but light at the same time.

“Now it’s time to switch. Who was guiding now will be guided and who was being guided will now guide.”

Agatha froze. Rio could see that she wasn’t even breathing normally anymore.

“Hey, Agatha?” she asked with a worried look.

Agatha was keeping her eyes buried on the floor.

Rio didn’t want to cross any boundaries. She didn’t touch her.

But she took one small step closer, enough for her voice to reach softer.

“You don’t have to do anything you’re not ready for. Okay?”

Agatha didn’t answer. Her shoulders were stiff, hands clenched loosely by her sides. Still staring at the floor like it might open up and swallow her whole.

Wanda’s voice floated again, light and encouraging.

“Take your time. Remember, this isn’t about doing it right, it’s about it’s about feeling safe.”

Rio looked at Agatha, really looked. And for just a second, she saw past the face Agatha always wore like armor.

She was scared.

So Rio took a breath.

“You’re safe. I promise.”

That made Agatha’s head lift a little—just a flicker. A glance that barely touched Rio’s face, but it was enough.

“I thought I was safe once,” she whispered but Rio almost couldn’t hear it.

That made Rio’s body freeze.

“It’s okay. We can just stay here without doing anything, yeah?” Rio smiled to Agatha.

Agatha sniffed and nodded slowly.

They stayed like that until the exercise ended. No steps taken. No dance begun.

But Rio knew something had shifted.

Not forward, maybe. But inward.

And that was something.

After the other pairs were done with the activity, Wanda spoke.

“That’s it for today! I hope you enjoyed this activity. If you did, next session will also be in pairs—but we’ll start to go a little deeper… always respecting everyone’s boundaries, of course.”

Agatha was already slipping out the door when Rio moved—instinct, maybe. Or hope.

“Agatha!” she called, just loud enough to stop her.

Agatha turned around.

Rio didn’t rush. She just smiled—small, sincere. “I liked being paired with you.”

For a beat, Agatha said nothing.

And then—she smiled.

Not wide. Not showy. But real. Soft around the edges, like something thawing.

“I had fun today,” she said. “More than I’ve had in years.”

Then she turned and walked away, the door clicking gently shut behind her.

Rio stood there for a moment longer.

Still.

Holding that smile like a fragile secret in her chest.

And for the first time in those two weeks, there was presence where there used to be silence.

Rio didn’t work—not because she was lazy, but because she didn’t have to. Her parents had left her more than enough. Her mom, a respected doctor. Her dad, a high-powered lawyer. What a combo, right?

Her mom was the first to go. She got sick—first a stroke landed her in the hospital. Just when it seemed she might get better, a hospital infection took her away.

That shattered their family, especially her dad. A year later, he couldn’t carry the weight anymore and took his own life.

Rio was 20 when everything happened.

Since then, time had stretched itself into something shapeless. Some days she’d wake up and it felt like years had passed. Others, like none at all.

She had the money. The house. The silence. Everything that looked like safety—until you lived inside it.

Grief doesn’t care how big the bed is.

Most days, Rio lived small. Quiet routines. Morning coffee. Long walks. Watering plants she didn’t remember buying.

The world outside moved fast. She didn’t.

People asked if she worked, and she never quite knew how to answer.

“No,” she’d say, politely. “I’m not working right now.”

And that was the truth. Just not all of it.

Rio has a friend—Alice, who was there for her since they both were 18. When her parents died, Alice and her family were really supportive. They invited Rio to live with them for as long as she needed but Rio politely declined the offer—she needed time for herself.

What hurt her the most was that her dad seemed to have gotten over her mom’s passing. Everything seemed fine. He seemed fine.

For a while, Rio was angry at him.

She thought he had moved on too quickly. He laughed at jokes again. He went back to work. He made breakfast like nothing had changed.

She wanted to scream at him. How are you fine? How are you not destroyed?

But grief has its tricks. It wears different masks.

He wasn’t fine. He was just quiet about it. And by the time Rio realized that, it was already too late.

She stopped trying to make sense of it after a while.

Grief doesn’t follow logic. Neither did healing.

That was then.

Now?

She was just trying to feel something again.

When Rio was finally ready to be in a relationship—which was two years later her parents death—she got into a toxic one. She didn’t know it was toxic at first. Of course not. It didn’t look like toxicity. It looked like Elena—sharp smile, fast talk, the kind of presence that made you feel like the world had been boring until she walked in.

They met at a bookstore event. Elena was standing at the back, leaning against a shelf like the whole place was her living room. She made some sarcastic comment about the author’s metaphors, and Rio laughed—the only one who did. That was the beginning.

Elena was everything Rio wasn’t. Loud. Reckless. Charming in the way that made waitresses blush and strangers overshare. She’d drag Rio into late-night drives, into spontaneous plans, into rooms full of people and noise. For a while, it felt good. Like living again. Like being shaken awake from grief’s long, dull sleep.

But then it started.

Little things, at first.

Elena never seemed to have her wallet. She’d laugh it off—“God, I’m the worst, can you get this one?” She’d borrow Rio’s car because hers “was in the shop,” but it always seemed to be in the shop. She said things like “You’re so lucky you don’t have to worry about money,” with a soft bitterness that made Rio feel guilty for every comfort she’d inherited.

Eventually, Elena stopped asking. She just expected. Groceries. Rent. A new phone. “It’s not a big deal, babe—you’re not even gonna feel it.”

Rio didn’t care about the money. That wasn’t the point.

It was how Elena made her feel when she gave. Like she was only worth loving if she was useful. Like she was lucky someone as bright as Elena even chose her.

And then came the control.

Subtle, at first. She’d make fun of Rio’s clothes. Say her music taste was boring. Roll her eyes every time Alice texted. She once said, “I don’t know why you still talk to her so much. You act like she’s your girlfriend.” It was half-joke, half-accusation. And it stuck.

Rio started pulling back. From Alice. From everyone. She thought maybe that’s what being in love meant—choosing one person so fully that the rest of the world faded.

But love wasn’t supposed to feel like erasing yourself.

By the end, Rio barely recognized her own routines. She stopped listening to her dad’s old records. Stopped watering her plants. Stopped laughing. Elena filled the space in her house and hollowed it out at the same time.

She didn’t leave because of one big fight. There wasn’t yelling. Just a slow erosion.

One night, Elena left to “grab drinks with a friend,” and Rio sat alone in the quiet. For the first time in a long time, she let the stillness settle instead of chasing it away. She put on a record. Ella Fitzgerald.

And something broke.

Not loud. Not dramatic. Just a quiet realization that she didn’t want this kind of love. Not anymore.

Rio wrote a letter. Short. Not angry. Just done.

She left it on the kitchen table. Changed the locks. Blocked the number. Called Alice the next morning and asked if she could come over.

Alice just said, “I’m already making coffee.”

It took months to feel like herself again—if she ever really did. But slowly, she rebuilt. Not everything. Just the small things. Like Wednesday lunch with Alice.

Wednesdays were her favorite. Not because of the day, but because they meant two hours with someone who made her feel like a person again.

Alice ran the most talked-about tattoo shop in town, and her schedule was usually packed—appointments lined up weeks in advance. But every Wednesday, from noon to two, was reserved just for them. No exceptions.

Each week, one of them chose where to go. Last Wednesday, it was KFC, because Alice had been craving fried chicken like it was a lifeline. True craving, no shame.

This week, the plan was different. They were meeting at Ça va, a “new” French bistro that had opened just three weeks ago.

Rio was already inside, waiting, wrapped in a soft sage-green sweater and flared jeans—feeling the chill of the day seep in from outside. Her thoughts drifted, as they often did lately, to Agatha—the woman who hadn’t left her mind since their session the day before.

She was the first person besides Wanda that Agatha had spoken to in therapy—at least since she’d started it.

Technically, they have only spoken to each other because of the pairing thing but that meant something to Rio and it meant something for Agatha too.

The bell above the door chimed, snapping her out of the thoughts.

Alice breezed in, all bold eyeliner and windblown hair, her leather jacket half zipped and cheeks pink from the cold. She spotted Rio and lit up instantly.

“Hey, RiRi,” she said, heading straight to the table. “Sorry I’m late. A guy wanted a phoenix rising out of a skull and his ex’s name covered up.”

Rio chuckled softly. “You’re not late. I was just… thinking.”

Alice arched a brow as she shrugged off her jacket and sank into the chair across from her. “About therapy girl?”

Rio didn’t answer right away. She just looked down at her hands, then nodded. “I don’t know what it is. She barely talked. But…”

“But it’s something.” Alice finished, not teasing—just understanding. “You feel it.”

Rio nodded again. “Yes, I do.”

Alice smiled, reaching for the menu. “Alright, let’s eat something absurdly french and maybe get you a glass of wine. You look like your soul needs to warm up.”

Rio gave her a small smile. “Only if you’re having one too.”

Alice smiled. “Oh, honey. I’m two sips ahead of you in my mind already.”

They ordered quickly—Alice choosing something with goat cheese because she liked the name, and Rio picking the daily special without asking what it was.

“I’m gonna assume anything with ‘du jour’ in the name tastes good,” Alice said, leaning back as the waiter walked away.

“It just means ‘of the day,’” Rio replied, taking a sip of her wine.

“Well then. I’m a cultured woman,” Alice said, deadpan. “Next stop, Paris.”

The conversation shifted to random topics—someone from high school who got married in Vegas, a terrible tattoo trend Alice hoped never came back, whether or not Ça va was worth a second visit.

By the time they left, Rio felt steadier. Not fixed. But less tangled.

Outside, the cold bit at their faces. Alice lit a cigarette and offered Rio one, knowing she’d say no.

“You okay?” she asked again, eyes on the street.

Rio pulled her sweater sleeves down over her hands. “Getting there.”

“Good. Just… don’t disappear into someone else’s mess, okay?”

Rio looked at her. “I won’t.”

Alice nodded. “Alright. Same time next week?”

“Always.”

They parted at the corner, Alice disappearing into the blur of traffic and chill, Rio standing for a moment longer, hands deep in her pockets, thinking about a woman with too many walls and eyes full of weight.

And then she started walking.

Rio was on her way to the grocery store. Just a few things—milk, fruit, something for dinner. Since she was already out, she figured she might as well get it over with.

She was a block away when she saw someone up ahead—familiar shape, familiar stillness.

Agatha.

Dark gray sweater, black beanie pulled low, cigarette in one hand and grocery bags in the other. Her head was slightly down. She moved quickly, like she didn’t want to be noticed. Maybe she didn’t.

Rio barely caught a glimpse of her face before she turned the corner.

There wasn’t time to wave. Not even time to decide if she should.

And just like that, Agatha was gone again.

After buying everything she needed—and some unnecessary stuff as well, Rio went back home.

Home was quiet, as always.

Rio dropped her keys in the bowl by the door, kicked her shoes off without looking. The grocery bags ended up on the kitchen counter, untouched. She’d deal with them later.

She walked straight to the living room and sat down on the couch—not collapsed, not curled up, just… sat.

The silence wasn’t heavy. But it wasn’t light either.

She leaned her head back, eyes on the ceiling. Not thinking, but also not not-thinking.

Recently, Rio had been remembering her parents too much.

She remembered one night, a week after her mom’s passing, waking up to find the kitchen light on. Her dad was standing by the sink, eating cold lasagna straight from the dish. “Didn’t feel like using a plate,” he’d said, not looking at her. And she hadn’t asked anything else. Just stood there with him for a moment. Two ghosts in the same house.

She also remembered that every morning, no matter how rushed, her mom took five minutes to sit with a cup of tea by the window, watching the world wake up. Rio wished she could’ve been brave enough to ask what she was thinking about in those moments.

Rio accepted the fact that those memories—good or bad will always come back. And that’s okay.

A new day had finally begun, and there was Rio—back at the studio.

She’d arrived earlier than usual, just in case.

(Just in case Agatha did too.)

When she walked in, the space was quiet, almost too quiet. Wanda stood by the mirror wall, sipping tea from a thermos and adjusting a speaker. Something about the silence made it feel heavier than Tuesday.

“You’re early!” Wanda called out, smiling. Then paused. Narrowed her eyes.

“You’re early?”

The second time wasn’t a greeting. It was a diagnosis.

Rio gave a sheepish nod. “Yup. Here I am.”

Wanda leaned back against the mirror. “Everything okay?”

“Uhum,” Rio said too fast, then cleared her throat. “Just… waiting.”

“For what?”

Rio shrugged like a teenager caught snooping in a kitchen drawer. “Nothing. Really.”

Wanda gave her a long, quiet look. Then let it go with a soft smile. “Alright. You don’t have to tell me now. But if you ever want to… I’m right here.”

“Thanks,” Rio whispered.

She slipped into her usual corner—she liked corners. Something about them made her feel held. Like she could pretend the rest of the room didn’t exist.

A few minutes later, Jacob and Benjamin arrived, laughing about something dumb and hand-gesture-filled. They greeted Wanda, threw Rio a wave. Then came Rose, headphones in, thumbs flying over her screen. She didn’t look up, but gave Rio a faint nod. That counted.

Rio was, of course, the oldest in the group. Older than Wanda, even. And sometimes that felt like armor. Other times, like glass.

Then Beatrice came in.

Still no Agatha.

Wanda stepped into the center of the room and clapped once. Her lips parted to speak—

The door opened.

Agatha walked in.

She looked… the same. But she wasn’t. Not really.

It was in the way she stood—shoulders drawn up tight, jaw clenched, hands balled into fists at her sides. Like her body was bracing for impact. Like showing up had cost her something.

She didn’t look around the room. She didn’t need to.

Rio felt it the moment their eyes met.

She didn’t want to be here.

But she was.

Agatha sat on the floor, away from everyone else. Not dramatically far. But far enough.

“Hello, Agatha!” Wanda chirped.
