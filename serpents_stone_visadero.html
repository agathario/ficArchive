Summary

Agatha was thrice blessed: by Demeter, by prophecy, by birth into power.

Thrice cursed: caged, controlled, claimed by a mother who saw only her value.

Then Rio came: an alpha who should have been beneath her notice, who smelled like earth and honey.

When their hands first touched, the vision tore through her. Sunlight through olive leaves. Bodies tangled in sheets. A tongue that split and tasted acrid. Dark eyes hollowed out into nothingness.

She saw what loving Rio would cost.

For one night, Agatha chose freedom.

The gods made her pay for it.

Chapter 1: Prologue

Before her world narrowed to the dark and cold echoes against blood-dampened stone, there was the seaside. 

She remembered that.

The sky bright and beautiful. The tides tickling her feet before pulling back in search of more trinkets to deliver to her. 

When she shook out her hair, salt would fall from it, showering down around her.

It was like being surrounded by a field of falling stars (she remembered those, too).

Only after she spent hours baking under the sun would she return home, running barefoot across tile that had grown hot during the day.

And always she would end up wishing she had stayed with her feet in the water a little longer.

At the water’s edge, she wasn’t alone. The gulls were always there with her, screaming as they fought over scraps the fishermen left behind.

In those days, omens were still just stories. The birds and winds spoke of other people's tragedies. 

She could still run home afterward, salt-crowned and sun-drunk, believing she would always be separate from the sorrows she witnessed.

Agatha was twelve the first time she understood that they were trying to tell her something.

That morning, she'd been searching for sea glass when three dark masked gulls flew inland, their wings beating against the wind's direction. 

Something felt different as she watched the trio disappear toward the city, a cool finger of dread trailing down her spine despite the sun wrapped around her shoulders.

By evening, the harbormaster's son had drowned.

But the uneasy feeling didn’t abate, even when they pulled his body from the nets and placed to lay in state, back in bed in his mother’s home where he belonged.

Misfortune always came in threes. The next death came two days later. 

A fisherman's wife, collapsed in the agora, lemons tumbling out of her carefully woven basket.

Then a child, taken by the fever in the night. Just the day before, Agatha had watched her dance with a ribbon on the theatre steps.

Agatha said nothing. What was there to say? That she'd seen birds flying the wrong way? That her skin had prickled with knowing? 

She was already barely tolerated, if not actively disliked. Would they call her mad? Worse would be if they called her cursed, a bearer of misfortune rather than its witness.

So she kept the knowledge tightly folded inside herself.

She returned to the shore each morning, but now she watched the gulls with a different hunger. 

No longer looking for shells or smooth stones, but for patterns.

Next time…. Next time, she would know better what to do. Perhaps in this way, if mortals could never be pleased, she would at least find the favor of a sympathetic god.

Her mother found her one afternoon, crouched in the sand, sketching the flight paths of cormorants with a stick. Circles within circles, arcs that cut across each other at a sharp angle. She stared so intensely at the sand reflecting the blinding sun that it made her eyes hurt.

"Come home, Agatha," her mother said, voice tired in a way that was far more common among laborers than council speakers. "It's not healthy, spending so much time alone. Unnatural. What calls you away so?"

Agatha didn't look up. The pattern wasn't finished yet. Three more lines and she would see…

Her mother's hand closed around her wrist, and though her touch could never be called gentle, this one hurt.

"I asked you a question."

The stick fell from Agatha's fingers, and words came tumbling out before she could stop them, childish and desperate in their honesty. 

"The birds, Mother. They're trying to tell me something. Something's going to happen. Something bad. They’ve been circling the temple for three days now, always at dawn, always seven of them. Seven is the number of—"

She stopped. 

The weariness was gone from her mother’s face. In its place was something bright and sharp, attention so focused it made Agatha's stomach clench. 

Her mother was looking at her the way she looked at the other council members during debates. The way she’d seen cats approach mice who were preoccupied with nibbling at grain.

"Tell me, darling," her mother said, her voice no longer tired at all. "Go on and tell me what the birds are saying."

Agatha's mouth went dry. "I... I don't know exactly. Just that something's coming. Something to do with the temple, or the priests, or…"

"When?"

"Before the new moon, I think."

Her mother's grip on her wrist tightened, then released. Agatha rubbed at the indent left behind. Her mother crouched down, bringing her face level with Agatha's, and smiled. 

Agatha couldn't remember the last time she'd seen her mother smile. It looked unnatural on her face, the way it stretched her thin lips and crinkled her eyes. She was almost certain she’d never seen such an expression directed at her.

"My clever girl," her mother said softly. "My blessed, clever girl. The gods have given you a gift."

Something in Agatha's chest constricted. This was wrong. She had expected dismissal, perhaps punishment for foolishness. Not this.

"We'll need to be careful," her mother continued, speaking more to herself now than to Agatha.

"Test it first, quietly. If you're right about the temple..." She stood abruptly, brushing sand from her chiton. "Come. We're going home. You'll wash and dress properly. And Agatha—"

She looked down at her daughter with that same bright, calculating expression.

"You will tell no one else about this. No one. Do you understand? This is ours."

Agatha nodded, though she didn't understand at all. She only knew that her mother's hand stayed on her shoulder as they walked back, but it didn’t hurt this time.

Yet, that sick feeling in her stomach (the one she'd learned to recognize as a warning) hadn't gone away.

If anything, it had gotten worse.

She should have listened to that feeling. She should have run back to the water’s edge and never left, just kept running until she finally touched the line that broke between the sky and sea.

But the gods rarely grant mercies to those who reach for too much and dare to see too clearly.

Closing her eyes, she tried to remember if the feet of the gulls had been yellow-orange or more orange-yellow. It seemed important, now, to get the details right. She had been careless with her memories before and it had cost her.

She heard not a squawk but a whimper, and sighed before turning toward the sound.

"Quiet now. You've already made yourself known. I have not forgotten you so soon."

Another whimper, wet and desperate.

"Shh. This is what you wanted, was it not?" She moved toward the man slowly, her bare feet whispering against stone. "You came to lay with me. So rest your head now."

There was a rustle as a head shook, or tried to. The paralysis made even small movements agony, she knew. It was a terrible fate being trapped inside one's own drowning body.

She wasn’t completely without sympathy.

"No?" She tilted her head, considering him in the darkness. Though eyes had long ago adjusted to the absence of light, his had not. 

He couldn't see her yet, could only hear her voice circling closer. 

"Then why did you come? To look upon me? To call me monster?"

She paused, letting the silence stretch out until she was certain it would not echo further.

"To slay me?"

The quality of his breathing changed and she could taste his sweat on the air.

"Ah." She lowered herself to the ground, folding one leg beneath her as she stretched out beside him, close enough that he would feel the warmth of her body in the cave's chill. 

Here she was close enough to smell the salt still clinging to his clothes, confirming he’d arrived by boat. They usually did. 

"How disappointing. The ones who come for violence are always so much less interesting than the ones who come for pleasure. At least those ones are honest about their appetites."

She propped herself up on one elbow, studying his paralyzed form. 

He was young. They always were, these would-be heroes.

Sent by mothers who wanted to elevate their sons' stations, most likely. An omega son who slayed a monster might be permitted to choose his own alpha, rather than be chosen. An alpha son might earn a council seat, a name that meant something beyond his father's shadow.

Perhaps he'd volunteered, eager to make a name for himself.

To become the man who killed the monster.

She breathed in slowly, tasting the air.

"Did they tell you I was beautiful once?" she asked softly, tracing a finger along the stone floor beside his rigid shoulder, not touching him directly. She had no need for that.

"They always mention that part. The tragedy is so much more appealing when the monster used to be lovely. As if my pretty face was the only thing lost. The only thing worth mourning by the world."

She leaned closer, her breath warm against his ear. This close, she could smell the scent-dampening herbs they'd made him rub into his skin. Wormwood and ash. As if hiding what he was would protect him.

"Would you like to know what you lost? Coming here?"

A strangled sound escaped his frozen throat.

"Your mother's hope, certainly. Your father's name and pride. Any children you might have sired." Her voice was almost tender now.

"The chance to proudly bear the mark of a mate against your neck. To know the weight of someone's teeth in that soft place, claiming you. Cherishing you."

Her fingers drifted closer to his throat, hovering over the unmarked skin there.

"But those are far away now, aren't they? It’s hard to think about in the abstract. Let me tell you what you've lost in terms you can understand."

She shifted, draping herself more fully alongside him, her body a line of heat against his paralyzed side. Her scent would be flooding his senses now. 

Idly, Agatha wondered what she smelled like these days. She couldn’t fathom it registering as human anymore.

"You’ve lost the ability to scream. To run. To close your eyes when you finally see what has become of me."

A soft hissing began above them, like wind through grass, like the whisper of scales on scales echoing off the chamber walls.

"To look away," she murmured, her lips nearly brushing the unmarked column of his throat, "when my darlings get hungry."

She could feel his pulse hammering beneath skin that would never bear a mate's claim.

She couldn’t allow such a waste.

The hissing grew louder, more insistent. Eager, writhing with appetites she no longer fully controlled. 

Sometimes she thought they were separate creatures entirely. Other times she knew they were simply the hungriest parts of herself, given form and fang.

She pushed herself up slowly, deliberately, letting him feel her weight shift away. The loss of her warmth would cause its own kind of terror in the cave's chill.

"I'm going to do you a kindness," she said, settling back on her heels beside him. Above her, the serpents swayed and dipped, their scales catching what little light filtered into the cave's depths. 

Their tongues flickered out, tasting his fear on the air.

"You see, most men who come here die seeing. And what they see in those final moments..." She shook her head slowly. "It breaks something. Some die of the horror before my girls even reach them. Their hearts simply... stop. Fear can be as fatal as venom, I've learned."

One of the serpents, the largest of them, lowered its head toward his face. Its black eyes gleamed.

"So I'll spare you that, at least." Her voice was soft now, almost gentle. "You won't have to see what I've become. What you tried to destroy."

She leaned forward, her hand hovering over his forehead.

"We will take your eyes first. Quickly. I promise you that much."

A tear rolled from the corner of his paralyzed eye, cutting a track down his temple.

"Hush now," she whispered. "This is the mercy. Trust me. You don't want to watch what comes after."

She sat back, her hand falling away.

"Darlings," she said simply. "His eyes. Be quick about it."

The hissing rose to a fever pitch. And then an unnatural silence followed by a wet popping sound.

She turned her face away, toward the cave mouth, while her darlings fed. 

Shame had never stopped hunger.

And she had been hungry for so very long.

Agatha’s chambers were comfortable. Silk curtains, plush cushions, a bed large enough for three. Her mother had spared no expense in making her cage beautiful, and after her presentation, it was ensured that it was comfortable as well.

There was a lovely window looking out, from which she could see the winding steps down to the lower districts. 

On days when the wind was right, she could smell honeyed pastries wafting right up to her sill, or catch the plucking of a lyre as a musician wandered the lower alleys.

Not long after her eighteenth year began, it had been deemed too dangerous for her to walk the streets unattended, though by whom her mother refused to say, instead obliquely waving the threat of unregistered alphas who may have passed the border or slipped their work sites.

Agatha doubted that, though. Her designation—that of a daughter of Demeter, a life-bringer, blessed by the goddess of harvest and fertility—was a gift the gods themselves had chosen for her. 

No froth-mouthed child of Ares would be fool enough to try to cross her, alpha or not. 

Alphas were made for war and labor, their bloodlust a curse as much as a gift. Ares' children through and through, born with violence and lust in their veins and little else. Useful for defending the city walls or breaking earth for planting, but hardly sophisticated enough for real ambitions. They just didn’t have the foresight.

The betas, touched by neither war nor fertility, were said to be Athena’s chosen: balanced, practical, the sensible backbone of society.

They ran the trades, managed the merchants’ guilds, kept the city functioning while others made history. Respectable, if unremarkable.

Privately, Agatha suspected Athena had simply been assigned the task of minding the middling. Someone had to give the unexceptional a patron, after all.

There was no harm in letting them mistake usefulness for importance.

Omegas were not only blessed once by the divine, but twice. Favored by Demeter, and marked by Hera for their role in carrying bloodlines forward, they were living vessels of continuity and inheritance.

To harm an omega—particularly one marked by prophecy, as Agatha was—was to invite divine retribution and public outrage in equal measure.

In theory, it made her untouchable.

In practice, it made her property.

Her mother’s warnings were just another kind of chain, prettier than most, but a binding nonetheless.

Misfortune came in threes, but so did fortune, apparently. Agatha had learned that sometimes, many times, more often than not, perhaps, the two were impossible to tell apart.

There was first her gift. The ability to read omens in flight and entrails, to hear what the gods whispered on the wind. If she'd been born without it, she might have lived an ordinary life. She thought that she would never have married and perhaps grown old watching the sea.

If she'd presented as beta instead, she could have set up a modest shrine on some hillside. She could have gathered the gossip of farmers and fishwives, weighed in on petty disputes over boundary stones, and borrowed tools. She would read fortunes for a few shining coins and a hot meal. A simple life, but one entirely her own.

Agatha had fantasized about it, late at night when sleep wouldn't come. The beta seer with her humble duties, her garden of herbs, her freedom to walk where she pleased. No silk cushions but no locked doors either.

Another fantasy came less often, usually tinged with a rippling feeling at the back of her neck she did not wish to examine. 

If she'd been omega without the prophecy, she might have been married off young to some appropriately matched alpha. 

She'd seen such arrangements. They didn’t seem that terrible. Omegas draped in fine silk and woven things, their alpha devoted and useful, kept properly under thumb. 

The alpha would work themselves ragged to keep their omega satiated: bringing gifts, providing comfort, offering their body and strength and devotion like sacrifices at an altar. Every instinct redirected toward pleasing one person.

It was what alphas were for, after all. To have their violence softened and repurposed into loyalty.

Sometimes, she allowed herself to imagine it.

Some nameless alpha kneeling at her feet, eyes dark with want, rough hands careful only with her. Watching her like she was the axis of their world.

Wanting nothing except her comfort. Her approval. Her touch.

Someone strong choosing softness for her. Someone fierce being gentle only with her.

It was a dangerous thought.

If she'd been only omega, only seer, she might have had choices. But she was both—twice gifted, the priests had proclaimed at her presentation ceremony. Blessed by Demeter, marked by prophecy, and daughter of a rising senator, born into a family that understood exactly how to leverage divine favor for earthly power.

The mathematics of her life were simple: one gift might have been manageable. Two would have been valuable. But three? 

Blessed by Demeter. Marked by Hera. Claimed by prophecy.

Three seals on a life that was never meant to be her own.

Too precious to waste on simple happiness. Too rare to risk on anything as frivolous as freedom.

Three, as she'd learned young, was the number of things coming that couldn't be stopped.

A half hour later, the Councillor's wife left with her fortune clutched close to her chest: modest success, no dramatic losses, exactly what she'd paid for. 

Her mother had gone to see the woman out, and would return shortly to parade in the next supplicant. Another merchant wanting to know about harvest yields. Another politician seeking advantage over a rival. Another and another and another until she’d wrung every coin from each greased hand.

She was interrupted by a knock at the door. Her mother never knocked, and if she did it wouldn’t be so tentative and uncertain.

Agatha turned, irritation flaring. “Appointment only,” she called. “My mother handles the schedule—”

And then she smelled it.

Olive brine and heavy soil and something wild beneath it, like wind carrying the fruit of a far-off land over open water. Not the perfumed oils of wealthy omegas, or the careful florals of betas.

Something untamed.

Something that did not belong in silk-draped rooms and scheduled destinies.

Her breath caught before she knew why.

“I—” A voice came from the other side. Female. Low. Slightly rough, like someone unused to speaking through closed doors. “I was told you might see me. I can pay.”

Agatha shouldn’t. Her mother would be back any moment. Would be furious at the breach of protocol.

But that scent answered for her.

“Come in,” she heard herself say.

The door opened slowly, and her breath escaped in one quick burst of an exhale.

The woman in the doorway was slightly taller than her with dark hair pulled back in a simple braid that fell over one shoulder. From her clothes, it was clear this wasn’t her usual client. Not a merchant’s daughter. Not a politician’s niece.

Her boots still carried the dust of her walk here. She must have come from some distance.

Then she noticed her face.

It was the kind poets gave to heroes at the start of their epic.

Sun-warmed skin. Sharp cheekbones. A mouth tightened by nerves but clearly more inclined to smile than frown.

Her eyes were dark as wet earth, steady and deep and unsettlingly familiar.

The woman stepped inside, then stopped. Her nostrils flared slightly, and something shifted in her expression. Agatha would have called it recognition if that were possible.

"You're the seer," the woman said.

"I am." Agatha's voice came out steadier than she felt. "And you?"

"I have questions. I was told you read fortunes. That you can see... paths. Futures."

"That's one way to describe it." Agatha moved back toward her reading table, needing the familiar territory. “May we trade a question for a name?”

The woman paused, then lifted her chin slightly.

“Rio,” she said.

The name was short, not one she had heard spoken before. It felt strangely important.

Agatha found herself repeating it without meaning to.

“Rio.”

She thought she imagined another echo, a stone dropped in water rippling out.

Clearing her throat, she shook out her hair. "What future do you seek, Rio?"

"I want to know if there's another path for me," Rio said quietly. "Something other than what I'm—what's expected."

Agatha's fingers stilled on the arrangement of bones and stones. "What is expected of you?"

Rio's hand drifted to the blade hung low at her hip. "I'm alpha."

The word hung in the air between them.

Agatha should have known. Should have recognized it in the scent, the build, the way Rio held herself like she was trying to take up less space than her body demanded. But there was something about her that didn't fit the mold of Ares' children. There was no aggressive posturing or crude swagger here.

There was such longing in her voice that Agatha felt something twist in her chest in recognition.

"Sit," she said, gesturing to the cushions across from her table. "Let me see what the gods will show."

Rio sat, moving with a grace that seemed at odds with her designation. Up close, her scent was stronger—that olive and salt, but underneath it something sweeter. Not quite sweet as honey, not as sharp as lemon. Something Agatha couldn’t put a name to no matter how much she wished to.

Rio extended her right hand, palm up. Agatha reached for it, meaning to trace the lines there, to read what the gods had written in flesh.

Instead, something tore open.

Light and heat and memory crashed through her all at once.

Sunlight through olive leaves.

Rough hands gentle in dark hair.

Salt air and laughter.

A small house with a garden.

Two bodies tangled in sheets.

Mouths meeting, desperate and tender.

A tongue that grew long then split and tasted acrid.

Bloodied walls. The taste of copper. Something, writhing and roiling. 

Dark eyes full of grief.

But also patience. Care. The kind of devotion it takes to tend growing things, to wait for harvest, to—"

She stopped, realizing she was saying too much, revealing too much at once. Projecting her own voice instead of the gods. A mistake for any seer.

Rio had gone very still.

Then, slowly, she turned her hand beneath Agatha’s, their palms fitting together without either of them quite meaning to.

A quiet sound left her—half breath, half laugh, disbelieving.

“You must be a seer, no other would believe such things,” Rio murmured.

Her thumb brushed, almost accidentally, against Agatha’s wrist.

It sent a small, dangerous shiver up her arm.

Outside, Agatha heard her mother’s voice in the hallway, sharp with instruction as she spoke to a servant. She would be here in moments.

The reading was over.

Rio would leave. Walk back out into the world. Take this moment with her like something half-remembered from a dream.

Unless.

“Come back,” Agatha heard herself say. “Tonight. After dark.”

Rio’s eyes widened. “What?”

"The vision. I need to understand it better. And I think..." Agatha's heart was racing now, her pulse loud in her ears as she scrambled. "I think you need to be here for me to see it clearly."

It was the flimsiest excuse she'd ever given. But Rio didn't call her on it.

"Where?" Rio asked quietly.

"There's a garden behind the house with purple flowers on the terrace. The one with the fig tree. There's a loose section of wall, you’re thin enough to slip through. Come after moonrise. When the house is quiet."

"Your keeper—"

"Will be asleep. Or occupied with her own schemes." Agatha forced herself to meet Rio's eyes. "Will you come?"

She watched the bob as Rio's throat worked. When she spoke, her voice was rough. "Why are you doing this?"

"Because," Agatha said instead, "the gods showed me something impossible."

Her mother's footsteps were closer now.

Rio stood, moving toward the door. But she paused at the threshold, looking back. Her dark eyes held something that nearly made Agatha race to grab her hand and follow after her.

"I'll come," Rio said.

Then she was gone, and Agatha was left alone with the scattered bones on the floor.

Her mother entered a moment later, nose wrinkling. "Was someone just here? It smells foul." She stopped, eyes narrowing. "Agatha. Who was in this room?"

"A petitioner," Agatha said calmly, though her hands trembled as she gathered the fallen bones. "Looking for guidance about her future."

"Without an appointment? Without payment?" Her mother's voice sharpened. "What did she want?"

"To know if there was another path for her. If she could choose something different than what was expected."

Her mother made a disgusted sound. "I hope you told her the gods don't rewrite destinies for convenience."

Agatha carefully placed the last bone back in its bowl. "Actually," she said quietly, "I told her that sometimes the path to what we want goes through the impossible."

Her mother stared at her for a long moment. "You're being cryptic. I don't like it when you're cryptic."

"I'm being a seer, Mother. It's what you made me."

There was a shadow of suspicion, perhaps, or the beginning of concern. But then she waved a dismissive hand. "Well, whatever you told her, I hope she paid. No charity readings, Agatha. We have a reputation to maintain."

"Of course, Mother."

When her mother finally left, Agatha returned to the window. The seven crows she’d been watching before had taken flight, circling once before heading west toward the setting sun.

Seven meant change. Seven meant something coming that couldn't be stopped.

The moon was a silver coin cast high up in the sky by the time Agatha slipped from her chambers.

Patience did not come easily to her. But she'd waited through dinner, through her mother's interminable discussion of next week's appointments, through the household settling into sleep.

Now the halls were quiet save for the occasional creak of old wood and the distant sound of the night watch changing positions in the street.

Her heart hammered as she made her way through the darkened corridors toward the east wing. She was being foolish. Reckless. Everything her mother had taught her not to be. Everything she would not allow herself to be.

But the vision had burned itself into her mind and propelled her forward now. Rio's hands in her hair. The taste of salt and honey. The possibility of being chosen rather than used.

The garden door gave way with barely a whisper. Cool night air kissed her heated skin as she stepped outside, and for a moment, she wondered if Rio would even come. 

If perhaps the alpha had thought better of this mad idea, had decided that sneaking into a senator's daughter's home to meet with an omega above her station was a risk not worth taking.

Then she saw her.

Rio stood beneath the fig tree, a dark shape among shadows, but Agatha would have known her anywhere. That scent, salt and earth and danger carried on the night breeze.

"You came," Agatha breathed.

Even in the moonlight, Agatha could see the nervousness in the set of her shoulders as Rio turned towards her. 

"I wasn't sure you'd meant it. Thought maybe once I left, you'd realize…" She stopped, then tried again. "I'm not supposed to be here."

"I know."

"Agatha Harkness. Daughter of Evanora. Your mother would have me thrown in chains. Or worse."

"I know that too."

Rio took a step closer, and Agatha could see she was carrying a small bundle wrapped in cloth. 

"I brought you something. It's not much, I just… when you said to come, I couldn't arrive empty-handed. That felt wrong."

She held it out, and Agatha took it with trembling fingers. The cloth fell away to reveal three objects that sparkled brilliantly even in the pale light.
