Chapter 1

Agatha felt recharged. She always did when there were at least three bodies at her feet and this time she’d managed five. She took a deep breath and looked at the purple weave swirling around her torso. It had been a while and this time it felt great. 

People were desperate in the aftermath of half of the population disappearing suddenly. Witches were scrambling with half their covens gone. If she wanted to, Agatha was sure that she could gather another group within a week. 

She was so lost in the thoughts of massive amounts of power that she had forgotten the reason she usually left fairly quickly after killing. 

But she didn’t expect to be able to see her.

It wasn’t the silent, swift, and regal visage she was used to. 

Rio stumbled into the cleaning and collapsed to her knees. A spray of flowers grew in a sweeping motion, like a speeding clock hand around Rio followed by their rapid death and decay. She cried out, clutching her abdomen and shrinking into a ball of a person, a black ichor weeping out of her eyes and the corner of her mouth. 

Everything seemed to calm and Agatha moved to her, falling to her knees, “What happened?”

Rio seized one last time, grabbing a fistful of earth under her hand. From her touch, a stream of mushrooms shot out in a line toward the bodies Agatha created, enveloping them at a lightning speed and dragging them underground. 

She closed her eyes, splaying out on the ground in front of Agatha, who didn’t know how to help. 

Rio’s chest was heaving like she couldn’t catch her breath for several seconds before she seemed to calm. She didn’t even seem to register that Agatha was there until Agatha pushed the hair out of her face and asked again, “What happened?”

Rio pushed herself into sitting. “Everything is wrong.” She seemed delirious and exhausted. She tucked a leg under herself in an attempt to stand and careened to one side, only staying upright because Agatha caught her. 

“Does this have to do with half of everyone disappearing?” Agatha asked, lowering them both back to the ground.

Rio nodded, trying to get up again. “The natural balance - it’s - I have to go.”

“Go where?” Agatha asked, guiding Rio into a standing position, but not letting her go in case she fell again.

“I’m -” Rio started, but another flash of flora sprouted around them, this time trees growing abnormally fast in a circle around them, reaching full heights of thirty feet before they started decaying from the inside out. 

“Wherever you’re going,” Agatha looked up at the massive trees all starting to crack and groan above them. “Take me with you.” She knew at least one if not all of them would collapse on top of them at any second. 

With a soft sigh, they blinked out of existence and reappeared somewhere else. The effort of moving them caused Rio to pass out completely, fully going limp in Agatha’s arms.

Agatha looked around. She found that they were in a small cottage that seemed very familiar in the most annoying way. She huffed and pushed Rio onto the bed with less care than she would have had she not realized that they were back in the cottage they shared when they were together hundreds of years ago.

Agatha tightened her coat around herself and looked around. It was exactly as she had remembered it, though a little chilly. There were plants growing everywhere and sunshine pouring through the windows. 

Agatha looked at Rio, passed out on the bed, then looked around. She wasn’t sure she could stand being there for very long so, as she did when she got uncomfortable, she left. Or at least she tried to. 

She moved to the front door of the cottage, wrenching it open and instead of a sunshine filled glade where they had spent years taking walks and foraging, she found a void. She took a tentative step toward the edge of the porch and looked down. There wasn’t much to see because the light from the house was swallowed in every direction. There was nothing. 

She stepped back inside and picked up the nearest small object, which happened to be a candle and threw it into the void. There was no sound and she could only track it for a second before it was swallowed. 

“Great,” she huffed and walked back into the cottage, letting the door close. 

Several attempts to rouse Rio were unsuccessful and the last one ended with Rio writhing in pain and screaming. Agatha gave up and decided to make herself at home. Again. 

She started a fire to warm the place up and grabbed a book to read. There was no getting out until Rio was ready and Rio was not in a state to do much. 

Agatha kept looking up from her book at Rio whose face would writhe in pain every so often. She would let out a small whine and then contort. The plants in the cottage would bloom and then whither in a rapid cycle in a massive fluctuation of power. 

Agatha would force her thoughts to ‘maybe the pain is what she deserves’ and ‘maybe since she’s less powerful she can be defeated’, but those thoughts always felt so hollow when it came to Rio. She crossed one leg over the other and leaned on the arm of the chair she was on, staring intently at her. She watched as Rio lay there, her lips parted and her eyes squeezed shut, against the sheets that used to be theirs. She was trying so hard to be angry, but she’d never seen Rio so weak.

Her eyes flickered to the kitchen. There was a small, curved knife on the counter. She could probably at the very least do some damage while she had the upper hand. 

Her jaw clenched when she looked back at Rio. The fingers around her stomach gripped hard and she squirmed, kicking the blankets away.

Eventually, Agatha couldn’t just watch. With a frustrated, “Ahhgg!” she pushed up out of the chair, tossing the unread book onto the table. She set the kettle on the woodburning stove, then dipped some cloth into the water in the wash basin. She moved to the bed and sat next to Rio. Her face was smeared with something Agatha couldn’t place, but it couldn’t be comfortable. She carefully cleaned Rio’s face and then rinsed out the rag. She brought it back and placed it on Rio’s forehead. 

“This isn’t a pathetic attempt to win me back right?” Agatha asked, pushing the hair out of Rio’s face. 

Rio’s only answer was a mewling followed by another surge in the plant life. 

Agatha exhaled loudly and stood up. “Okay, so I will do some research and see if we can get you stabilized so I can go home.” Agatha moved to the hoards of books they had both collected over the years. It was all starting to come back to her where she might find more information about green witches and the natural balance. She had to stand on a stack of books and reach for a tome on the top of a bookcase. But as she pulled it down, a cascade of letters fell with them as well. 

She ducked her head as they fell, watching them come to rest on the ground. As soon as she knelt to pick them up, she realized what they were. They were the love letters they’d written each other. Rio would leave one on her pillow when she left to do her job. Agatha would leave one in a tree nearby, knowing Rio would find it. Rio would leave one on the open windowsill and Agatha would tuck one into the pocket of Rio’s cloak. 

Since she was older and more jaded it seemed like something two naive lovebirds would do, but she remembered how sweet it was. She remembered how reading Rio’s letters made her feel. She had been alone for so long it took her a while to trust Rio, but once she did it felt like she’d never be alone again. 

In the present, Agatha gathered all the letters and shoved them back onto the bookcase. The cottage was a museum to their relationship and some things were best relived in memory. 

Agatha found a few books that would help with pain relief and sleep, but nothing that would clue her into how to really help Rio. Half of all life disappearing instantaneously was not something the witches of old would have written about.

With what she could find, she made a tea that she hoped would deaden Rio’s pain enough to maybe get her lucid enough so she could assist in her own treatments. The problem was getting her to drink it.

It was warmer in the cottage since the fire so she shed her coat, picked up a spoon from the kitchen, and brought the concoction with her to the bed. She knelt down next to the bed and dipped the spoon into the tea. “This is going to taste like grass which I’m sure you love.” 

The barb made Rio smile through the pain. Her head lolled over toward Agatha and she could barely open her eyes, her hands gripping hard on her stomach. “This is the end.”

“What?” Agatha blinked, spoon poised just away from Rio’s lips. 

Rio shook her head and rolled over on the bed so she was on her side facing Agatha. She stretched her back and groaned. “I failed.” She cleared her throat. “There’s another one - co-coming.” She seemed to choke on something and blood trickled out of the corner of her mouth. 

“What?” Agatha shook her head, initially rejecting the revelation. “No.” She became horrified at the prospect. Rio was always there. She was always around and Agatha always knew how to find her. “What will happen to you?”

Rio closed her eyes and smiled, waving her hand into the air. A shower of dark green sparkles rained from her hand as black veins crawled up her face. 

Tears flooded Agatha’s eyes. “No. You can’t leave not like this.” She set the tea down and turned her head so it was much the same direction as Rio’s and rested her hand on her cheek. “How do I stop this?”

Rio put her hand on top of Agatha’s and pressed it to her cheek. “I don’t think - don’t think…” She winced. “Don’t worry about me. You’ll fi-finally be fr-free.” 

The tears in Agatha’s eyes started to fall. She leaned forward, her forehead on Rio’s. She whispered, “Don’t leave me.”

Rio let out a sad smile and tilted her head up to kiss Agatha’s forehead. “You are my greatest love.” The black veins took a deeper root into her, squeezing her chest so it felt like she couldn’t breathe. “You have to go.”

“No,” Agatha shook her head and pulled back to look at Rio with teary conviction. “I’ll fix this. I can save you.” She moved her hand to the back of Rio’s head and took a fistful of her hair. “Don’t let go. Fight. You have to live so I get to kill you.” When Rio didn’t answer she leaned closer and demanded, “Promise me.”

Rio tilted her chin back and writhed. She forced herself to open her eyes and at seeing Agatha’s determination and teary face she nodded, “I promise.”

Agatha was up like a shot at the promise. She started throwing books around, looking for the answer. She frantically read through pages that couldn’t help her and tossed them away when they wouldn’t help. 

Agatha’s hands started shaking when she saw Rio’s eyes slip closed and soft motes of black start to float away from her body. “You said you’d fight,” Agatha snarled. 

Another smile crossed Rio’s face. “Anything for you, my love.” Her writhing had stilled and she seemed too weak to move. 

Agatha dropped the book she was holding and put her hands on her head as she paced. She couldn’t think of anything. There was a panic that was blurring out all of the knowledge in her brain. Tears continued to pour down her face and she muttered, “You have to stay. Even if I have to -” Then it dawned on her. 

She ran to the dresser and pulled out the first scarf she saw. She moved to the bed and knelt next to it, taking Rio’s hand in her own. She started wrapping their joined hands in the scarf and chanting. 

Rio opened her eyes and looked curiously at their joined hands. 

“Don’t get any ideas,” Agatha’s reply came like an automatic retort that required no thought as she continued chanting. 

Rio smiled again. “I’d never dream of it.”

As Agatha chanted, the purple light around their hands grew and grew and grew until it enveloped them. The light coalesced into a rope that wound itself around Rio’s body, starting at her legs, snaking around her torso, then coiling around her throat. 

They both started to rise from where they had been, bodies floating in the middle of the cottage they’d shared so many centuries ago. Agatha held fast to Rio’s hand, not that she could let go even if she wanted to. The rope around Rio constricted and on instinct her free hand went to claw at the one around her neck. 

“Let it happen,” Agatha instructed gently. She squeezed Rio’s hand in hers, trying to reassure her.

Rio’s eyes met Agatha’s and the instinctual fear of the rope around her throat faded. In Agatha’s eyes, she found a concern and an earnesty that was so rare. She let her arm relax, floating freely in the air next to them. 

When she sensed the spell completing itself, Agatha let out a last, massive burst of energy. The blinding purple light exploded around them and everything faded. 

Chapter 2

Agatha woke up in the field where she’d first met the stumbling, pained Rio. Agatha’s kills had started to decompose in the circle they were standing in when they fell. Rio was on the ground next to her, green cloak covering most of her body. The only thing reaching out of it was her hand that was still tied to Agatha’s. 

“Hey,” Agatha scooted across the bloody grass closer to Rio. She pushed Rio’s hood back with the tips of her fingers and saw the blood was gone. The black ichor was gone. She slowly woke up as if from a deep slumber. 

Agatha busied herself with untying their clasped hands and then sat up, letting Rio push up off of the ground into a seated position on her own. Rio looked around like she wasn’t sure where she was. She furrowed her brow and then put her hand on her chest. “I’m bound.”

“Sort of,” Agatha replied, rolling the scarf up in her hand and stuffing it into her coat pocket, both of which shouldn’t still be on her person if Rio’s hallucination had been normal. “You’re bound to me.” 

Rio looked up at Agatha from the ground where she was still orienting herself. 

Agatha offered her hand to Rio and Rio took it, using Agatha’s strength to stand. She still seemed disoriented, but shook out her cloak and set her shoulders. She raised her hood and closed her eyes. “They’re gone.”

Agatha took a moment to observe Rio. “The one that comes after?”

Rio nodded slowly and opened her eyes. She met Agatha’s eyes with deep question. 

Agatha looked away from Rio, adjusting the buttons on the cuff of her coat sleeves. “I finally got what I wanted. I can’t die because if I go, you do too.”

It unnerved Agatha how quiet Rio was. She was hoping for a sharp retort or Rio biting back at being leashed. She took a chance and looked back at Rio. She huffed as soon as she realized Rio was reaping the people that she had just killed. She muttered, “Back to work already.”

When Rio was finished, she stood over the last body, shifted her weight from one foot to the other. Then she asked while Agatha was busy pretending to be interested in something in the opposite direction. “How does this work? I can feel that we can’t be apart for very long without…” she gestured to her torso, “Unraveling.”

Honestly and truly, Agatha hadn’t thought that far. She did what she could to keep Rio from ‘unraveling’ or being replaced in a panicked moment of experimental hope. “We’re centuries old adults.” Agatha lifted her chin and straightened her posture. “We can… figure something out.”

Rio seemed to accept that answer. Her eyes, as always, were daggers that saw right through Agatha. She looked away almost timidly, “Thank you. For saving me.”

“Yeah well,” Agatha tried to play it off like she didn’t go crazy trying to save her. “You owe me.”

Rio bowed her head in acquiescence. She couldn’t argue with Agatha over that point. “I’ll be back.”

“How do you know-” Agatha started, but Rio was gone in a whirl of leaves and grass. Agatha huffed and rolled her eyes. “Well, try not to unravel.”

Chapter 3

Agatha was making tea in the kitchen of a house she had borrowed when she sensed Rio aparate nearby. She looked casually over her shoulder and saw Rio seemed to be in a bit of pain, but no worse for wear. 

“Tea?” Agatha asked, looking back to the distribution of tea leaves. 

“Sure,” Rio moved into the foyer of the house, removed her cloak and hung it on the coat rack near the front door. It was a magical coak that could disappear at will, but it was polite that she was trying. 

“Kill anyone fun today?” Agatha asked, partly so it wasn’t silent anymore and partly to be a bitch.

Rio seemed to be intent on quiet and brooding at the table so she replied with a simple. “No.”

“You’re going to be a really fun roommate,” Agatha poured the hot water over the leaves. “I can tell.”

Rio didn’t answer. She was leaned back in one of the wooden kitchen chairs, with her booted foot up in another one, flexing her hand in the air, watching all of the plants in the kitchen get greener and fuller. A vine started to weave its way around the top of the wall just under the ceiling, infusing itself into the drywall and bordering the windows. 

“Don’t mess up this house,” Agatha walked with the teacups to the table and sat down across from Rio. “I just acquired it.”

“It’s big,” Rio looked up at the ceiling like she could see the second floor. She weaved her hand up higher doing something Agatha couldn’t see. 

“What are you doing?” Agatha asked, looking up. 

“There’s mold upstairs,” Rio drug her hand from one side of the ceiling to the other as if wiping. “And a leak in the bathroom pipes.”

“Can you fix it?” Agatha asked, picking up her teacup.

Rio grinned wickedly. “Is that your favor?”

“When I come to collect my favor, you’ll know it,” Agatha took a sip of her tea. “But as far as roommate stuff goes, feel free to grow some better tea than this. It’s stale.”

“I’ll get some seeds when I go out,” Rio had finished sealing the leak upstairs with the minerals she extracted from the water and picked up her tea. 

Drinking tea together was quiet and Agatha started going over what she needed to do to gather the next group of witches to execute. “I plan to stay here for a while so I need you to wear normal clothes in case the neighbors see you.”

Rio nodded, silently sipping her tea. 

Agatha looked down at her own clothes that were probably a tad more renaissance faire than one would expect inside of a suburban New England house. “I should too.” 

“Do I need a cover story?” Rio asked, her face shifting from brooding to interested and curious. “A traveling troubadour maybe?”

“Maybe you don’t talk to anyone else,” Agatha let out a smile, without looking at Rio. “No one uses troubadour unpretentiously anymore.”

“Oh,” Rio’s eyebrows rose. 

Agatha took another sip of her tear and got lost deep in thought. When she pulled herself back to the present, she realized Rio was staring at her. When she looked back, Rio’s eyes dropped immediately to her tea. 

Agatha was a bit unnerved by how familiar it felt and stood up. “I’m going to go through the clothes upstairs.” She swept out of the kitchen, leaving Rio and her half-drunk tea behind.

Chapter 4

As far as unexpected roommates went, Rio was easy. She knew all of the things that agitated Agatha so she avoided them. She would leave treats out for her from around the world and made sure the kettle was always hot. 

One rainy evening, Agatha had had a fairly successful day and had gotten four witches to agree to meet her in the woods in the next week. She celebrated with a glass of wine in the kitchen, realizing, as she tended to do, just how isolated she was from every other person on the planet. She couldn’t confide in anyone. She couldn’t get someone in on her con game, lest they try to stop her or worse steal the con for themselves. 

She stared at the kitchen wall, feeling a well of loneliness that had never gone away after… 

There was a flutter of wind in the living room that rattled the chandelier and a few pages of the open books on the coffee table. She rose to her feet and marched into the living room. 

Rio was taking off her cloak, smelling of salt and ocean air. She looked up from placing it gently on the back of the couch to see Agatha standing in the kitchen doorway, her hands glowing purple and fury on her face. 

Rio squared her body with Agatha’s, but didn’t draw her knife. She stood, relaxed and patient. 

“Take out your knife,” Agatha told her through gritted teeth. 

Rio did as instructed, but let it hang limply by her side. 

“Fight me,” Agatha hurled a blast of purple at Rio, striking her in the shoulder. It knocked her backwards into the fireplace, but didn’t knock her off of her feet. She took a step toward Agatha and stood still again. 

“Fight me!” Agatha roared. She used her magic to pick up the couch and hurl it at Rio. 

Rio, normally adept at dodging basically anything, let the couch hit her and knock her to the ground. She pushed it off of her and stood up, moving to stand in front of Agatha again. 

“Why won’t you fight me!” Agatha pointed her hands at the ground, pushing white hot purple flames up around Rio’s feet, burning away her clothing and charring her skin from the floor to the ceiling. 

Agatha watched the face of Death reveal itself, the half skull in a permanent smile as the skin was burned away. 

At the sight of it, Agatha let the flames die out. She watched Rio’s organs patch themselves up and her skin regrow over her muscles. She watched her face take it’s familiar form and found her eyes to be sympathetic and not angry with her which made Agatha all the more furious. 

“Why won’t you fight back!” Agatha marched up to Rio, grabbed her by the throat and pinned her against the wall. 

“You know why,” Rio breathed out. 

“Because you owe me,” Agatha snarled. “I could unmake you right here, right now.”

Rio put both of her hands around Agatha’s wrist, but didn’t try to pull her hand away from her throat. “You’re better than lying to yourself.”

“You think I can’t break the binding?” Agatha asked, eyes shining with tears. 

Rio didn’t answer. She just reached forward and wiped a tear from Agatha’s cheek. 

The simple, tender action put Agatha on her heels. She took a step back, taking her hand from Rio’s neck. In the shock of it, she turned around and walked out of the living room, finding her way up the stairs, into the master bedroom and into the bathroom. She turned on the shower and stood under the water with her clothes on, closing her eyes against the spray. 

She hated more than anything that Rio’s presence was comforting to her. She resented it. She hated that she couldn’t just let her go and deal with whatever Death came after. 

It was dredging up things in her that she thought were long dead and buried. The polar opposite emotions she felt when looking at Rio were so confounding. 

When she got out of the shower, she hung her soaked clothes up to dry and put on some pajamas of the previous owner. It was a matching pajama pants and shirt, but it was silk so she could let the faux pas slide. 

Agatha crawled into the bed and picked up the book she had left on the nightstand. She didn’t want to see Rio again for the rest of the night so she read until she fell asleep, half her mind on the witch downstairs.

Chapter 5

Rio had taken to dressing like a normal person fairly well. Her affinity for long flowing coats and scarves could be construed as peculiar, but as she followed Agatha around the grocery store, no one paid her a second look. 

“I read online that most roommate arrangements come with some kind of shared meal preparation schedule,” Agatha started explaining. She had avoided looking at Rio for the most part and leading the way with the shopping cart was a great excuse for it. 

“I don’t have to eat,” Rio reminded her out of earshot of any other patrons. 

“But I do and I’m tired of cooking,” Agatha picked up a mesh bag of tomatoes and put them in the cart that Rio promptly took out of the cart and replaced with a different one. 

Rio grinned, “Does this mean you miss my cooking?”

“It’s the only thing I miss,” Agatha snapped and pushed the cart away. 

Rio stood in place, amused for a moment before following Agatha. 

On the way back to the house, they passed a construction wall that had been covered in missing persons fliers. 

Agatha glanced around the wall, seeing people who meant nothing to her. Then she spotted a child. A toothy grinned school picture in front of a fake backdrop of trees. 

“What happened to them?” Agatha asked, a rare moment of earnesty. 

Rio’s looked over at the wall of faces. She shook her head. “I don’t know. They’re not dead, but…” She turned away from the wall, keeping stride with Agatha. She sighed. “I don’t know.”

Chapter 6

A few planning days later, Agatha was in the upstairs bath, leaned back with her eyes closed. The water was almost too warm, but the lavender scent was strong and calming. She felt Rio reappear nearby. When she reached out through their invisible tether, she felt her just outside of the bathroom door. 

She opened her eyes and looked toward the door. She could just make out Rio’s legs outstretched from the gap between the floor and the door. She was sitting on the floor outside of the bathroom, though Agatha couldn’t make out what she was doing.

“If you’re going to sit out there, make yourself useful,” Agatha called, trying to be callous, but finding a comfort in Rio’s presence. 

Three small water lilies bloomed in the bath around Agatha, each a pristine white that let out a pleasant floral scent. 

Outside of the bathroom, Rio was exhausted, having spent too long away from Agatha. She was still getting used to having a time limit for reaping. She leaned back on the wall next to the bathroom door with her legs stretched out from under her, only wearing a large t-shirt she’d found in a dresser in the second bedroom. She had a book in her lap that she had planned to read while she waited to feel better, but instead of reading it to herself, she started reading aloud.

Agatha listened to Rio read from a book of poems, letting her calm, even voice relax her even more through the wooden bathroom door. There was a pause in the flow of the poems and Rio started a new one. 

It took Agatha a few lines to listen in, but when she heard Rio's voice quiver she felt the verses hit straight into her chest.

 

"I love you only because it's you the one I love;

I hate you deeply, and hating you

Bend to you, and the measure of my changing love for you

Is that I do not see you but love you blindly."

 

Rio was getting choked up reading, and had to clear her throat to continue. She mumbled the next few lines, then cleared her throat again.

 

"In this part of the story I am the one who

Dies, the only one, and I will die of love because I love you,

Because I love you, Love, in fire and blood. "

 

When Rio choked out the end of the poem, the only sound in the house were the tears that were tracking down Agatha’s face falling into the bathwater. 

After a long pause, there was a rush of wind from under the door and Rio was gone. Agatha’s eyes lowered to the lilies in the water as they wilted from the heat without her protection. Agatha picked one up and brought it to her nose, inhaling the scent as deeply as she could. 

Chapter 7

When Agatha awoke the next morning, the house smelled of cinnamon and warm bread. She pulled the duvet cover tight over her shoulder. It may smell incredible, but she was also still tired.

Agatha reached out through their connection, feeling along it like a safety rope in a dark cave with her mind. Her intention was to let Rio know that she’d be down in a few minutes after pushing herself out of sleep, but it was a half-awake gesture that was sloppy and groping.

In the connection she heard faint screaming. It sounded like Rio was screaming. “No! Get back!” Then more screaming, like she was being torn apart. 

Agatha was out of bed, flying down the stairs, following the tether between them. She dropped to her feet in the doorway of the kitchen, skidding to a stop, hands up, swirling in purple, ready to blast. 

What she found was Rio, turning around confused, holding a cinnamon bun on a plate. 

Agatha looked around for any hidden danger. She swallowed and tried to find an illusion or an enemy. “I…You’re okay.”

“That might be the nicest thing you’ve ever said to me,” Rio set the cinnamon bun down on the counter. 

Agatha was disheveled and wearing green plaid pajama pants with an oversized t-shirt. She let the purple fade and looked around one last time. “You were screaming.”

“I wasn’t,” Rio answered. She shifted her jaw and gestured to the stove. “Tea?”

Agatha reached through the tether again and she was starting to hear it again when Rio, the one in front of her, snapped. “Stop.”

Agatha immediately released the tether. She narrowed her eyes at Rio. “What is that?”

“It is my burden,” Rio answered gravely. She moved to the kettle and turned on the fire beneath it. 

Agatha walked to the table and put her hands on the back of a chair. She was losing patience. “What is your burden?” 

“It doesn’t matter,” Rio muttered. 

Agatha flipped her hand out, a thread of purple grabbing Rio’s wrist and turning her around. “What is it?!” 

“Ghosts!” Rio lost her cool and squared with Agatha, though not aggressively. Her stature was definitely defensive. Her right foot slid back like if she anticipated getting hit and wanted to stay on her feet. 

Agatha furrowed her brow, “Ghosts?” She studied Rio’s hard expression, “Of… everyone?”

“No,” Rio shook her head. “Just a few that need to be watched.”

“Are you fighting them?” Agatha asked, softening. She pulled out her chair and sat down. 

Rio shrugged. “Sometimes.” She took a deep breath. “When they get… rowdy.” 

Agatha drummed her fingers on the table and waited for Rio to turn to her, setting a fork next to her anxious fingers before asking, “Is it dangerous?”

“To anyone but me,” Rio moved back toward the kettle to prepare the teacups.

Agatha picked up the fork and looked at the cinnamon bun. “It hurts you.”

Rio raised her eyebrows in acquiescence. “It can.”

Chapter 8

In her limited capacity, Rio had taken on a few more mortal tendencies like eating and sleeping. Agatha would catch her eating a sandwich in the kitchen in the middle of the night and dozing off on the couch in the living room.

It seemed that Rio was avoiding her while having to stay nearby so she’d huddle up in the guest room or stay in the kitchen when Agatha was in bed. She’d trail far behind when Agatha was out of the house.

The night before Agatha was to meet the witches in the woods, she knocked on the open guest room door where Rio was napping.

Rio slowly opened her eyes and picked up her head a bit.

Agatha leaned on the doorframe, pressing her shoulder into it and drummed her fingers on it. “I uh, wanted to see if you were coming tomorrow. Fresh bodies and all.”

Rio turned on her side and propped her head up. “Yeah. I’ll be there.”

“Okay,” Agatha nodded, “I’ll wait for you then.”

Rio sensed that Agatha was lingering and she was learning how to be tired so she fell back into old habits. She lifted the blanket next to her, sending a silent invitation she hadn’t attempted in centuries.

Agatha hesitated in the doorway, but it was so appealing and no one had touched her in so long. She moved into the room and sat down on the side of the bed away from Rio. 

She moved to lay down, her back to Rio who let the blanket drop over her, letting her arm settle with it. Her arm found its natural place around Agatha’s waist as her body pressed to Agatha’s back.

In the dark, Agatha closed her eyes feeling a rush of touch, not just any touch, but the touch of someone who would protect her and love her. The chaste touch of someone who knew her.

In the dark, Agatha whispered, “I still hate you.”

“Okay,” Rio answered, her breath caressing the back of Agatha’s neck as she drifted off to sleep.

Agatha lay there in that bed, staring at the wall for several minutes. Then she rested her arm on top of Rio’s securing her arm in place. She closed her eyes and let herself fall asleep in the arms of the person she loved and hated the most on the planet.

Chapter 9

“That was tidy,” Agatha looked down at the bodies in front of her. She felt stronger. She felt the magick moving through her body, like a rippling, coiling snake. 

She would normally celebrate, but she was arrested by watching Rio, kneel down next to each body in turn and silently tilt her head. Then she’d reach out a hand, grasp something Agatha couldn’t see, and pull it away. Once she’d gotten each one, she swirled her hands together and pushed up. 

Rio also seemed a bit stronger. The weariness in her face faded a bit and she stood up straighter. 

“This seems mutually beneficial,” Agatha commented, turning away from the bodies on the ground to start the walk back to the car. 

“Always has been,” Rio’s voice was like a whisper in her ear and she was gone again.

Agatha enjoyed her short walk through the woods and her short drive back to their house. She sat at the kitchen table, going over her next move. She needed to figure out where she was going next. She had a laptop open, a map, and a small dish of water with a needle in it. She was using a ruler to draw lines on the map, referencing the laptop and the bowl of water. 

“High tech,” Rio commented, making her way to the refrigerator. 

“Adapt or die,” Agatha answered automatically, but then realized who she was talking to. She waved her hand in the air. “You know what I mean.”

Rio opened the refrigerator and reached for a cardboard carton of blueberries. Her fingers had just hit the fuzzy side of it when she froze. She straightened up, let the refrigerator close, and drew her knife. “Get away from the windows. Don’t look outside.”

Agatha knew when Rio used that tone of voice, she shouldn’t argue. She quickly stood up, backed away from the kitchen and moved to the side so she was hidden from the doorway.

She leaned against the wall and had expected a blast of window glass coming inward. Instead there was a soft hissing noise. She heard her own name being whispered on the air as it became putrid and sour. 

Rio, who Agatha had never really heard have to recite an incantation, started quietly chanting. As her chant went on, the house started to rumble and quake. Agatha held onto the wall as the chandelier swayed above her and the half-broken couch rattled across the floor. 

Agatha chanced a peek around the corner and saw arms and faces in various states of decay, smashing soundlessly on the windows like they were trapped in an aquarium. The chanting seemed to be pushing them away, making space between the house and whatever they were. Revenants if she had to guess. 

And all it took was one eyelidless eyeball to roll her way and Agatha was spotted. There was a horrible screeching and the revenants broke through whatever Rio was doing and charged toward Agatha. 

“I told you not to look!” Rio yelled over the screeches. She gave up on the barrier wall and threw up a wall of writhing twisting vines through the wall of the house, and ran back toward the living room. “We have to go!”

Rio took one step past Agatha that vaulted her into the air and she grabbed Agatha’s hand as she went. Agatha’s only reaction was to blast a hole in the roof as they soared toward it. Once they were above the house, Rio let go and Agatha was in charge of flying herself. 

The revenants sprang after them like rockets shooting into the sky, a streak of gray-black smoke following them. 

Rio turned in the air, flying backwards, throwing blasts at the revenants as they went. Her green-black magick didn’t seem to do much to them so Agatha gave it a shot. Her purple slowed them down enough that Rio could start another chant. This one was different and with each word a revenant disappeared into a puff of smoke. 

Agatha joined her chant and they started disappearing faster. When the last of them poofed away, Agatha pointed to a park most likely in the next town so they could set down. 

As soon as her feet touched the ground, Agatha asked, “What the hell was that?” gesturing to the sky. 

“I’m still figuring it out,” Rio was immediately closed off and turned away from Agatha. 

“Hey,” Agatha grabbed Rio’s arm and spun her around. “You start telling me what the hell is going on. Especially if it’s going to demolish my house.”

Rio’s tongue ran across her bottom teeth on the right side and she seemed to be gathering the words. “I was gone.” She waved her hands in the air, tilting her head back so her hair fell away from her face, and then spinning around to face away from Agatha, starting a short pace away. She turned back around. “Unraveled for maybe a second.” She steepled her fingers as she walked. “Long enough to create an opening for someone else.” 

“Another Green Witch?” Agatha asked, starting to piece things together. She put a booted foot on the seat of a picnic table and hoisted herself into sitting on the top of it.

Rio tilted her head. “Sort of.” She moved her hands between them, revealing the tether connecting them, a single purple cord from Agatha that wrapped around Rio’s entire body in elegant lines and knots. “I took back most of it when I,” she gestured again to the tether and then let it dissipate. “But they still have some power and they released some… revenants to hunt me down.”

“We get rid of the… Other Green Witch ?” Agatha offered. “The revenants…”

“Will be loose, but easier to reclaim,” Rio answered. She put her hands on her hips and looked at Agatha.

“And you don’t know who the Other is because?” Agatha asked, resting her elbows on the tops of her knees and her chin on top of two closed fists. 

Rio furrowed her brow, “Why would I?” 

“So you can… die?” Agatha asked. 

Rio tilted her head from side to side. “Not really, but in a way.”

“I love your straight answers,” Agatha pushed off of the picnic table and stood up. “So, we find a way to hide you, find a way to figure out who is next in line to be the grim reaper, and find some idiot to stand between us and the revenants while we get the Other.”

 Rio looked to the side like she was trying to find an issue with the plan. Or an alternative plan. The problem was that as someone whose job was pretty straight forward, she didn’t have to think herself out of situations very often. “Oookay.” Then she came back around to the beginning of the plan - the part where Agatha was helping her. 

“You can’t become Death,” Rio looked at Agatha. “There is no succession.”

“I hate your job,” Agatha rolled her eyes and shook her head. “I don’t want it.”

Rio shifted her jaw trying to find Agatha’s angle. Rio was smart, but Agatha was crafty in a way Rio had never had to be. She could weave favors and deals and plans into something that seemed so benign. 

“You can’t bring him-” Rio started. 

Agatha moved like a wraith into Rio’s space, lowering her voice to a dangerous level. “If his name comes out of your mouth, I will release this tether right here, right now.”

Rio swallowed and took a step back. A fury that Agatha had never seen directed at her overtook her face, but Agatha refused to back down. They stared at each other, both trying to bore into the other’s brain with daggers from their eyes until Rio huffed, turned around, and disappeared in a breeze. 

“Coward!” Agatha yelled after her. 

Chapter 10

Agatha walked through the park and used a bit of illusion magick to scare a teen walking to their car into dropping their keys and running back into the wooded part of the park. He tripped once, scrambling to get back up, and keep running from the massive, illusory dog Agatha sent after him. 

She picked up his keys and got into his car, wrinkling her nose at the strong patchouli smell in the car. She rolled down the windows and created a soft breeze to move it out. Then she turned on the car, was assaulted with some kind man and guitar music that she turned down almost all the way. 

She wasn’t sure where she was going, but she stopped at a gas station to get some road trip snacks. She flicked her wrist at the cashier and covered them in a cloud of smoke that moved with them while she gathered what she wanted, grabbed her own bag from behind the counter, and walked back out to the car. 

As she approached the car, she saw Rio in the passenger’s seat, one booted foot on the dashboard. She decided that she wanted to be mad that she was in the car. 

“Done throwing a fit?” Agatha snarked, opening the door. 

Rio didn’t answer. She held a folded piece of lined yellow paper out for Agatha between her first two fingers. 

Agatha took it and opened it. It was an address. 

“Get rid of the body and the house is yours,” Rio answered, turning her head to look out the window. 

Agatha was pretty sure she knew where the street was and stuffed the paper in the cupholder. “You couldn’t get rid of the body?”

Rio replied offhanded, “Not my job.”

Chapter 11

The new house was a large Victorian in a neighborhood where the houses were a decent distance apart and they wouldn’t be easily observed. Massive trees surrounded the house, creating a barrier between the house and the outside world. 

The inside was lavishly furnished and Agatha was impressed upon entering. She would never let Rio know that though. “It’ll do.” 

Rio moved to the couch and flopped down on it, sinking in the soft, green fabric. “You better hurry. The body is decomposing.”

Agatha let out a half growl of annoyance before starting her search of the house for a decomposing body. She found it in the bathtub with a plugged in tube tv that appeared to have been resting on the edge of the tub and had fallen in. There was a smoke and burnt hair smell in the bathroom. 

The decomposition process had already started, but the water had cooled in the bathtub to slow it. Agatha flexed her hands toward the body and started lifting it out of the water with her purple. She slowly moved with it to the window, having to carefully keep the water from dripping on her feet. She had to hold the body with one hand and open the window with the other. Then she unceremoniously shot the body out the window. 

She left the window open to let the foul air out and used her magick to pull the bathtub drain. Once the water started draining out of the tub, Agatha breezed out of the room and started surveying the house in earnest. 

She decided that the master bedroom would be hers. There was a massive four-poster bed in the middle of the room and a reading nook of bay windows. Most importantly no one had died in the connected bathroom.

Agatha was opening wardrobes and snooping around when she heard a soft knock on the doorframe. She turned around and found Rio leaned on the frame, hair messy from what seemed to have been a nap. “I’m going to get food. Do you want anything?”

“Uh, yeah,” Agatha was knocked back on her heels at the offer. They had been snippy with each other earlier and if she was honest, Agatha was still holding onto some of it. But Rio, leaning on that door frame, her hair sort of tossed around and her clothes ill-fitting and rumpled, there was something spectacular about her. 

Agatha shook herself out of her trance with a swallow and waved flippantly. “Whatever you’re having.” 

Rio put her hands in her pockets and nodded. She started to turn away, but turned back. “Do you remember when we were on that boat in Pacaya Samiria and you translated those carvings in those old rocks at the mason’s place?”

Agatha furrowed her brow in thought and then snapped her fingers. “The serum? It’s was a- It made spirits corporeal.”

Rio nodded. “You think it would work on revenants?”

“Only one way to find out,” Agatha smiled. She pushed some framed photos off of the nightstand and picked up a notepad that had been under them. “We need a potions guy.” She wrote down her short list. “Three more witches. Protection, divination, and potions.”

“Do you think any of them work at the burger place I’m going to?” Rio asked. 

Agatha looked at her list and then ripped the page off. “I’m coming with you. We have a stop to make.”

Chapter 12

“Why are we waiting?” Rio asked before taking a sip from the straw of her drink. She eyed the building across from them. Several faded and broken neon signs announced ‘Madame Calderu’s Psychic Readings’. 

“I just watched a whole bridesmaid party go in there,” Agatha took a fry from the bag and popped it into her mouth. “I don’t have the temperament for them today, or any day.”

Rio smiled, watching Agatha’s root around in the bottom of the burger bag for stray fries. It was a painfully normal moment sitting in a dead man’s 1975 Mercedes across the street from the psychic’s shop. Before Agatha could catch her enjoying the moment, Rio turned back to her own food, content to keep the moment for herself. 

It was so easy to fall back into old habits. When Agatha was trying to rearrange herself in the driver’s seat, Rio just held her hand out so Agatha could put her drink in it and have more space. Agatha tossed her pickles into Rio’s burger wrapper without asking and didn’t speak on it. They accidentally switched drinks a few times and no one fussed. It was so easy to move around in each other’s space even though the tension would eviscerate a lesser being.

The door burst open in the psychic shop and a young woman ran out, tears and mascara streaming down her face. Two more young people ran after her, followed by one who was walking and laughing so hard she was in tears as well. 

Once they had cleared the block, Agatha got out of the car and walked across the street. She waited for Rio to saunter up behind her and warned, as she opened the door. “Don’t be weird.”

In defiance of the order, Rio hissed at Agatha as she walked past, into the shop. 

It smelled as one would expect a psychic shop to smell. Incense was still burning near the back of the shop and Rio peeled off to the right, inspecting the retail section while Agatha approached the table with the crystal ball on it. From the back of the building a woman with long, curly salt and pepper hair breezed in. “Welcome.”

She had a congenial smile on her face until she saw Agatha standing here. “What do you want?”

“I need something divined,” Agatha put her hands on the table where it seemed like readings were done. 

“Everyone knows what your past and future is Ms. Harkness,” Lilia Calderu crossed her arms. “Witch murder.” She tilted her head and seemed to realize that someone else was in her shop. She looked over at Rio, who wasn’t looking at her and narrowed her eyes. “What are you doing here?”

“She is the problem,” Agatha answered. She materialized the tether between them and yanked on her end so Rio was pulled backward toward them. She stumbled for a moment, but caught her footing. Her face morphed from serene shopper to furious indignation in an instant. She immediately drew her dagger and leapt at Agatha in a righteous fury. 

“Stop!” Lilia yelled, hands up. “Not in my shop!” 

Rio twisted in mid-air, several feet away and above Agatha, becoming a breeze speckled with dead leaves that shot through Agatha’s space. Agatha threw up an arm over her face and the leaves stuck in the thick sleeves of her coat, poking her arms through it.

When Rio coalesced next to her, she slipped her dagger back into the sheath. Before Agatha could react, Rio pulled a wad of money out of her pocket and dropped it on the reading table. 

Lilia’s eyes got wide. 

Agatha was irritated as she shook leaves off of her coat, but seemed to calm at the sight of the money. She adjusted her stance to be less hostile and more presentable. “So? How about it?”

For the moment, Lilia ignored Agatha and looked to Rio. “Your aura is different.”

Rio shrugged. “It’s split.”

“That’s what it is,” Lilia snapped. She moved to Rio and started looking around her head and shoulders. “Fractured is more like it.” She lifted Rio’s arm and examined her palm. Then she dropped it, blinking hard. “Shouldn’t have done that.” 

Rio looked at her own hand, not sure what was weird or different about it. 

There was a power surge in the building and Lilia’s eyes got wide. Her attention shot to Rio. “You have to win.” She stood up and put her hands on Rio’s face. “There are divergent futures. You have to win to keep us on the right path.”

Rio looked at Agatha over Lilia’s shoulder. She wasn’t sure how to take the information. 

“What are the chances you know the protection witch and potions witch that will give us the best chance?” Agatha asked, moving to Lilia to put her hands on Lilia’s shoulders and guide her away from Rio. 

“I’ll need some time,” Lilia turned and walked around the table to sit in front of her crystal ball. She didn’t reference it or touch it. She pulled her phone out of her cardigan pocket and started poking at it. 

“Ookay,” Agatha pulled her coat tight around herself. “How long? It sounded like we don’t have a lot of time.”

“Some things can’t be rushed,” Lilia waved her off. Then she froze in place, dismissing arm in the air. The lights around them started to flicker and the building rumbled. She cocked her head. “Chicken Coop.” Then she frowned. “Kale.”

“Jen Kale?” Agatha groaned and rolled her head back. “Can we reroll that second one? There’s gotta be a better potion maker.”

“That’s what I got,” Lilia shrugged. She looked at her phone and turned it toward them. “Chicken Coop is a bar at the edge of town. Underground magick fighting. You’ll find your protection witch there.”

Chapter 13

The Chicken Coop had more motorcycles outside than Agatha anticipated. She grabbed Rio’s arm and pulled her around the side of the bar. “We have to change clothes.” She pulled her purple from her palm and sprinkled some around herself and around Rio, changing their conspicuous clothes into jeans and button down shirts. Rio was wearing a green and blue flannel and Agatha had a slightly dressier purple button down. 

Agatha was unbuttoning the cuffs around her wrists and rolling up her sleeves. “Did you know about the divergent futures?”

Rio shook her head. “I can’t see into the future.”

Agatha accepted what Rio said at face value. “Alright,” Agatha stepped around Rio. “I haven’t lost yet and I don’t plan to start now.”

Inside, the bar had wood paneled walls, a stage with a wall of chicken wire in front of it, and a significant number of Westview’s working class. 

“Ugh,” Agatha wrinkled her nose. “I hate it here.” She folded her arms in front of herself and gestured for Rio to peel off. “Start a fight. That should bring her out.”

Agatha wasn’t really surprised that Rio didn’t instantly start a fight on the other side of the bar. They were both weaving through the crowd, Agatha pretending to be genial and Rio slipping from one grouping of shadows to the next. 

Agatha had an eye out, scanning each person she passed, but she wasn’t sure what a protection witch would look like. If she’d have weapons or a distinctive look like a leather jacket with spikes or a mohawk. 

It had been far too long inside of the junky place and she hoped that as someone more conspicuous than her, Rio would have drawn her attention already. 

However, when she looked across the bar, she didn’t find Rio diligently searching for the witch. At the bar, Rio had been cornered by a woman a bit taller than her with a charming smile and a casual hand in her pocket. 

She watched her brush Rio’s hair away from her face and lean in to speak into Rio’s ear. 

Rio seemed to genuinely smile and lean back casually on the bar. 

Agatha could not get over the audacity of someone flirting so blatantly with Rio in front of her . Who was this person? Some common witch. Some common witch that would be powerless and dead soon. 

Agatha pushed people out of the way, drawing the attention of everyone, including Rio and the woman as she approached. 

“Hey,” Agatha asked, pushing past the woman to speak directly to Rio, annoyance making her jaw tight when she asked, “Find the protection witch?”

There was a moment of assessment before Rio broke out in a knowing smile. She tilted her head with an innocent blink, “Who?”

“Oh a protection witch?” the woman with the audacity asked, taking a step to the side so she could be part of the conversation, “Alice?”

Agatha did another reorganization of her emotions, pushing the jealousy and possessiveness into the deep dark part of her psyche she pretended wasn’t there. She leaned casually on the bar. “You know her?” Agatha asked, her hand slipping behind Rio, across the wooden bar to grab the bright green handle of a cocktail knife.

A hand clasped around her wrist and the bartender said, “Drop it.”

“Drop what?” Agatha asked, feigning innocence. When Rio stepped to the side and everyone could see what she was holding, she smiled sheepishly, “Oh I thought that was my drink.” 

“You haven’t gotten a drink yet,” the bartender put her hands on her hips. 

“I assume you’re out of bordeaux,” Agatha sneered, crossing her arms and resting the back of her hand against the bottom of her chin. She turned back to the woman who had been about to get stabbed, “So, Alice?”

“I think she’s about to go on,” the woman gestured behind her toward a large pit in the middle of the room. 

With an appalling lack of fanfare, the crowds started to move to the pit. 

Agatha had no interest in the fight. If Alice was a pit fighter then that was all she needed to know. She needed someone who was going to throw their body in front of her without being scared of the pain and if she did this kind of thing for fun, she definitely wasn’t scared of the pain. 

She picked up a pen from a jar behind the bar and a napkin. Then she scribbled down her new address. She passed it to the bartender. “Tell Alice to come to this address. I have a very lucrative job for her.”

“That’s not my jo-” the bartender started, but Agatha was already walking away. 

Agatha had to force herself not to look back to see if Rio was following her. She was stacking reason upon reason to be mad on top of each other, hoping they would add up to something she could stand on. But so far it was shaky. 

She was relieved when she pushed open the door to the outside world and turned to go to the car, when she glanced out of her peripheral vision, she saw Rio walking out the door as well. 

Chapter 14

The drive was silent and Rio seemed in deep thought, while Agatha was deeply thinking about what Rio could be thinking about. 

When they pulled into the driveway, they found Lilia sitting on the front porch steps, a large canvas garment bag over her shoulder. 

“Uh hey,” Agatha tried to play off her confusion and mild alert. “What are you doing here, Lucy?”

“Lilia,” Lilia corrected her. “This address was on a piece of mail in the money you gave me.” She glanced at Rio and then back at Agatha. “We need to talk.”

Agatha made some tea as the reluctant hostess while Rio and Lilia sat at the table. Lilia kept looking at Rio and then quickly looking away like she was trying to catch a glimpse of the sun without going blind. 

“I can’t imagine that you showing up on our doorstep is good news,” Agatha brought two cups of tea to the table and sat down, setting one in front of Lilia and pointedly not making one for Rio. 

“I don’t get a lot of answers for questions that I ask, but I get a lot of answers for things I didn’t,” she replied, putting her hands around the teacup. She seemed to remember something and unzipped one of the pockets of the garment bag and pulled out a notebook. It was an old leather bound notebook with soft sides and pages that weren’t all the same width. 

She opened it to the page marked with a red ribbon and turned it toward them. “I saw this a few hours ago.”

The sketch was bordered on the bottom by a sketched silhouette of a town. Above it, pencil shaded clouds parted and a heavily colored-in rift appeared in between them. 

“What is this?” Agatha asked, “What am I looking at?”

“It opens,” Lilia pointed to the dark lightning bolt shaded in in the sky. “The door you guard. It opens.”

Agatha looked to Rio’s reaction for how she should react. She wasn’t sure what to do with the information herself. She knew there were parts of what Rio did that she had never explained. 

Rio was stone still, staring at Lilia’s finger pointing at the chasm. She had stopped breathing and stopped blinking. Her eyes glazed over as she was lost in thought. 

She blinked and then abruptly stood up. The action startled Agatha, who instinctively pushed her chair back, away from the table. 

“We need the potions witch,” Rio started toward the door without looking back. “Now.”

Chapter 15

“Do you know the end?” Agatha asked, after they’d all been closed into the elevator that was shooting upwards. It wasn’t directed at Rio who hadn’t said a word and was staring at the elevator doors like they were keeping her caged. 

“I don’t know the order of what I see,” Lilia told her. 

“What she saw was a thousand years ago and it almost ended everything,” Rio finally spoke up just before the doors opened. She stepped out, leaving the two witches behind her aghast until the doors started to close again. 

Agatha stuck her arm out and stopped the doors so she and Lilia could scramble out after her across the polished marble floors. 

“Ms. Kale isn’t having visitors today,” a security guard at a massive set of double doors was telling Rio as she approached. With a wave of her hand, Rio sent the security guard flying across the room and with a wave of her other hand, the doors in front of them blasted off their hinges. 

“She’s not one for knocking huh?” Lilia asked Agatha as they followed, stepping over the splintered shards of the wooden door. 

“This is her knocking,” Agatha answered, her voice laced with an unspoken warning to stay out of her way. 

When they caught up with Rio, she was standing in a very plush and pastel living room in front of a woman in a bathrobe with under eye patches on, holding a coffee cup. One wall of the living room was a very organized wall of clear apothecary shelves, all lit from beneath, neatly displaying their contents. The perpendicular wall was all windows overlooking the downtown area. 

“I should have known,” Jen Kale leaned to the side to see around Rio, as she peeled off her under eye patches. “Is this your demon pet, Harkness?”

Agatha put her hands up. “I’m just the entourage.”

“Yeah right,” Jen turned back to Rio. “What the hell do you want?”

“A serum to turn ghosts corporeal,” Rio answered. “First seen in Pacaya Samiria. Pre-Columbian.” 

Jen scoffed and started to turn away, “Come back with a real request.”

Agatha slinked up next to Rio and leaned close to her. “Told you she couldn’t do it,” Agatha said, sotto voce.

Jen paused her retreat back into her high rise apartment. “Unless you have the ingredient list on you, no one can do it.”

Lilia moved to stand on the other side of Rio. “It is a matter of some importance.” She cleared her throat. “There is a struggle over the dominion of death. There is a fork in the road ahead and… one of them is… empty.”

Jen shifted her jaw. “And you are?”

“Lilia Calderu,” Lilia introduced herself with a reverent nod. “First generation Divination.”

Jen hummed. “I recognize that name.”

“I have a store downtown,” Lilia offered. 

Jen shook her head. “Leggings?”

“Oh,” Lilia nodded, “That too.”

“Well you owe me a new pair because the fleece ones I bought two months ago already came apart at the seams,” Jen put a hand on her hip. 

Lilia threw her hands up and turned her head away. “Sorry, no refunds.”

Agatha was generally annoyed with anyone being more annoying than her so when she rolled her eyes away from the conversation, she saw Rio tense next to her. Her instinct was to reach over and grab her arm, unsure of what was going to happen. 

She watched Rio’s eyes search all of the floor to ceiling windows, methodically and frantically. Then she found what she was looking for and Agatha’s eyes followed it. 

It was another cloud of revenants moving in their direction. A bulk of black and gray smoke with faces and hands reaching out the front of it. 

“That’s why we need the serum,” Agatha kept hold of Rio’s upper arm and pointed at the quickly darkening sky. 

Jen and Lilia both turned at the same time to follow Agatha’s direction. 

“What the hell is that?” Jen demanded, gesturing to the quickly approaching anomaly.

“Revenants,” Lilia breathed out. “A lot of them.”

“Get back,” Rio moved her arm in front of Agatha, a hand on her waist, pushing her behind her. 

It was a stupid and chivalrous thing for Rio to do. Agatha had helped last time the revenants followed them after all. But it was instinct, just like when Agatha’s hand went to Rio’s waist from behind and squeezed just above her hip. “We have to go.”

It was just loud enough for Lilia and Jen to hear it. Lilia was willing to believe her immediately and was the first one to turn from the coming onslaught to move to the interior of the building. 

Agatha pulled Rio backwards a few steps before turning around to follow Lilia. 

It took a moment of realization on Jen’s part, but she grabbed a large, soft sided leather bag from under her couch and started following them. 

They were all in the hallway when the glass of the apartment shattered like wet tissue paper, blowing cold air and shrapnel in through the broken door. 

Rio waited for Jen in the doorway of the open elevator Lilia and Agatha were already in. Jen careened into the elevator right into Lilia and Rio stepped into the elevator. 

Jen waved her hand at the doors that glowed with nearly imperceptible pink runes and closed just as the first featureless face smashed into it. The elevator started shooting downward and Lilia grabbed onto the closest person to her. Rio allowed her to hang onto her arm, even putting her hand on top of Lilia’s as the elevator shot downward.

“What did you do?” Agatha demanded of Jen.

Lilia closed her eyes, “I don’t want to die in an elevator.”

“Not very many people die in elevators from falling,” Rio commented offhandedly, like stating a fact would keep them from dying or make Lilia less frightened.

“It’s an emergency exit,” Jen answered. Nearly all at once a burst of air pushed at the bottom of the elevator and it slowed to a gentle stop. The doors slid open like a reverse guillotine. Jen sighed deeply. “I’m glad it worked.”

“You’ve never used it before?” Agatha gawked, following as Jen strode out of it. 

“Not all of us are constantly getting chased out of every place we’ve ever lived by an angry mob,” Jen answered, moving through the parking garage that opened up before them. “I’m out of here.”

Agatha was going to lay in bed and force Rio to stay and explain herself, but Lilia’s warning got her up and moving. She went to the open bedroom door and yelled, “How’s that potion coming, Kale?”

“It’s still simmering!” she yelled back. “Someone didn’t get everything I asked for.” 

Agatha kicked off her pajama pants and pulled on her own pants, muttering. “Can’t please some people.”

Rio handed Agatha her shirt and dusted off her long, purple coat that had fallen to the ground in her process of getting undressed for a bath the night before. 

“You’re not going to do anything stupid, are you?” Agatha asked, shrugging on her coat. 

Rio moved Agatha to the full length mirror in the room and had her face it. She took the locket Agatha had always kept on her and looped it around Agatha’s neck. She carefully fastened it in place and met Agatha’s eyes over her shoulder. “Most definitely.”

Chapter 24

“When was the last time you drove?!” Jen asked, holding on to anything and everything to keep from bouncing into the door or falling out of her seat into Alice. 

“It’s my first time!” Rio cackled, leaning forward in her seat with both hands on the wheel. “Can you tell?”

“Where the fuck are we?” Alice yelled. She unbuckled her seatbelt and crawled back into the third seat with Agatha. She could see the cloud of revenants following them through the field they were rumbling across. 

“The last sign I saw said Massachusetts,” Lilia grabbed onto the handle over the window to brace herself. 

“How did we get here so fast?” Jen yelled over the rumbling of the rocks kicking up and hitting the bottom of the SUV.

“Lilia,” Rio glanced in the rearview mirror and said urgently. “Here.” She thrust a small gathering bag in her hand and spoke to her very quickly in a language no one else understood. 

Alice saw something flying at them, faster than they were driving and grabbed onto Agatha, pulling her into the floor of the SUV. 

Lilia looked gravely at Rio for just a moment before nodding. She leaned to the side against the door of the SUV as the back window shattered. An entire person’s body flew through the back window, over the middle seat and burst through the windshield. 

When everyone got brave enough to look up from their places of cover, the SUV was slowing down. Agatha pushed Alice off of herself and crawled forward to the middle seat. 

“Where’s Rio?” Jen asked, finding the driver’s seat empty as the SUV continued to slow and drive toward a grove of trees. 

Agatha grabbed onto her chest, making the tether visible. She watched it stretched out in a line away from her getting thinner and thinner as it went. It seemed to still be stable and she was thankful she hadn’t also been yanked out of the windshield. 

“There!” Agatha pointed. In front of the SUV, a figure seemed to be flying very fast, had burst through the SUV and grabbed Rio, pulling her out of the car with it.

Lilia grabbed the wheel and navigated them as far into a grove of trees in front of them as she could without being able to use the gas. Eventually, they stopped by hitting a tree, barely tapping it. 

Everyone scrambled out of the SUV in a flurry. “What do we do?” Jen asked. 

“Stick to the plan,” Lilia stated, tucking the bag Rio had given her into her coat pocket. “We take care of the revenants. She takes care of the Other.”

There was a massive boom, and a spray of dirt and trees flew away from them, like a meteor had hit the ground a few hundred feet in front of them.

Jen hoisted the professional pesticide sprayer tank on her back and picked up the handle. “I hope this sprays really far away.”

Alice stepped in front of her, summoning her spectral orange shield. “Me too.” She looked up at the cloud of revenants still flying toward them. 

Agatha summoned her purple into both of her hands, ready to start blasting, but also listening to the fight Rio seemed to be having on the other side of the trees. She knew she was supposed to be on Team Revenant, but she was itching to run after Rio.

“Incoming!” Alice yelled. 

Jen pointed the end of the pesticide sprayer, filled with her corporeal potion up at the approaching cloud. She pulled the trigger, bracing herself for some kind of kickback. A very gentle mist started spraying over them. 

Agatha sputtered, having gotten some in her mouth. “Disgusting. That tastes like old carrots.” 

“You want try that again?” Alice asked. “This time with magick?” She threw a blast out at a revenant that pushed the one back, but the rest were still rushing toward them. 

“Right,” Jen put her finger on the trigger again and concentrated. She pulled the air around her down and into the liquid holding tank on her back. She pulled the trigger again and the potion started spraying outward like a firehose, creating a protective circle around the three of them. 

The revenants ran right into the circle, the potion landing on them. Where it touched, the smoky spirits started to harden. Feathery outlines of faces and arms got hard and turned to stone. The weight of being corporeal seemed to pull them down toward the ground and slow their flying speed. Agatha and Alice started blasting, beams of orange and purple picking them off one at a time. 

Then Jen realized something was wrong. 

Three of them. 

Three of them?

Jen looked around, continuing the spray of the cloud of revenants. “Where’s Lilia?”

In the grove, somewhere between the rest of the coven fighting revenants and Rio fighting the Other, Lilia followed the small hand drawn map Rio had given her. It was filled with exactly the kind of details a normal, non-green witch would find extraneous. The trees and grass and rocks in the area were drawn in perfect number and shapes, and directions were dependent on that. 

She eventually came upon what she was supposed to find. There was an indention in the ground, a long oval of rocks protected by a tree that had fallen over it, but not landed on it. Like a moss covered tent. 

She closed her eyes, said a prayer to the Divine Mother, and extracted the components Rio had given her. She didn’t know who was under all of the rocks, but she prayed that the sigil would work.

“Aren’t you tired?” the Other asked, looking at her fingers that had been sharpened to dangerous points. 

Rio shook her head, the gash on her stomach healing near instantaneously. “I just got here.” She ran a hand through her hair to get it out of her face and shrugged. “I mean, I’m a little bored.”

The Other licked her bottom lip. “Why not let someone else take over? Dominion of the dead deserves a new queen.” 

Rio threw her head back and laughed. “Queen?” She slid her right foot back to ready for another attack. “You have a massive delusion of what my job is. I’m a glorified courier with a pretty set of keys.” She wagged her knife at the Other. 

The Other narrowed her eyes, her focus moving to the knife. “Doesn’t matter,” The Other raised her hands, dripping with a green sap, preparing for the next round of attacks. “The keys are mine.”

Rio adjusted her grip on the knife. “You’ll have to kill me first.”

The Other grinned, canine fangs extending downward. “With pleasure.”

The fighting had died down on one side of her as Lilia finished the sigil she had created with the powders Rio had given her. She finished the incantation when she heard footsteps raking through the leaves behind her. 

“What are you doing?!” Agatha bellowed. She tossed a blast directly at Lilia who threw her arms up to block it. 

There were tears in Agatha’s eyes when she advanced on Lilia, ready to abandon all magick and throttle her with her bare hands. “Do you know whose grave that is?” Agatha demanded, having just realized herself a moment ago. 

“She said it was for you,” Lilia took a step back, but was steadfast in her words. 

“Rio?” Agatha asked, stopping near Lilia, just out of arm's reach. 

Lilia nodded. “She said you’d understand when it was all over.” 

Agatha looked over at the grave of her son that she had dug hundreds of years ago. It was mostly intact with a fallen tree protecting it. The rocks had just sunk down a bit into the ground. Now there was a sigil on top of it in colored powders and Lilia’s magick. She looked it over trying to decipher what it was for. “A-a stasis? What does that do?”

Lilia shook her head. “She didn’t tell me why.”

Agatha set her jaw and started to move toward the grave, intent on clearing off the powders and the magick. 

“Agatha,” Lilia reached out and put her hand on Agatha’s that had just started to glow with purple. “I think you should trust her.”

“You don’t know her,” Agatha dropped the magick and looked at Lilia.

“But you do,” Lilia smiled gently. 

Agatha swallowed and wiped her eyes. She held Lilia’s eyes for a long moment before she heard Rio cackling in the distance. 

Agatha looked up. There was one person that could answer her questions. She took off, marching in the direction of the fight over the dominion over death, not entirely sure which side she would be joining when she got there.

The field Rio and the Other were fighting in was mostly upturned dirt by the time Agatha got there. It looked like the entire place had been bombed for days. The Other ran up a small dirt hill, launched herself off of it and came down in a flurry of sharp finger blades and teeth. 

Rio shoulder rolled out of the way, a puff of dirt coming up where the Other landed. The Other had pivoted to leap at Rio, but Agatha saw Rio see her out of the corner of her eye. They held each other’s eyes over the long distance, Agatha questioning and Rio resolute. In an instant before she could dodge out of the way of the aerial assault coming her way, Rio turned her knife outward and got the Other in the stomach as she landed on top of her. 

Rio abandoned the knife, shouldered her off, and took a step away as the green sap from the Other started pouring out around where the knife was still protruding from the wound. 

The Other looked down like they couldn’t believe they’d been stabbed, then their shock faded into a deranged grin. She put both of her hands on the hilt of the knife and pulled it out of herself. Her wound oozed and she wavered but she didn’t seem to care. She was holding the knife like it was everything she’d ever wanted. 

And her first swipe was at Rio. 

Agatha made the tether corporeal and yanked it, pulling Rio out of range of the blade. 

“So that’s how you did it,” the Other started floating, rising above the dirt. She set down near Agatha whose hands started glowing purple. The Other smiled serenely and looked at the blade in her hand again. “I’ll make you a deal.” She gestured to Agatha with the tip of it. “I’ll let you live and you get your pick of any spirit behind the veil.” She turned her arm to point the blade at what appeared to be a massive rift forming in the sky. 

“Wha-” Agatha started. She opened her mouth to speak again, but she couldn’t. “I-” It was a chance. It was a chance with Nicky. A chance to bring him back. She swallowed and looked at Rio who was on one knee from when Agatha had pulled her out of the way. 

Rio nodded once, keeping her eyes locked on Agatha’s. 

There was only a moment’s hesitation before Agatha released the tether, Rio fell to the ground, writhing again and screaming. 

The Other started laughing maniacally, every mote of black and green that fell off of Rio was absorbed into her as the briar crown that would occasionally rest on Rio’s head started to form on hers. 

The rift in the sky started widening and very suddenly the bottom of it shot to the ground. Agatha could hear Rio screaming in agony. She could see her breaking up and floating away, absorbed by the new Green Witch. 

But then she heard him. An echo in her head. “Mama?” 

“Nicky,” Agatha turned from the end of the battle in front of her and ran for the rift. When the ground under her started growing and shifting and decaying, she left her feet and started flying toward it. She could feel him. She used her mind to pull him to the surface. When she arrived at the rift a small, familiar hand reached through the veil and she grabbed it. 

The second she grabbed it, a golden ball shot through the sky away from the rift. 

The grass and the trees all around were rapidly growing and decaying and dying. Flowers pushed through the dirt and withered nearly immediately. Everything was in a constant flux of life and death. 

The hand in Agatha’s hand was gone, but she took off like a bullet, following the mote of gold, back toward the grove where she’d left Lilia. She could see everything around her growing and dying and blooming and decaying. 

Alice was running in the opposite direction toward the fray, but Agatha paid her no mind. She could feel him. 

When she landed, Lilia was digging in the dirt where the grave was. Everything inside of the sigil she had set was still blooming and growing. Agatha tore at the flowers and the vines, using magic to carefully sculpt away the dirt. 

She heard him whimper in the dirt and finally reached in to pull him out. He was covered in dirt, but showed no signs of decay. He coughed and sputtered as she grabbed him, holding him close to her. 

“You’re alive,” she sobbed. She felt his arms encircle her as well. 

He wiped his face off on her shoulder. “What happened?”

She closed her eyes and rocked him in her arms. She pulled away to look at his face. His perfect face. She pushed the dirty hair out of his eyes and hugged him again. “You’re okay. I’ve got you.”

Everything else faded around them. There was shrieking and explosions. There was a darkening sky and then a soft gentle rain started to fall. 

When she had finally remembered there was a world outside of her arms, she looked around wondering if she needed to get Nicky to safety. 

But when she looked around, she saw Lilia respectfully taking shelter a distance away under a tree and Jen moving to join her. Agatha looked past her and the rift in the sky was gone. She looked toward where she had left Rio screaming and in agony, but she couldn’t see it through the trees. 

“Why are you crying?” Nicky asked, looking at Agatha’s face. He took her face in his hands, her tears and the rain running between them. 

“I’m happy to see you,” she smiled and pulled him close again. 

She heard him inhale sharply a moment later. She looked up to see Alice propping up a very faint looking Rio. Her Death crown was firmly on her head and her knife in her hand. 

“What happened?” Agatha stood up straight, keeping her hand on Nicky’s shoulder. 

“Death broke the rules,” Rio answered with a wry and tired smile. “So there had to be a new one.” She sheathed her knife.

Agatha took a moment to process what had just happened. “You let her become Death so she would break the rules and -” 

“You’re here!” Nicky called, breaking away from Agatha to run to Rio. 

She let go of Alice and dropped to one knee to intercept him. He hugged her tight around the neck and she closed her eyes, tears leaking out of her bottom lashes. She held him around his back and squeezed him tight. 

“Are you hurt?” he asked softly. 

She smiled and pulled him away, holding him at arm’s length to get a good look at him. “Not anymore.” She pulled him back in and kissed his forehead, then released him. 

He ran back to Agatha, hopping into her arms. “Are we sleeping out here tonight? I’m hungry.”

Chapter 26: Epilogue 2

Alice seemed to pause. “Yeah?”

“Thank you.”

“Wow, someday I’ll come into my house and it’ll be quiet,” Agatha moved to stand next to Rio. 

“I thought it was Rio’s house,” Alice offered, looking up from the basketball game. 

“Only legally because she’s the only one of us that has a legitimate job and provable income,” Jen replied, looking up from her laptop, “You know, besides…” She ran her thumb across her throat when Nicky wasn’t looking. 

“They don’t just give these mugs out to anyone,” Rio looked up at Agatha and took a sip from the mug in her hand, showing the front of it that said, #1 Daddy . 

Agatha leaned over and whispered to Rio. “Don’t make me kill you in front of your son.”

Rio chuckled and used the proximity to sneak a kiss. “You can’t kill me.” 

“We’ll see about that,” Agatha’s threat dissipated with the kiss. 

“I still have my shop,” Lilia replied. “That is provable income.”

Jen blinked and looked at Lilia. “Really?”

Lilia rolled her eyes. “Not much, but it’s there.” She found Nicky in the chaos of the kitchen. “You want to help set the table?”

“Yes!” Nicky bounced over to help Lilia carry plates and silverware to the table. 

Rio took Agatha’s hand and pulled her into her lap. Agatha wrapped an arm around Rio’s shoulders and rested her head on Rio’s. 

“How’d it go?” Rio asked casually.

“I keep forgetting to go,” Rio shrugged. “But they think I’m on an archeological dig or something.” She looked at Nicky and then back at Agatha. “I stashed some Danish king’s stuff in a bear cave a while ago. I’ll go get it next week.” She paused. “Have you thought about the other thing?”

Agatha looked at Rio warningly. “It’s dangerous.”

“We’ll both be there,” Rio replied. “We can all be there.”

Agatha flexed her jaw and looked at Rio. Then she looked at Nicky. She looked back at Rio. “Fine. But if anything happens to him, I get to flay you.”

“Deal,” Rio smiled. 

Agatha narrowed her eyes at Rio. “You’re a bad influence.”

“How is he going to be a proper witch without knowing how?” Rio asked back. 

“Yeah, yeah,” Agatha kissed her quickly and stood up. “Some of us didn’t have proper coven upbringings and didn’t learn to hexenbesen until being chased out of Swansea.”

“Hexenbesen?” Alice perked up at the word, stopping her stirring. “Are we doing it?”

Everyone’s eyes snapped to Agatha and the room was quiet.

“After dinner,” Agatha announced to the room. “If everyone is up for it, we are going to take Nicky on his first hexenbesen.”

There was an explosion of excitement from the rest of the coven. 

“It’s flying?” his eyes got wide after he caught several pieces of several different stories about what they were about to do. He looked to his Mama. “Mama? It’s flying?”

Agatha couldn’t stop a smile at his excitement. She nodded, “Yes.”

He thrusted both of his fists up in the air, “Yes!”
