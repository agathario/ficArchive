Chapter 1

Agatha Harkness sat in the green room, her fingers tracing endless circles over her worry ring. The muted roar of laughter and applause from the studio audience beyond the curtain only made the familiar thrum of nerves in her chest more pronounced. Her heart raced, the rhythm as uneven as it always was before she stepped into the spotlight. This was routine for her—these pre-show jitters that tightened her throat and made her palms sweat. She’d learned long ago to channel them, to turn them into fuel for her chaotic stage presence. Still, no amount of success or accolades had ever dulled this moment: the quiet, vulnerable pause before everything began.

She absently smoothed a hand over her perfectly styled hair, an artful mass of soft, natural curls that framed her face. Deep rich chocolate sprayed with glitter that glinted faintly under the fluorescent green room lights—a detail that her stylist had insisted would "catch just right under the stage lights." Her makeup was bold and smudged to perfection, her dark eyeliner winged out in sharp points that seemed to echo the sarcasm in her tone whenever she spoke. Her outfit was every bit as deliberate: a cropped leather jacket over a black lace body, paired with high-waisted pants in a deep plum that hugged her long legs. Her heeled boots were scuffed just enough to give her look an edge of rebellion, like she’d just walked offstage at a concert but somehow managed to make it glamorous.

This was the Agatha Harkness the world knew: sharp, chaotic, untouchable. The industry loved to call her a force of nature, a wild card, someone who could throw out a sarcastic comment so perfectly timed it would dominate Twitter for days. Her fans had built her up into a myth, an icon who could do no wrong and she played into it when the cameras were rolling. What they didn’t see—what no one outside her tight circle of friends and family ever saw—was the girl sitting in this chair, fidgeting with more than one worry ring and chewing on the inside of her cheek.

The people closest to her, though? They knew the truth. They knew the version of her who called home every chance she got, who sent voice notes to her best friend at two a.m. because her anxiety was keeping her awake. They knew the version of her who swore she’d never get used to the noise of fame, who still jumped when fans screamed her name in public because a part of her couldn’t believe it was her they were screaming for. They knew the vulnerable, self-deprecating woman who needed to decompress with hours of reruns of NCIS after every show.

Her family—especially her mother, Lilia—had seen the cracks in the armor long before the world ever knew who she was. Agatha owed everything to her mom. She’d been the one to pull her out of the wreckage of her childhood, to show her what love and stability looked like after years of never knowing them. Growing up in an abusive home, Agatha had thought safety and happiness were things other people had, things she was never meant to touch. It wasn’t until Lilia had fostered her, then adopted her, that she realised there was more to life than surviving. Her mom’s belief in her hadn’t just saved her—it had built her, brick by brick, into the person she was today.

But sometimes, sitting alone like this, she still felt like the scared, angry teenager she used to be. She thought of Lilia now, of the quiet house she still called home, and felt a pang of homesickness so sharp it caught her off guard.
