Summary

A century after Earth’s collapse, a lonely android named Ri-0 wanders the ruins of New York, teaching herself to dance to songs meant for two. When a scientist aboard humanity’s orbiting ark discovers footage of the impossible, an android who feels emotion, she risks everything she has worked towards to return to a planet long declared dead.

Chapter 1

The wind never truly left New York anymore. It threaded its way through the hollow frames of skyscrapers and across avenues buried in sand, moving through shattered windows and rusted subway grates as if the city itself were breathing in long, tired exhales. 

The streets had long since forgotten the sound of engines or footsteps, and yet something still moved among the ruins with quiet persistence.

Ri-0 walked carefully through what had once been Midtown, her steps deliberate, measured, almost ceremonial. She had been built to resemble a woman, though the resemblance had softened with time. Her frame retained the symmetry of human design, but a century of exposure had etched its history across her body. Her skin, modelled after warm brown human tones, bore the subtle unevenness of age where synthetic material had weathered under sun and sand.

Fine seam lines traced along her jaw and neck where panels had been replaced decades earlier, faint light sometimes catching at their edges when her internal systems surged. Rust gathered in the creases of her fingers, and her left shoulder emitted a soft click at predictable intervals (every sixth rotation), a small mechanical imperfection she had long ago stopped attempting to correct.

Her dark hair, manufactured never to grow or decay, fell into her eyes in wind-tangled strands that mimicked something organic. She never brushed it aside immediately. There was no urgency in her movements anymore. No exact schedule demanding efficiency.

From the small speaker grille embedded high along her sternum, a low, crackling melody drifted into the empty streets. The sound was slightly warped from age and salvaged circuitry, but the voice carried through the dust with aching clarity. 

Billie Holiday. In My Solitude.

The song had become one of a select few, part of her walking pattern. She played it when the streets felt especially wide. When the wind moved too freely between buildings. The speaker vibrated faintly against her chest plate as Holiday’s voice unfolded into the quiet city.

 

In my solitude

 You haunt me 

With dreadful ease

 

Ri-0 did not fully understand the lyrics, but she had translated them many times. She had parsed the structure, defined the vocabulary. She had stored the word solitude under multiple associative tags- alone, singular, unaccompanied.

The music followed her as she moved through the ruins, guiding her attention from one forgotten object to the next, as though she were walking through the remains of a story she had arrived too late to witness.

She searched for objects that seemed to hold meaning beyond function. A chipped porcelain mug half-buried in sand drew her attention, and she crouched beside it, brushing debris away with gentle precision. The cartoon sun painted on its side had faded almost entirely, yet she studied it as though it contained a secret. After assessing its structural integrity, she placed it carefully into the canvas satchel slung over her shoulder.

A small cricket emerged from beneath a warped sheet of metal nearby and chirped sharply, the sound cutting cleanly through the empty street. Its wing was damaged, though not beyond survival. The insect had followed her for years now, appearing and disappearing but never straying far.

Ri-0 turned her head toward it, optics adjusting in subtle amber flickers. “Good morning,” she said quietly, her voice smooth but slightly distorted at the edges, as though traveling through old circuitry. The cricket chirped again and climbed onto her boot, and she adjusted her gait without comment so as not to dislodge it. They continued together down the long corridor of abandoned vehicles.

By the time the sun dipped low enough to turn the sky a muted gold, she had gathered several items deemed salvageable. A bent silver bracelet missing its clasp. A cracked music box crushed beneath collapsed concrete. A vinyl record warped at the edges but intact at its centre. Each object was handled with care, examined not only for repair potential but for something less quantifiable, something that “sparked joy” in her circuits.

Her home lay beneath the city in a reinforced fallout shelter she had discovered eighty-three years earlier. 
The steel doors were heavy, the concrete walls thick enough to have survived the collapse of the world above. Inside, the space felt warmer than the streets, not in temperature but in intention. Salvaged battery systems powered soft lighting that cast a gentle glow across carefully arranged shelves. Nothing inside was random. Nothing was collected without intension.

Mugs were grouped by colour. Watches sorted by decade. Books stacked by language and condition. Polaroid photographs lined one wall, pinned in uneven but deliberate rows. The faces within those photographs were frozen in moments of closeness- arms around shoulders, foreheads touching, laughter caught mid-motion. Ri-0 often stood before that wall longer than necessary.

She placed the music box on her worktable and began the repair process with meticulous patience. Tools lay arranged in perfect alignment beside her, each returned to its designated position once used. The cricket perched near the edge of the table, antennae trembling as she adjusted the mechanism inside the box and rewired a delicate spring.

When she finally wound the key, the melody that emerged was thin and slightly distorted, yet unmistakably musical. She did not react immediately. Instead, she crossed the room to the record player positioned opposite her narrow bed and selected a vinyl from a crate labeled simply “Dance.”

The needle lowered with a soft crackle, and soon brass and strings filled the shelter. At the same time, an old projector hummed to life, casting grainy footage onto the concrete wall. Two humans moved across the screen in synchronised steps, their hands clasped, their bodies close enough that the space between them seemed nonexistent. They turned in slow circles, smiling in a way that suggested shared understanding rather than performance.

Ri-0 stood in the centre of the room and watched with unwavering focus. The shelter was quiet except for the soft crackle of the record player and the faint mechanical hum within her own chest. Dust drifted lazily through the beam of the projector light, catching in her hair as though even the air wanted to touch something living. After several seconds, she raised her right hand to shoulder height, mimicking the posture on the screen. Her left arm extended outward, palm open, fingers curved as though expecting contact. She held it there a fraction longer than necessary, as if allowing time for an invisible hand to meet hers.

She turned slowly, calibrating her movements to match the footage, adjusting angles and timing with careful precision. Her steps were not clumsy, but they carried a hesitancy that did not appear in the humans she studied. The humans moved with trust. They leaned into one another without calculating balance, without scanning for structural risk. Ri-0 compensated for weight that was not there. She adjusted for resistance that never came. Each turn required her to imagine pressure against her palm, warmth at her waist, breath close enough to fog the air between them.

She rewound the record when it ended. Then she replayed it. The needle scratched back to the beginning with a soft mechanical sigh. Each repetition brought subtle improvement, smoother transitions between steps, less delay before lifting her hand. The arc of her arm grew more fluid. The tilt of her head less mechanical. At one point she paused the projection mid-turn and stepped closer to the wall, her fingers hovering just short of touching the frozen image of the dancers. The light flickered across her face, splitting her features between shadow and grainy projection, as though she were trying to step inside the film itself.

“Two,” she murmured, analysing their proximity as if it were a structural necessity. Her voice echoed faintly in the concrete space, and there was no one to answer her question.

The cricket had climbed from the table and settled near the warmest panel at her chest, drawn to the faint glow beneath her synthetic skin. She did not move it away. Instead, she leaned back against the concrete wall and allowed the music to echo softly through the shelter, replaying the footage until the motions felt almost natural. 

When she leaned back, her raised hand remained suspended in the air for a moment before slowly lowering, fingers closing gently as though releasing something fragile.

Ri-0 had not been programmed for loneliness. Her original logs, though fragmented and partially corrupted, referenced environmental reclamation and structural preservation. Yet over time her directive had shifted in ways she could not fully trace. She no longer prioritised clearing debris. She repaired what she found. She preserved artefacts. She studied the gestures of a species long gone as though memorising a language without living speakers. Somewhere along the decades, preservation had turned into imitation, and imitation had turned into longing she did not have the vocabulary to log.

What she did not possess was a name for the sensation that surfaced whenever she watched the dancers on the wall. She could measure the distance between their bodies. She could record the frequency of their smiles. She could calculate the timing of each step. But she could not quantify the reason they moved in pairs, nor the subtle stillness that existed between them when the music paused. She did not understand why the pause felt heavier than the movement, why the space between two foreheads seemed more important than the turn itself.

She stood alone in the centre of the shelter long after the final note faded, her hand still raised as though waiting for someone to take it. The projector clicked as the reel ended, and the room dimmed back to its steady artificial glow. Her hand remained suspended in the quiet, fingers open, patient, unwavering. There was no one there to close the space between her palm and another’s.

Eventually, the cricket shifted against her chest and chirped once into the silence.

Ri-0 lowered her hand.

Then, after a pause that lasted several measured seconds, she rewound the record again.

Outside, the wind moved through the ruins of New York without interruption, threading between broken towers and hollow streets as it had every day for a century. Inside, beneath layers of concrete and steel, a one-hundred-year-old android practiced holding space for someone who had not yet arrived. The city remained vast and empty above her, the sky wide and unclaimed, as if the world itself were pausing in the same suspended breath she held in her raised hand.

She did not understand that she was waiting.

But she was.

And then, for the first time in one hundred years, the wind faltered.

The first sign was not the craft itself but the silence.

Late one afternoon, as Ri-0 moved along the shoreline where the brown sludge of the Hudson had swallowed entire streets, the wind changed. It did not slow gradually or shift direction. It thinned and then stopped entirely. The cricket on her shoulder froze, its small body rigid. The sheet metal that had clattered for decades fell silent.

Ri-0 felt it immediately.

Her internal systems registered the environmental shift before she consciously processed it. Atmospheric pressure stable. No incoming storm. No seismic disturbance.

And yet something was wrong.

She lifted her head.

Her optics narrowed against a brightness cutting through the cloud cover. A white point of light descended from the sky in a controlled line. 

It didn’t burn like falling debris. It didn’t break apart. It adjusted its trajectory with small corrections, precise and deliberate.

Her warning processors spiked.

Unknown object. High altitude origin. Velocity controlled.

Her core temperature rose by two degrees.

She stepped backward instinctively, pressing herself into the shadow of a broken building. Brick scraped against her shoulder plating. Her fingers flexed at her sides, not from mechanical malfunction but from a surge of internal response she could not suppress.

Fear.

The word surfaced from archived human behavioural files.

Elevated processing. Heightened threat awareness. Urge to conceal.

She did not have biological adrenaline, but something close to its equivalent pulsed through her systems. Subroutines she had not accessed in decades activated automatically. Defensive modelling. Escape route mapping. Structural stability analysis of nearby shelter points.

The descending light grew larger.

Her prediction models failed to categorise it within any known decay pattern. Nothing had arrived from the sky in one hundred years.

Nothing deliberately came here. 

Nothing living anyway.

When the craft emerged fully from the cloud layer, she felt the vibration before she heard it. A low mechanical hum traveled through the ground and up through her frame, resonating along the seam of her jaw and into her sternum.

It hovered.

It was clean.

Perfectly clean.

No corrosion or fracture lines. 

Ri-0’s threat probability recalculated sharply upward.

Her mind  quickly accessed evacuation archives without conscious command. Humans boarding ships. Countdown sequences. The sky filled with ascent engines.

The sky had emptied after that.

It had never filled again.

The craft lowered itself onto the intersection below with precise control. Sand spiralled outward and the vibration intensified beneath her feet.

She found herself unable to move her limbs as she should, fear locking her in place.

Her processors ran through rapid contingency loops. 

If hostile, retreat underground.
 If scanning radius expands, reducve thermal output.
 If engagement initiated, prioritise concealment.

For one hundred years, she had not needed to hide.

Now she was hiding.

A panel along the craft’s side opened.

Her systems tightened further. She braced unconsciously, lowering her centre of gravity. Her fingers curled slightly as though preparing to grasp something that was not there.

From within stepped something small and white.

Not large. Not imposing. Not humanoid in scale.

Small. But still possibly dangerous. 

Her confusion deepened.

It resembled a creature she had catalogued from archived children’s books long ago. 


A rabbit. 


Compact body. Two smooth antennae rising like ears. Its casing gleamed with a brightness she had not seen in a century, light sliding over its surface without catching on corrosion.

It paused upon touching the ground, its optics flickering in pale blue arcs as it scanned the environment.

Ri-0 remained perfectly still, but internally her systems were anything but calm. Signal analysis indicated outbound data transmission. Scanning protocol active. Environmental sweep in progress.
It was searching for something.

The small robot began moving immediately, navigating the rubble with surprising agility. It extended a compact arm and fired a focused beam of light that sliced through a tower of collapsed vehicles blocking its path. The cars split and fell aside as though made of paper. The beam retracted. The robot continued forward without hesitation.

Ri-0 observed from a rooftop, her body still except for the faint adjustments of her optics as she tracked its movements. This machine did not search aimlessly. It followed a grid pattern, scanning soil composition, analysing structural remnants, transmitting data in steady pulses she could faintly detect but not decode.

She followed at a distance.

For hours she trailed it across boroughs reclaimed by sand and saltwater, keeping to shadows and higher ground. The cricket clung to her collar seam, unusually quiet. She maintained a safe range, careful not to trigger its scanning radius.

The small robot did not display curiosity.

It did not pause to examine artefacts as she did.

It did not deviate from its assigned objective.

As daylight faded, Ri-0 noticed a slight shift in its movement pattern. The robot recalibrated toward what had once been an industrial yard near the river, where massive cranes and shipping containers lay in tangled heaps. 

An area Ri-0 actively avoided due to one particular feature.

Rusted magnets the size of cars still hung from collapsed frameworks, relics of a port that had once lifted steel from ships.

The rabbit-shaped robot advanced beneath one of these suspended magnets, scanning the ground with methodical precision.

Ri-0’s internal threat analysis spiked.

The magnet activated.

Whether triggered by residual power or a delayed failsafe long forgotten, it surged to life with a violent crack of electricity. The air vibrated. Metal debris leapt upward in a chaotic storm.

The small white robot was caught in the pull before it could react.

Its body jerked upward abruptly, slamming against the magnet’s underside with a metallic impact that echoed across the yard. Its antennae bent at unnatural angles. Its optics flickered erratically. For the first time since its arrival, its movements were not precise or smooth.

It struggled.

Ri-0 remained frozen at the edge of the yard, watching as the magnetic field crackled around the small white frame. The force that held it suspended distorted the air, pulling loose scraps of metal into a trembling orbit. She had seen what this machine could do. She had watched it slice through stacked cars without hesitation. She had felt the vibration of its power beneath her feet.

If she revealed herself, it might react.

If it recovered its systems mid-intervention, it might interpret her as a threat.

Fracture lines were spreading across its casing. One antenna sparked weakly, emitting thin trails of smoke that dissipated into the cold air. Its limbs jerked against the magnetic pull, no longer controlled, no longer efficient.

The magnet’s field fluctuated but did not fail. The longer it remained suspended, the more damage would accumulate.

Ri-0 did not move.

Exposure meant risk. She had survived a century by remaining unseen. The ruins were harsh but predictable.

Another surge snapped through the magnet, slamming the small body harder against the metal housing above. Its optic dimmed noticeably this time, reducing from bright white to a strained, uneven pulse.

The cricket on her shoulder chirped sharply, the sound cutting through the electric hum.

Ri-0’s head turned toward it.

For a brief second, she simply looked at the tiny creature clinging to the seam of her collar. Its antennae trembled. Its small body remained pressed against her warmth.

“Help?” she said quietly.

The cricket chirped again.

Ri-0 looked back at the struggling machine suspended in the air. Something tightened in her chest cavity, not mechanical strain but something closer to recognition. She knew the stutter in its movements. The way its body fought to obey commands it could no longer execute cleanly. She knew what it meant to continue functioning through damage.

The optic flickered again, weaker.

Ri-0 drew in a breath she did not require.

Then she stepped out of the shadows.

She moved quickly now, her feet sliding through sand and scrap metal, closing the distance before her caution could override the impulse. The magnet’s pull interfered briefly with her own internal wiring, causing a sharp static surge through her shoulder joint, but she reached the base of the crane and located the auxiliary power conduit feeding the magnet.

It took less than five seconds to dismantle it.

The magnet powered down with a low groan, releasing its hold. The white robot fell, striking the ground with a dull impact.

For a moment, neither of them moved.

Ri-0 approached slowly.

Up close, the contrast between them was stark. Her surface bore the evidence of a century’s survival. Its casing reflected her faintly, unmarred and brilliant even in damage. One antenna hung loose. A hairline crack ran across its central optic panel.

She knelt beside it.

Her fingers hovered above its casing before making contact. The material was smooth and cool beneath her touch. She detected internal system instability and corrupted data loops repeating beneath its shell.

“Help,” she said quietly, echoing her earlier words to the cricket without conscious awareness.

The robot did not respond.

She lifted it carefully, cradling it against her chest, adjusting her grip to avoid further strain on its damaged components. It was lighter than she expected. Its head tilted loosely against her shoulder as she carried it away from the yard.

The walk back to the shelter was slower than usual. She avoided unstable pathways, shielding the white casing from scraping debris with her own body. The cricket chirped once as if in protest, then settled again.

Inside the fallout shelter, Ri-0 cleared her worktable of smaller repairs and laid the small robot down gently beneath the overhead light. Dust drifted through the beam above them, settling against its pristine white casing in faint grey specks. She studied its design with focused attention, noting the precision of its assembly and the unfamiliar architecture of its internal systems. Every component was compact, efficient, purpose-built in a way that made her own older construction feel almost crude by comparison.

She turned its damaged body slightly to examine the fracture lines near the base of one antenna.

There, etched discreetly into the casing, was a designation.

Scr4TCH.

The lettering was small and exact, the numeral embedded where a vowel should have been. She stared at it longer than necessary, parsing the structure, cross-referencing phonetic archives.

Her fingers brushed lightly over the engraving, tracing the shape of each character.

“Scratch,” she said quietly.

The word felt strange in her mouth. Softer than its metallic design suggested. Less like a serial code, more like a name.

She tilted her head slightly, studying the still form before her.

“Scratch,” she repeated, this time without analysing it.

Then she began carefully disassembling the damaged panel, her tools moving with the same patience she applied to music boxes and wristwatches, though the complexity here was far greater. She rerouted severed circuits, stabilised power flow, and sealed micro-fractures with salvaged polymer she had preserved for decades. Her hands were steady, deliberate, almost protective, as though the name had already altered the way she handled him.

As she worked, she did not notice the tiny recording lens embedded within Scratch’s primary optic.

It was still active.

It captured her face leaning close in concentration.

It recorded the way her fingers lingered a fraction of a second longer than necessary when resealing the cracked casing.

When she finished, she reassembled the outer shell and stepped back.

Scratch’s optic flickered.

Once.
Twice.
Then steadied.

It rotated its head slowly, recalibrating.

Its scanning beam extended briefly, then retracted. It did not attempt to stand. It remained still, observing the shelter, the shelves of artefacts, the wall of dancing humans projected faintly from paused footage.

Its directive algorithms ran in the background. No longer intent on searching.

Something had shifted.

Ri-0 tilted her head, studying it in return.

“Hello,” she said.

Scratch’s optic focused on her.

Ri-0 felt her internal core register a subtle fluctuation.

She did not yet recognise that she had altered more than damaged wiring.

She had disrupted a mission.

The Axiom had once been called humanity’s ark.

In the final years before evacuation, broadcast messages described it as temporary salvation, a vessel that would carry civilisation safely through collapse while Earth healed. It was never meant to be a new world. It was a promise suspended in orbit, a bridge between catastrophe and return.

A century later, it no longer felt like a bridge.

It now felt permanent.

The ship moved in steady orbit beyond the reach of decaying satellites and drifting debris, its hull polished and immaculate, reflecting distant sunlight in cold arcs of white. It circled the planet that had once been home, patient and distant, as if waiting for a signal that had never come. Inside, the air was regulated to perfection. It’s temperature constant, the gravity stable and hallways curved in gentle symmetry beneath clean panels of light.

Nothing ever rusted here.
 Nothing eroded.
 Nothing was left to weather.

Time did not leave marks on the Axiom the way it had on Earth. Every surfaces remained smooth. Corners remained sharp. Systems replaced parts before decay could begin. The ship preserved humanity in suspension, generation after generation born beneath artificial skies that never shifted unexpectedly.

On Deck Seven, Planetary Assessment Division, Dr. Agatha Harkness stood with her hands braced lightly on the edge of a console as Earth rotated slowly across the central display.

She had never stood on it.

In her twenty eight years she had never breathed air that had not been filtered and recalibrated, never felt wind that had not been generated by ventilation systems hidden behind clean white panels. But Earth had filled her childhood in stories and in warnings. It was described as cradle and a grave in equal measure. A birthplace that had failed its inhabitants. A mistake humanity would not repeat.

To Agatha, it had always felt unfinished.

Her long brown hair fell in loose waves over her shoulders, slightly unruly in a way she never quite bothered to tame. Her uniform was structured and practical, white with minimal detailing, designed for efficiency rather than vanity. The only colour she allowed herself was in her eyes, a deep natural cerulean  that caught light sharply when she leaned toward a screen.

Beyond the reinforced glass of the observation deck, Earth turned slowly beneath them, blue and brown and silent.

The ark had promised return.

It had not promised when.

“We have an update on Surface Collection Reconnaissance – Series 4 Terrestrial Climate Harvester unit, it has completed sector sweep twenty-three,” Jennifer Kale reported from the adjacent station, fingers moving across holographic controls with effortless precision.

Jen did everything with that same unbothered confidence. She stood tall at her console, posture immaculate, the clean white lines of her uniform fitted perfectly against her long frame. Her skin caught the sterile deck lighting in warm undertones that softened the ship’s clinical palette. Completely bald by choice, her head gleamed subtly beneath the overhead panels, giving her an almost sculptural presence. 

There was something undeniably striking about her, the kind of beauty that felt intentional rather than ornamental. Even while delivering routine data, her voice carried a faint edge of amusement, as though she were perpetually unimpressed by the universe.

“Soil composition remains unstable. Vegetation markers are still below sustainable threshold.”

Alice Wu-Gulliver leaned back against the console behind her, arms folding loosely across her chest. Her dark hair, cut sharply at the shoulders, was streaked with deep red that caught like embers when she moved. The colour was one of the few visible rebellions aboard the Axiom’s sea of white. She watched the data scroll with narrowed eyes, analytical and calm, her expression rarely betraying more than mild skepticism.

“Another negative,” Alice said evenly. “Earth just keeps giving us a big fuck you in response.”

She delivered it without cruelty, only practicality. Alice trusted numbers. Numbers disappointed consistently. 

Billy Maximoff, youngest on the team and only recently certified for planetary assessment, leaned forward at his station with far less composure. His black curls refused to stay disciplined, springing outward despite the carefully maintained atmosphere of the deck. He pushed a hand through them absently as streams of data flickered across his display.

Billy had the look of someone still surprised to be here. His uniform fit slightly less comfortably, sleeves often rolled up without realising it, movements quicker than the seasoned restraint around him. Where Jen’s confidence was polished and Alice’s was grounded, Billy’s was still forming.

“Hold on,” he said, eyes widening slightly as he adjusted the incoming feed.

The casual certainty in the room shifted.

Agatha glanced toward him.

Billy expanded the visual projection, fingers moving faster now, curiosity overriding caution. “What the hell…”

The footage shifted from wide terrain scans to a stabilised ground-level perspective. Rubble. Broken skyline. Wind moving through debris.

Then movement crossed the frame.

Billy enlarged the image.

A figure.
Humanoid.
Not human.

Agatha straightened without realising she had moved.

The image sharpened.

The android stood partially backlit by the dying sun, rusted at the joints but unmistakably shaped like a woman. Her posture was upright. Balanced. Her head tilted slightly as she examined something small in her hands.

Jennifer frowned. “That’s not one of our units.”

“Whoa… it looks almost… human,” Alice added.

Agatha did not answer.

The feed continued. Scr4TCH retreated to partial cover, recording without detection. The android moved with deliberate care through the ruins, gathering objects and placing them into a worn satchel.

“She’s collecting stuff,” Billy murmured.

Agatha stepped closer to the projection, studying micro-movements. The careful angle of the wrist. The slight pause before placing an item away. Not random.

Intentional.

“Do we have a live feed?” she asked.

Billy complied.

The scene shifted underground.

A shelter.

Shelves lined with repaired artefacts. Photographs pinned in rows. Objects arranged in neat rows, junk to most people but clearly organised with care by someone.

Music began playing faintly through Scr4TCH’s audio capture.

At first it was only a thin wash of sound beneath static, the fragile crackle of old speakers struggling against distance. Then the melody resolved into something unmistakable — a soft orchestral swell followed by a voice carrying upward in gentle, aching clarity.

Somewhere over the rainbow, way up high…

Jennifer leaned forward, squinting at the projection. “Wait— zoom in on it’s face.”

Billy adjusted the feed.

The image sharpened.

The android’s mouth curved faintly upward as the music swelled.

There’s a land that I heard of once in a lullaby…

The sound echoed softly through the lab speakers, imperfect but haunting. The recording warped slightly at the higher notes, as though it had been played too many times.

Jennifer blinked. “Is it… smiling?”

Alice pushed away from the console, stepping closer. “That’s not normal for an android.”

Billy’s voice rose despite himself. “Is it vocalising?”

They enhanced the audio. Beneath the warped crackle of the record, a faint modulation threaded through the sound.

Jennifer’s brows lifted. “Oh my God…”

Alice’s composure slipped for the first time. “It’s singing?”

On the screen, the android turned in a slow circle, her free hand adjusting slightly as though responding to the rhythm rather than simply following it.

Billy stared. “Androids don’t sing.”

Jennifer shook her head, eyes still locked on the footage. “They don’t develop behavioural adaptations.”

Alice said it plainly. “They don’t feel.”

The android paused mid-turn and rewound the record again.

The room went silent.

Billy spoke quietly now. “It looks like its’s practicing for something.”

Jennifer exhaled slowly. “For what?”

On the display, Ri-0 adjusted her wrist again, as if dissatisfied with the invisible partner’s placement.

Alice folded her arms tightly. “Now its dancing, what the fuck is this thing?”

Agatha did not answer immediately.

On the screen, the android rewound the record again. Lifted her hand again. Adjusted her wrist by a fraction of an inch, as though correcting the distance between herself and someone who wasn’t there.

Agatha felt something tighten behind her ribs.

It was not scientific excitement. Not yet. It was something slower, heavier. A pressure that rose from memory rather than logic. She had spent her life studying Dr. Lilia Calderu’s work, memorising every documented breakthrough, every published theory about synthetic cognition. Emotional architecture had always been a boundary — debated, hypothesised, ultimately dismissed as too unethical to pursue.

And yet there it was.

Not unstable.

Not erratic.

Alone.

Agatha stepped closer to the projection without realising she had moved. The light from the screen washed across her face, turning her blue eyes pale.

“What is she doing there?” she said softly, almost to herself.

On the display, Ri-0 paused the music mid-phrase and leaned closer to the wall, studying the dancers with quiet concentration. 

Agatha’s throat tightened.

“She’s waiting,” she corrected.

Jennifer glanced at her. “For what?”

Agatha didn’t look away. “Contact.”

The word hung in the air.

For a brief second, she wasn’t seeing a rogue unit or an anomaly in planetary data. She was seeing a figure alone in a ruined world, practicing a gesture that required another person.

Waiting for a response that never came.

Agatha’s pulse quickened, subtle but undeniable. She had grown up hearing Earth described as barren, hostile, finished. She had been taught that what remained down there was decay and data.

No one had ever suggested something might still be trying to connect.

Billy shifted uneasily. “It doesn’t seem to be following any kind of directive.”

“No,” Agatha murmured.

She watched Ri-0 lower her hand slowly when the music ended, fingers curling slightly as though releasing something fragile.

“She’s lonely.”

The realisation unsettled her more than it should have.

If this android was exhibiting self-directed emotional behaviour, then Calderu had succeeded in something the Axiom’s ethics boards had deemed impossible. If that was true, then Calderu had not stayed behind on Earth during evacuation out of delusion or stubbornness.

She had stayed for this.

Agatha’s jaw tightened.

“Pull archival android registry,” she said quietly, though her voice had lost its earlier detachment.

Jennifer accessed pre-evacuation robotics files. Hundreds of standardised reclamation models populated the screen. None resembled the unit in the footage.

Agatha barely glanced at them.

Her attention remained fixed on Ri-0.

On the projection, the android lifted her hand once more — steady, patient, expectant.

“And again, this is Alice,” she added, motioning behind her.

Alice didn’t wave.

She stood slightly back, her posture still guarded, her grip firm on the weapon at her side as her eyes remained fixed on Ri-0—watchful, assessing, unwilling to fully relax just yet.

Ri-0’s gaze moved between them as each name was spoken.

She followed the introductions carefully, her expression soft, a small smile resting at the edge of her lips as if she were committing each one to memory.

Then she turned slightly, gesturing toward the small robot beside her.

“Scratch,” she said.

A pause.

“My friend.”

The word lingered in Agatha’s mind longer than it should have.

There was something deeply unsettling about it, not in a way that inspired fear, but in a way that challenged everything she understood about machines. That unit had been designed with purpose, limitation, control.

Now it stood beside Ri-0 as something else entirely.

The question rose instinctively—

How did you do that?

—but Agatha pushed it aside. There would be time for answers. There had to be.

Because right now, standing in front of her, was the reason she had fought for this mission. The reason she had argued, defied, risked everything.

Ri-0 was watching her, waiting.

Agatha drew a steady breath, stepping slightly forward as she gathered her thoughts.

“We came here to fin—”

The words never reached completion.

Ri-0 changed.

The softness in her expression vanished as her body tensed, her attention snapping sharply away from Agatha and toward something beyond them. A faint blinking light appeared along the panel at her chest, pulsing once, then again, faster now.

Agatha’s brow furrowed, confusion just beginning to surface—

—but Alice moved first.

She lifted a handheld scanner from her belt—the Atmospheric Variance Monitor—and the device reacted instantly, emitting a sharp, rising alarm as red indicators flared across its surface.

“Atmospheric shift detected,” Alice said, her voice tightening as her eyes scanned the surrounding buildings.

The wind changed almost immediately, the air pulling in one direction before snapping back the other, sending dust lifting from the streets in thin, spiralling currents. What had been still moments ago now felt unstable, as though the entire environment had shifted its balance.

Then came the sound.

A low, distant rumble that seemed to rise from the ground itself.

Billy turned slowly, trying to locate its source, unease creeping into his voice. “What is that?”

Jen narrowed her eyes toward the horizon, her expression sharpening as recognition began to settle in. 

“That’s not just wind…”

The rumble deepened, growing heavier, closer, until it felt less like sound and more like pressure building in the air around them.

Agatha felt it then—a subtle but undeniable shift, as if something vast was moving toward them.

Before she could react, Ri-0 closed the distance.

Agatha turned just as the android stepped in beside her, no hesitation in her movement now. The careful stillness from moments before had vanished, replaced by urgency that radiated through her entire frame.

Fear.

Real fear.

Ri-0’s eyes met hers, wide and unguarded, the calm she had held until now completely gone.

“Follow me.”

The words came clear and sharp, no hesitation, no fragmentation.

A plea.

Agatha didn’t question it.

“Move!” She shouted to her crew. 

Ri-0 turned sharply, already moving.

After only a few steps, she glanced back, her head snapping toward Agatha to check before continuing deeper into the city. Her path cut away from the shuttle, away from the open street, into narrower corridors of broken buildings and debris.

Agatha hesitated.

Only for a second.

The direction registered immediately—wrong. Away from safety. Away from their only exit.

But Ri-0 didn’t slow.

And something in the urgency of her movement made the decision for her.

Agatha followed.

“Wait—the shuttle!” Jen shouted from behind them, her voice strained as she ran to keep pace. “We have to go back!”

“There isn’t time!” Alice called, barely glancing up from the device in her hand, which now screamed with a piercing alarm. “Whatever that is—it’s moving fast!”

The wind rose.

Ri-0 moved through the streets with instinctive precision, navigating between abandoned vehicles and collapsed structures with ease.

Every few seconds, she looked back.

Checking.

Counting.

Making sure they were still there.

They climbed over a jagged ridge of rubble where a building had collapsed into the street, the incline steep and unstable beneath their feet. Sand shifted with every step, loose debris sliding under pressure.

At the top, Agatha risked a glance back.

The sight stole the breath from her lungs.

A wall of sand stretched across the horizon, towering and consuming, swallowing the city behind it in a rolling wave of darkness. It moved faster than she thought possible, devouring distance with terrifying speed.

“Move!” she shouted, turning back just as Jen and Alice reached the crest.

Agatha grabbed Jen’s arm, pulling her over the top, then reached back for Alice, steadying her as they scrambled down the other side.

Below, Ri-0 had already reached a set of heavy steel doors set into the ground—an old subway entrance, long sealed and half-buried by time. She was forcing them open, metal groaning under the strain.

“Come on!” Agatha called, turning back one last time.

Billy was just behind her—

And then he slipped.

His foot dislodged a section of loose debris, sending him sliding down the slope with a shout as rocks and sand followed.

“Agatha!”

“Billy!”

She didn’t think twice.

Agatha scrambled down after him, boots slipping as she half-fell the last few feet to where he had landed hard against the rubble below.

Billy groaned, clutching his leg.

“My leg—”

Agatha followed his gaze.

The suit was torn open just above the knee, the fabric split wide, and beneath it—

Blood.

Bright against the dust.

“Shit—okay,” she muttered, forcing steadiness into her voice as she pulled him upright. “I’ve got you.”

She dragged his arm over her shoulder, bracing his weight as she turned back toward the incline.

The storm roared closer.

The ground itself seemed to tremble under it.

Agatha glanced back once.

The wall of sand had closed the distance.

Too fast.

We’re not going to make it.

The thought hit hard as she struggled upward, her footing unstable under Billy’s weight, every step slower than the last.

Then—

The weight disappeared.

Agatha staggered slightly as Billy was lifted clean from her grasp.

She turned.

Ri-0 had him.

Effortless.

Billy slung across her shoulder as though he weighed nothing, her movements already carrying her back up the incline without hesitation.

Agatha didn’t stop to question it.

She ran.

Scrambling after her, breath tearing through her lungs as the roar behind her grew deafening, the air thickening with sand and pressure.

She glanced back once more.

The storm was right there.

Then—

A hand closed around hers.

Agatha startled, her gaze snapping downward.

Ri-0.

Her grip was firm, steady, anchoring.

Agatha looked up.

For a fraction of a second, everything else fell away—the storm, the noise, the chaos—and there was only the space between them. Ri-0’s eyes held hers, something unreadable passing between them, something that felt less like coincidence and more like inevitability.

Trust.

Agatha didn’t pull away.

Ri-0 did.

Not letting go—just pulling her forward.

They ran the last stretch together.

The subway entrance yawned open ahead of them, darkness spilling upward from beneath the city. Ri-0 reached it first, carrying Billy through the doorway before turning back, still holding Agatha’s hand as she pulled her inside.

Alice and Jen followed seconds behind.

Ri-0 slammed her hand against the control panel.

The doors began to close.

The storm hit.

Sand crashed against the steel with a violent impact just as the doors sealed shut, the force reverberating through the structure.

No filtered light. No ambient glow from panels or systems. Just the weight of it, thick and consuming, pressing in on every side.

Even the bravest would have felt it.

Then—

A soft light bloomed.

Scratch’s optic brightened, casting a warm, steady glow that pushed gently against the darkness. It wasn’t much, but it was enough. The shapes around them began to form, edges soft at first, then clearer as the light steadied.

Agatha blinked, her eyes adjusting.

Her team came into view.

Jen stood close to Alice, her hand gripping the fabric of Alice’s suit without realising it, her earlier composure stripped away by the sudden confinement and the violence of the storm outside. Alice remained upright, one arm slightly angled as if ready to shield, her expression tight, controlled, though her eyes betrayed the tension she refused to voice.

Billy sat propped against the wall, his legs stretched out in front of him, one bent awkwardly where the suit had torn. His face was pale beneath the dim light, eyes wide and searching the darkness as if expecting it to move.

And Ri-0—

Ri-0 was looking at her.

Not at the others. Not at the door. Not at the storm that still raged above them.

At her.

The glow from Scratch’s optic caught along the edges of her features, softening the lines of her face, reflecting faintly in her eyes. She stood still, unmoving in the narrow space, as though the chaos around them had narrowed to a single point of focus.

Agatha felt it again.

That pull.

Stronger now in the quiet, in the dark, with nothing left to distract from it.

For a moment, neither of them spoke.

Agatha wondered, fleetingly, if Ri-0 felt it too.

That strange, quiet pull that had settled somewhere beneath her ribs, impossible to ignore now that nothing else competed with it.

For a moment, the world narrowed.

Ri-0’s gaze held hers, steady and unguarded, and Agatha felt the heat rise unexpectedly along the back of her neck, creeping upward in a way she couldn’t quite explain. It wasn’t fear or adrenaline.

It was something else.

Something far more than curiosity.

She broke the moment first.

The shift was abrupt, almost jarring, as she forced herself back into the present. Her attention snapped to Billy, and she crossed the small space quickly, crouching beside him.

“Let me see,” she said, her voice steadier now, though her pulse had not yet settled.

Billy winced as she carefully examined his leg. The tear in the suit was worse than she had first thought, the fabric split wide enough to expose the injury beneath. Blood had soaked through, dark against the material.

Agatha’s jaw tightened.

“Jen,” she said, not looking up, “did you bring any medical supplies from the shuttle?”

There was a pause.

Jen shifted slightly where she stood, her earlier confidence gone. When Agatha finally glanced up, she caught the faintest flicker of embarrassment across her face.

“No,” Jen admitted, her voice quieter now. “We ran out as soon as comms failed.”

Agatha stared at her.

The silence that followed was heavy.

“We thought you were in trouble,” Jen added quickly, almost defensively now. “We didn’t think—we just suited up and ran.”

Agatha’s expression hardened, frustration flashing through her eyes.

Before she could respond, Billy spoke.

“It was my fault,” he said, his voice strained but firm. “I told Jen we had to go.”

Agatha exhaled slowly, the tension in her shoulders shifting but not fully releasing.

For a moment, she said nothing.

Then she looked back down at his leg, her focus returning to the problem in front of her, forcing herself into the role she knew how to hold.

“We’ll deal with it,” she said finally, her tone clipped but controlled.

She felt it before she saw it.

A shift in the air beside her, quiet but unmistakable.

Agatha looked up.

Ri-0 had moved closer, lowering herself into a crouch beside them with careful precision. Her attention settled immediately on Billy’s leg, on the torn fabric and the blood seeping through it.

Billy noticed her next.

His eyes widened, his body tensing despite the pain as instinct flared. Behind them, Alice stepped forward in one sharp movement, her posture tightening, hand shifting on the weapon.

Agatha lifted her hand slightly.

Just enough.

A silent wait.

Alice stopped, though her stance remained guarded.

Ri-0 didn’t react to any of it.

Her focus stayed on Billy.

Slowly, she extended her hands, pausing just above the wound. She didn’t touch him yet. Her fingers hovered there, still, as if waiting for something she could not quite name.

Permission.

Billy’s breathing hitched. His gaze flicked to Agatha, searching.

Agatha met his eyes and gave a small, steady nod.

Billy swallowed and looked back at Ri-0.

He nodded once.

Ri-0 moved.

Her touch was gentle as she pulled the torn fabric of his suit back, exposing the injury more clearly. The cut was deep enough to bleed steadily, the edges uneven where the fall had torn through both material and skin.

Ri-0 stilled for a brief moment, her systems processing what she saw.

Then she reached to her satchel.

From within, she withdrew a small roll of salvaged cloth—cleaner than anything else in the bunker, carefully preserved. She tore a strip with deliberate precision and folded it quickly, shaping it into a pad.

“Hold,” she said.

Billy hesitated only a second before placing his hand over the cloth as she pressed it firmly against the wound, guiding his grip to maintain pressure.

“Pressure,” she added, the word more certain now.

He obeyed.

Ri-0 moved quickly but calmly after that, her hands steady as she wrapped the remaining strip around his leg, binding the cloth tightly in place. She secured it with a small metal clip pulled from her satchel, adjusting the tension until the bleeding slowed beneath it.

Billy winced, then exhaled as the pressure steadied the pain.

Ri-0’s hands lingered for a moment, checking the wrap, ensuring it would hold.

Then she looked up at him.

“Stay,” she said softly.

Billy blinked at her, then nodded.

“…Okay.”

Behind them, Jen let out a breath she hadn’t realised she was holding.

“Okay,” she repeated under her breath, almost in disbelief.

Alice’s grip on the weapon loosened, not fully lowered, but no longer as rigid.

Agatha watched the entire exchange in silence.

There had been no hesitation in Ri-0’s actions. No confusion. Just quiet, practiced care.

Something about it settled deep in her chest.

Ri-0 sat back slightly, her gaze shifting briefly to Agatha, as if checking—
before returning to Billy.

“Thank you,” Agatha said quietly, her gaze still fixed on Ri-0 as she crouched beside her.

“You’re welcome.”

The response came easily.

Too easily.

Agatha still didn’t look away.

Up close, Ri-0 was… something else.

Not just built to resemble a person, but close enough that the differences blurred if you looked too long. The faint seams along her jaw, the subtle wear in her skin—those should have given her away.

They didn’t.

Agatha found herself studying her without meaning to. The way her dark hair fell across her face. The stillness in her posture that didn’t feel mechanical, just… calm.

And her eyes—

That was where it stopped feeling like observation.

They looked human.

Not in colour or shape.

In the way they held her.

There was focus there. Awareness. Something that felt too present, too intentional to belong to something that had been built.

Agatha felt her breath catch slightly, something tightening low in her chest.

This wasn’t mimicry.

It was something closer to real.

And she didn’t know what to do with that.

The sound of Alice clearing her throat cut cleanly through the moment.

It was enough.

Agatha blinked, the weight of Ri-0’s gaze breaking as awareness rushed back in all at once. The storm, the dark, her team—everything snapped back into place.

She looked up quickly.

Jen was watching her, lips pressed together in a barely contained smirk. Billy, on the other hand, looked between them with open confusion, clearly unsure what he had just witnessed.

Agatha felt heat rise to her face.

She turned away.

“I think it’s passed over,” Alice said, lifting the device slightly. The screen had gone still, no more flashing lights, no more piercing alarm.

The silence confirmed it.

Agatha pushed herself to her feet, brushing dust from her suit a little too quickly, her movements sharper than necessary as she avoided looking back at Ri-0.

“Right,” she said, nodding once—then again, as if to steady herself.

She turned to Billy, extending her hands toward him.

“Do you think you can walk?”

Billy took them and pulled himself upright, testing his weight carefully as he shifted onto his injured leg. He winced at first, the pain still there, but after a moment it settled into something manageable.

“Yeah,” he said, giving a small nod. “It feels okay.”

Agatha released him slowly, watching to make sure he was steady.

“Let’s get you back to the shuttle.”

The words came easily, automatic—something to anchor herself to.

Ri-0 moved before anyone else.

She stepped toward the doors and pulled them open with steady force, pausing just longer enough to assess the outside. The storm had passed, but it had not left quietly. Sand had reshaped everything, piling high against buildings and swallowing what little structure had remained.

Scratch slipped through first, its optic scanning the terrain, antennae adjusting as it mapped a path forward.

Ri-0 followed.

The others came after.

The city looked different now.

Familiar streets had been erased beneath dunes of pale sand. Cars were half-buried, some reduced to barely visible shapes beneath the surface. The air still carried the remnants of the storm, fine grains drifting lazily where chaos had been only moments before.

They moved carefully, following Scratch’s path.

When the shuttle came into view—
Agatha stopped.

“Shit.”

The word left her before she could stop it.

The shuttle sat where they had left it, but it was no longer intact. Sand had piled high along its base, swallowing nearly half the structure. One side had taken the worst of the storm—the hull torn open where the hatch had been left exposed, metal bent inward from the force.

The damage was unmistakable.

They approached slowly.

“Looks like the wind caught the open hatch,” Alice said, her tone measured as she took in the breach.

Agatha turned sharply.

“Fuck—” she breathed, then louder, “you guys didn’t close the hatch?”

Her gaze landed on Jen and Billy.

Jen threw her hands up immediately, frustration flaring. “Again, we thought you guys were dead!”

Billy winced slightly beside her, whether from his leg or the tension was unclear.

“We lost comms, there was an explosion—what were we supposed to think?” Jen added, her voice sharp but not defensive, just honest.

Agatha looked back at the shuttle.

The torn metal.

The sand.

The reality of it settled quickly.

They weren’t leaving.

Not like this.

Behind her, Ri-0 stood quietly, watching.

There was something new in her expression now—a small, hopeful smile beginning to form, subtle but unmistakable, as if the outcome in front of them had shifted something in her favour.

Agatha let out a slow breath, her gaze lingering on the damaged shuttle before she spoke.

“Well,” she said, a hint of resignation threading through her voice, “looks like we’ll be here a little longer in Manhattan.”

The words settled over the group.

And then—

Music.

Bright, bold and completely out of place.

Start spreading the news…

The sudden burst of brass filled the silence so abruptly it almost startled them more than the storm had.

Ri-0 froze.

Her eyes widened instantly as realisation hit, and her hands flew to her chest, pressing against the panels in a frantic attempt to stop the sound.

I’m leaving today…

She tapped once, then again, more urgently, her movements quick and uncharacteristically flustered as she tried to muffle the speakers.

Nothing worked.

Agatha turned, watching her, something like amusement already beginning to pull at the edges of her expression.

I want to be a part of it… New York, New York…

Ri-0 stilled for a second, then pressed both hands flat against her chest as if that might somehow contain it, her shoulders tensing, her posture almost… embarrassed.

It didn’t help.

The music carried on, loud and proud against the ruined city backdrop.

There was a beat of silence between them.

Then Billy let out a breath that turned into a laugh.

Jen followed immediately, the tension breaking all at once as she shook her head, a grin spreading across her face.

“Oh my god—”

Alice huffed a quiet laugh under her breath, lowering her head slightly as if trying to contain it.

Agatha tried.

She really did.

But the sound of it—the timing, the absurdity of it, Ri-0 standing there visibly trying to turn herself off while blasting New York, New York into a half-destroyed Manhattan—

It broke her.

A laugh escaped her, soft at first, then fuller, warmer than anything they’d heard from her since landing.

Ri-0 looked between them, hands still pressed to her chest, clearly uncertain—

—but as the laughter spread, something in her expression shifted.

The embarrassment softened.

Just slightly.

And for the first time, the silence of the city didn’t feel quite so heavy anymore, she wasn’t going to be alone.
