Chapter 1

Chapter Notes

Hello and welcome to this new AU!

Rio has been in the Westview National Park for as long as she could remember. The pure essence of nature is her home. The drip of melting dew at dawn, the flutter of wings in the distance and the familiar crunch of pine needles and dry leaves, especially during the summer, under her boots she’d worn through three times took care of her. This is her world, and she wouldn’t have it any other way.

By the time the sun peeks through the horizon of the mountains to the east, Rio already checked the fence and radioed in a bear sighting to the head ranger, Lilia Calderu. The call went unanswered, as most did, but she didn’t mind. She doesn’t mind the silence, it’s rare for someone to make the difficult trek to her neck of the woods, even the most experienced hikers.

Her parents of course hoped she’d take over the whole park - Lilia even offered the position to her straight out of high school - but Rio didn’t want the irritability of the meetings or bureaucratic nightmare of funding to ruin her relationship with Westview. 

As she hikes up shore from the nearby river, the radio on Rio’s hip crackles to life. 

“Good morning Lady Death, you up?” Alice asks. She was one of the few full-time staff under Lilia that she was training for her inevitable retirement. Rio likes Alice, even if they’ve only met at most ten times, but still, she’s happy that the future of this park is going to someone dedicated to protecting the environment as she knows Alice will.

“Since before the sun champ,” Rio responds.

“Good, head’s up, a traveler is headed your way. Male around 5 '11, brown hair, brown eyes, lean build,” Alice reports neutrally.

Rio pauses. She hasn’t had anyone her way all summer and she was hoping she’d add another busy season to her streak but this damn kid just had to go and break it. She checks her watch, reading 10:15 a.m. and groans.

“Is he lost?” 

“No, but he might be if he’s going down The Road. Solo traveler I tried to recommend an easier trail but he seemed oddly determined for this particular path.”

Rio exhales.

Of course, leave it to a man who’s overconfident in his abilities to venture one of the deadliest hiking paths in the U.S.

“Got it, I’ll keep an eye out for him.”

“Thanks,” Alice says. After thirty seconds of no follow up, Rio returns to the river before her.

She hikes her way through the familiar roots of the windy trail known as The Road back to her home. It was more a legend than a trail really. It curved gently along the mountainside like it was always there, waiting for the next hiker to test their endurance. No one had for a long while, some were ambitious and tried to venture down past the extremely narrow ridge but as soon as they saw the deep drop off, they decided they valued their life and turned back. Those who were brave enough to try their luck beyond were rewarded with the beautiful untouched essence of nature. 

And that’s exactly where Rio’s cabin resides. 

It’s a small, sturdy, and inconspicuous structure that no one knows exist unless they’re looking for it. From above, it’s nearly hidden in the tree line of the forest - shaded by the tall evergreens, blanketed in a protective layer of moss that’s older than Rio herself; like nature has adapted to it, tucking it into its side and protecting it like it’s precious secret.

The interior is modest: aged timber walls, a slanted tin roof that’s patched over after years of wear and tear with a narrow porch just wide enough for a chair and a hammock. The only way to access her cabin is a path that winds off The Road, veering down the opposite side of the ridge where hikers don’t think to look. Rio prefers it this way.

By midday, the sun hangs high and heavy, casting sharp shadows across the shoreline of the forest floor. 

Rio sits on the edge of her porch, arms dangling by her side and legs crossed over the banister. Normally she’d be deep into her patrol routes - checking the waterline, monitoring nesting grounds, clearing the windfall off the Road itself, but today she’s stuck watching the ridge, waiting for the idiotic teenager to maybe not get himself killed.

Hours pass. The forest is still. Most would call its silence unsettling, but to Rio it’s comforting. She even feels a little FOMO from the trees, but despite her demeanor, she has a heart and doesn’t want this idiot to die on her watch.

She checks the ridge, then her watch again. Nothing.

Usually by now Alice or Lilia even would’ve radioed in, signaling that the ambitious hikers have returned and she’s free of her temporary but necessary duty of watch dog but she hasn't received any call.

Then, she sees him through her binoculars.

The rustling branches in the distance are as unnatural as pigs flying and a boy emerges in her line of sight. He may look the part of an experienced hiker, packed with all the right equipment, but Rio can see the inexperienced naivety in his features. His eyes dance the horizon as he takes in his reward for this dangerously beautiful journey. He snaps a few photos on an old camera of the picturesque view and Rio expects him to turn back around. There’s not much more to capture but instead he proves his insanity, when he puts away the vintage device and starts to cross the narrow walkway.

She can see the tension in his shoulders, uncertainties dripping from his facial features and something in Rio snaps.

Rio snatches her radio and a tether, then bolts towards the ridge.

To any stranger they’d be lost in an instant, but Rio knows these paths like the back of her hand, and knows the shortcuts in order to reach him in the fastest time.

Within ten minutes she reaches the ridge and freezes. She’s horrified when she sees him dangling off the edge of the bedrock. In an instant, she ties the rope to a nearby tree trunk and slinks down the mountainside to secure the kid. It’s a hasty knot but she doesn't have time to worry about that, his grip was already slipping.

“You're going to be okay, just grab onto the rope and pull yourself up,” she instructed.

He does exactly that and pulls himself over the edge. 

“Okay don't-”

The next thing she knows she’s plummeting a good twenty feet. She doesn't remember hitting the jagged rock, just the distinct crack somewhere in her body before slamming into a flat stone below.

Rio’s senses are immediately muddled, a sharp ringing in her ears. Then fire immediately radiates throughout her lungs but she can't focus on that right now, what matters is making sure the kid isn't the next to follow.

She tries to sit up, but the fire burns hotter everywhere in her body, pain soaring through her side. Her breaths are jagged, sharp, and shallow, like she can't get enough oxygen on her own. The taste of copper floods her mouth. Shit. This is NOT how I die.

The most she can muster is angling her neck uncomfortably where she can see his head over the edge, eyes wide, frozen.

“Hey!” Her voice is hoarse, but she simply wills the adrenaline through her body. “Back up! Get away from the edge!” 

He doesn't move, if anything he becomes more blurred. Everything hurts, but she tries again.

“Go! Get a ranger! Get help!” She finally sees his blurry head nod and disappear off in the distance.

Rio can't take it anymore, the fire is too hot, the copper too bitter, the breaths too shallow. She doesn't know how much time has passed, but slowly everything fades to black. 



~~~

 

Everything is black and heavy, then she hears it - a faint beep cutting through the void. It’s rhythmic, mechanical. Stable. Like a metronome in the distance. It anchors her, pulling her closer to something she can't put her finger on yet.

Then the voices cane, muted and distorted. Like it’s underwater. Floating, she thinks she is, or drowning. She can't tell which way is up but still, she swims. 

She doesn't know how many but she recognizes one of them as the kid she saved. 

“It happened so fast. One minute she’s there, the next- she saved me.”

The rest doesn't matter, he’s safe.

She lets herself float in the darkness for a little while longer. 

The voices still come and go.

“O2 levels are stable.”

“Bleeding has ceased.”

“We can take her off sedation, she might wake in the morning.”

The water feels lighter, more shallow like a wave bringing her to shore. This time, she rides the wave to shore.

 

~~~

 

White is the first thing Rio sees as her eyelids flutter open. 

The beeping is the first thing she hears, though no longer muffled, but crystal clear, and loud. Alive.

Her throat burns, her chest heavy, like a cinder block is laid over her body. Every inhale is labored, but she feels the flow of oxygen through her nose, and can trace it down to her lungs.

A shadow moves at the edge of her vision, a chair scraping against the floor like nails on chalkboard. Then a voice.

“Holy shit.”

She knows that voice.

She turns her head ever so slightly, the dull ache restraining most of her movements, but she does it anyway.

“You're awake.”

It’s quiet, cautious, full of disbelief.

“Did- did the kid make it?” Her voice is scratchy, like the rocks found a place there.

“Thanks to you,” he says, eyes softening.

Relief loosens every muscle in Rio’s body. She stops resisting the pain, letting the exhaustion in her body close her heavy eyelids. The last thing she remembers is him fumbling for the call button.

 

~~~

 

The next time she wakes, it’s quiet. 

No beeping or voices, just the quiet shuffle of footsteps muffled by the glass she can't see.

Her eyelids are slow to open, they feel lighter than they were the last time. The light isn't blinding, just a soft dim glow illuminating the room in a gentle haze.

She blinks, and then she notices the shadow in the chair nearby. It’s not him, but a woman sitting cross legged in a tailored suit. She’s reading over a tablet with a kind of poise that makes the silence feel deliberate.

The sudden movement jerks her attention away from the device, towards the patient in the bed, and her features soften.

“You’re awake.” Her voice is low and smooth. Controlled, like someone meant to capture attention.

Rio doesn't answer right away. Her throat is still dry, like it’s filled with cigarette ashes.

“Don’t. You’ve been through hell and back.”

She doesn't listen. She shifts to sit up and instantly regrets it. A sharp, searing pain shoots through her entire side and she almost blacks out at the sensation.

She falls back, breathing shallow. Her right arm is retained in a sling, cradling her chest.

The woman stands up, pressing a button nearby and immediately the pain settles her body.

“Where…” Rio doesn't recognize her own voice.

“Private practice,” she says. “You’ve been here for two weeks.”

Her eyes widened at the revelation. She cranes her head again, noticing the sterile elegance of the room. She swallows, hard.

“The kid…”

“Unharmed, thanks to you. He’ll be back, needed to go shower.”.

“Why are you here?”

“Someone needed to make sure you stayed alive,” she stands from her chair, readjusting her suit. “Rest, you’ll need it,” was all she said before leaving the room. Rio stares after her, the room falling silent except for the steady beeping coming from the monitor next to her.

 

~~~

 

A while later, a real doctor finally comes through to her room.

“Hi Rio. I’m Dr. Jennifer Kale. I’ll be overseeing your treatment. How would you rate your pain on a scale of one to ten?”

“About a 9 doc.”

She nods, writing something on the tablet in her hand and Rio’s eyebrows scrunch. “You’re lucky, a fall like that could've killed you. You suffered two fractured ribs, and a dislocated shoulder. It’s going to take you time to heal and you’ll need physical therapy for your shoulder. In the meantime, the best thing you can do for yourself is rest, we’ll manage your pain here and ensure everything heals properly.”

Rio nods, trying to process it all. Her mind is still foggy and she feels like she can only process about seventy-percent of what she was just told.

“You’ve been here for two weeks now,” right. That’s what the woman had said. “We’ll keep you here a few more days for observation, make sure everything is on track. Then we can discuss your discharge.”

“Discharge?” Rio echoes. “I don't live in the city.”

“Agatha’s told me to tell you not to worry about that, or the cost of your stay.”

She’s too tired to argue, or to even ask who this Agatha person is, so she only nods.

Dr. Kale moves to the monitor by her bed, writing down more notes then heads to the door.

“Seriously, rest. I’ll be back to check in later.”

With the soft click of the door, Rio’s mind is left racing with a million thoughts and a million more questions, none of which she has the energy to chase.

 

~~~

 

The kid comes back a little while later, with a bag in tow. He takes a double step when he feels Rio staring at him, perplexed.

“You’re awake,” he says. It’s quiet, familiar, filled with breathy joy. “I have snacks if you're hungry, the hospital food is shit.”

“Thanks kid,” she rasps though she’s not sure she can stomach anything more than ice chips right now.

“I should be the one thanking you,” he replies, eyes suddenly distant. There’s something haunted in the way he says it, like he’s replayed the fall a million times. “I’m Nicky by the way. Agatha told me to give you this.”

He rummages through his bag and pulls out a sleek tablet, like the one the woman had.

“Agatha?” She reaches for the device and stares blankly. The name rattles around her head, like it should mean something.

“Yeah, my mom. She’s at work right now but wants you to have it.”

She inspects the device, it’s light, not too heavy. On the back there’s a distinct ‘H’ in the center carved through the smooth silver metal. The screen illuminates life with a simple background, a mountain backdrop and for the first time, Rio misses home.

Rio hums her thanks to Nicky. He takes a seat nearby and opens a bag of chips.

“So, did Jen catch you up on everything?” She nods. 

“Yeah, she said a few more days to make sure I’m healing properly.”

“Cool, she mentioned you’ll need physical therapy. My mom’s already got a place lined up.”

There’s pride in the way he relays the information, soft, but she can hear beneath the surface that it sounds distant.

“She did all that for me?”

“Yeah, she’s thorough. What she says goes,” he shrugs like he’s used to it. He shakes his head out of his thoughts. “So, what do you want to watch?”

“Uhm, something light, easy.”

He nods, like he isn’t expecting anything else. Nicky unlocks the tablet in her hands and props it up on the table tray. He opens an app and scrolls until he finds a show that Rio doesn't recognize. To be honest, she doesn’t recognize any of it. The pictures move so fast that she doesn’t have time to register each option on the screen.

Eventually he lands on a show about a group of detectives in the city and Rio’s already lost the plot. Things are happening so fast on screen, there’s so many characters with varying personalities, but the distraction is nice. It’s a funny show, she realizes, but with each chuckle, there’s a sharp pain that comes through her chest. Nicky doesn’t seem to notice however, he’s too busy watching the show.

She doesn’t know how many episodes pass before exhaustion wafts over her eyelids again. Rio closes them, still trying to hear what’s going on, but sleep wins in the end.

 

~~~

 

The next time Rio wakes, Agatha is back. She’s not in a suit this time, only a pair of slacks and a loose cardigan. It must be the weekend. 

The tablet on her table is shut off and laid down on the table. She doesn’t know what time it is, but the sun beaming through the window by her bed still shines brightly.

“Good morning,” she says. Gone is the authoritative voice Rio heard the other day, and replaced is one of calm, composed, gentle.

Rio blinks at her, eyes still adjusting to the light. “Morning?”

“You were out for a while. Nicky said you laughed at the show, he’ll take it as a win.”

Rio hums faintly at the memory. She remembers detectives, she remembers laughing, she remembers the pain. “Hurt like a bitch.”

Agatha sets her tablet down and leans forward on her elbows. “Jen said the discharge procedure will be ready by tomorrow night.”

Rio takes a shallow breath. Relief floods her system, glad to be free of these machines and the endless nothing, then fear bobs in her throat. She doesn’t know if she can face the park or the town.

Agatha seems to catch the flicker of tension in Rio’s jaw. “You don’t have to go back right away,” her voice is smooth, measured. “You’ll need time and privacy.”

Rio scoffs lightly. “And where am I going to get that?”

“With me.”

She huffs a small breath, too dry and painful to be real, but it’s something. “Why?”

Agatha doesn’t flinch, she expected the resistance, but she remained undeterred in her decision. “Because, you saved someone important to me. And you’re going to need help, something tells me you’ll only re-injure yourself if you were left alone.”

The silence prolongs. Rio finally breaks the eye contact she was holding with Agatha and turns away. “I’m not some fix-it charity case.”

“I never said you were.” Her voice doesn’t waiver, it’s patient, reassuring.

“I’m not good with favors.”

“It’s a good thing this isn’t one,” Agatha goes to stand once again. “I’ll have a car ready tomorrow afternoon. Jen will be in later to bring in some of the paperwork and go over details.”

She straightens her sweater out, her silhouette sharp against the light lavender wall. 

“You always this generous with strangers?” She asks, not accusatory, simply curious.

Agatha pauses at the handle, her breath hitching slightly. “You’re not a stranger,” and she was gone.

The sun has disappeared behind the dark clouds and she can hear rain pellets against the glass outside. Nicky comes back in a purple NYU hoodie, the hood soaked through and another bag. 

“Last night here, you excited to finally ditch this joint?” He smiles at the prospect of spending more time with her.

“Yeah kid.” Rio’s smile doesn’t exactly reach her eyes, but it’s there, subtle.

“I’m not a kid, I’m nineteen, I turn twenty in November,” he jests. Rio’s mind is still foggy. She just shrugs at the information. 

“Shouldn’t you be in school?” Her eyes drift down to his hoodie.

Nicky returns the shrug. “Doesn’t start till next week, but even then, I’m taking independent studies so I’ll still be around.”

“What are you in for?” She asks, intrigued. She wonders if he takes after his mom, she’s still not quite sure what Agatha does for work, maybe a lawyer, definitely someone with authority. 

“Film.”

This takes Rio by surprise, she expected something authoritative with big suits and a briefcase. Something like his mother.

Still, he pulls the tablet from his bag and props it up on the table, like he did last time. 

“Figured we’d get another few episodes in,” he queues up the same show, something titled Brooklyn something and hits resume.

Rio still has no idea what’s going on in the show, but the humor is much appreciated. She knows that not all police precincts are run like the one in the show. It’s definitely more lively than the one in Westview. She’s pretty sure the last time they were involved was when Mrs. Davis, the mayor’s wife, called them trying to get a cat out from a tree.

When the credits roll on the episode, he pauses. His voice cuts through the sound of trumpets coming from the speakers.

“Hey - what’s Westview like year-round?”

She turns her head towards him, blinking in surprise. “Why?”

He shrugs, still looking at the tablet. “I’ve been going ever since I was a kid. Mama took me a couple times a year. I noticed it hasn’t really changed over the years.”

Rio exhales, a short laugh. It doesn't hurt as much as the previous day, but a dull ache still radiates throughout her chest. “That’s not far off.”

“Do you miss it?”

“Every day.”

He hums, clearly dissatisfied with her answer but doesn’t press. Instead, he hits play on the next episode, but her mind isn’t focused on it.

In her head, she’s back in her cabin, where nature fills her senses. She longs for the smell of pine, the feel of natural wood, the gravel beneath her feet.

She hasn’t realized she’s dozed off until the roaring thunder outside wakes her up. Nicky’s still by her bedside, headphones in, watching something else on the tablet.

She shifts slightly, wincing at the movement. Her eyes drift to the rain outside. She feels like a fish out of water, boxed in by the fluorescent light and the lavender walls and artificial ceiling tiles.

Back home, storms didn’t sound like this. They were calm, she could see the clouds moving through the sky, sometimes at a fast pace, other times agonizingly slow.

Still, she closes her eyes and for a moment, she’s back on her porch, with a chipped mug in hand and damp wooden planks beneath her bare feet, the sound of rain pelting the natural earth and the rush of the stream through the thunder.

Rio exhales slowly, letting herself stay there for a little while longer.

A soft knock on her door pulls her back to reality. Nicky’s eyes dart up to the nurse entering the room and he moves off to the side, out of her way.

“Hey Rio, how are you feeling?” Her voice is sweet, like honey she thinks, but she supposes that’s her job.

“Like I fell twenty feet,” she jokes.

“Well, that did happen. time for meds.” She takes the I.V. line in her arm and pushes a needle into it and replaces the saline bag hanging on the hook. “You’re confirmed to be released tomorrow afternoon, is someone going to be here to pick you up?”

“I’ll be here,” Nicky speaks up.

“Good, it’s nice to have someone to look after you.”

“I’m mostly here for snacks.”

Rio rolls her eyes but grins, barely. 

The medication hits her system like a soothing massage. She hates it, she can’t wait until she’s off of it completely. She doesn’t like not being able to feel things properly, being reliant on machines and foreign drugs in order to simply exist.

“Alright, I’ll leave you two to get back to it. Press the call button if you need anything and get some rest. You’ll need it for tomorrow.”

With that, she’s gone. Rio’s head rolls back against the crunchy pillow, her mind getting lost in the haze of medication.

Nicky resumes whatever he’s watching but Rio lets the weight of the medication pull her back to slumber.

“Alright, as discussed you’re scheduled to start PT next week. You should take these twice a day at first, then as needed with food,” Dr. Kale instructs, her tone unwavering and firm. She places several orange pill bottles on the table next to Rio’s bed.

Agatha leans against the far wall of the room, arms crossed, listening with intent as her old friend hands the bottle over to Rio. 

She nods in affirmation under the sharp gaze of her now guardian, she awkwardly signs the release forms with her non-dominant hand and Dr. Kale leaves the room. Agatha offers a quiet “Thanks Jen,” which takes Rio back slightly.

“I have some clothes for you,” Agatha says, picking up a rugged duffel back from the floor and bringing it to Rio’s bedside. 

When she rummages through, she’s surprised to see familiar flannels and sweatpants and a few photos tucked away in the side pockets. Her chest tightens - someone went to her home.

“I had Lilia get some of your clothes and personal items from your cabin when I called her and explained everything. Sent a helicopter so no one had to hike down. Didn’t want to risk it,” she explains when she catches the look on Rio’s face.

She pulls out a faded green Westview National Park zip up hoodie and slips her good arm through the sleeve, letting the other side drape her sling. It’s awkward, not ideal, but it smells like pine and rain, home.

Rio shimmies into a pair of loose sweatpants with quiet determination. Agatha watches, mildly amused, but stays where she is. She doesn’t offer help. Not yet. Not unless Rio asks.

She wants her to feel like herself again, even just a little.

It takes longer than Rio would like to get moving. She swings her legs over the edge of the bed slowly, every muscle groaning in protest. The tile is cool under her bare feet. Too clean, too smooth, too unfamiliar. She pulls out a pair of old worn slides, the foam bringing her more comfort than she realizes. Agatha moves to her side without a word, letting her get there on her own terms.

They don’t speak much as they make their way through the halls of the private practice. Just the soft squeak of sneakers on the floor and the low hum of the lights filling the silence. Nicky had left earlier to pull the car around. Rio’s glad, she hates the thought of him seeing her like this: slow, small, weaker than she’s used to.

Outside, the rain has settled to a mist, just enough to dot the pavement and she can see the small dots against the cotton. 

A sleek black car with tinted windows sits at the curb, engine running, door already open.

Agatha steadies her gently with one hand at Rio’s back. “Watch your step,” she murmurs. It’s not a command, just a presence.

Rio nods and leans into the motion as she angles herself into the seat. The firm leather below her is cool to her touch. Every small shift sends a new wave of pain throughout her ribs and shoulder, but Agatha doesn’t rush her.

Once she’s in, Agatha reaches across her chest and buckles the estbelt, careful not to jostle her arm. Their faces are close for a moment, breath mingling in the narrow space between them.

“Comfortable?” Agatha asks softly.

Rio exhales through her nose. “Define comfortable.”

A small smirk tugs at Agatha’s lips. “Survivable, then?”

“Barely.”

Agatha closes the door with care, circling to the driver's side. When she slides in and pulls away from the curb, Rio lets her head rest against the cool surface.

The car hums beneath them, a low, steady rhythm that almost lulls Rio back to sleep. She watches as raindrops chase each other across the glass, her breath fogging a small crescent against the window.

Agatha’s hands stay steady on the wheel, her knuckles relaxed, but her eyes flick to the mirror every few minutes, checking on her.

“You doing okay?” She asks eventually, voice low.

Rio doesn’t open her eyes. “I’m fine.”

A short pause engulfs the car. Nicky is busy on his phone, typing away and the soft clicking of the keys keeps Rio awake.

“You don’t have to pretend,” Agatha adds, softer this time.

Rio opens her eyes again, gaze still trained on the misty concrete jungle blurring by. “I’m not pretending. I’m just tired.”

Agatha nods, understanding threaded through the silence.

“Do you want music?” She asks after a moment.

“Something calm.”

Agatha nudges Nicky’s side and he immediately connects his phone to the bluetooth in the car. Instantly, a loud bass thrumming jolts through the speakers, making Rio wince and Agatha shoot her son a pointed look.

“Sorry! Sorry, wrong playlist,” Nicky fumbles, panicked, scrolling wildly. “Sorry, sorry- hang on-”

The bass cuts, replaced by the soft strum of an acoustic guitar. 

Rio exhales, eyes fluttering shut again. “Better.”

Nicky slinks down in his seat. “Thought I had the right one queued up. My bad.”

Agatha shakes her head, the smallest smile tugging at the corner of her mouth. “You had one job Nicky.”

“It was an honest mistake. Besides, technically you didn’t say no to trap remixes.”

Rio huffs a quiet laugh from the backseat. “That would’ve definitely sent me back to the hospital.”

The car settles again into calm, the music weaving through the hum of tires on wet pavement. Agatha drives on, steady and quiet.

 

~~~

 

Rio doesn’t remember falling asleep, but she wakes to the soft click of the car shifting into park and another dim, overhead light. 

She blinks slowly, groggy and disoriented. She feels trapped again when she sees concrete at every corner. Her head is still against the window, chilled glass pressed to her temple. The walls of the parking garage are smooth concrete, clean and well-lit, not a speck of grime in sight. She catches a glimpse at a few other cars parked in the area but her mind is focused elsewhere.

Agatha’s already stepped out of the driver’s seat, her door closing with a muffled thud. She rounds the car and opens Rio’s, peering in gently.

“Hey,” she says, soft enough not to startle. “We’re here.”

“Where’s here?” Rio asks, voice thick with sleep.

“My place. Penthouse. Elevator’s over there.”

Rio shifts and winces, her body stiff and aching from the drive and awkward position. “Of course you live in a penthouse.”

Agatha smirks. “Would you expect anything less?”

Nicky hops out and opens the trunk, already gathering bags. “Come on, it’s got the best view of the skyline and a bathtub that could probably heal broken bones.”

“That’s not medically accurate,” Agatha calls over her shoulder.

“Then build one mama.”

Agatha squeezes her hand lightly as they reach the car. “Lucky for me.”

The drive is short. Just a few turns through the neighborhood before the streets open into the small town center with wide sidewalks and neatly trimmed trees lining the blocks; the kind of place to bully like everything important is within walking distance.

Agatha shows as they approach a modest diner tucked between a florist and a bookstore. Nothing flashy, just an old neon ‘Open’ sign humming through the window and a true of cars parked out front.

Rio glances out the window, half-smirking. “You really don't do flashy out here, do you?”

Agatha shuts off the engine with a small smile. “Sometimes simple is better.”

Rio unbuckles, giving a sidelong look. “That’s not the brand you advertise.”

Agatha arches a brow as she steps out of the car. “I contain multitudes.”

Rio huffs as she falls into step beside her. “That's one way to put it.”

Inside, the diner hums with low conversation - nothing invasive, just the usual background noise of a place that’s existed long enough for people to stop being impressed by it. The waitress behind the counter gives Agatha a brief nod, like she's a familiar face, but leaves it at that.

They’re both led to a booth in the back.

Rio slides in, her eyes drifting across the room for a beat before settling on Agatha. “You've brought people here before?”

Agatha leans back slightly. “No, not like this.”

Rio lets the words settle, voice dry but with a small pull of a smile. “Didn’t know I was making history.”

Agatha smiles softly in return. “You are.”

Before Rio can deflect again, the waitress arrives, setting down two mugs of coffee without needing to ask. She offers a polite nod and quietly retreats, giving them space.

Rio glances at the cup, then back at Agatha. “You’ve definitely been here before.”

Agatha lifts her mug with a small shrug. “I raised Nicky here until he got sick.”

Rio glances up at her, eyes softening a little. “You don’t talk about that part much.”

Agatha swirls her coffee gently, gaze dropping for a moment. “There’s not much to say that doesn’t sound heavier than it needs to be.”

Rio watches her for a beat, then says quietly. “He turned out okay."

Agatha huffs a quiet laugh. “Stubborn, argumentative, mouthy… yeah. He’s fine.”

Rio smiles faintly behind her mug. “Wonder where he got that from.”

Agatha shoots her a small look over the rim of her cup. “I’m deeply offended.”

Rio lifts her hands slightly, playing innocent. “I’m just saying - genetics are weird.”

Agatha shakes her head, but there’s warmth behind it. “You really don’t know when to quit, do you?”

Rio smiles, voice softer now. “Hasn’t failed me yet.”

Agatha watches her for a moment, eyes warm. “You always this cooperative on first dates?”

Rio huffs. “Can’t say I’ve tested the sample size.”

Agatha’s smile deepens, but she doesn’t tease it. She just nods once. “Well. You’re doing fine.”

The waitress returns, setting down two plates of waffles, and vanishes just as quietly.

Rio eyes her plate before glancing back at her. “They know your order?”

Agatha lifts her coffee cup with a small smile. “Old habits.”

Rio smirks faintly. “How many secret places like this do you have?”

Agatha tilts her head, pretending to think. “Enough. Not as many as people assume.”

Rio hums, cutting into her waffle. “Guess we’re both good at keeping people out when we want to.”

Agatha watches her for a moment, voice softer now. “I was. For a long time.”

Rio glances up, meeting her gaze. “Not anymore?”

Agatha gives a small shake of her head, smile faint but certain. “Not with you.”

Rio doesn’t say anything at first. She just holds eye contact a beat longer than usual before looking back down at her plate. The silence between them shifts - still quiet, but heavier in a different way now.

After a few moments, Rio speaks again, her voice lower, careful. “Do you ever miss it? Acting? I imagine someone who studied it has a lot of creative passion and I suppose it’s a way to release it.”

Agatha smiles, like she wasn’t expecting the question but appreciates it anyway. “That’s one way to put it.”

She pauses, thinking for a moment. “There was something freeing about it, yes. Pretending to be someone else meant I didn’t have to sit in my own mess for a while, even after I started using.”

Her thumb brushes lightly against the rim of her coffee cup. “It let me feel like I was in control of something.”

Rio watches her, processing that. Then, voice quiet but sure. “And now you build things instead.”

Agatha’s smile shifts, softer. “Now I build things that matter.”

Rio gives a small nod. “And you still get to stay behind the scenes.”

Agatha hums in agreement. “Exactly where I prefer to be.”

The silence lingers for a moment, the clinking of plates and low hum of conversation filling the background. Rio traces a slow line along the edge of her coffee cup, her gaze fixed on it.

Then, quieter, she says, “I think that’s what’s been hardest for me.”

Agatha tilts her head. “What is?”

Rio’s thumb pauses for half a second before she answers. “Not having anything to go back to.”

Agatha’s gaze softens. “What about the park?”

Rio exhales through her nose, gaze fixed on the table. “I’ve thought about it… I want to go back. I just don’t know what it’s going to feel like when I do.”

Agatha’s gaze stays soft. “That’s okay.”

Rio’s fingers trace along the edge of her plate. “It’s not the place I’m nervous about. It’s me.”

Agatha gives her hand a gentle squeeze. “Whatever’s waiting, we’ll face it together.”

Rio glances up at her, meeting her eyes for a beat longer than usual. “I know.”

The quiet stretches again, but this time it’s easier with no pressure to fill it.

They finish their breakfast without rushing - Agatha barely touches her second cup of coffee, Rio working through the waffles in steady bites. It’s not about the food anymore. Just the simple comfort of sitting across from each other, like they’ve done this a hundred times before.

Eventually, Rio sets her fork down, nudging her plate forward slightly. “I’m good.”

Agatha reaches for the check without a word. There’s no silent debate, no polite back-and-forth. Rio doesn’t move for a wallet she doesn’t even have on her. She just lets Agatha handle it, which somehow feels like a bigger surrender than anything either of themsays out loud.

They step outside into the crisp air. The sun’s a little higher now, spilling lazy light across the quiet street.

Agatha unlocks the car, and Rio moves toward the passenger side but lingers for a second before opening the door.

Agatha catches the hesitation. “You okay?”

Rio adjusts her jacket, voice quieter. “Yeah. Just… not in a hurry to be stuck inside yet.”

Agatha then watches her for a beat, then tips her head toward the open road. “Then we won’t be,” but she doesn’t offer anything else.

The drive doesn’t take long. Agatha weaves through the quiet backroaods like she’s done it before, well she has.

Eventually, she turns into a small lot, pulling up in front of a faded sign that reads ‘PUTT PARADISE - OPEN YEAR ROUND.’

The course is practically empty. Just a few scattered cars, a couple of kids chasing each other near the oversized fiberglass giraffe at hole two. It’s the kind of place that doesn’t expect anyone important to show up.

Agatha parks and cuts the engine.

Rio glances out the window. “You’ve officially surprised me.”

Agatha smiles faintly, unbothered. “I’m only slightly offended it took mini golf for me to surprise you.”

Rio huffs softly, unbuckling. “You’re full of surprises. This just wasn’t on the list.”

Agatha rounds the car as Rio steps out. “Good. Keeps things interesting.”

Inside, the teenager behind the counter barely glances up as Agatha hands over a few bills. They’re handed two sleek black putters and a bin of bright neon balls.

Rio reaches in first, pulling out a green ball without hesitation.

Agatha watches her for a beat, then selects a purple one with a faint smirk. “Very on brand for both of us.”

Rio raises a brow. “You rehearsed that one, didn’t you?”

Agatha feigns offense. “Please. My wit is entirely natural.”

They step out onto the first hole, no scorecard, no real rules.

Agatha gestures toward the green. “Ladies first.”

Rio lines up her shot, careful but casual, and sinks it with two clean strokes.

Agatha hums, impressed. “I should’ve known you’d be suspiciously good at this.”

“Believe it or not, I haven’t touched a putter since I was in high school.”

Agatha smirks as she steps up to the mat. “And yet, here I am about to be thoroughly outplayed.”

Rio watches, deadpan. “There’s no way you were always beat by a child.”

Agatha liens up her shot, barely glancing up. “Nicky was ruthless. Still is.”

She taps the ball. It skims past the edge of the obstacle, narrowly avoiding a full disaster, and settles just short of the hole.

Rio arches a brow. “You gonna use Nicky as an excuse for everything?”

Agatha smiles as she steps back, unrepentant. “When it fits.” She taps her ball in the next shot.

Rio lines up for the next hole, not even glancing at Agatha. “Convenient.”

“Practical,” Agatha corrects, watching her sink the putt cleanly.

They continue moving through the course with soft banter, the kind of comfortable rhythm that’s become second nature to them. Rio’s shots stay consistent and steady even when her energy subtly shifts. The kind of tired that doesn't announce itself, but that Agatha’s learned to notice anyways.

By hole eight, Rio takes just a little longer to square her shoulders. The pauses between the swings stretch an extra breath or two. Nothing dramatic, just quiet fatigue creeping in around the edges.

Agatha adjusts without comment, letting the pace slow naturally.

When she lands a lucky bank shot at hole nine, Agatha offers a small grin. “See? Dramatic arc.”

Rio huffs softly. “That or dumb luck.”

“Let me have my narrative.”

By hole ten, the competition softens, but never fully disappears. The banter has thinned into easy, steady focus. Neither of them is playing for anything but the quiet.

Rio’s shots are still sharp - she’s always been precise when she wants to be - but Agatha notices the slight extra beat between each swing. A fraction longer to set her feet. A little more care lowering into position. The kind of fatigue that only someone who knows her well would see.

Agatha never mentions it. She adjusts her own pace, keeping things easy.

At hole twelve, Rio sinks another clean shot. She leans on the putter for half a second longer before stepping aside. 

Agatha watches the small pause and keeps her tone light. “I should’ve made you carry both putters. Could’ve slowed you down a bit.”

Rio huffs softly. “You’re already losing. No need to embarrass yourself further.

Agatha smirks, lining up her shot. The ball clips the barrier, bounces wide, and rolls off to the edge.

She stands up, entirely unbothered. “Still plenty of time for my comeback.”

Rio shakes her head faintly, that dry little almost-smile pulling at her mouth again. “I admire the optimism.”

They fall back into rhythm. Agatha lets Rio lead, watching without hovering, knowing full well where Rio’s limits sit, but respecting the fact that Rio won’t ask for a break until she chooses to.

By hole fifteen, Rio’s shifts between holes have grown quietere, more deliberate. She’s not struggling - but she’s burning energy.

The air stays comfortably cool for early afternoon - not hot, not cold, just bright and crisp enough that it makes the whole palace feel slightly surreal. The sun cuts sharp through the sky but not at an angle yet.

Agatha lands a clean shot on sixteen, glancing toward Rio with a small grin. “Dramatic arc.”

Rio exhales through her nose, amused but a little breathier now. “Keep telling yourself that.”

At hole seventeen, Rio lines up her shot, takes a slow breath, and sinks it with precision. Her balance wavers for a split second after, but she steadies herself quickly, like it’s instinct now.

Agatha clocks it, but says nothing.

Finally, they reach hole eighteen. It’s one of those absurd contraptions with the oversized spinning barrel that swallows the ball when you time it right.

Rio steps up first, narrowing her focus, watching the rotation carefully before tapping her ball through the gap cleanly. The ball disappears with a soft clunk into the machine.

Agatha steps up beside her. “Still not convinced you haven’t been secretly training.”

Rio smirks faintly, not rising to it. “I’m just talented."

Agatha taps her ball through on the second spin, letting it drop into the barrel behind Rio’s.

As they turn to leave, Agatha’s hand settles lightly against Rio’s back - no pressure, just a small steady point of contact as they walk toward the car.

Back in the lot, Agatha opens up the passenger door first, standing there for a brief beat. “You okay?”

Rio pauses before sitting, one hand braced lightly on the frame of the car. “Yeah. Just need to sit.”

But before she drops fully into the seat, she glances at Agatha, eyes holding hers for a little longer than usual.

The quiet stretches for just a moment, not uncomfortable, just there. Like Rio’s trying to stretch the moment for her memory

“Thank you,” Rio says softly. “For today. It was nice to just exist again.”

Agatha’s smile is small, but steady. “Anytime.”

There’s another beat of quiet, and this time, Agatha leans in just a little, giving Rio every chance to pull back.

Rio doesn’t.

The kiss is soft, brief - nothing grand, just two people allowing themselves the space to exist a little closer than before. When they part, neither speaks right away.

Agatha’s voice stays gentle. “Now let’s get you home.”

Rio huffs a quiet breath, the corner of her mouth tugging into the faintest smile. “Yeah.”

She settles into the seat fully now, exhaling as she leans back.

Agatha closes the door gently, circling around to the driver’s side.

The drive home stays quiet, comfortably so. The early afternoon sun filters through the windshield, casting warm streaks of light across Rio’s face.

For the first stretch, Rio stays awake, eyes drifting out of the window, half-lidded. But as the road hums steady beneath them, her head eventually tips toward the glass, eyes slipping closed.

Agatha keeps her gaze forward, adjusting her speed almost unconsciously. She doesn’t need to look over.

She just drives, letting Rio rest.
