Agatha groans before she can stop herself and tries to play it off as a cough. 

 

“Rio’s grades are slipping in my class. She hasn’t been submitting her work.”

 

Agatha’s heart skips a beat. Logically, she knows that Rio’s probably not feeling great, but she wasn’t expecting this to affect her school work. A headache starts to throb behind her eyes. 

 

“I met with her, and she discussed that it's because she’s having some financial difficulties and needed to get a second job. A roommate situation fell through, and she’s now paying twice as much rent as she used to. Her stipend can’t cover it, so she’s putting in twenty hours a week at Trader Joe’s on top of all her classes, and tutoring.” 

 

Agatha is relieved that Rio’s trouble in school isn’t directly related to her, but that relief is quickly taken over by concern. 

 

“I want to submit her for an emergency relief grant with financial aid, and was wondering if you’d be willing to sign off on my application for her? I need another faculty member's approval. You know how bureaucratic this all can get.” 

 

“Of course.” Agatha breathes out. Send it over now, and I’ll sign digitally. 

 

“Great.” Lilia smiles. “Take care, Agatha. I’m here when you’re ready to stop isolating.” 

 

“Yeah, yeah,” Agatha grumbles. 

 

Lilia sends the document, and Agatha signs and sends it back. But worry is still swirling in her gut. Poor Rio. She wishes she could help out somehow. Then, she realizes she can.

 

 Agatha feels a little like a stalker, but she uses the school database and gets Rio’s address. She puts on a pair of jeans, an oversized sweater, and some big sunglasses, and drives to the bank. The maximum amount her bank will let her take out in cash is $5,000, but that should at least help for the next couple of months. Agatha puts the cash in a sealed envelope, writes Rio’s name on it, and drives to Rio’s address. 

 

Panic-donating to a student with whom you are on bad terms is not the behavior of an emotionally regulated person, but Agatha has to do something to help. Rio’s place is a dilapidated-looking multifamily in the shitty part of town. Luckily, Rio’s car isn’t in the driveway. Agatha sighs in relief. The alpha won’t see she’s here and know that’s where the money came from.  Agatha walks up to the mailbox and sees R. Vidal printed on one of the slots. She slips the money in, quickly walks back to the car, and speeds home. She smiles for the first time in so long that her cheeks ache from the unfamiliar stretch.
