Introductory movement

Boston always had this dampness this time of the year – its air reeked with the scent of damp earth and dying, brown leaves. It was a stark contrast to the usual sterile tang that usually permeated Rio’s world. Formaldehyde and cold steel, much more familiar smells, were replaced by the cloying sweetness of decaying foliage – a reminder of her current situation. She was running.


Not just running, fleeing – hunted.


The rain plastered her dark hair to her face, blurring the already dim streetlights of the outskirts of New Salem, a small neighborhood near Boston. The name, a cruel irony, served as a constant reminder of the past – one where women like her were burned at the stake for the crime of existing and being feared by men.


Rio, Dr. Rio Vidal, forensic pathologist for the Boston PD, was a witch. There weren’t many by now. The Trials took place to erase all her kind – they almost succeeded. And although being a witch today was not a crime, it was still frowned upon in every aspects of society. Laws forbid them from using magic. Schools and universities often took measures to make sure they did not enrolled, or eventually lag behind in class and get expelled. Marriage with or between witches was not permitted and they would often have a hard time owning property or companies. Yes, the echoes of the Salem Trials still resonated well into this city – fire was no longer the reaper, poverty and prejudice taking its place.

So, Rio was… the exception. And she was so because her identity of a witch was very well hidden throughout the years – she had her late mother to thank her for that.

But now, she was a suspect of murder. Not just any murder, but a series of them. All seemingly accidental deaths of women, all women with a subtle, almost imperceptible shimmer of magic around their souls – all witches. All deaths that she, as the coroner, was assigned to autopsy.

The first ones, Rio recorded as natural causes or unfortunate accidents. However, has months went by, she started to notice the subtle distortions in these death’s signatures, the faint residue of drained energy – telltale signs of deliberate, targeted eliminations. Murders.

She had dared to look closer, to question who was responsible for these deaths, even though her boss told her not to – “No one cares about witches. They probably deserved it. They are always like this, killed each other’s. They don’t know better; too dumb to even go to school anyway. It’s no waste, I’ll tell you that.”

And in doing so, she had become a target. Somehow.

Well, she knew how. Rio started to suspect that the Boston PD, or at least a faction within it, was hunting witches. And she, a witch in their midst, almost certain to have stumbled upon their secret. What she didn’t know how was: how they found out. How they found out about her suspicions and how they found out about her identity as a witch. Because she was always careful – but now she was being incriminated as the author of these murders.

So, to be running and hunted – for the first time in her life – made her feel like a caged animal. And caged animals rarely had the best decision-making process.

That was what led her to New Salem.

There was only one place she could go. One place where the shadows held more than just darkness – to the likes of her, at least. Lilia’s.

Lilia was the matriarch of the Serpent’s Coil, a known and dangerous gang that operated in Boston. Her restaurant – her façade – was in New Salem, a seemingly normal city neighborhood, but where no sane soul would dare to go, much less in a rainy night such as this.

But Rio was starting to believe that her sanity needed to be questioned. She had to, because she was contemplating the flickering neon sign of this women’s restaurant: “Lilia’s”. Not much left to hide – which is how Rio knew this woman was to be feared: she displayed her name in big, bold letters for the city to see where she was.

But her mother told her about Lilia, when Rio was still a child. She told her how Lilia was an old fortress, like the last remaining coven, hidden within the heart of the Boston's crumbling infrastructure. If Rio was to be in any trouble, Lilia’s was to place to seek.


And Rio wondered just how many mothers would tell these stories to their witch children. Of how Lilia was to be their only chance at the precarious balance between survival after exposure.
The rain intensified, almost mirroring the growing dread in her heart. She was a scientist, a woman of logic and reason. The cold, hard truth was that she had given up on her magic for science – a desperate attempt to a normal life. And now her life had shattered – and the magic she had tried to suppress for so long was rumbling inside her, feeding on her emotions of loss, rage and desperation.


Let go.


She suppressed it all again. That voice in her head – or in her heart, she never knew.


With one last sigh, she left the graffiti-covered walls of the greasy alley she was in, and crossed the road to Lilia’s – the distant wail of sirens echoing the soundtrack to the city's forgotten corners.


She pushed the rusted door of the restaurant open, a creaked sound climbing up Rio’s neck. There truly was nothing in New Salem that was helping her journey in search of safe heaven.


Rio was at first surprised to see that this actually looked like a restaurant. A small one, sure – but a functional restaurant still. There was a faint smell of cooked onions and garlic in the air, embraced by the subtle energy, so small Rio almost missed it. All Serpent’s Coil members were magic users. Witches, Rio corrected in her mind. This was most definitely the base for Lilia’s gang.
Two women were eating dinner at a corner table. A boy (a teenager?) was behind the counter, turning the pizza ovens off. All three looked at her with a menacing curiosity.


“Yes?” Asked one of the women. Her voice stern.


Rio felt the need to clear her throat before speaking. She could not afford to have her voice fail at a moment like this.


“I am looking for Lilia Calderu.”


She heard the boy make a small gasp while the same women raised an eyebrow.


“Oh?”, she interjected. “And who might be looking for her?”


“My name is Rio Vidal.” Rio was really trying to sound calm and sure of herself. She wasn’t.


She women stood from the table – her faded pink dress looked too thin for this autumn whether – and walked to Rio with strong steps and an almost mocking smile.


“I’m sorry Miss Vidal, I must not be acquainted with your royalness. Because surely a peasant women would not dare come looking for Lilia with manners like yours.”


This was a treat, of that Rio was sure.


“Care to name your family crest? Your status? Your bank account?”


No, this was mockery.


Rio must have looked like a spooked animal, because the boy intervened.


“Don’t be like that, Jen. She looks desperate.” She was, and she hated that he could see that. “Are you looking for help?”


The boy saved her. The boy. This was a new low for Rio, for sure. But she couldn’t be picky now. Not when she really was desperate.


“Yes. I really need to speak to Lilia Calderu. Tell her Maria Vidal sent me.” This wasn’t completely true, and she wasn’t sure it was going to work. But this was a time for a hail mary.


The boy was quick to disappear behind a door at the back of the place, and Rio mentally thanked him for the hurry.


Jen, however, stood her ground in front of Rio – serving as a barrier between her and the back door. Her obsidian eyes were piercing her, a mocking smile on her lips. Rio decided to look at the second women in the room. The small, dark-haired (with a red strip?) women was still enjoying her pasta dinner, seemingly oblivious to what was happening – although Rio doubted that was true. She was not going to test that.


It took three very long minutes for the boy to return, the whole time with Jen’s narrowed eyes glued to her.


“She said you can come in.”


His expression was unreadable. Despite that, Rio felt her breath let go – she didn’t know she was holding it.


Jen, on the other hand, looked offended. When she turned to the boy with a question on her lips, he was quick to answer: “Agatha is there.”, as that was an explanation alone.


Yet, this seemed to ease Jen just the tiny bit. Enough to step aside to let Rio pass and walk down the dimly lit hallway, closer to the heavy wooden back door.


“Good luck.” Whispered the boy, with a smile on his face, pushing the door open and stepping aside. God, thought Rio, to be teenage and naive again, as she entered, hesitant, into the back room. She wasn’t sure what awaited her, but she knew there was no turning back now.


She was expecting to meet Lilia by now, but Rio just entered another hallway. But this one was different, much narrower. Here, the air was thick with the scent of incense and something metallic, like iron or blood. The walls were lined with peeling wallpaper, its floral pattern faded. The floor creaked under her weight as she moved forward, and Rio reconsidered her teenage choice to become a horror movie enthusiast – even with that, and with the fact that her literal job (did she still had it?) was being a coroner, the whole scene just creeped her brains out.


Yup, she though, if this was a horror movie, I would definitely be walking into my own death trap. Rio’s heart pounded in her chest as she approached the second door.


Before she could touch it, the door swung open. Rio could see the floor covered in a big, thick, patterned rug as she stepped in. The big chandelier in the ceiling was not casting a bright light – and Rio wondered if that was why there were scented candles burning in every corner. Rio noticed music playing so softly she could barely hear it – in fact, she wasn’t sure she was actually listening to Frank Sinatra, or it was just a suggestion in her head from seeing the vinyl from the corner of her left eye. 


Behind the large black table – its surface cluttered with jars, artifacts and papers – sat a woman. Lilia Calderu. With silver-streaked hair pulled back into a sleek bun, and a long, yellow robe over her shoulders and back. The shiny pendant shaped like a coiled serpent was as commanding as her figure.


But Rio sensed that she was not the one filling the room with a not so faint hum of magic, a vibration that was making the hairs on the back of her neck stand. Rio looked at the presence on the edge of the table.


A woman with an imposing presence filled the space on that side of the room. She wasn’t taller than Rio, and her tailored black suit – that hugged her figure – matched perfectly her piercing blue eyes, locked onto her from the moment she stepped into the room. Rio swallowed the lump in her throat when she looked at the long and disheveled brown hair that framed a sharp face. Yes, the magic that was making the air difficult to breath was definitely coming from this woman.


A sparkle in this room would cause a city-wide explosion, for sure.


“Dr. Vidal.” Lilia’s smooth voice broke her trance.


And Rio was a bit thrown away by the softness of that voice. Yes, her mother had told her about Lilia Calderu – but the stories were conflicting.


Because her mother always said to stay away from Lilia’s gang and New Salem, saying it was not a place for a young, respectable lady to be. The Serpent’s Coil had a fame of violence and drugs over the city, and Rio has heard all about it since she started working with the Boston PD.


But every now and then – maybe just three times in her life – when her mother looked sad or afraid over something she refused to explain, she would tell Rio that in case of trouble, Lilia would offer her protection. She would make sure Rio would know where to find Lilia, and she had to say her mother’s name. But Rio was a child then, and never quite questioned this. Her mother had died when she was just a teenager, and left more questions than memories.


But this wasn’t the time for sentimentality. She had come here for a reason. And Lilia seemed to know who she was.


“You know me?” Her calling by her work title was not missed by Rio.


Lilia smiled, a faint, knowing curve of her lips reaching her eyes. “Of course. Witches don’t get through college, my dear. They don’t get to be doctors. They don’t get to live in the center of the big city. And they certainly don’t get to work with the Boston PD.” Her voice still low and smooth, but something else behind it now. “But then again, they don’t usually renounce the witch they are.”


Ah, there it was, judgement.


“But I suppose that was to be expected by Maria’s daughter.”


Rio swallowed hard, not sure if coming here had been a good idea. “You knew my mother?”. She was sure she framed it as a question.


“Of course. Maria was a remarkably smart woman. And fiercely protective of her daughter. I’ve always admired her resolutions, despite our disagreements.”


Rio opened her mouth inquisitively but she was cut by Lilia. “But you are not here for your mother.” She motioned her hand for Rio to seat at the chair on the opposite side of the table. “Seat.”


And Rio did.


“I need your help. Your protection.” Rio said, her voice with a small tremble despite the efforts to keep it steady. “I think there is someone killing witches and making it look like accidents or natural causes.” Lilia’s hard expression told her to keep going. “I don’t really have concrete evidence, but I’ve found many inconsistencies in the autopsies. So far, I’ve encountered this in five women.” She sighed.


“I’ve tried communicating this to my bosses, but they’ve disregarded it. At first, I thought it was because these women were witches”, Rio looked guilty saying: “and cops just don’t care about them. Then I started to look further into these deaths. But this morning, I had cops at my door accusing me of killing them all myself. I think I was getting close to the truth, and someone sent those cops to shut me up.”


She was almost out of breath when she finished: “I feel like I’m being hunted. I don’t know who to trust or where to go.” admitted Rio, with a small voice. “I think they know I’m… I’m a witch too.” She did not enjoy the foul taste in her mouth as she pronounced those words.


Rio thinks she never, in her life, ever said this out loud. Never admitted to her true nature. 


Lilia leaned back in her chair, her expression suddenly colder. “And what makes you think I can help you, Dr. Vidal? The world is not what it once was. Witches are no longer feared for their power; they are despised. It has been so for a long time. Even the Serpent’s Coil is a shadow of what it used to be. We survive, but we do not thrive. This has been the life of a witch.”


Rio clenched her fists, a hot frustration bubbling up inside her. “I don’t have time for your lectures. If you can’t help me, just say so, and I’ll leave. But if you can, then tell me what I need to do. I’m not asking for much – just a place to hide and some information that can help me clear my name and stop whoever is behind this.”


Lilia looked unwavering, and Rio stood in her chair, giving way to a flicker of anger – was she starting to sweat? Was the room getting hotter? "They are killing us, Lilia!”


“Us?”


Rio’s spine froze to the eco of that voice. The women with the blue eyes finally moved from her statue-like presence. She was what looked like an inch closer to Rio, enough that she could smell the faint scent of jasmine and something darker and primal. And Rio could not muster enough strength in her to look away from those eyes – feeling almost like she was drowning in an ocean, her skin no longer hot.


Lilia slowly stood and walked around the table.


“You have your mother’s fire, I’ll give you that.” Lilia said, her voice soft but with an edge of steel. “That’s good. You’ll need it. But fire alone won’t save you. You must understand that we are not a charity. If I am to help you, you will need to prove your worth – and to prove you are not here to kill us. Afterall, for all we know, you renounce your nature – who’s to say you aren’t the killer?”


Rio felt again a surge of anger at Lilia’s words, but she couldn’t deny the truth in them. She had spent years burying her magic, convincing herself that she could live a normal life if she just tried hard enough.


“What do you want from me?” Rio asked, her voice barely a whisper. With her world crumbling around her, she was feeling desperate, restless and hungry.

Hungry?

“You’ve spent too long suppressing your magic, Dr. Vidal. You’ve tried to live as one of them, to blend in and pretend you’re something you’re not. But you can’t outrun who you are. Not anymore.”

“What do you want from me?” Rio asked twice, her voice stronger now.


Get hungry, Rio heard a voice in her head.


Lilia’s smile widened, and for the first time, Rio saw a glimpse of something dangerous in her eyes. “I want you to embrace who you are. I want you to stop running and start fighting. The world has tried to erase us, to silence us, but we are still here. It’s bad enough to be erased by others, I can’t let us be erased by our own. And if you want to survive, you’ll need to remember what it means to be a witch.”


Rio’s mind raced as she tried to process Lilia’s words. She had spent so long trying to distance herself from her magic, from the part of her that made her different. Her heart sank a little, as she had hoped for a more straightforward solution.


She wasn’t even quite sure if she would be able to do what was being ask – Rio didn’t even know where to start. But her survival instinct kicked in time. “Fine.”


Lilia’s smile turned almost predatory as she turned to walk back to the table to pick a small, carved box. She opened it and withdrew a vial filled with a dark, swirling liquid. “Like I said before, your mother was smart and very protective of you. It is no wonder that you don’t feel the pull of magic like other witches do.” she said, holding the vial out to Rio. “You will have to be taught the ways of magic and witches, but you have a block that needs to be taken care of first.”


Rio instinctively took the vial with her hand, her fingers brushing against Lilia’s as she did so. “A block?”


“Your mother put a protection spell on you, blocking your chains of magic. You can still use it, but you are very weak now. That is not good if you are thinking of fighting back whoever is hunting you.”


Rio stared at the vial, her stomach churning with fear and doubt. She didn’t know what was in it, truly – and the scientist in her was having her reasonableness being tested to the limit today. She had so many questions – and so little time.


Rio hesitated for only a moment before uncorking the vial and bringing it to her lips. The liquid was bitter and thick, and it burned as it slid down her throat. Almost immediately, she felt a strange warmth spreading through her body, through her arteries and veins, and she could feel something stirring within her, adjusting. Something ancient.


She felt a wave of dizziness. Rio stumbled, clutching the edge of the table for support, her vision blurring.


Lilia seemed surprised. “It is not supposed to affect you that much. That potion only takes care of the block. After so many years ignoring your magic, it should be too dormant for you to feel anything already.” She was now by Rio’s side. “Not before training, anyway.” Rio heard her say, in the direction of the other women.


Set me free.


“I’m fine”. Rio lied, closing her eyes and clenching her fists until she felt her vision going back to normal, and the room coming to a stop. She felt her heart beat faster and a sense of something untamed in her chest. Maybe this is what anxiety feels like. She wasn’t going to let this overwhelm her.


“What’s next? Any more trials for me to prove my worth?” Rio asked with mockery, her teeth clenched as she fought the nausea that was coming from seeing the room spin a few seconds ago.
With this, the blue-eyed women quickly grabbed Rio’s shirt with one hand and forcibly pulled her against the wall – dragging Rio’s body for a few steps with ease.


Rio felt a pain in her back before hearing the other women dangerously say: “Show some respect.”


"Agatha," Lilia said, her voice laced with authority, "Rio is under my protection now”. Agatha did not move one inch. “I’ll have you in charge of her safety. This seems too dangerous to have anyone else. The first thing now is to make sure she stays hidden, to let things cool off a bit, before considering any next steps.”


Agatha nodded, far from thrilled about the arrangement. She let go of Rio’s shirt, but her gaze was still relentless. "As you command, Lilia."


“Take her to one of our safehouses, in the city.” Agatha nodded again, this time turning away from Rio to get her coat that was hanging near the door.


Lilia turned to Rio this time. “She will keep you safe. You will have time to embrace your magic, and in the meantime, we will assist in uncovering the truth behind these murders. We cannot allow witch hunters to think we will not fight back.” Lilia continued. “Agatha is one of our most powerful witches. You would do well to listen to her.”


Rio nodded, shaking the feeling that she was stepping into something far more complicated than she’d anticipated.


“We should go fast.” Agatha said, now sounding irritated. “It’s not safe to stay in one place for too long.”


Rio hesitated, then followed Agatha out of the room after mouthing a “thank you” to Lilia.


As they made their way back through the hallway, her heart was still racing. Rio tried to quiet all her thoughts. She had to focus now on her safety. She would go to the safehouse, let things cool off, then find a way to figure out who was behind the murders, and put them into jail. Only after all of that, would she think about the magic that wanted to take over her body.

Agatha took a step in Rio’s direction before she spoke – her voice still mingled with Death’s: “The ley lines still need repairing.”

The words landed like stones. Agatha took another involuntary step forward, her boots crunching over the brittle, dead earth. Her hands twitched at her sides – reaching, stopping, curling into fists.

Rio's gaze held hers. “And they need a witch's life for that.” A pause. The wind stirred the ashes around them. “I'm sorry, Agatha.”

Agatha's breath left her in a rush. The world seemed to tilt.

“No.”

It wasn't a refusal. It was a plea. A whispered word, raw and fractured, barely audible over the sudden roar of blood in her ears. Her throat burned.

“Rio, please–"

Her voice broke.

And then–

Lilia stepped forward.

 

Rio had just wiped the tears from her face, her fingers trembling against her damp cheeks, when she felt Lilia’s presence beside her. The older woman moved with quiet grace, her footsteps barely stirring the leaves beneath her.

“Rio.” Lilia’s voice was soft but firm. “Can I talk to you?”

Rio swallowed hard, forcing herself to meet Lilia’s steady gaze. The woman’s dark eyes held a depth of sorrow and resolve that made Rio’s chest tighten. “Yeah, of course, Lilia.”

A heavy silence settled between them, broken only by the distant rustle of wind through the trees. Lilia exhaled slowly, as if steadying herself. “Rio… I know how the ley lines work.” Her fingers twitched at her side, as though resisting the urge to reach out. “Given the state you say they’re in, I doubt they’ll accept anything less than a sacrifice. And I know…” She hesitated, her voice dropping to a near whisper. “I know it’s always been the Death’s vessel who pays that price.”

Rio’s breath hitched. She clenched her fists, nails biting into her palms, as if the pain could anchor her against the tide of memories threatening to pull her under. Her mother’s face flashed behind her eyelids – her smile, her tears, the hollow emptiness in her eyes the last time Rio had seen her alive.

Lilia sighed, her shoulders sagging under an invisible weight. “That’s what your mother was so afraid of. Why she couldn’t bear the thought of you inheriting that burden.”

Rio squeezed her eyes shut, fighting back the sting of fresh tears. She didn’t need the reminder – not now, when the wound felt so raw. But Lilia pressed on, her voice unwavering.

“But Rio, I can’t let you do that.” She stepped closer, her hand finally lifting to rest gently on Rio’s arm. “I owe your mother more than I can ever repay. And when the time comes… I’m asking you to take me instead.”
Rio jerked back as if burned. “No, Lilia – I can’t allow that. This is my responsibility. My fate.”

Lilia’s expression hardened, a flicker of stubborn determination lighting her gaze. “Then I’ll ask Death directly. Take me. I’ve lived a long life – longer than I ever expected. And I should have stopped Evanora when we were younger. This is my chance to make amends.”

Rio opened her mouth, but no words came out. The weight of Lilia’s resolve pressed down on her, leaving her breathless.

“Rio.” Lilia continued, her voice softening again, though her grip on Rio’s arm tightened slightly. “I need you to look after my girls. And Billy. They won’t understand my choice. Agatha…” Her voice wavered for the first time, a crack in her composure. “Agatha will be devastated. Please – take care of her. I know I’m asking too much, adding another burden to your shoulders. But do this for me.”

Rio’s throat burned. She wanted to argue, to scream that it wasn’t fair – that Lilia had no right to ask this of her. But the plea in the older woman’s eyes silenced her. Because Lilia wasn’t just asking. She was saying goodbye.

 

“I’m sorry, girls.” Her voice was soft, but it carried across the ruined clearing like a struck bell. “I loved being a witch.”

She turned toward Death, her back straight, her head high – a smile on her lips and a single tear on her eye.

And before anyone could stop her – before Agatha could lunge and scream – Lilia strode into Death her arms outstretched.

Golden light erupted from her skin.

And a white explosion was felt.

 

When the world finally stilled, only the four of them remained in the meadow. Rio’s body still bore the marks of her ordeal – black veins spiderwebbing up her neck, creeping along her cheek like cracks in porcelain – despite her wings retracting and her face all hers again.

She stood motionless, her gaze fixed on Agatha, whose face was a portrait of devastation.

It had all happened too fast. The others hadn’t even realized that Rio had crossed into the realm of the dead and returned, guiding Lilia’s soul along the way. But Agatha knew. She had felt it – the moment Evanora slipped from this world, and the moment the woman who had become her real mother too.

Rio didn’t move. She wasn’t sure what Agatha needed – rage, grief, silence. Whatever it was, Rio would give her space. Time.

Then, without warning, Agatha took a sharp breath and stumbled forward. Her steps were hurried, unsteady, as if her body moved before her mind could catch up. And then–

She crashed into Rio, arms locking around her in a desperate, bone-crushing embrace. A sob tore from her throat, raw and unrestrained, her fingers digging into Rio’s back like she was afraid she might vanish too.

Rio barely registered the flare of pain from her wounds. All that mattered was the warmth of Agatha against her, the proof that she was here, alive, and – somehow – not angry. Rio wrapped her arms around her, holding her as tightly as she dared.

Jen picked up Lilia's serpent pendant from where it had fallen in the grass. “Damn stubborn old witch.” She muttered, her voice thick.

Alice limped over, her injured leg bandaged with strips of her own shirt. “She always had to have the last word.”

They didn’t like Lilia’s choice, but they knew why she had made it.

And for now, that had to be enough.

 

When Rio woke, she felt the sunlight in her face. She moved her face to the other side.

Still, the sun spilled across the bedsheets, warm and golden, painting the room in soft morning hues. The sheets barely covered her legs, tangled from restless sleep, and her back still ached – deep, persistent throbs that came from the wounds of Death’s wings had forced her to sleep face-down.

She was in Agatha’s bed. Agatha’s apartment.

After everything, Agatha hadn’t wanted to stay in Lilia’s house. Jen, Alice, and Billy had taken the apartment above Lilia’s restaurant, but Agatha had asked – no, insisted – that Rio come here. With her.

A gentle touch was tracing along Rio’s spine, feather-light, as if afraid to hurt her further. Agatha’s fingers were playing mindless patterns of purple magic on her naked back. When she felt Rio stir – and Rio’s green magic respond to her touch – Agatha leaned down, pressing a kiss between her shoulder blades, with her hair falling over Rio’s arm.

“Stay here a little longer.” She murmured, her voice thick with sleep.

Rio had no intention of moving, so she just hummed in response.

The last two days had been slow and quiet – a stark contrast to the chaos that had consumed the weeks before. She didn’t want to think about work. Didn’t want to think about the drug dealers scrambling now that Evanora was gone, or the city’s confusion over the magical surges that had shaken Boston.

All she wanted was this: the softness of the sheets beneath her, the steady rhythm of her own breathing, and the knowledge that Agatha was nearby, already brewing coffee.

Rio.

Death’s voice echoed in her mind, familiar.

“Hm?” Rio mumbled into the pillow.

Do you remember when you asked about my connection with Agatha?

Rio stilled. “Yeah. It’s because Evanora tried the transfer between us, right?”

No. Death’s tone was unreadable. Truth is, I don’t have a connection with Agatha. You do.

Rio frowned, her hand scratching her eyes awakened. “What do you mean?”

A pause.

The reason you’re my vessel, Rio, is because you stepped into my world the moment you were born. You died during labor. And when my magic resonates with infants in the underworld, I forge a bond – and they return to life as my vessels. But you and Agatha arrived at the same time. She died in the womb. And when I tried to revive you… your soul refused to go back without her.

Rio’s breath caught.

So, I had revived her too. Death continued. You’ve been tied to her since before either of you took your first breath. Every time I saved her, it was because you asked me to – even if you didn’t realize it. You’d sensed her pain and you’d called me to her.

Rio exhaled slowly, her fingers tightening in the sheets and her eyes finally opening. “Why are you telling me this now?”

Because mortals cling to these things. You call it fate. Soulmates. A rare hint of something almost like amusement colored Death’s voice. I’ve never seen a bond like yours. I didn’t know what to call it. And when I thought you might have to sacrifice yourself to save the world… telling you seemed cruel.

Rio swallowed hard.

“Thank you.” she whispered. “For everything.”

Agatha reappeared in the doorway, holding two steaming mugs. “Oh, already thanking me for the coffee?” She teased, sitting carefully beside Rio.

Rio smiled as Agatha helped her sit up, mindful of her injuries. She took the mug, their fingers brushing, and pressed a slow, lingering kiss to Agatha’s lips before taking a sip – a clear sign of devotion: tasting Agatha before tasting coffee.

Agatha’s hand settled on Rio’s hip, tracing absent circles as they sat in comfortable silence.

“I was thinking,” Agatha said after a moment, “maybe we could stay in today. Jen and Alice are checking what the police knows about the magic surges. We could have a lazy day and stay here. Just… us.”

Rio leaned her head into Agatha’s shoulder, warmth spreading through her chest.

“Coffee in bed and lazy days?” She murmured. “Marry me now.”

Agatha let out a breathy laugh. She leaned to plant a kiss on Rio’s exposed neck. “Buy a girl some diamonds first, Grim Reaper.”

And Rio smiled – because she did know of a few jewelry stores near the Downtown Crossing metro station.
